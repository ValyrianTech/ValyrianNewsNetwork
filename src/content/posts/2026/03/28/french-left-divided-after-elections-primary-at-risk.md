# French Left Divided After Elections, Primary at Risk

French political parties are grappling with deep uncertainty following the recent municipal elections, with the planned left-wing presidential primary now seriously threatened by infighting within the Socialist Party. Green Party leader Marine Tondelier declared on Saturday that "the ball is in the socialists' court" regarding the primary's future, highlighting the fragmented state of French politics as parties prepare for the 2027 presidential race.

## Context: Municipal Elections as Presidential Barometer

The 2026 French municipal elections, held on March 15 and 22, served as the last major electoral test before the 2027 presidential election. According to [Le Monde](https://www.lemonde.fr/politique/article/2026/03/28/apres-les-municipales-les-partis-politiques-en-plein-flou-pour-la-presidentielle_6674843_823448.html), while parties across the spectrum claimed victories in the fragmented local results, the elections primarily accelerated the race for presidential candidacies across political camps. The left-wing parties, in particular, face deepening divisions over strategy and alliances that threaten their unity ahead of the crucial national vote.

## Socialist Party Crisis Threatens Primary

The planned October 11, 2026 left-wing presidential primary is now in serious doubt due to internal crisis within the Socialist Party (PS). Socialist leader Olivier Faure, who supports organizing a primary for non-Mélenchonist left candidates, has been put in minority by party cadres who believe the process cannot deliver victory in 2027, especially without key figures like center-left MEP Raphaël Glucksmann participating.

Sébastien Vincini, a Socialist official close to Faure, has argued that a primary would "launch an open-air competition" and "a race to differentiate" that would be "deadly" for left-wing unity. Instead, he suggests "a governance pact" centered around a "Socialist embodiment" figure—a significant departure from the planned primary process.

## Green Party Positions Itself as Mediator

Green Party leader Marine Tondelier has positioned her party as attempting to hold the fragmented left together. In an interview on France Inter reported by [Le Figaro](https://www.lefigaro.fr/politique/primaire-a-gauche-la-balle-est-dans-le-camp-des-socialistes-affirme-marine-tondelier-20260328), Tondelier stated: "We cannot win alone, neither us nor them. And I would like them to hear it because I see their settling of scores right now."

Regarding the Socialist infighting, Tondelier pointedly asked: "Because if they manage to do their pu-putsch against Olivier Faure, what happens next?" She criticized what she called "peremptory declarations" from PS figures and announced she would publish a manifesto titled "Ce que nous vous devons" ("What we owe you") on Friday, April 3, describing it as "a sort of manual for defending the primary."

## Historical Divisions Resurface

The current crisis echoes historical divisions within the French left that have undermined its presidential prospects for years. Since the 2017 presidential election when Benoît Hamon (PS) and Jean-Luc Mélenchon (La France Insoumise) both ran, splitting the left-wing vote, unity has been elusive. The 2022 attempt at a unified primary that selected Jean-Luc Mélenchon—who came third with 21.95% of votes—demonstrated both the potential and limitations of left-wing cooperation.

The municipal elections exposed particularly sharp tensions between the more radical La France Insoumise (LFI) and the moderate Socialists over local alliance strategies. These tensions have now spilled over into the presidential preparation phase, with some Socialists wanting to completely bypass the planned primary.

## Right-Wing Contrast: Relative Stability

While the left fragments, the right appears more organized in its presidential preparations. The Rassemblement National (RN) has a clear succession plan with Jordan Bardella likely to be the candidate if Marine Le Pen remains ineligible. Les Républicains (LR) are debating their candidate selection process but show less public division than their left-wing counterparts.

This contrast in organizational stability could prove significant in the 2027 race, where a united front has historically been crucial for electoral success.

## Implications for 2027 Presidential Election

The left's fragmentation increases the likelihood of a right-wing victory in 2027, continuing a trend that has seen the French left struggle in presidential elections since François Hollande's 2012 victory. The current crises could lead to significant realignment of French left-wing politics, potentially diminishing the Socialist Party's role or prompting new political formations.

The failure of this primary attempt could also end the left's experiment with unified primaries, returning to the previous model of multiple left-wing candidates competing against each other—a scenario that has historically benefited right-wing candidates.

## What's Next

The immediate question is whether the October 11 primary will actually take place. With key figures like Raphaël Glucksmann refusing to participate and growing Socialist opposition to the process, the primary's prospects diminish daily. Marine Tondelier's upcoming manifesto may provide a roadmap for primary defenders, but without Socialist buy-in, its impact will be limited.

Longer term, the municipal election results—where Socialists will govern approximately 800 municipalities—will test whether local governance success can translate to presidential politics. The coming months will reveal whether the French left can overcome its divisions or whether the 2027 presidential election will mark another chapter in its ongoing fragmentation.

As Marine Tondelier warned about the current infighting: "When we mine a battlefield, even if one day the war ends, the mines remain. So what is happening now, this ongoing process, has an irreversible side." The question for the French left is whether this irreversibility leads to permanent division or forces an eventual reconciliation.
