# Council of State Suspends Migration Minister's Decision on Asylum Seekers

Belgium's Council of State has suspended a decision by Asylum and Migration Minister Anneleen Van Bossuyt to cease providing social assistance to asylum seekers who already enjoy protection in another European Union member state. The ruling, issued on Friday, March 27, 2026, prevents potentially vulnerable individuals from facing "complete destitution" and having to "sleep in the street," according to the court's assessment [reported by BRF](https://brf.be/national/2062805/).

## The Council of State's Ruling

The Council of State (Conseil d'État/Raad van State), Belgium's supreme administrative court, intervened through an "extreme urgency" procedure. According to the BRF report, the court found that the minister's decision "exposes immediately the persons concerned to the risk of finding themselves in a situation of complete destitution and having to sleep in the street."

The court also noted that "the minister should have consulted the legislation section of the Council of State before issuing instructions to Fedasil," referring to the federal agency responsible for asylum seeker reception in Belgium.

## Background and Minister's Policy

Minister Anneleen Van Bossuyt of the Flemish nationalist party N-VA had sought to implement stricter asylum measures that included limiting or withdrawing material assistance—such as board, lodging, and social aid—for asylum seekers already granted protection elsewhere in the EU. This policy approach emerged amid ongoing pressure on Belgium's asylum reception facilities.

The BRF report indicates this marks the second judicial intervention in the minister's approach, following a previous suspension by Belgium's Constitutional Court.

## Legal and Humanitarian Implications

The Council of State's decision highlights the tension between migration policy objectives and judicial oversight of fundamental rights. By prioritizing protection against destitution, the court has reaffirmed the importance of safeguarding vulnerable individuals from homelessness.

The immediate implication of the ruling is that asylum seekers previously denied assistance must now receive support through Fedasil, preventing homelessness among this vulnerable population.

## Significance of the Judicial Intervention

The suspension represents a significant legal check on the government's migration policy. As Belgium's supreme administrative court, the Council of State has exercised its constitutional role in reviewing the legality of administrative decisions.

The ruling ensures that, at minimum, vulnerable individuals will not face street homelessness as Belgium's legal and political systems continue to grapple with complex migration challenges.

## Unanswered Questions and Next Steps

While the ruling provides immediate protection for affected asylum seekers, several questions remain:

## Looking Ahead

The Belgian government now faces the task of developing an alternative approach that complies with court rulings while addressing legitimate concerns about reception capacity. The Council of State's intervention serves as a reminder of the importance of judicial oversight in protecting fundamental rights, even in politically charged policy areas.

For now, the suspension stands as a reaffirmation that vulnerable individuals must be protected from destitution, regardless of their immigration status or previous protections in other EU countries.
