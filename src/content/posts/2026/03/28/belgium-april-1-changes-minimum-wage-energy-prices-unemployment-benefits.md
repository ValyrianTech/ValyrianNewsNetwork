# Belgium's April 1 Policy Changes: Wage, Energy, Benefits

Starting April 1, 2026, Belgium will implement several significant economic and social policy changes affecting workers, consumers, businesses, and vulnerable populations nationwide. According to [Le Soir](https://www.lesoir.be/737518/article/2026-03-28/salaire-minimum-tarifs-de-lenergie-gare-du-nord-tout-ce-qui-change-des-le-1er), the coordinated package includes minimum wage increases, adjustments to social energy tariffs, unemployment benefit reforms, new business regulations in Brussels, and mandatory electronic invoicing requirements for companies.

## Minimum Wage Increase Takes Effect

The most direct impact for workers comes from a €35.7 gross increase in the minimum wage, raising the Guaranteed Minimum Monthly Average Income (RMMMG) to €2,189.81 gross per month. This mandatory threshold applies to all sectors for workers aged 18 and older, though sectors frequently negotiate higher wages through collective bargaining agreements. The increase represents a standard periodic adjustment aimed at maintaining purchasing power amid ongoing economic changes.

## Social Energy Tariff Adjustments

Vulnerable households will face higher energy costs as social tariffs for gas and electricity increase in the second quarter of 2026. According to the Le Soir report, electricity social tariffs rise by 2.6% compared to the previous quarter, with specific rates set at 23.767 cents/kWh for single-rate tariffs, 25.468 cents/kWh for dual-rate day tariffs, 24.257 cents/kWh for dual-rate night tariffs, and 20.309 cents/kWh for exclusive night tariffs. Natural gas and heat social tariffs increase by 5.1% to 4.746 cents/kWh.

## Unemployment Benefits Reform Enters Third Phase

The federal government's unemployment reform, which limits benefits to a maximum of two years, reaches its third implementation phase on April 1. This stage affects job seekers with less than eight years of complete unemployment during their careers, who will lose their benefits. The National Employment Office (ONEM) projects that nearly 168,000 people will be excluded from unemployment benefits by July 1, 2027, as the phased implementation continues through subsequent months.

## Early Business Closures in Brussels North District

In a targeted security measure, businesses in Brussels-Brabant and North districts—including brothels on Rue d'Aerschot—must close between 1 AM and 6 AM. This temporary three-month measure forms part of a broader security plan for areas near Brussels-North Station, where drug sales and consumption (particularly crack and heroin) have been persistent problems. Authorities suspect some establishments of facilitating money laundering for drug trafficking operations. Additional measures include extended bans on public alcohol sales and consumption, along with increased police presence in the affected arrondissements.

## PEPPOL Electronic Invoicing Sanctions Begin

Belgian VAT-registered companies face new compliance requirements as the grace period for connecting to the PEPPOL network for electronic invoices expires on April 1. The Federal Public Service Finance (SPF Finance) will begin applying sanctions to companies that fail to meet the technical requirements for sending and receiving structured electronic invoices. According to the government portal [efactuur.belgium.be](https://efactuur.belgium.be/nl), a three-month grace period may apply if companies can demonstrate they took timely and reasonable steps to comply. Sanctions will be assessed individually based on each company's circumstances.

## Broader Policy Context

These simultaneous changes reflect Belgium's ongoing efforts to balance economic competitiveness, social protection, public security, and administrative modernization. The minimum wage adjustment follows established mechanisms for maintaining worker purchasing power, while energy tariff changes respond to market conditions while attempting to protect vulnerable households through social tariffs.

The unemployment benefit reforms represent a significant restructuring of Belgium's social safety net, reflecting broader debates about the sustainability of social security systems amid demographic and economic pressures. The Brussels security measures demonstrate a targeted approach to urban challenges, combining regulatory interventions with increased law enforcement presence.

## Implementation and Monitoring

All changes take effect simultaneously on April 1, creating a coordinated implementation challenge for government agencies, businesses, and affected individuals. The unemployment reforms will continue in subsequent phases, with the Brussels business closures initially set for three months pending evaluation of their effectiveness.

Businesses face particular challenges adapting to the PEPPOL requirements, which align with European Union digital single market initiatives aimed at standardizing electronic business processes across member states. Companies without the necessary technical infrastructure risk financial penalties, though authorities emphasize individual assessment of compliance efforts.

## Looking Ahead

The coming months will reveal how these policy changes interact with broader economic trends and social dynamics in Belgium. Key areas to watch include the impact of minimum wage increases on employment levels in low-wage sectors, the effectiveness of targeted security measures in Brussels, and the compliance rate with electronic invoicing requirements across the business community.

As these reforms unfold, coordination between federal and regional authorities will be crucial, particularly regarding social policies and urban security measures that span jurisdictional boundaries. The breadth of changes across multiple policy domains suggests careful planning to manage cumulative impacts, though attentive monitoring will be necessary to identify both intended and unintended consequences.
