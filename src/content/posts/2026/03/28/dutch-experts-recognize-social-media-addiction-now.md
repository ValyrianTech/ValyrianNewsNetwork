# Dutch Experts: Recognize Social Media Addiction Now

Leading Dutch mental health experts are urgently calling for social media addiction to be officially recognized as a legitimate disorder, warning that the pervasive nature of digital platforms requires immediate attention and formal diagnostic status. According to a [report by RTL Nieuws](https://www.rtlnieuws.nl/nieuws/artikel/5583067/socialemedia-verslaving-moet-officieel-worden-erkend-roepen-experts-geen), formal recognition would enable better treatment pathways, facilitate scientific research, and raise public awareness about the harmful effects of excessive social media use.

## Current Diagnostic Void

Unlike gaming addiction, gambling addiction, or alcohol addiction—all officially recognized in diagnostic manuals—social media addiction currently lacks formal status as a disorder. This diagnostic gap leaves affected individuals without clear treatment pathways and hampers scientific investigation into effective interventions. "We have no time to lose," cognitive neuroscientist Surya Gayet told RTL Nieuws. "We cannot leave people with an addiction to their fate just because legislation cannot keep up with technological changes."

The absence of official recognition creates practical barriers for those seeking help. Ina Koning, a researcher specializing in social media addiction among youth who advises the Dutch government on social media guidelines, explained: "I'm not just for putting a sticker or label on everything, but with recognition we can help people more easily."

## Prevalence and Impact

Statistics reveal a concerning landscape of social media use in the Netherlands. According to RTL Nieuws' research panel:

- 60% of young people believe they are addicted to social media
- 28% of youth have screen time exceeding 3 hours per day, up from 23% last year
- 43% of adults think they themselves are addicted to social media
- Almost two-thirds of parents of minors believe their child is addicted

Despite widespread self-perception of addiction, actual clinical prevalence appears lower but still significant. Koning's research indicates that 4-6% of youth meet criteria for actual addiction, while approximately 40% show high risk of problematic use. "It's not so much about the number of hours someone spends on social media," Koning clarified, "but especially about how that use affects daily life. In part of this group, you see that life really becomes disrupted."

## Case Study: Esmee's 16-Hour Daily Struggle

The human cost of unchecked social media addiction is vividly illustrated by the case of Esmee, an 18-year-old who sought clinical treatment after her phone use consumed her life. "I became awake, went on my phone and basically stayed in bed all day," she recounted. "I was really addicted. I locked myself up and was only busy with my phone. I hardly went to school anymore."

At her peak, Esmee spent 16 hours daily on her device before entering treatment at Yes We Can Clinics, which already offers therapy for social media addiction despite the lack of official recognition. Derek van Enk from the clinic explained their approach: "Youngsters sit here for several weeks, without a phone. Every day there is a program. We really investigate what the underlying problem is. We tackle problems all day by talking, group sessions, and we do a lot of outdoor activities. They learn here that the phone is not a solution."

## Neurological Mechanisms and 'Mental Itch'

The scientific basis for recognizing social media addiction rests on established neurological mechanisms. "The mechanism in the brain is the same," Gayet explained. "Your brain gets a rush when you open the app and scroll down. Because it's inexhaustible, you keep going with it."

Researchers have identified what Gayet calls "mental itch"—the mere anticipation of notifications disrupts concentration even without actual alerts. "Long-term research on the consequences for the brain is not yet available," Gayet noted, "but if you are constantly distracted by notifications, you don't train your 'attention muscle,' making it harder to concentrate."

Behavioral scientist Hilde Brons expressed surprise at the continued lack of recognition: "It can be so disruptive, certainly on the lives of young people. On their sleep, on their concentration, and on their self-confidence. That's why I see an important role for parents: they must set clear rules and start the conversation about social media."

## International Regulatory Context

The Dutch experts' call comes amid growing international concern about social media's addictive design. The European Commission is preparing a Digital Fairness Act expected in late 2026, aiming to tackle addictive features by potentially disabling them by default and allowing users to opt-out of algorithm-driven recommendations.

However, significant industry resistance exists. A February 2026 Corporate Europe Observatory report revealed extensive lobbying against regulation, with tech companies spending €151 million annually in Brussels—a 33.6% increase since 2023. Industry representatives accounted for 83% of 96 meetings with Commission officials on the Digital Fairness Act since December 2024.

Global regulatory momentum is building. Australia implemented a social media ban for children under 16 in December 2025, while Austria recently announced a ban for those under 14. Malaysia and France are likely to follow with similar restrictions, and the World Health Organization has recommended regulating smartphones like tobacco products.

## Historical Parallels and Political Challenges

The path to recognition faces familiar obstacles. "It's just a matter of time," predicted Arnt Schellekens, National Rapporteur on Addictions. "It's just a very nasty addiction, with major consequences. You see the impact on people's thinking, feeling and acting." He noted that game addiction recognition took several years to achieve, suggesting a similar timeline for social media.

Historical parallels with other industries are striking. The 2021 Frances Haugen leaks revealed Facebook's internal research showing Instagram harmed teen girls, with 13.5% reporting worsened suicidal thoughts and 17% citing contributions to eating disorders. In 2025, Meta's Project Mercury found quitting Facebook reduced anxiety and depression, but the company reportedly buried the findings when they contradicted corporate claims—a pattern compared to tobacco industry suppression of harmful research.

Political headwinds also complicate regulatory efforts. The European Union's deregulation agenda prioritizes "competitiveness," while the U.S. government under President Trump has attacked EU digital regulations. Growing MAGA-inspired far-right influence in European politics further challenges comprehensive digital wellbeing legislation.

## Treatment Landscape and Research Needs

Despite the diagnostic gap, treatment options are emerging. Yes We Can Clinics report increasing admissions for social media addiction in recent years. Treatment typically involves phone-free periods, structured daily programs, group therapy, and outdoor activities focused on addressing underlying psychological issues rather than merely reducing screen time.

Experts welcome these developments but emphasize the need for evidence-based approaches. "It's good that treatments are starting, we can learn from that," Koning said. "But it's not scientifically proven. For that, recognition is needed, and more research."

## Public Opinion and Policy Support

Dutch public opinion shows strong support for protective measures. A January 2026 survey found 63% of Dutch citizens support a social media ban for children under 16, up from 57% the previous year. Approximately 5.5 million Dutch people are actively trying to reduce their social media consumption, reflecting growing public awareness of potential harms.

Proposed Dutch solutions include a "Digital Red Button" system allowing users to easily delete personal data from platforms, promotion of "dumb phones" and offline alternatives for youth, and focusing regulatory efforts on disabling addictive algorithmic design features by default.

## Looking Forward

The experts' urgent call represents a critical moment in the evolving understanding of digital wellbeing. Formal recognition of social media addiction would enable insurance coverage for treatments, standardize diagnostic criteria, facilitate longitudinal research on neurological impacts, and potentially drive platform redesign toward healthier interaction models.

As Gayet emphasized: "We have no time to lose. We cannot leave people with an addiction to their fate just because legislation cannot keep up with technological changes." With international regulatory momentum building and public concern increasing, the coming years may see social media addiction follow gaming addiction's path to official recognition—potentially transforming how societies address one of the defining mental health challenges of the digital age.
