# Houthis Launch First Missile Attack on Israel in Current Iran War

Yemen's Iran-backed Houthi rebels launched their first missile attack on Israel since the outbreak of the current Iran-Israel war, marking a significant expansion of the conflict to include Yemen-based forces. The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) detected and intercepted the missile early Saturday morning, triggering sirens in southern Israel as the Houthis confirmed the attack was in response to Israeli strikes across the region.

## The Attack and Immediate Response

According to [Haaretz](https://www.haaretz.co.il/news/politics/war-2023/2026-03-28/ty-article-live/0000019d-3159-d77a-a7ff-fff9758b0000), the IDF first detected a missile launch from Yemen toward Israel at approximately 06:41 local time on March 28, 2026. The Home Front Command immediately warned of impending sirens in Yeruham and surrounding areas in southern Israel.

By 10:29, the Houthis confirmed they had fired toward Israel, stating in an official announcement that their attack was "in response to Israeli attacks in Iran, Lebanon, Iraq, and Palestinian territories." The group emphasized, "We will continue until aggression stops on all resistance fronts," signaling their intention to maintain pressure on Israel.

## Strategic Threat to Global Shipping

The attack was accompanied by a significant economic threat from Houthi leadership. At 12:31, senior Houthi official Mohammed Mansour escalated tensions by threatening to close the Bab el-Mandeb Strait, a critical global trade route through which approximately one-eighth of world commerce passes. In an interview with Qatari Al-Araby TV, Mansour stated that his organization was "considering closing the Bab el-Mandeb Strait" and noted the Houthis were "managing this struggle gradually."

This threat represents a major escalation beyond the immediate military confrontation, potentially impacting global shipping lanes that connect the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden and ultimately the Indian Ocean. The Bab el-Mandeb Strait serves as a vital choke point for oil shipments from the Persian Gulf to Europe and North America.

## Pre-Attack Warnings and Regional Context

The Houthi attack followed explicit warnings issued earlier the same day. At 00:01 on March 28, the Houthis had declared they would join the war if Israel or the United States used the Red Sea to attack Iran. A spokesperson for the Houthi military forces stated, "Our fingers are on the trigger," and indicated the group would join the fighting even if additional allies joined Israel and the U.S. in combat against Iran.

The attack occurs on the 29th day of the widening Iran-Israel war, which has already drawn in multiple regional actors. The Houthis, formally known as Ansar Allah, have controlled northwest Yemen since 2014 and have served as a key Iranian proxy, receiving military, financial, and political support from Tehran.

## Historical Precedent and Capabilities

The Houthis possess demonstrated capability to threaten regional shipping and Israeli territory. During previous regional conflicts, the group has launched attacks toward Israel and conducted operations against maritime traffic in the Red Sea. Their control of strategic Yemeni coastline positions them to potentially disrupt one of the world's most important shipping corridors.

## Implications and Regional Response

The Houthi entry into the conflict represents more than just another missile attack on Israel. It opens a new front in the Arabian Peninsula and introduces the possibility of economic warfare through the threatened closure of critical shipping lanes. While the immediate military threat to Israel may be limited given the country's advanced air defense systems, the strategic implications for regional stability and global trade are substantial.

The attack underscores the interconnected nature of Middle Eastern conflicts and the challenge of containing escalation in a multi-proxy war environment. As the conflict enters its second month with new fronts opening, the international community faces increasing pressure to broker a ceasefire before regional stability and global economic security suffer irreversible damage.

## What to Watch Next

The critical question now is whether the Houthis will follow through on their threat to close the Bab el-Mandeb Strait to shipping. Such action would compound existing economic pressures from regional conflict and could have significant consequences for global trade patterns and energy markets.

Military analysts note that while Houthi missile attacks pose an additional challenge for Israel's air defense systems, the real threat lies in the economic domain, where Houthi control over the Bab el-Mandeb Strait gives them leverage over approximately 12% of global trade.

As diplomatic efforts continue across the region, the Houthi attack on Israel serves as a stark reminder that in today's interconnected Middle East, local conflicts rarely remain local for long, and economic choke points can become strategic weapons in broader geopolitical confrontations.
