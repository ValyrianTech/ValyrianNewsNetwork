# Major Fire at Hazardous Waste Facility in Maastricht Near Belgian Border

A major fire broke out early Saturday at a PreZero hazardous waste processing facility in the port area of Maastricht, Netherlands, approximately 2 kilometers from the Belgian border. The blaze, which involved dangerous materials including batteries and other hazardous substances, prompted a large-scale emergency response with specialized equipment including firefighting robots, drones, and vehicles with extended firefighting arms. According to [Le Soir](https://www.lesoir.be/737509/article/2026-03-28/important-incendie-dans-un-entrepot-de-dechets-dangereux-proche-de-la-frontiere), the fire has been brought under control, but significant environmental concerns remain due to the nature of the materials involved and the proximity to residential areas on both sides of the Dutch-Belgian border.

## Context and Location

The fire erupted in the early morning at the PreZero Special Waste facility located at Ankerkade 21 in the Beatrixhaven port area of Maastricht. PreZero, a German-based waste management and recycling company part of the Schwarz Group, operates a hazardous waste processing division at this location, handling materials including batteries, accumulators, and other dangerous substances requiring specialized treatment. The facility's proximity to the Belgian border—just 2 kilometers as the crow flies from Lanaken, Belgium—raised immediate cross-border concerns about potential environmental and public health impacts.

Emergency services were alerted shortly after the fire started and responded with what [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/03/28/grote-brand-in-loods-met-gevaarlijk-afval-in-maastricht/) described as a "large-scale deployment" involving multiple fire departments from the region. The Safety Region South Limburg (Veiligheidsregio Zuid-Limburg) coordinated the response, which included assistance from the industrial fire department of the Chemelot chemical complex in Geleen.

## Advanced Firefighting Technology Deployed

To combat the blaze in the hazardous environment, emergency services deployed cutting-edge firefighting technology. According to reports from both Le Soir and VRT NWS, a firefighting robot on caterpillar tracks was sent into the building to extinguish flames and gather information with built-in cameras, allowing firefighters to assess the situation without exposing themselves to danger. The robot "can be sent inside the building to avoid exposing firefighters to any danger," noted Le Soir.

Additional specialized equipment included a drone team from the Chemelot industrial complex fire service for aerial assessment, a vehicle with an extra-long firefighting arm (over 30 meters in reach), and multiple elevating platforms (hoogwerkers). Marcel Penders, spokesperson for Safety Region South Limburg, explained to local media that "the extinguishing will take some time and a firefighting robot has been deployed because there are hazardous materials in the warehouse."

## Environmental Monitoring and Public Health Advisory

Despite the hazardous nature of the materials involved, continuous environmental measurements showed no dangerous concentrations of substances released into the air, according to fire department statements reported by [AD.nl](https://www.ad.nl/binnenland/grote-brand-in-loods-met-gevaarlijk-afval-in-maastricht-brandweer-rukt-massaal-uit~a38b0d42/). "Measurements show that no dangerous concentrations have been released. There are also no casualties," a fire department spokesperson told VRT NWS.

However, authorities issued precautionary health advisories due to the smoke plume moving northeast toward Bunde in the Netherlands. Residents in Maastricht, Bunde, and the Belgian municipality of Lanaken were advised to keep windows and doors closed and turn off mechanical ventilation systems. Some residents reported odor nuisance, prompting officials to reiterate the same precautions.

## Current Status and Structural Assessment

As of late morning on March 28, the fire was reported to be under control, though extinguishing operations were expected to continue for some time. The building remained standing, but an engineer was en route to evaluate its structural integrity and determine whether it was safe to enter. A large safety perimeter remained cordoned off around the facility, making nearby businesses inaccessible.

"Which hazardous substances are present in the burning warehouse at the Ankerkade is not yet known," a fire department spokesperson told Le Soir. "Our reconnaissance units are taking measurements." The exact cause of the fire also remained under investigation.

## Cross-Border Implications and Regulatory Context

The incident highlights the complex challenges of hazardous waste facilities located near international borders. Previous incidents involving dangerous materials in border regions have underscored the need for coordinated emergency response between Dutch and Belgian authorities. The Maastricht facility's location in a port area adds additional environmental concerns, as firefighting runoff could potentially contain hazardous materials that might affect water systems.

PreZero's operations in Maastricht specialize in processing "Special Waste" including batteries, chemicals, and other hazardous materials requiring specialized treatment. The company, part of the German Schwarz Group that also owns discount supermarket chains Lidl and Kaufland, has faced increased scrutiny of hazardous waste facility safety protocols following similar incidents elsewhere in Europe.

## What's Next: Environmental Remediation and Investigation

While immediate firefighting efforts focus on complete extinguishment and structural assessment, longer-term concerns include potential environmental remediation needs. Soil and water testing may be required to ensure no contamination resulted from the blaze or firefighting operations. The incident will likely prompt regulatory review of safety protocols at hazardous waste facilities, particularly those located near populated areas or international borders.

For residents on both sides of the border, the incident serves as a reminder of the potential risks associated with industrial facilities handling dangerous materials. As emergency services continue their work, authorities maintain that current measurements show no immediate health threat, but advise continued precautionary measures until the situation is fully resolved.

The deployment of advanced firefighting technology like the robot and drone team represents a growing trend in emergency response to hazardous incidents, allowing safer assessment and intervention in dangerous environments. As investigations continue, the focus will shift to understanding the fire's cause, assessing full environmental impact, and implementing any necessary safety improvements to prevent similar incidents in the future.
