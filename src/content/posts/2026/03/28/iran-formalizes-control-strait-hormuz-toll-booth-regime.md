# Iran Formalizes Control Over Strait of Hormuz, Establishes 'Toll Booth' Regime

Iran has begun formalizing its control over the strategic Strait of Hormuz, establishing what shipping intelligence firm Lloyd's List Intelligence calls a "de facto 'toll booth' regime" that could generate billions in annual revenue while violating international maritime law. The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) has taken control of the critical waterway, requiring ships to enter Iranian territorial waters, undergo inspection, and pay fees for passage—with at least two vessels already paying tolls in Chinese yuan.

According to [AP News](https://apnews.com/article/iran-hormuz-shipping-tolls-china-de5159966cde7de7b964b3c2c67eec07), shipping traffic through the strait has fallen by 90% since the start of the "Iran war" in early March 2026, sending global oil prices skyrocketing and creating alarming shortages in Asian nations that depend on Persian Gulf oil. Only about 150 vessels have transited since March 1—barely more than one day's normal traffic before the conflict.

## Context: The World's Most Critical Oil Chokepoint

The Strait of Hormuz, a 21-nautical-mile-wide passage between the Persian Gulf and the Gulf of Oman, represents the world's most important artery for oil shipments. Approximately 20 million barrels of crude oil pass through daily—about 20-30% of global oil trade—along with significant volumes of liquefied natural gas (LNG). For decades, Iran has threatened to close the strait during regional tensions, but the current conflict has seen Tehran transform that threat into reality.

Iran's actions appear driven by both strategic ambition and economic necessity. As one of the world's most heavily sanctioned countries, Tehran sees toll collection as a potential revenue source to compensate for restricted access to global markets. "Iran has been a little taken aback by how successful its (Hormuz) strategy has been—by how cheap and how comparatively easy it is to hold the global economy hostage," said Dina Esfandiary, Middle East lead at Bloomberg Economics, in an interview with CNN.

## The 'Toll Booth' System in Practice

Shipping intelligence reports reveal a carefully orchestrated system. Normally, vessels use a two-lane shipping channel in the middle of the strait, but increasingly ships are taking a northern route around Larak Island, placing them in Iran's territorial waters. According to Lloyd's List Intelligence, entities wanting safe passage must submit details to "approved intermediaries" of the Revolutionary Guard, including cargo information, ownership details, destination, and complete crew lists.

Approved vessels receive a code and are escorted by IRGC vessels. Oil shipments receive priority, and all vessels undergo "geopolitical vetting." While not all ships pay direct tolls, at least two have done so, with payments settled in Chinese yuan—a detail confirmed by multiple intelligence sources.

The system has created dramatic shifts in shipping patterns. About half of vessels turn off their Automatic Identification Systems (AIS) before passing through, reappearing on the other side in the Gulf of Oman. This caution is understandable: at least 18 ships have been attacked and seven crew members killed since the conflict began, according to the UN's International Maritime Organization.

## Iranian Legislation and International Response

Iranian parliament is working on legislation to formalize the toll collection system. Mohammadreza Rezaei Kouchi, an Iranian parliamentarian, told the Fars and Tasnim news agencies—both close to the Revolutionary Guard—that "parliament is pursuing a plan to formally codify Iran's sovereignty, control and oversight over the Strait of Hormuz, while also creating a source of revenue through the collection of fees."

The international response has been swift and condemnatory. US Secretary of State Marco Rubio warned that establishing a tolling system would be "illegal" and "unacceptable," stressing the need for a global plan to confront it after the war ends. Following a G7 meeting in France, foreign ministers from the group emphasized "the absolute necessity" to restore "safe and toll-free freedom of navigation."

Regional powers have been particularly vocal. Sultan al-Jaber, CEO of the UAE's state-run Abu Dhabi National Oil Company (ADNOC), called Iran's control "economic terrorism." In a speech in Washington, he stated: "Weaponizing the Strait of Hormuz is not an act of aggression against one nation. It is economic terrorism against every consumer, every family that depends on affordable energy and food."

Jasem Mohamed al-Budaiwi, Secretary General of the Gulf Cooperation Council, said Iran's collection of fees represents "an aggression and a violation of the United Nations agreement on the law of the sea."

## Legal Violations and Precedents

Iran's actions appear to violate established principles of international maritime law. Article 19 of the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS) requires countries to allow "innocent passage" of peaceful vessels through their territorial waters. While neither Iran nor the United States are parties to UNCLOS, many of its principles are considered customary international law.

"Imposing transit fees is a violation of the rules of transit passage," said James Kraska, a professor of international maritime law at the US Naval War College, in the [CNN report](https://www.cnn.com/2026/03/28/middleeast/iran-strait-of-hormuz-toll-intl). "There is no legal basis under international law for a coastal state to charge fees in an international strait like Hormuz."

Historical precedent offers little support for Iran's position. In the 19th century, Denmark imposed transit fees through the Danish Straits but abolished them following international protests through the 1857 Copenhagen Convention.

## Economic Implications and Revenue Potential

The financial stakes are enormous. Normally, 20 million barrels of crude oil pass through the strait daily—roughly equivalent to 10 very large crude carriers (VLCCs). At a reported fee of $2 million per tanker, this could translate to $20 million daily or about $600 million monthly from oil alone. Including LNG shipments, monthly revenues could exceed $800 million—rivaling Egypt's Suez Canal revenues, which typically range from $700-800 million monthly.

For Iran, this represents a potential windfall. The country's monthly oil export revenue in 2024 was approximately $4-5 billion, meaning toll collection could supplement sanctioned income by 15-20%.

However, the disruption has already had severe global consequences. Asian nations dependent on Persian Gulf oil face alarming shortages, while global energy markets have been thrown into turmoil. The conflict has forced countries worldwide to take emergency measures to secure fuel supplies, with wealthy nations pledging record releases of emergency oil reserves in a bid to calm surging prices.

## Strategic Implications and Future Scenarios

Iran's new demands in peace negotiations include formal recognition of its sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz—a condition not present in previous diplomatic exchanges. This suggests Tehran seeks to transform temporary wartime leverage into permanent strategic advantage.

Mojtaba Khamenei, in his first purported address as Iran's new supreme leader, emphasized that the leverage of blocking the waterway "must continue to be used." This indicates that control over Hormuz has become central to Iran's strategic calculus, regardless of how the current conflict concludes.

The situation presents several possible futures:

1. **Permanent Toll System**: Iran succeeds in establishing a recognized toll collection regime, fundamentally altering global shipping economics and giving Tehran ongoing leverage over energy markets.

2. **International Force Intervention**: A coalition led by the US or other powers intervenes militarily to reopen the strait, potentially escalating the conflict.

3. **Diplomatic Resolution**: Negotiations lead to a compromise where Iran reopens the strait in exchange for sanctions relief or other concessions.

4. **Continued Paralysis**: The shipping industry remains effectively paralyzed, forcing permanent rerouting of energy supplies and fundamentally reshaping global trade patterns.

## What's Next: Global Economy at a Crossroads

The Strait of Hormuz crisis represents more than a regional conflict—it's a stress test for the global economic order. Iran has demonstrated that a single nation can effectively hold 20% of the world's oil trade hostage, revealing vulnerabilities in global energy security that extend far beyond the Middle East.

As Richard Meade, editor-in-chief of Lloyd's List, told CNN: "This is happening. And I suspect it's going to happen slightly more frequently if we don't see some progress in terms of the negotiations. But as we speak right now, the shipping industry is effectively in paralysis."

The international community faces difficult choices: accept Iran's de facto control and the precedent it sets for other strategic chokepoints, or mount a coordinated response that could escalate military tensions. Meanwhile, the crisis has accelerated discussions about energy diversification and renewable alternatives, with some analysts suggesting it may ultimately strengthen the case for transitioning away from fossil fuel dependence.

For now, the world watches as Iran formalizes what began as wartime leverage into what could become a permanent feature of global trade—a "toll booth" at the world's most critical energy chokepoint, with implications that will reverberate through global markets and geopolitics for years to come.
