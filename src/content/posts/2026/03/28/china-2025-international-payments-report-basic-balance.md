# China Reports $735 Billion Current Account Surplus in 2025

China's State Administration of Foreign Exchange (SAFE) released its 2025 International Payments Report on March 27, 2026, revealing the country maintained basic balance in its international payments with a substantial $735 billion current account surplus offset by significant capital outflows. The report, [published by SAFE](https://www.safe.gov.cn/en/2026/0327/2406.html), shows China's external economic relations remained stable despite global uncertainties, reflecting both continued export strength and increased overseas investment.

## Context: Understanding International Payments Balance

A country's international payments balance tracks all economic transactions between residents and non-residents over a specific period. The "basic balance" concept refers to the equilibrium between the current account (trade in goods and services, income flows) and the capital and financial account (investment flows). For China, achieving basic balance indicates macroeconomic stability and prudent financial management, particularly important amid ongoing global trade tensions and economic volatility.

China's 2025 economic performance was marked by achieving its 5.0% GDP growth target and recording a record $1.2 trillion trade surplus for the year. These factors set the stage for the international payments data, which provides a comprehensive view of how China's economic interactions with the rest of the world evolved during a period of significant global uncertainty.

## Key Data from the 2025 Report

According to the official SAFE data, China's current account recorded a surplus of $735.0 billion (RMB 5,243.4 billion) in 2025, equivalent to 3.7% of GDP. This was accompanied by a capital and financial account deficit of $773.5 billion, resulting in what the report describes as "basic balance" in international payments.

The current account surplus comprised several components:
- **Goods trade:** Surplus of $106.06 billion
- **Services trade:** Deficit of $23.81 billion  
- **Primary income:** Deficit of $10.95 billion
- **Secondary income:** Surplus of $2.21 billion

Total current account transactions reached $8.3 trillion, representing a 3% year-on-year increase. The financial account (excluding reserve assets) showed a deficit of $82.01 billion, with direct investment recording a $7.72 billion deficit, portfolio investment a $42.56 billion deficit, and other investment a $29.32 billion deficit.

In the fourth quarter of 2025 alone, China's current account surplus was $243.8 billion, with a goods trade surplus of $310.3 billion partially offset by a services trade deficit of $48.5 billion.

## Analysis: What the Numbers Reveal

The [People's Daily reported](http://finance.people.com.cn/n1/2026/0328/c1004-40690547.html) that "China's foreign exchange market showed strong resilience and vitality, with market expectations and transactions being rational and orderly. The RMB exchange rate remained basically stable at a reasonable and balanced level." The newspaper emphasized that the simultaneous increase in current account surplus and financial account deficit "reflects closer ties with the world economy and a more open attitude to integrate into the global community."

Several key insights emerge from the data:

**1. Continued Export Dependence:** The substantial goods trade surplus highlights China's ongoing reliance on manufacturing exports for economic growth, despite government efforts to boost domestic consumption. This export strength contributed significantly to the record $1.2 trillion trade surplus China reported for 2025.

**2. Capital Outflows for Global Investment:** The significant financial account deficit reflects increased overseas investment by Chinese entities. As noted in the People's Daily article, "the current account surplus formed capital inflows, which were transformed into outward investment applications under the financial account, achieving payment balance." This suggests Chinese capital is increasingly flowing abroad for international operations and diversified asset allocation.

**3. Services Trade Deficit Persists:** The services trade deficit, while smaller than the goods surplus, indicates China continues to be a net importer of services. This includes areas like tourism, education, and intellectual property, where China still relies on foreign providers.

**4. Reserve Assets Growth:** China's reserve assets increased by $4.68 billion in 2025, indicating continued accumulation of foreign exchange reserves despite the capital outflows.

## Implications for China's Economic Strategy

The 2025 International Payments Report arrives as China implements its "15th Five-Year Plan" (2026-2030), which emphasizes developing "new quality productive forces" and enhancing domestic demand as the main driver of economic growth. The basic balance achievement supports the government's narrative of stable, managed economic transition while highlighting ongoing structural challenges.

From a policy perspective, SAFE indicated that foreign exchange authorities will "better coordinate development and security" and aim to create a foreign exchange policy environment that is both "flexible" and "well-regulated." The goal is to maintain stability in the foreign exchange market and basic balance in international payments while supporting the development of new quality productive forces during the 15th Five-Year Plan period.

Internationally, China's massive trade surplus continues to be a point of tension in global trade relations, particularly with the United States and other Western economies. However, the capital outflows provide liquidity to global financial markets and support partner countries' development, potentially easing some geopolitical friction.

## Looking Ahead

The SAFE report concludes with a forward-looking statement: "Looking ahead to 2026, foreign exchange management departments will better coordinate development and security, focus on building a 'more convenient, more open, more secure, and smarter' foreign exchange management system, strive to create a foreign exchange policy environment that is both 'flexible' and 'well-regulated,' and effectively maintain the stability of the foreign exchange market and the basic balance of international payments."

China's ability to maintain basic balance in its international payments amid global economic headwinds demonstrates the resilience of its external sector. However, the data also reveals the continued challenge of rebalancing the economy away from export dependence toward domestic consumption-driven growth. As China navigates the implementation of its 15th Five-Year Plan, the international payments dynamics will remain a critical indicator of both economic stability and structural transformation progress.

The report's findings suggest that while China's economic integration with the global economy continues to deepen, the nature of that integration is evolving—from primarily an export powerhouse to an increasingly significant global investor. This dual role presents both opportunities and challenges as China seeks to maintain economic stability while pursuing its development objectives in an uncertain global environment.
