# German Deepfake Case Sparks Mass Protests, Exposes Legal Gaps

**Tens of thousands have taken to the streets across Germany** to protest the handling of a deepfake case involving television presenter Collien Fernandes, who accuses her ex-husband Christian Ulmen of creating fake profiles and distributing AI-generated pornographic content over several years. The case has exposed significant legal gaps in Germany's approach to digital sexual violence and sparked a major political debate about violence against women, migration, and digital rights.

According to [NOS](https://nos.nl/artikel/2608153-duitsland-beschermt-daders-deepfakezaak-tv-ster-brengt-duizenden-op-de-been), protesters in Berlin, Hamburg, and Hannover carried signs reading "The shame must change sides" and "We must learn from Spain," referencing Germany's weaker legal protections compared to its southern neighbor. More demonstrations are planned for this weekend in Cologne, Kiel, and Nuremberg.

## Context: A Years-Long Pattern of Digital Abuse

Fernandes, a 36-year-old German TV presenter, alleges that Ulmen, 49, created fake online profiles impersonating her over several years. These accounts were used to spread deepfake pornographic images and videos, engage in sexual chats with "hundreds of men," and conduct phone sex while pretending to be her. Fernandes described the experience as virtual rape, telling [Der Spiegel](https://www.spiegel.de/netzwelt/netzpolitik/collien-fernandes-erstattet-anzeige-gegen-ex-mann-christian-ulmen-a-6abfb991-1665-4469-9c8e-3cc5a2cb4f29), "I was sexually available to everyone."

The scale of abuse is staggering: thousands of manipulated photos and videos of Fernandes have circulated online, including "provocative selfies, nude photos, and group sex porn," according to the NOS report. In a 2024 documentary, Fernandes herself chatted with someone pretending to be her, possibly her then-partner Ulmen.

## Legal Failures and Jurisdictional Battles

Germany's legal system proved inadequate to address the case. Creating fake pornographic content is not criminalized in Germany, leading Fernandes to file a complaint in Spain where such acts are illegal. After initial dismissal by German prosecutors citing lack of evidence, the case was reopened on March 27 following investigative reporting by Der Spiegel.

[Der Spiegel reported](https://www.spiegel.de/panorama/collien-fernandes-staatsanwaltschaft-ermittelt-gegen-christian-ulmen-a-c33da315-c479-43ab-9763-b7d22708f4a5) that the Itzehoe public prosecutor's office is now investigating Ulmen for stalking (§238 StGB), with potential additional charges being examined. Prosecutors had initially claimed Fernandes failed to submit requested documents, but her lawyer Christina Clemm stated the client had been "cooperative at all times" and provided "very specific investigative approaches."

Fernandes has been vocal about Germany's legal shortcomings, telling [Tagesschau](https://www.tagesschau.de/inland/collien-fernandes-interview-ulmen-100.html), "Germany is an absolute perpetrator's paradise for online sexual abuse." She noted that while about 5% of domestic violence cases are reported, only 2.4% of digital violence cases reach authorities, with most being dropped.

## Political Response and Controversy

The case has triggered significant political action and controversy. German Justice Minister Stefanie Hubig this week proposed new legislation that would impose two-year prison sentences for creating or sharing deepfakes and criminalize filming clothed body parts with sexual intent. The latter provision addresses situations like that of Yanni Gentsch, a Cologne woman whose [case against a man who filmed her while jogging](https://www1.wdr.de/nachrichten/rheinland/lauf-gegen-voyeur-aufnahmen-100.html) highlighted existing legal gaps.

However, the proposals have faced criticism. Legal experts quoted by [LTO](https://www.lto.de/recht/hintergruende/h/gesetz-digitale-gewalt-hubig-gesetz-deepfakes-kritik-dav) argue some measures are too broad and could infringe on privacy. The German Lawyers' Association (DAV) stated the legislation "shoots beyond the target" and warned against "legislative quick fixes" in response to the Fernandes case.

Chancellor Friedrich Merz sparked additional controversy by linking violence against women to immigration, stating "a significant portion of violence is committed by migrants." This drew heavy criticism, particularly since Fernandes is the daughter of Indian-Portuguese and Hungarian migrants. Critics accused Merz of diverting attention from structural violence issues.

## Public Outcry and Personal Risk

The public response has been overwhelming. Fernandes attended a Hamburg protest wearing a bulletproof vest under police protection due to death threats. "I stand here with a bulletproof vest and under police protection because men want to kill me," she told the crowd emotionally.

Her appearance came despite online and far-right media questioning her story. "It's no wonder that so many women don't have the courage to tell what was done to them," Fernandes said. "We must break the wall of silence!"

## Statistical Reality and Broader Implications

German federal police estimate that 1 in 5 women and 1 in 7 men are victims of digital sexual violence in Germany. Researchers estimate hundreds of thousands of deepfake victims in Germany alone, though exact figures are elusive due to low reporting rates.

The Fernandes case represents a watershed moment in Germany's approach to digital sexual violence. It has exposed significant legal deficiencies, mobilized public opinion, and forced political action. The case sits at the intersection of multiple critical issues: gender violence, digital rights, migration politics, and AI governance.

## What's Next

In the short term, legal proceedings will determine whether Spain or Germany has jurisdiction. The legislative process for Hubig's proposals will face parliamentary debate, with the Greens already pushing for stronger measures including consideration of power dynamics in cases involving workplace relationships.

Medium-term implications include potential establishment of important digital violence jurisprudence and possible EU-wide legislation, as the European Union has already classified non-consensual sharing of intimate or manipulated material as a form of cyberviolence that member states must address by mid-2027.

Long-term, the case may be remembered as the catalyst for much-needed legal and societal changes in how Germany addresses digital crimes, balancing privacy protections with victim safety in an increasingly AI-enabled world.
