# Air France Exits Orly After 80 Years for Charles de Gaulle Hub

Air France concluded eight decades of operations at Paris-Orly Airport (ORY) on March 28, 2026, consolidating all mainline flights at Paris-Charles de Gaulle Airport (CDG) in a strategic move that marks the end of an era for French aviation. The final Air France flight at Orly, flight AF6231 from Nice, landed at 21:55, closing a chapter that began in 1946 when the airline established Orly as its long-haul base for transatlantic services.

According to [Le Figaro](https://www.lefigaro.fr/societes/80-ans-apres-son-premier-vol-air-france-quitte-l-aeroport-d-orly-pour-se-recentrer-sur-paris-charles-de-gaulle-20260328), this consolidation was first announced in autumn 2023 and represents a fundamental restructuring of Air France's Paris operations. The airline stated that "this consolidation of operations at Paris-Charles de Gaulle will facilitate international connections and strengthen connectivity of regions and overseas territories."

## Historical Significance

Air France's relationship with Orly dates to the post-World War II era when the airport transformed from a local aerodrome to an international gateway. On July 1, 1946, the airline inaugurated its Paris-New York route via DC-4 aircraft with refueling stops in Shannon and Gander, establishing Orly as the visible heart of French aviation prestige. Throughout the golden age of air travel, Orly served as the launch point for iconic aircraft including the Lockheed Constellation and Caravelle, and was central to Air France's domestic shuttle services with hourly rotations to major French cities.

The airport held profound symbolic importance for Air France's identity in Paris, serving as its long-haul base from 1946 onward. The carrier's withdrawal concludes an 80-year period during which Orly was a visible part of the airline's operational and brand identity.

## Strategic Drivers for Consolidation

Three primary factors drove Air France's decision to consolidate at Charles de Gaulle:

**Post-Pandemic Market Shifts:** Domestic air travel demand has sustained decline since COVID-19, with increased videoconferencing reducing business travel frequency. [Passenger volumes at Paris airports only returned to pre-pandemic levels in 2025 according to Groupe ADP data](https://www.straitstimes.com/world/europe/air-france-leaves-paris-orly-airport).

**Competitive Pressures:** Strong competition from high-speed rail (TGV) on domestic routes and low-cost carriers like EasyJet challenged Air France's competitiveness on routes like Paris-Nice. The airline noted it was "responding to declining passenger demand, with increased video-conferencing and reduced domestic business travel - both accelerated during the Covid-19 pandemic - and a shift in France towards train journeys."

**Economic Factors:** Declining profitability on domestic shuttle services created pressure to optimize fleet utilization and reduce operational fragmentation between two Paris airports.

## Operational Changes and Passenger Impact

Starting March 29, 2026, Air France's low-cost subsidiary Transavia becomes the primary operator at Orly for the Air France-KLM group, taking over key domestic routes. Transavia will operate eight daily flights to Toulouse, eight to Nice, and two to Marseille from Orly.

Meanwhile, Air France strengthens its hub operations at Charles de Gaulle with enhanced frequencies: 12 daily rotations to Toulouse, 12 to Nice, and 10 to Marseille. All overseas territory flights (Pointe-à-Pitre, Fort-de-France, Saint-Denis de la Réunion, Cayenne) will also be consolidated at CDG.

The only exception to the consolidation is flights to and from Corsica, which remains under Air France branding at Orly as part of a Public Service Delegation requirement.

## Industry Implications and Future Outlook

This move reflects broader trends in European aviation, including hub consolidation as airlines rationalize multi-airport operations and the growing importance of low-cost subsidiaries within legacy carrier groups. Transavia's expanded role at Orly represents strategic market segmentation between full-service and low-cost operations.

For passengers, the changes mean:
- **Business travelers** may face longer transit times to/from southern Paris but gain better international connections through the strengthened CDG hub
- **Regional travelers** will have improved connectivity to international destinations via CDG
- **Orly-based travelers** transition to Transavia for domestic routes, potentially benefiting from lower fares but experiencing a different service model

Unanswered questions remain about long-term viability: Will Transavia successfully capture the domestic market from Orly? How will Orly's terminals be repurposed following Air France's departure? And will improved CDG hub connectivity compensate for reduced Orly services for regional economies?

## Conclusion

Air France's departure from Orly after 80 years represents more than an operational change—it symbolizes the transformation of European aviation in the post-pandemic era. While ending a historic chapter in French aviation, the consolidation positions Air France for more efficient operations and potentially stronger international competitiveness. The success of this transition will depend on effective execution of the Transavia strategy at Orly and seamless integration of regional traffic into the Charles de Gaulle hub, developments that will be closely watched as indicators of broader trends in airline strategy and infrastructure utilization.
