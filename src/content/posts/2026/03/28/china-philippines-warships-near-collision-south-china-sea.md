# China-Philippines Naval Vessels Nearly Collide in South China Sea Dispute

Chinese and Philippine warships narrowly avoided a collision in the disputed South China Sea on March 25, 2026, with both sides accusing the other of dangerous maneuvers near the Spratly Islands. The incident marks the second confrontation between the two nations' naval forces this month and highlights escalating tensions in one of Asia's most contested waterways.

## Context of the Dispute

The South China Sea, through which an estimated $3 trillion in trade passes annually, is claimed in whole or part by China, the Philippines, Vietnam, Malaysia, Brunei, and Taiwan. China asserts "indisputable sovereignty" over most of the sea based on its "nine-dash line" claim, while the Philippines bases its claims on the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS). A 2016 ruling by the Permanent Court of Arbitration rejected China's claims, but Beijing has consistently rejected the decision.

Recent years have seen increasing Chinese assertiveness in the region, with frequent incidents involving Chinese coast guard and naval vessels harassing Philippine resupply missions to its outposts. The March 25 incident follows a pattern of escalating encounters, including a March 7 incident where a Chinese warship directed its fire control radar at a Philippine frigate near Sabina Shoal.

## Conflicting Accounts of the Incident

According to the [People's Liberation Army Southern Theater Command](http://military.people.com.cn/n1/2026/0328/c1011-40690485.html), spokesperson Senior Colonel Zhai Shichen stated that PLA Navy Ship 532 was conducting a routine patrol in "waters under China's jurisdiction near Zhubi Jiao of China's Nansha Qundao" (Subi Reef in the Spratly Islands) when it detected a Philippine Navy vessel. The Chinese side claims it issued repeated radio warnings to the Philippine ship 507, which allegedly ignored the warnings and "dangerously approached" the Chinese vessel.

"The operation of the Philippine Navy ship was unsafe and unprofessional, which could have easily led to a maritime accident," Zhai said in a statement carried by [CGTN](https://news.cgtn.com/news/2026-03-28/China-urges-Philippines-to-control-operations-of-maritime-air-troops-1LS83yyorks/p.html). "Worse still, the Philippine side made a false accusation, claiming that the Chinese ship made a 'dangerous approach'."

The Philippine version, detailed by [Colonel Nep Padua of the Western Command](https://www.philstar.com/headlines/2026/03/28/2517342/philippines-vessel-avoids-crash-china-warship), presents a starkly different account. According to Manila, the BRP Benguet (LS507) was conducting lawful maritime operations near Pag-asa Island (Thitu Island) when the Chinese Type 054A-class guided-missile frigate Binzhou (532) executed "an unsafe and unprofessional maneuver by closing in and passing at a dangerously close distance."

"Such dangerous maneuvers are provocative and constitute a clear violation of the Convention on the International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea," Padua stated.

## Vessel Disparity and Historical Context

The incident highlights the significant power asymmetry between the two navies. The Chinese vessel involved, Binzhou (532), is a modern Type 054A-class guided-missile frigate commissioned around 2013. In contrast, the Philippine vessel BRP Benguet is a World War II-era landing ship tank commissioned in 1944, making it 82 years old at the time of the incident.

This encounter follows a pattern of Chinese harassment of Philippine resupply missions. According to [USNI News](https://news.usni.org/2026/03/27/philippine-navy-ship-avoids-collision-during-chinese-frigate-harassment-in-south-china-sea), the Philippines relies on these aging vessels for resupply missions to its westernmost islands, and past encounters have seen the Benguet contend with dangerous maneuvers from various Chinese warships.

The March 25 incident is particularly notable as it represents the first publicized harassment attempt of an Armed Forces of the Philippines-manned resupply vessel in the Spratly Islands since a provisional agreement reduced incidents following a June 2024 confrontation at Second Thomas Shoal.

## Broader Implications and Regional Security

The conflicting narratives reflect the fundamental disagreement over sovereignty and jurisdiction in the South China Sea. China refers to the location as "Zhubi Jiao" (Subi Reef) in "China's Nansha Qundao," while the Philippines calls it "near Pag-asa Island" in the "West Philippine Sea"—both referring to the same general area but using nomenclature that reflects their competing claims.

The incident occurs amid strengthened U.S.-Philippines defense cooperation and precedes an Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN) Summit scheduled for May 2026 in Cebu, where discussions on a Code of Conduct for the South China Sea are expected to continue. Despite the tensions, the Philippine Department of Foreign Affairs has assured that COC talks will proceed during the summit.

## Legal and Diplomatic Dimensions

The Philippines' accusation that China violated the Convention on the International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea (COLREGS) adds a legal dimension to the dispute. COLREGS, established to prevent collisions at sea, requires vessels to maintain safe distances and take early action to avoid close-quarters situations.

Both sides have used military spokespersons rather than foreign ministries for their initial statements, suggesting the incident is being framed primarily as a military rather than diplomatic matter. This approach may reflect both nations' desire to avoid escalating the situation to full diplomatic crisis while still asserting their positions.

## What's Next

The incident underscores the persistent risk of miscalculation in the South China Sea, where close encounters between vessels of claimant states have become increasingly common. The power asymmetry between modern Chinese naval assets and aging Philippine vessels raises concerns about the potential for accidental collisions that could trigger broader conflict.

Observers will be watching for several developments in the coming weeks:
- Whether diplomatic channels between Beijing and Manila address the incident
- The U.S. response given its mutual defense treaty with the Philippines
- How the incident affects ASEAN Code of Conduct negotiations
- Whether this marks a new pattern of Chinese naval rather than coast guard harassment of Philippine vessels

As both nations maintain their competing claims and military presence in the disputed waters, the risk of similar incidents—and potentially more dangerous confrontations—remains high. The March 25 near-collision serves as another reminder that despite diplomatic efforts, the South China Sea remains one of Asia's most volatile flashpoints.
