# Iran Warns US Over Strait of Hormuz Control as EU Expands Mission

Iran's military has warned the United States it will "never be able to establish control over the Strait of Hormuz," according to a statement from a senior Iranian armed forces representative reported by [TASS](https://tass.ru/mezhdunarodnaya-panorama/26945469). The warning comes as the European Union expands its naval missions in the Red Sea and western Indian Ocean but explicitly excludes participation in securing oil and gas transports through the strategic waterway for now. Meanwhile, Belgium has indicated willingness to join a potential Hormuz mission but only under strict conditions including a permanent ceasefire and international mandate.

## Strategic Context: The World's Most Critical Oil Chokepoint

The Strait of Hormuz, a narrow passage between the Persian Gulf and Gulf of Oman, represents one of the world's most critical energy chokepoints. Approximately 20-30% of global oil trade passes through its 21-mile width, with shipping lanes just 2 miles wide in each direction. The waterway's closure by Iran in early March 2026, following US-Israeli strikes that killed Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, has sent global oil prices soaring above $100 per barrel and added €14 billion to the European Union's fossil fuel import bill in just 30 days, according to EU Energy Commissioner Dan Jorgensen [as reported by DW](https://www.dw.com/en/iran-war-eu-calls-tehran-to-discuss-strait-of-hormuz/live-76600314).

## Iran's Defiant Warning

Ebrahimi Zolfaghari, representative of Iran's central headquarters of armed forces "Khatam al-Anbiya," delivered a defiant message to Washington on March 31. "They are mistaken if they think that with the help of information warfare, demonstration of supposedly advanced weapons and equipment, as well as terrorist acts against children, women, men, scientists and brave commanders of the armed forces of the country, they can force the mighty and proud Iran, which has never bowed its head to enemies in history, to surrender, or realize their dream of establishing control over the Strait of Hormuz," Zolfaghari stated. "They will bury this dream forever."

The Iranian warning referenced earlier threats from US President Donald Trump, who said the United States would destroy all of Iran's power plants, oil wells, and the island of Kharg in the Persian Gulf if a ceasefire agreement is not reached soon and the Strait of Hormuz is not unblocked.

## EU Naval Mission Expansion with Limits

While Iran issued its warning, the European Union moved to expand its naval missions in the region—but with important limitations. According to [BRF](https://brf.be/international/2063561/), the EU will expand operations in the Red Sea and western Indian Ocean to include monitoring suspicious activities related to critical underwater infrastructure, training activities for Djibouti's naval forces, and cooperation with Yemen's coast guard. However, the bloc will not participate in securing oil and gas transports through the Strait of Hormuz for now.

The EU naval missions primarily aim to protect against Houthi rebel attacks from Yemen and combat piracy in the region. This cautious approach reflects European leaders' repeated rejections of military involvement in the Strait of Hormuz throughout March, despite growing economic pressure from the waterway's closure.

## Belgium's Conditional Participation

Belgium has emerged as a case study in European caution regarding Hormuz operations. Prime Minister Bart De Wever clarified on March 31 that "Belgium has no intention whatsoever of participating in the Iran war" but would consider joining a maritime operation in the Strait of Hormuz under strict conditions. "Belgium is prepared to participate in a maritime operation in the Strait of Hormuz, but only under strict conditions," De Wever told [BRF](https://brf.be/national/2063596/), specifying that a permanent ceasefire and international mandate would be required.

The Belgian position aligns with a French-led initiative by President Emmanuel Macron, who is attempting to advance a Hormuz mission with about a dozen interested countries. Belgium's expertise in mine-clearing makes it a potentially valuable participant, but its conditions reflect broader European reluctance to be drawn into direct military confrontation with Iran.

## Diverging US and European Approaches

The developments highlight a growing divergence between US and European approaches to the Hormuz crisis. While Iran warns against US control of the strait, President Trump has taken an aggressive stance, telling NATO allies on his Truth Social platform: "All of those countries that can't get jet fuel because of the Strait of Hormuz... I have a suggestion for you: Number 1, buy from the US, we have plenty, and Number 2, build up some delayed courage, go to the Strait, and just TAKE IT."

In contrast, European Council President Antonio Costa has urged diplomatic engagement, telling Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian to "engage positively on the diplomatic track, notably with the UN to ensure the freedom of navigation in the Strait of Hormuz." This diplomatic approach comes as the Pentagon reports focusing operations on targeting Iran's "minelaying capability, their naval assets," with US forces having taken out "more than 150 ships" according to military briefings [reported by DW](https://www.dw.com/en/iran-war-eu-calls-tehran-to-discuss-strait-of-hormuz/live-76600314).

## Economic and Regional Implications

The economic toll of the month-long closure continues to mount. Beyond the EU's €14 billion additional fossil fuel costs, the United Nations estimates Arab countries have lost 6% of GDP ($186 billion) in one month of war [according to UN Assistant Secretary General Abdallah Al Dardari as reported by DW](https://www.dw.com/en/iran-war-eu-calls-tehran-to-discuss-strait-of-hormuz/live-76600314). US gasoline prices have crossed $4 per gallon for the first time since 2022, creating domestic political pressure [as reported by DW's live blog](https://www.dw.com/en/iran-war-eu-calls-tehran-to-discuss-strait-of-hormuz/live-76600314).

Regionally, Iran's parliamentary Security Commission has approved a plan to impose fees on ships traversing the Strait of Hormuz, potentially creating a new revenue stream while asserting sovereignty over the waterway. The move could further complicate international navigation rights and set precedents for other strategic chokepoints.

## What Comes Next

The immediate future of the Strait of Hormuz hinges on several factors: the progress of US-Iran peace negotiations, European willingness to participate in any international mission, and Iran's ability to maintain its closure amid military pressure. With Belgium's conditions representing a potential model for European participation, any Hormuz mission would likely require both diplomatic breakthroughs and clear international authorization.

As global energy markets remain volatile and shipping companies seek alternative routes, the economic pressure for resolution continues to build. However, with Iran defiantly warning against US control and European powers cautious about military escalation, the path to reopening the world's most critical oil chokepoint remains fraught with diplomatic and security challenges. The coming weeks will test whether economic necessity can overcome geopolitical divisions to restore free navigation through the Strait of Hormuz.
