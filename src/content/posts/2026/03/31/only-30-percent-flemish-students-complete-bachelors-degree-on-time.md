# Only 30% of Flemish Students Complete Bachelor's Degree On Time

New statistics reveal that just 30% of students in Flanders complete their bachelor's degree within the standard three-year timeframe, according to [official data published by the Flemish government](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/03/31/30-procent-van-de-vlaamse-studenten-behaalt-zijn-bachelordiploma/). While this represents a slight improvement from the previous year's low of 28%, the rate remains significantly below the European average of 40% and marks a concerning decline from historical levels around 2009 when 35-36% of students graduated on time.

## Context and Historical Decline

The Flemish higher education system has experienced a steady erosion in timely graduation rates over the past decade and a half. In the 2008-2009 academic year, 36% of bachelor students completed their degrees within three years. By 2012-2013, this figure had dropped to 30%, where it has largely remained despite fluctuations. The current 30% rate for students who began their studies in 2022-2023 represents what officials describe as "a slight improvement after a low point" but still falls far short of both historical performance and European benchmarks.

Beyond the 30% who graduate on schedule, approximately 20% of Flemish students require one extra year to complete their bachelor's degree, while another 10% need two additional years. These extended timelines increase costs for students and families while delaying entry into the workforce.

## Graduate Programs Show Similar Challenges

The trend extends beyond traditional bachelor programs to graduate programs (graduaatsopleidingen), which typically last two years. According to the same statistics, only 26% of students in these programs complete their studies within the standard timeframe, a figure that has remained stable at around 25% since 2020-2021. For graduate programs started in 2019-2020, a concerning 45% of students still haven't obtained their diplomas.

Study success rates—measured as the percentage of credits passed in one year—show some positive movement in bachelor programs, increasing from 61% in 2021-2022 to 65% in 2024-2025. However, graduate program success rates have declined from 65% to 59% over the same period, while master programs maintain a high 92% success rate.

## Student Union Cites Secondary Education Quality

Nele Van Hoyweghen, chair of the Flemish Student Union (VVS), attributes part of the problem to declining quality in secondary education. "I think we really shouldn't underestimate the impact of the declining quality of our secondary education," Van Hoyweghen stated in the VRT report. "Students start higher education insufficiently prepared as a result."

Van Hoyweghen also expressed caution about implementing new policy measures before evaluating existing ones. "It's not that long ago that Ben Weyts (the previous education minister) implemented the measure where you have to pass all first-year courses within 2 years, otherwise you have to stop your studies," she noted, referring to the so-called "hard cut" policy. "We're now waiting for the effects of those measures, but it's still too early for that. We therefore want to wait for that evaluation before new measures come."

## Government Response and Policy Tensions

Flemish Minister of Education Zuhal Demir (N-VA) is working on new measures to increase success rates, as announced in January 2026. These measures target what the ministry describes as "eternal students"—those who extend their studies indefinitely. However, the student union urges a more measured approach.

Van Hoyweghen calls for consultation with all stakeholders in the sector. "Because only by looking at the data in detail can we see which measures will also have an effect and which measures mainly cause side effects and, for example, put mental well-being under pressure," she explained.

## Broader Implications and European Context

The Flemish completion rate of 30% compares unfavorably with the European average of 40%, raising questions about the region's competitiveness in higher education. The decline from historical levels also suggests systemic challenges beyond individual student performance.

Education experts point to multiple contributing factors, including Flanders' open access policy that allows most students to enter higher education without strict entrance exams. This policy, while promoting accessibility, may contribute to mismatches between student preparedness and program demands.

## Looking Ahead

The coming months will be crucial for Flemish higher education policy. Minister Demir's proposed measures will face scrutiny regarding their potential effectiveness and unintended consequences, particularly concerning student mental health and equity. Meanwhile, the student union's call for evidence-based evaluation of existing policies before implementing new ones highlights the tension between accountability measures and student support systems.

As Flanders grapples with these challenges, the 30% completion rate serves as both a warning sign and a benchmark for improvement. The slight increase from 28% to 30% offers a glimmer of progress, but substantial work remains to bring Flemish higher education in line with European standards while maintaining the region's commitment to accessibility and quality.
