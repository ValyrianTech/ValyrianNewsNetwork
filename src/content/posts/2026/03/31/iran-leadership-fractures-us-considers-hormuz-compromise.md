# Iran Leadership Fractures as US Considers Hormuz Compromise

Three interconnected developments are reshaping the complex geopolitical landscape of the Middle East as the US-Israeli war against Iran enters its fifth week. According to multiple reports, former US President Donald Trump is reportedly willing to end the conflict even if the strategically vital Strait of Hormuz remains closed, while intelligence assessments reveal significant fractures within Iran's leadership structure. Simultaneously, Iran's judiciary has announced death sentences for collaborators accused of working with Israel and the United States, highlighting the regime's domestic crackdown amid escalating tensions.

## US Willing to Accept Closed Strait of Hormuz

President Donald Trump has told aides he's willing to end the US military campaign against Iran even if the Strait of Hormuz remains largely closed, according to a [Wall Street Journal report](https://www.haaretz.co.il/news/world/middle-east/2026-03-31/ty-article/0000019d-418f-d991-affd-cf8f1c3f0000) covered by Haaretz. Administration officials assessed that forcing the critical waterway back open would extend the conflict beyond Trump's timeline of four to six weeks.

The US strategy now aims to achieve main military objectives—hobbling Iran's navy and missile stocks—before winding down hostilities and pressuring Tehran diplomatically to resume free flow of trade through the strait. If diplomatic pressure fails, Washington would press allies in Europe and the Gulf to take the lead on reopening the strategic chokepoint, which handles 20-30% of the world's oil shipments.

White House spokeswoman Caroline Lewitt indicated that Trump wants Gulf states to help fund the war, telling reporters, "I think that's something the president would be very interested in having them do." Gulf allies including Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates, Kuwait, and Bahrain are reportedly pressuring Trump to continue the war, arguing Iran hasn't been weakened enough.

## Fractures in Iranian Leadership

The US-Israeli war against Iran has fractured the Iranian government, complicating its ability to make decisions and coordinate larger retaliatory attacks, according to [intelligence assessments reported by the New York Times](https://www.haaretz.co.il/news/world/middle-east/2026-03-31/ty-article/.premium/0000019d-4282-d679-ab9d-c6cfb1250000). Several dozen Iranian leaders and their deputies have been killed since the war began four weeks ago, creating significant leadership gaps.

Surviving leaders reportedly have difficulty communicating and cannot meet in person due to fear of interception and targeting. While Iran's security and military agencies continue to function, the government's ability to plan new strategies or policies has been weakened. With different leaders in place, Iranian negotiators may have little knowledge about what their government is willing to concede in potential diplomatic talks.

Hard-liners within the Islamic Revolutionary Guards Corps have become more influential, exerting more power than the religious leadership nominally in charge. The new supreme leader, Mojtaba Khamenei, has not been seen in public, and US and Israeli intelligence believe he was wounded during the war. Before the conflict began, Iran built a decentralized control system allowing local commanders to make strike decisions without direct orders from Tehran, which may now be complicating coordinated responses.

## Iran's Death Sentences for Collaborators

Iran's judiciary announced death penalties for defendants accused of espionage or collaboration with "hostile states"—specifically Israel and the United States—according to [reports from Israel Hayom](https://www.israelhayom.co.il/israelnow/article/20237719). The punishment includes asset confiscation in addition to the death penalty.

So far, over 1,000 arrests have been reported, including for photographing sensitive sites. A new law states that distributing documentation that could aid attacks will also be considered aiding the enemy, significantly expanding the legal framework for prosecuting perceived collaborators.

## Regional Diplomacy and Negotiations

Regional powers are attempting to mediate the Hormuz issue, with Turkey, Egypt, and Saudi Arabia discussing possibilities for reopening the strait as a "confidence-building measure" between the warring parties. According to diplomatic sources, proposals have been presented to both Washington and Tehran, though Iranian Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmail Baghaei called them "unreasonable" and stated that "the US position in diplomacy cannot be trusted."

Secretary of State Marco Rubio has maintained that the Strait of Hormuz will reopen "one way or another," while Trump has threatened that "if there is no agreement, and the Strait of Hormuz does not 'open for business' immediately, we will end our lovely stay in Iran with an explosion and complete destruction of all power plants, oil wells, and Kharg Island."

## Analysis and Implications

The simultaneous emergence of these three developments suggests the conflict may be approaching a potential turning point. The US appears willing to accept a significant strategic compromise—keeping the Strait of Hormuz closed—to end hostilities, while Iran's leadership struggles with internal fractures that complicate negotiations.

Iran's harsh domestic measures against perceived collaborators suggest a regime under pressure, seeking to maintain control through intimidation as it faces military setbacks and leadership losses. The death sentences also serve as a warning to potential dissenters within Iran's borders.

From a military perspective, Iran's decentralized command structure—designed to ensure continuity—may now be hindering coordinated strategic responses. The reported influence of hard-line Revolutionary Guards elements could make diplomatic resolution more challenging, as these factions typically favor military solutions over negotiated settlements.

## What's Next

The coming days will reveal whether these developments lead to diplomatic breakthroughs or further escalation. Key questions remain about the specific terms the US is offering Iran, the actual authority of Mojtaba Khamenei, and how Gulf states will respond to Trump's request for war funding.

Regional powers like Turkey, Egypt, and Saudi Arabia may play increasingly important roles as mediators, particularly regarding the Strait of Hormuz issue. Pakistan has also positioned itself as a potential peace broker in the conflict.

The complex interplay between military objectives, diplomatic maneuvering, and internal political dynamics in both the US and Iran suggests that while the war may be approaching a potential conclusion, the path to lasting stability in the region remains uncertain. The outcome will likely shape Middle East geopolitics and global energy security for years to come.
