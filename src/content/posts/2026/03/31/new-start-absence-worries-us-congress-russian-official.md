# New START Absence Worries US Congress: Russian Official

A senior Russian official has revealed that the absence of the New START nuclear arms control treaty is causing concern among US Congress members, highlighting growing strategic uncertainty between the two nuclear powers. The revelation comes as Russian media warns that weapons supplied to Ukraine could backfire on NATO, with the Pentagon considering diverting critical munitions to the Middle East amid the ongoing conflict with Iran.

## Context: A World Without Nuclear Limits

The New START Treaty, the last remaining nuclear arms control agreement between the United States and Russia, expired on February 5, 2026, without extension. According to [TASS](https://tass.ru/politika/26944091), the US position is that Washington hopes to conclude a "better treaty" that would also include China, but for now, the world's two largest nuclear arsenals operate without verifiable limits for the first time since the early 1970s.

Svetlana Zhurova, First Deputy Chair of the Russian State Duma Committee on International Affairs, told TASS that during a recent Russian parliamentary delegation visit to the US, Congress members expressed concern about the treaty's absence. "[US House Representative] Anna Paulina Luna, who invited us, began her speech by saying that we are nuclear powers, and we should try to be in dialogue," Zhurova stated. "I am sure that this (absence of New START) worries the American side, but probably this is a subject for negotiations at a different level and with different people."

## Congressional Concerns and Strategic Environment

The Russian official's claims align with concerns expressed in Washington. On March 26, 2026, the Senate Armed Services Committee held a hearing where senators described facing "historically dangerous' strategic threats." Senator Jack Reed (D-RI) stated: "We are operating in a historically dangerous strategic environment."

However, not all US lawmakers view the treaty's expiration negatively. Senator Roger Wicker (R-MS) argued that the New START Treaty's expiration was needed to "reset the strategic balance." This divergence reflects broader debates in Washington about nuclear arms control in an era of renewed great power competition.

## Weapons for Ukraine: A Potential Backfire on NATO

Simultaneously, Russian media is highlighting growing tensions within NATO over weapons supplies to Ukraine. According to [Kommersant](https://www.kommersant.ru/doc/8551237), the Prioritized Ukraine Requirements List (PURL) program faces potential disruption as the US considers diverting weapons to the Middle East.

The PURL program allows European NATO allies to purchase US weapons for Ukraine, with countries committing about $4 billion. However, the Pentagon is reportedly considering diverting weapons intended for Ukraine, particularly air defense interceptors like Patriot missiles, to the Middle East for the war with Iran.

## Transatlantic Tensions and Strategic Trade-offs

The potential diversion of Ukraine weapons exposes deepening transatlantic tensions. European allies have refused to support the US in the Iranian campaign, with Finnish President Alexander Stubb stating: "This may sound a bit harsh, but Iran is not my war, my war is Ukraine." German Chancellor Friedrich Merz criticized Trump for not consulting allies before the Iran operation.

These divisions come as the Trump administration has pressed NATO allies to deploy assets to help reopen the Strait of Hormuz, requests that Europeans have largely rebuffed. The situation creates a strategic dilemma: European nations want continued weapons flows to Ukraine, while the US faces munitions shortages in its conflict with Iran.

## Analysis: Strategic Implications

The convergence of these two issues points to broader strategic instability. First, the absence of New START removes a critical transparency mechanism between Washington and Moscow when communication channels are already strained.

Second, potential diversion of Ukraine weapons highlights limitations in the US defense industrial base. PURL has supplied about 75% of missiles for Ukraine's Patriot batteries. Any significant diversion could undermine Ukraine's air defense capabilities.

Third, the growing transatlantic rift over Iran threatens NATO cohesion. European refusal to support the US campaign creates alliance tensions that adversaries might exploit.

## What's Next: Navigating Strategic Uncertainty

Looking forward, several developments will shape this evolving landscape. The US and Russia may eventually return to negotiations on a successor to New START, potentially with Chinese participation.

On the Ukraine weapons front, European NATO members may need to increase defense production to compensate for potential US munitions shortages.

Finally, the US-Iran conflict's duration will significantly impact these calculations. A prolonged campaign would continue straining US munitions stockpiles, forcing difficult choices between theaters.

As Svetlana Zhurova noted, the New START issue requires "negotiations at a different level and with different people." The same might be said for resolving NATO's internal tensions over weapons supplies and strategic priorities in an era of multiple simultaneous crises.
