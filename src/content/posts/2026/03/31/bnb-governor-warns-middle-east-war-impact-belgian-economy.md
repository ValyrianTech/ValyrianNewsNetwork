# BNB Governor Warns Middle East War Could Trigger Belgian Recession

The Governor of Belgium's National Bank (BNB), Pierre Wunsch, has issued a stark warning that a prolonged Middle East conflict could push Belgium into a technical recession, with the country's depleted fiscal resources leaving it "truly vulnerable" to market reactions. Testifying before the House Finance Committee on March 31, 2026, Wunsch presented the bank's annual report while highlighting the economic risks posed by the ongoing Iran war.

## Context: Belgium's Precarious Fiscal Position

Belgium enters this crisis with significantly weakened economic defenses compared to previous global shocks. The country's budget deficit widened to 5.3% of GDP in 2025, with public debt standing at 107% of GDP. According to Wunsch, "the resources we had in 2022 to weather the crisis are no longer there"—a reference to the fiscal buffers used during the COVID-19 pandemic and the initial phases of the Russia-Ukraine war.

The Middle East conflict, which began on February 28, 2026 with joint US-Israeli airstrikes targeting Iranian leadership, has already caused significant global economic disruption. Iran's closure of the Strait of Hormuz on March 4 disrupted 20% of global oil supplies and significant liquefied natural gas (LNG) volumes, according to the [Wikipedia analysis of the conflict's economic impact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_impact_of_the_2026_Iran_war). Brent crude oil surged 55.32% from $72.48 to $112.57 between February 28 and March 27, while European gas prices nearly doubled.

## The Governor's Dire Warning

In his testimony before the House Finance Committee, Wunsch delivered specific economic projections that paint a concerning picture for Belgium's economic future. "If the war in the Middle East drags on, we are truly vulnerable and it would only be a matter of time before the markets react," he stated, as [reported by Le Soir](https://www.lesoir.be/738021/article/2026-03-31/largent-est-epuise-le-gouverneur-de-la-bnb-alerte-sur-limpact-de-la-guerre-au).

The BNB Governor outlined three potential outcomes if the conflict persists:

1. **Technical Recession Risk**: "Very low growth or even a technical recession in Belgium"—defined as contraction of GDP over two consecutive quarters
2. **Fiscal Deterioration**: The deficit could increase by 1-2% of GDP
3. **Market Vulnerability**: Belgium would be "truly vulnerable" to negative market reactions

Wunsch emphasized the critical importance of government policy in navigating these challenges: "If that is the case, we can certainly survive it. But if the government fails to bring the deficit under control, to continue taking measures, then we are very vulnerable."

## Analysis: Belgium's Specific Vulnerabilities

Belgium faces particular risks from the Middle East conflict due to several structural factors:

### Energy Dependence
As a significant energy importer, Belgium is directly exposed to the global energy price shocks caused by the Strait of Hormuz closure. The European energy crisis has been exacerbated by historically low gas storage levels—estimated at just 30% capacity following a harsh 2025–2026 winter—causing Dutch TTF gas benchmarks to nearly double to over €60/MWh by mid-March.

### Industrial Impact
Belgium's energy-intensive industries, including chemicals and manufacturing, face surcharges of up to 30% to offset rising electricity and feedstock costs. This threatens both competitiveness and potentially leads to permanent deindustrialization in some sectors.

### Monetary Policy Constraints
The European Central Bank (ECB), where Wunsch serves on the Governing Council, already postponed planned interest rate reductions on March 19 due to inflation concerns. Eurozone inflation jumped to 2.5% in March 2026 primarily due to the energy shock, limiting the ECB's ability to support growth through monetary policy.

## Broader European Context

The Middle East conflict represents Europe's second major energy crisis in four years, following the 2022 Russia-Ukraine war. The economic impact analysis notes that "the crisis has been characterized by a severe energy-supply shock and industrial strain, with the European Central Bank warning that a prolonged conflict will likely trigger a period of stagflation."

Several European economies face similar challenges:
- **Germany and Italy**: At high risk of technical recession due to energy dependence
- **United Kingdom**: Inflation expected to breach 5% in 2026
- **Industrial sectors**: Chemical and steel manufacturers imposing significant surcharges

## What's Next: Policy Imperatives and Monitoring Points

Governor Wunsch's testimony underscores several critical policy requirements for Belgium:

### Fiscal Credibility
"It is before all important that the government can pursue a credible policy," Wunsch emphasized. This includes controlling the deficit and implementing measures to strengthen economic resilience.

### Energy Security
Diversifying energy sources and supply chains becomes increasingly urgent as global trade routes face disruption. The simultaneous war between Afghanistan and Pakistan has further complicated Central Asian trade routes, while Houthi involvement threatens Red Sea shipping.

### Economic Monitoring
Key indicators to watch include:
- Energy price developments and their pass-through to consumer inflation
- Industrial production data from energy-intensive sectors
- Government deficit figures and debt sustainability metrics
- Market reactions to Belgium's fiscal position

## Conclusion

Pierre Wunsch's warning represents one of the most direct assessments of Belgium's economic vulnerability to the Middle East conflict. With depleted fiscal resources and significant energy dependence, Belgium faces a challenging period that requires both prudent policy management and external factors—particularly the duration of the Middle East conflict—to break favorably.

The Governor's stark assessment that "the money has run out" serves as a sobering reminder that economic resilience requires both preparation during calm periods and disciplined response during crises. As Belgium navigates these challenges, the government's ability to maintain fiscal credibility while supporting vulnerable sectors will be critical to avoiding the technical recession scenario Wunsch outlined.
