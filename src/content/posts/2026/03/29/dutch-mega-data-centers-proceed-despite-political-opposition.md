# Dutch Mega Data Centers Proceed Despite Political Opposition

At least seven hyperscale data centers will proceed with construction in the Netherlands despite recent parliamentary opposition and growing environmental concerns, according to industry representatives. The massive facilities, including projects in Amsterdam and Lelystad, have secured permits before recent regulatory restrictions took effect, creating a legal loophole that allows their development to continue.

## Context and Regulatory Background

The Netherlands finds itself at the center of a Europe-wide dilemma: balancing urgent digital infrastructure needs against environmental sustainability and community interests. Data centers currently consume approximately 5% of the country's total electricity, with grid operators anticipating this share could double in coming years. The energy consumption of a single hyperscale facility equals that of more than 200,000 households.

In 2022, the Rutte IV cabinet introduced limits for hyperscale data centers, capping them at 10 hectares and restricting grid connections to 70 megawatts. New hyperscales were only permitted in three designated areas: the Eemshaven in Groningen, the Schiphol region, and the Kop van Noord-Holland in the municipality of Hollands Kroon.

## Political Opposition and Legal Loopholes

Despite these restrictions, at least seven hyperscale projects will move forward because their permits were issued before the 2022 regulations took effect. The Dutch House of Representatives recently adopted a motion calling for a halt to large-scale data center construction, but according to [NL Times](https://nltimes.nl/2026/03/29/permitted-large-data-centers-amsterdam-lelystad-move-ahead-despite-opposition), the decision came too late for these already-approved projects.

"How is this possible?" asked Pieter Grinwis of the ChristenUnie in Parliament earlier this month. "That housing cannot be built, and entrepreneurs cannot decarbonize their businesses, while in Amsterdam a massive data center is being built for the American Microsoft?"

Former minister Mona Keijzer of Housing and Spatial Planning explained to Parliament that permits for Amsterdam and Lelystad had already been issued before the rules took effect, meaning the new restrictions don't apply to those projects.

## Local Community Impact

The human impact of these developments is most acutely felt in communities like Haarlemmermeer, just under the Schiphol area, where four future hyperscales have already been approved. The municipality already hosts more than 30 existing data centers, with additional facilities still under construction.

Two new projects are facing strong resistance from residents who say the centers are being built "practically in backyard" of residential neighborhoods. "I see the importance of data centers, but do not build them in residential neighborhoods," residents Marije and Guusje said in a recorded statement as construction continues nearby.

This local opposition reflects a broader trend across the Netherlands. The province of Utrecht decided in 2021 to prohibit data center development entirely. Municipalities including Leiden, Breda, and Westland are also limiting new projects or moving toward outright bans, allowing them only under exceptional circumstances.

## Industry Perspective

Industry leaders argue the backlash is misguided. Stijn Grove, chair of the Dutch Datacentra Association (DDA), maintains that data centers are essential infrastructure. "Data centers are simply indispensable," Grove told NL Times. "The discussion now focuses on centers operated by Microsoft or Google, but hospitals, universities, and the Tax Authority also need storage. We would rather not depend on foreign companies for that. That data must be stored here."

Grove also argues that large hyperscale facilities are more energy efficient than numerous smaller centers, though this claim is contested by environmental scientists who question whether the societal benefits justify the substantial energy and climate impacts.

## European Context and Future Implications

The Dutch situation reflects broader European tensions as the European Commission aims to triple data center capacity within seven years (by 2033) through its upcoming Cloud and AI Development Act, scheduled for unveiling in May 2026. This expansion is part of a strategic effort to close the gap with the United States and China on artificial intelligence development.

However, Europe faces significant challenges in this expansion. Approximately 50% of European data centers exceed a Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE) ratio of 1.6, indicating poor energy efficiency. Water consumption presents another major issue, particularly in water-stressed regions like Spain's Aragón. Public opposition is growing across the continent, and electricity grids, as noted by Michael Winterson of the European Data Center Association, "are not built for the size [of demand]."

## What's Next

The future of four additional hyperscale developments in the Netherlands now depends on whether Minister Elanor Boekholt-O'Sullivan will act on Parliament's request and attempt to block them. The minister faces a difficult decision: heed parliamentary calls for stricter enforcement or allow projects that have already cleared regulatory hurdles to proceed.

In the medium term, the Netherlands must grapple with the potential doubling of data center energy consumption and increased strain on its national electricity grid. Long-term solutions will require innovative approaches including greater renewable energy integration, efficiency improvements, and potentially reconsidering where such energy-intensive infrastructure should be located.

The ongoing tension between digital sovereignty and environmental sustainability will likely continue to shape policy debates across Europe as the continent pursues ambitious digital transformation goals while confronting pressing climate challenges.
