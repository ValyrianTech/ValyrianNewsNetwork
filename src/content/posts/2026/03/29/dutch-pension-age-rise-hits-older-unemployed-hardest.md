# Older Unemployed Disproportionately Affected by Dutch Pension Age Increase

Older unemployed individuals in the Netherlands are facing extended periods without income as the government plans to accelerate increases to the state pension (AOW) age, according to a [report by NU.nl](https://www.nu.nl/economie/6390515/oudere-werklozen-flink-de-dupe-bij-verhoging-aow-leeftijd.html). With 106,000 people aged 45-75 currently jobless despite hundreds of thousands of open vacancies, the policy change highlights persistent age discrimination in the Dutch labor market.

## Context: Rising Retirement Age Meets Labor Market Challenges

The Dutch state pension age has been gradually increasing and is currently set at 67. The government's plan to further raise the retirement age comes alongside proposals to shorten unemployment benefit (WW) duration, creating what labor experts describe as a "perfect storm" for older workers who lose their jobs.

This situation is particularly acute given the tight labor market, where many companies struggle to find suitable personnel despite hundreds of thousands of open vacancies, the NU.nl report notes.

## Key Statistics and Expert Reactions

According to Statistics Netherlands (CBS) data cited in the report, 106,000 people between ages 45 and 75 are currently unemployed, representing a significant portion of the total 395,000 unemployed Dutch citizens.

Joske Paumen, board member of social developer Start Foundation, attributes this paradox to deep-rooted age discrimination. "It is deeply rooted and the group of older people without jobs will become increasingly larger," Paumen told NU.nl. "We still hear the same reasons about older employees. They are too expensive, not fast enough, or have a greater chance of getting sick. Employers remain hesitant about that."

Rob Witjes, labor market expert at the Employee Insurance Agency (UWV), echoed these concerns, noting that "these problems have been playing out for decades. That has to do with many companies seeing older employees as a cost item. And that while older employees are loyal and have experience and quality."

The government's dual approach—raising the pension age while shortening unemployment benefits—creates particular hardship for older job seekers. Dick Koerselman, interim chairman of the FNV trade union federation, warned that "the AOW age is going up, the WW is being shortened. That hits this group the hardest, they will end up on welfare."

## Union Response and Political Implications

The FNV is preparing actions starting May 1, 2026—International Workers' Day—coinciding with the inauguration of new chairman [Hans Spekman](https://www.nu.nl/economie/6388629/oud-politicus-hans-spekman-wordt-nieuwe-voorzitter-fnv.html), a former politician. While specific action plans haven't been disclosed, the union has made clear its intention to resist what it views as a violation of the pension agreement between the government and social partners.

Koerselman described the government's plans as "unbelievable, antisocial and downright unreliable" because they contradict previously agreed terms. This breach of trust has sparked union fury, with FNV canceling a meeting with the cabinet in early March and declaring their trust "betrayed," according to earlier NU.nl reporting.

Opposition parties have also mobilized against the plans, setting the stage for a significant political battle over social security and intergenerational fairness.

## Looking Ahead: Challenges for Older Workers

For the 106,000 older unemployed Dutch citizens, the immediate future looks challenging. They face the prospect of longer job searches in a market biased against their age group, reduced unemployment benefits, and delayed pension eligibility.

Their situation highlights the need for complementary policies addressing age discrimination and supporting retraining for older workers, rather than simply extending working lives without addressing labor market realities.

As the debate continues, the fundamental question remains: how can pension system reforms balance fiscal sustainability with social protection, particularly for vulnerable groups like older unemployed individuals who bear the brunt of transition costs?
