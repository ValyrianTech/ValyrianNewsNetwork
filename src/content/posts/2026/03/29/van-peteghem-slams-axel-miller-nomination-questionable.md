# Van Peteghem Slams Axel Miller Nomination as 'Questionable'

Belgian Finance Minister Vincent Van Peteghem has publicly condemned the nomination of former Dexia CEO Axel Miller to lead the country's sovereign wealth fund, calling the move "particularly questionable" and citing Miller's troubled track record during the 2008 financial crisis. The controversy erupted after MR (Mouvement Réformateur) party leader Georges-Louis Bouchez announced Miller's nomination as chairman of SFPIM (Société Fédérale de Participations et d'Investissement), which manages €11.5 billion in federal assets and oversees the partial privatization of Belfius Bank—the institution that replaced the failed Dexia Bank Miller once led.

## Context: From Dexia Debacle to Sovereign Wealth Fund

Axel Miller's nomination is fraught with historical irony. As CEO of Dexia Bank from 2006 to 2008, Miller presided over the institution during the global financial crisis. In September 2008, Dexia required a €6.4 billion bailout from Belgian, French, and Luxembourg governments, ultimately collapsing and being replaced by Belfius. A Belgian parliamentary commission later concluded Miller "failed to do his job" and was responsible for the bailout that cost taxpayers billions.

Now, Miller is being nominated to chair SFPIM, the federal holding company that manages Belgium's strategic investments and is currently overseeing the partial privatization of Belfius. The appointment would place the former Dexia CEO in charge of managing the successor to the bank he helped collapse—a situation critics call "perverse" and "several bridges too far," as [reported by VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/liveblog/ook-minister-van-peteghem-niet-te-spreken-over-voordracht-axel-~1771325690894/).

## Political Firestorm Erupts

The nomination sparked immediate backlash across Belgium's political spectrum. Finance Minister Vincent Van Peteghem (CD&V), who also serves as Deputy Prime Minister, delivered the most scathing criticism. In an interview with [Le Soir](https://www.lesoir.be/737616/article/2026-03-29/vincent-van-peteghem-la-nomination-daxel-miller-est-particulierement-discutable), Van Peteghem stated: "I find this decision particularly questionable. I had to clean up a part of his shit in the previous legislature." He added, "For my party, it would never be an option to put forward someone with such a track record. Look only at the irresponsible and unnecessary risks that man took as CEO of Dexia."

Van Peteghem's criticism was echoed by SFPIM's own deputy chairman, Koen Schoors, who threatened to resign over the nomination. "If people prove four times that they leave a financial mess when they leave, why reward them with a fifth chance?" Schoors asked in a television interview. "That is really a very stupid idea. My resignation is therefore a promise."

Opposition parties united in condemnation. PVDA, Groen, and Vlaams Belang all criticized the move, with PVDA fractieleader Sofie Merckx sarcastically suggesting Bouchez might next nominate former minister Didier Reynders as CEO of the National Lottery. Independent MP Jean-Marie Dedecker, who served on the parliamentary Dexia commission, called Miller's nomination a "gross disgrace," stating: "We concluded it was the biggest fraud case ever in this country. And who was the boss of that fraud? Mr. Axel Miller."

## Bouchez's Defense and Political Implications

MR leader Georges-Louis Bouchez remains defiant, defending his former chief of staff as "much more competent than the people who are criticizing him now." Bouchez argued that Miller bears no personal responsibility for Dexia's collapse, asking rhetorically: "Has Axel Miller caused the global banking crisis? That's ridiculous." He maintains the nomination will proceed regardless of criticism.

The controversy exposes deeper tensions within Belgium's coalition government and raises fundamental questions about political appointments. As [BRF reported](https://brf.be/national/2063166/), Van Peteghem called the nomination "particularly strange and concerning," highlighting the ongoing struggle between political patronage and merit-based selection in Belgian governance.

Rudy Aernoudt, Bouchez's current chief of staff, acknowledged the systemic problem while defending his predecessor: "The real problem is political appointments. I think it's wrong to shoot at the person. This shows that we should appoint based on objective policy." He advocated for external selection via headhunters rather than political nominations.

## Analysis: Governance and Accountability Questions

The Miller nomination controversy touches on several critical governance issues. First, it questions whether individuals responsible for major financial failures should lead public investment bodies. Second, it highlights Belgium's persistent struggle with political patronage in state appointments. Third, it raises concerns about the credibility of Belgium's financial governance at a time when SFPIM manages strategic investments worth €11.5 billion.

There's also significant irony in the timing. SFPIM is currently managing the partial privatization of Belfius—the bank created from Dexia's ashes. Having Miller oversee this process creates a perception problem that could affect investor confidence and the privatization's success.

Economist Koen Schoors framed the issue as one of democratic principles: "In a democratic country like Belgium, competence must be more important than connection." This sentiment resonates with broader public frustration over political appointments that appear to prioritize loyalty over qualifications.

## What's Next for the Controversial Nomination

Despite the criticism, Bouchez insists the nomination will proceed. However, it requires formal government approval, and Van Peteghem's strong opposition suggests CD&V could block it within the coalition. The controversy comes at a sensitive time for the government, which is already navigating economic challenges and geopolitical tensions.

Several key questions remain unanswered: Will Koen Schoors follow through on his resignation threat if Miller is appointed? How will this affect the Belfius privatization process? And will this controversy spark broader reforms to Belgium's political appointment system?

The Axel Miller nomination has become more than a personnel decision—it's a litmus test for political accountability in Belgium. As the debate continues, it exposes fundamental tensions between political loyalty, professional competence, and public trust in government institutions. The outcome will signal whether Belgium's political class is willing to confront its appointment culture or continue business as usual, rewarding past failures with prestigious positions.
