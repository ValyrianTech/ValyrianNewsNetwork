# French Truckers Protest Fuel Price Hikes as Government Defends €70M Aid

French truckers have launched nationwide protests against soaring fuel prices, blocking key motorways as Economy Minister Roland Lescure defended the government's €70 million aid package, insisting the targeted assistance "are not crumbs." The protests, which began Saturday on the A7 south of Lyon and at junctions near Clermont-Ferrand, represent growing tensions between transport sectors and a government constrained by fiscal limits amid what Lescure calls "a new oil shock" stemming from the U.S.-Israeli war on Iran.

## Context of the Fuel Price Crisis

The current fuel price surge represents France's latest energy crisis, with global oil markets destabilized by Middle East conflict. Economy Minister Roland Lescure has characterized the situation as "a new oil shock," drawing parallels to previous energy crises while emphasizing the government's constrained fiscal position. The crisis comes as the French government remains committed to reducing its budget deficit below 5% of GDP, limiting options for broad-based intervention.

This tension between immediate economic relief and long-term fiscal responsibility frames the current standoff, with truckers demanding subsidies similar to those implemented during the 2022 Ukraine war energy crisis, estimated at €400 million. The government's targeted, time-limited approach reflects both budgetary constraints and uncertainty about the duration and intensity of the current price shock.

## Government's Targeted Response

On March 27, the government announced a €70 million aid package specifically targeted for April, drawing from existing ministry budgets without requiring updates to the 2026 budget law. According to [Le Figaro](https://www.lefigaro.fr/conjoncture/70-millions-d-euros-ce-ne-sont-pas-des-miettes-la-reponse-de-roland-lescure-aux-routiers-en-colere-apres-la-hausse-des-carburants-20260329), the package provides support for farmers, small logistics companies, and fishing boats affected by fuel price increases.

Economy Minister Roland Lescure defended the measures in an interview, stating: "70 million euros in aid are not crumbs, an aid equivalent to 20 cents per liter of diesel either." He emphasized the government's "gradual, targeted, sector-specific" approach limited to April, adding: "We will adapt them depending on the evolution of the crisis, which remains unpredictable in duration and intensity. We need agility to act appropriately."

While refusing to freeze fuel prices, Lescure acknowledged that "regulation of margins remains always possible," noting that while prices have risen, they haven't increased more than elsewhere with "contained margins." The government's position reflects Prime Minister Sébastien Lecornu's red line against general cuts to fuel duties that would jeopardize deficit reduction targets.

## Escalating Trucker Protests

Trucker organizations including ORTE (Organisation des transporteurs routiers européens) and FNTR (Fédération nationale des transporteurs routiers) launched protests on March 28, with plans for major expansion into the Île-de-France region on March 30 before going nationwide. The initial blockades on the A7 motorway south of Lyon and at A71/A89 junctions near Clermont-Ferrand represent what protesters call merely "a start" of their mobilization.

Trucker sentiment, as captured in Le Figaro's reporting, summarizes the industry's position: "It's a start but it's not enough." The sector demands more substantial intervention, with Jean-Christophe Gautheron, an OTRE member in Auvergne-Rhône-Alpes, estimating that 2,000 company failures would cost the state €16 billion in lost tax and social security revenue. He calls for €400 million in direct subsidies as a "temporary measure, but essential to prevent our businesses from going under."

Jean-Marc Rivera, ORTE delegate, explained the industry's frustration: "Businesses on the ground are telling us 'you're at an impasse' in discussions with the government. We have given our regional branches the freedom to express their discontent whenever and however they choose."

## Industry Structure and Economic Pressure

The French logistics sector's vulnerability stems from its structure, with many companies operating as small businesses with fixed-price contracts unable to pass on sudden fuel cost increases. Joachim Dahmani, operations manager at AS Transports JLF, highlighted the practical challenges: "Prices are still not frozen, the increase is still significant. I checked with TotalEnergies [who have a fuel cap in place] to try and save money, but they're starting to run out of fuel."

This structural vulnerability creates a domino effect throughout the French economy, as logistics shortages could disrupt supply chains across multiple sectors. The government's targeted approach aims to support the "most exposed sectors" while maintaining fiscal discipline, but industry representatives argue the scale remains insufficient given the magnitude of the price shock.

Vincent Jeanbrun, Minister of City and Housing, acknowledged the broader fiscal context: "Our country spent far too much for dozens of years... Today our means are limited in general for all subjects." This acknowledgment of constrained resources underscores the government's balancing act between immediate crisis response and long-term fiscal sustainability.

## Analysis: Fiscal Constraints vs. Economic Reality

The current standoff represents a classic tension in economic crisis management: immediate relief versus long-term stability. The French government's €70 million package, while framed as substantial by Lescure, represents approximately 17.5% of the €400 million truckers estimate as necessary based on 2022 precedent.

Several factors complicate the government's position:

1. **Fiscal commitments**: The deficit reduction target below 5% of GDP limits broad fiscal interventions
2. **Uncertain duration**: The Middle East conflict's trajectory makes predicting fuel price evolution difficult
3. **Sectoral specificity**: Targeting logistics while excluding other affected sectors creates political tensions
4. **European comparisons**: Italy's implementation of ~25c per liter fuel price cuts through duty reductions creates comparative pressure

Industry estimates of potential €16 billion in lost revenue from company failures present a compelling counter-argument: targeted prevention may prove more fiscally responsible than dealing with widespread bankruptcies. This calculation will likely influence government decisions as protests intensify.

## What's Next

The protests' planned expansion on March 30 represents a critical test for both sides. Several developments will determine the crisis trajectory:

1. **Protest scale**: The Île-de-France mobilization's success will indicate industry solidarity and public support
2. **Government flexibility**: Whether the government adjusts its position as Easter weekend travel approaches
3. **European coordination**: Potential EU-level responses to energy price shocks
4. **Conflict evolution**: The Middle East situation's impact on global oil markets

Lescure's emphasis on "agility" suggests the government maintains room for adjustment, particularly if protests disrupt critical supply chains or gain significant public sympathy. However, the Prime Minister's deficit reduction red line establishes clear boundaries for any escalation in government support.

The coming days will reveal whether the government's targeted approach can satisfy industry demands or whether France faces a prolonged transport sector confrontation with broader economic implications. As fuel prices continue to pressure multiple sectors, the government's crisis management capabilities face their most significant test since taking office.
