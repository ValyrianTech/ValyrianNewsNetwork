# European Parcel Tax Could Generate Billions for Belgian Budget, Analysis Shows

A proposed European Union parcel tax set to take effect in July 2026 could generate several billion euros for Belgium's federal budget, according to recent analysis. The tax, which targets online purchases from outside the EU, aims to level the playing field between European and non-European retailers while providing significant revenue for cash-strapped member states. Belgium stands to benefit disproportionately due to its position as a major entry point for such packages through Liège Airport.

## Context and Background

The European parcel tax represents a significant policy shift in response to the flood of small packages entering the EU from outside the bloc, particularly from China. According to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/03/28/levert-de-europese-pakjestaks-belgie-enkele-miljarden-op/), in 2024 alone, some 4.6 billion parcels worth less than €150 entered the European Union, with 91% originating from China. These packages have historically been exempt from customs duties under the EU's €150 de minimis threshold, creating what European officials describe as unfair competition and safety concerns.

EU finance ministers decided in December 2025 to implement a two-tier taxation system for these packages. The move comes amid growing concerns about product safety standards, fraud through undervaluation of goods, and the environmental impact of processing billions of small packages annually. The tax specifically targets major Chinese e-commerce platforms like Shein, Temu, and AliExpress, which have dramatically increased their European market share in recent years.

## Tax Structure and Implementation Timeline

The new taxation system consists of two components with staggered implementation dates. Starting July 1, 2026, a €3 import levy will apply to all packages from outside the EU worth less than €150. This temporary measure will remain in place until 2028, after which normal import duties based on product categories will apply.

A second fee, described as a "handling fee" or "behandelingstaks," is expected to be introduced in November 2026. According to Kristian Vanderwaeren, the administrator-general of Belgian Customs, this fee is likely to range between €2 and €3 per package. The handling fee represents compensation for the administrative burden placed on customs authorities processing the massive volume of small packages.

## Revenue Projections for Belgium

Belgium's potential windfall from the new tax regime stems from its strategic position as a logistics hub for European e-commerce. Via Liège Airport, 1.3 billion such packages entered the European Union in 2025 alone. If this volume remains unchanged, the import levy alone would generate €3.9 billion in 2027, with €1.95 billion expected for the second half of 2026.

Under EU revenue-sharing rules, member states retain 25% of collected import duties, forwarding 75% to the EU budget. For Belgium, this would translate to approximately €1.45 billion from the import levy over 2026 and 2027 combined, assuming package volumes remain stable.

The handling fee presents even greater potential revenue. If set at €3 per package and applied to 1.3 billion packages, it would generate €3.9 billion annually. Initially, the European Commission has proposed that handling fee revenues from 2026-2027 go entirely to the country where packages enter. From 2028 onward, this revenue would shift to the EU budget, with Belgium retaining only 25% (potentially dropping to 10% depending on final negotiations).

## Policy Context and Objectives

The parcel tax initiative addresses multiple policy objectives simultaneously. First, it aims to create fair competition between EU retailers, who must comply with extensive regulations and taxation, and non-EU platforms that have operated under different rules. As Swedish Finance Minister Elisabeth Svantesson noted in Politico coverage, "free trade doesn't mean you get to flood the market with whatever garbage you feel like selling."

Second, the measure addresses product safety concerns. The European Commission has noted that many packages entering the EU don't comply with European safety standards, posing risks to consumers. Third, it tackles fraud through systematic undervaluation of goods to avoid existing tax thresholds.

French Finance Minister Roland Lescure welcomed the flat tax as "a major victory for the European Union," reflecting broader European efforts to assert digital sovereignty and protect the single market's integrity.

## Belgian Domestic Considerations

The European tax arrives at a critical time for Belgium's federal finances. The government had previously considered implementing its own €2 national parcel tax, with estimates suggesting it might generate €300 million annually. However, officials shelved these plans in December 2025 in favor of the more lucrative EU levy.

Trade federation Comeos had advocated for a Belgian handling fee, estimating potential revenue of €2 billion based on 2024 package volumes. The significantly higher projections for the EU tax reflect both the broader scope of the measure and Belgium's strategic position in European logistics.

## Uncertainties and Implementation Challenges

Despite optimistic revenue projections, several uncertainties could affect final outcomes. Consumer behavior may change in response to the tax, potentially reducing cross-border purchases from non-EU platforms. Large e-commerce players might develop strategies to circumvent the tax, and package volumes could fluctuate due to broader economic factors.

Implementation presents practical challenges for Belgian customs authorities, who must process billions of small packages annually while ensuring compliance from non-EU sellers. The administrative burden of collecting and distributing revenues across the EU also requires careful coordination among member states.

Political considerations remain, particularly regarding final revenue-sharing percentages in the EU budget negotiations and potential trade tensions with China and other affected countries.

## What's Next

The European Commission is expected to make final determinations on the handling fee amount by summer 2026, deciding between €2 and €3 per package and considering potential variations for goods stored in special distribution centers before online sale.

For Belgium, the tax represents a significant opportunity to bolster federal finances while positioning the country at the forefront of EU efforts to regulate digital commerce. As the implementation dates approach, attention will focus on customs capacity, enforcement mechanisms, and the tax's impact on consumer behavior and e-commerce patterns.

The ultimate revenue remains unpredictable, dependent on multiple variables including final fee determinations, enforcement effectiveness, and market responses. However, for Belgium's "armlastige" (cash-strapped) federal budget, as described in VRT NWS reporting, each additional euro represents welcome relief amid broader fiscal challenges.

As the July 2026 implementation date approaches, European authorities will need to balance revenue generation with maintaining smooth trade flows, ensuring the new tax achieves its objectives without unduly disrupting the digital economy that has transformed consumer behavior across the continent.
