# Belgian Expert Accuses PM De Wever of 'Caesarism' Over Senate

A prominent Belgian constitutional law professor has launched a scathing critique of Prime Minister Bart De Wever's plan to abolish the Senate, accusing him of "Caesarism" and constitutional manipulation. In a [published opinion piece in Le Soir](https://www.lesoir.be/737612/article/2026-03-29/le-senat-face-au-cesarisme-de-bart-de-wever), Anne-Emmanuelle Bourgaux argues that the proposed abolition violates constitutional procedures and represents a dangerous concentration of power that threatens Belgium's democratic foundations.

## Context: The Senate's Historical Role and Political Backdrop

The Belgian Senate, established in 1831 as part of the original bicameral system, has survived two world wars, the Royal Question, major social upheavals, and six state reforms. While its powers have diminished over time—currently limited to constitutional matters, inter-parliamentary mediation, and reports on multi-level governance—it played crucial roles in historic legislation including abortion (1990), euthanasia (2002), same-sex marriage (2003), and medically assisted reproduction (2007).

The push for abolition comes from the "Arizona Coalition" government formed in January 2025, comprising five parties across Belgium's linguistic divide: N-VA (Flemish nationalist), MR (Francophone liberal), Les Engagés (Francophone centrist), Vooruit (Flemish socialist), and CD&V (Flemish Christian democrat). The coalition agreement, reached after three days of intense negotiations in a military barracks, dedicates approximately one-third of a single page to Senate abolition within its 208-page document.

## Constitutional Violations and 'Constitutional Butchery'

Professor Bourgaux's central argument focuses on constitutional procedure. According to Article 195 of the Belgian Constitution, constitutional revision requires specific declarations published in the official Moniteur Belge, which define the precise perimeter of what can be revised during a legislative term. The current declarations, published on May 27, 2024, include only 14 articles related to the Senate within their revision perimeter.

"A trentaine d'autres articles font aussi référence au Sénat et eux, ils ne font pas partie de ce périmètre," Bourgaux writes. "Il n'est donc juridiquement pas possible de supprimer purement et simplement le Sénat sous cette législature."

The government's proposed solution, according to the constitutional expert, constitutes "constitutional butchery." The plan involves first revising the 14 articles within the perimeter using standard procedures, then temporarily revising Article 195 itself to reach five additional articles outside the perimeter, and finally using Article 198—intended for technical "toileting" like renumbering articles—to eliminate the Senate from the remaining 27 constitutional references.

"Quand la Constitution ne correspond pas à ses désirs, un démocrate s'incline," Bourgaux argues. "Un César fait l'inverse : il force la Constitution."

## Political Timeline and Democratic Deliberation Concerns

The abolition process has followed an accelerated timeline that critics say lacks proper democratic deliberation. After a single Senate committee session on its own abolition on November 17, 2025—where De Wever reportedly "did not hesitate to recall to order the slightest trembling of the executioner's hand"—the process stalled until suddenly being revived with votes scheduled for March 30 in commission and April 3 in plenary session.

"Dans une démocratie représentative digne de ce nom, une certaine qualité délibérative devrait entourer une décision aussi cruciale," Bourgaux contends. "C'est compter sans le césarisme de Bart De Wever."

Fourteen civil society associations have requested an open public debate on the Senate's future, highlighting what Bourgaux describes as the "surreal" situation where "citizens fly to the rescue of institutions attacked by the authorities who should protect them."

## Analysis: Implications for Belgian Democracy and Federalism

The "Caesarism" accusation reflects deeper concerns about De Wever's leadership style and the future of Belgium's complex consensus democracy. As the first Flemish nationalist prime minister in Belgian history, De Wever has positioned himself as a unifying figure, having "traded his aggressive Flemish nationalist rags for the costume of a calm Belgian prime minister," according to Bourgaux.

However, the Senate abolition approach suggests a different reality. The expert warns that if De Wever "persists and signs as the gravedigger of the Senate, Caesar will be naked. He will demonstrate that only the first shovel matters to him. That of beginning to dig the grave of Belgian democracy."

The constitutional questions extend beyond procedural technicalities. Belgium's federal structure, with its delicate balance between Dutch-speaking Flanders and French-speaking Wallonia, relies on multiple checks and balances. The Senate, while weakened, represents one of these mechanisms. Its abolition without clear replacement for representing federated entities raises questions about regional representation in a country where linguistic and regional tensions have historically required careful institutional management.

## What's Next: Political and Constitutional Crossroads

The immediate future hinges on the scheduled votes. The government needs a two-thirds majority for constitutional revision, which the Arizona Coalition lacks alone. Potential support from the French-speaking Socialist Party (PS), which supports abolition but wants constitutionalization of abortion rights, faces obstacles from coalition partner CD&V's opposition to abortion constitutionalization.

Beyond the immediate political arithmetic, Bourgaux suggests alternative futures for the Senate: transforming it into a citizens' assembly building on Belgium's international recognition for sortition experiments, or strengthening it as a counterweight to the "febrile, media-driven, and conflictual" Chamber of Representatives and the opaque executive Committee of Concertation.

As Belgium approaches this constitutional crossroads, the debate transcends institutional reform to touch fundamental questions about democratic norms, constitutional integrity, and leadership in a complex federal state. The outcome will test both the resilience of Belgium's consensus traditions and the limits of constitutional revision in the face of determined political will.
