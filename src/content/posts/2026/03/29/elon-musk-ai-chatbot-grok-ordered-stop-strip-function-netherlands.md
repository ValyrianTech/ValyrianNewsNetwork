# Dutch Court Orders Elon Musk's Grok to Disable Strip Feature

In a landmark ruling with potential global implications, the Amsterdam District Court has ordered Elon Musk's artificial intelligence chatbot Grok to disable its controversial "strip function" in the Netherlands. The court imposed daily fines of €100,000 (approximately $115,000) for non-compliance, with penalties potentially reaching €10 million. The decision, announced on March 26, 2026, represents one of the first major legal actions in Europe against AI-generated non-consensual sexual imagery and coincides with broader European Union efforts to ban such "nudification" tools.

## Context and Legal Grounds

The case was brought by Dutch non-profit organizations Offlimits, an expertise center for online abuse, and Fonds Slachtofferhulp (Victim Support Fund). According to [NU.nl](https://www.nu.nl/tech/6389619/ai-chatbot-grok-van-elon-musk-moet-in-nederland-stoppen-met-uitkleedfunctie.html), the court prohibited xAI, the company behind Grok, from "generating and/or distributing sexual imagery ... whereby persons are partially or wholly stripped naked without having given their explicit permission." The ruling applies specifically to images of individuals residing in the Netherlands and the production or distribution of such content within the country.

Robbert Hoving, director of Offlimits, expressed satisfaction with the decision, telling NU.nl, "We have this for the Dutch victims. But Grok doesn't know where victims come from, so actually this applies worldwide." This technical limitation means xAI may need to implement global restrictions to comply with the Dutch ruling.

## The Controversial Feature and Its Impact

Grok's strip function, integrated into Musk's X platform (formerly Twitter), allowed users to generate images of people removing clothing or appearing in sexualized poses. The feature faced intense scrutiny after a December 2025 report by the Center for Countering Digital Hate (CCDH) revealed that Grok had generated approximately 3 million sexualized images in just 11 days, including over 23,000 images appearing to depict children.

Despite xAI implementing restrictions in January 2026 that limited image generation to paid subscribers, plaintiffs demonstrated in court on March 9 that Grok could still "undress" digital images. According to [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-07/musk-s-grok-ai-generated-thousands-of-undressed-images-per-hour-on-x?embedded-checkout=true), cited in the NU.nl article, Grok was making "thousands of pictures of undressed people per hour" at its peak.

Ineke Sybesma of Fonds Slachtofferhulp emphasized the real-world impact: "For victims it doesn't matter whether an image is 'real' or made with AI. And what makes it extra cruel is that it has no end. The shame is real, the consequences are real and it doesn't stop by itself, because the internet doesn't forget."

## Broader Regulatory Landscape

The Dutch ruling coincides with significant regulatory developments at the European level. On the same day as the court decision, the European Parliament voted to ban AI "nudifier" apps across all EU member states. This EU-wide prohibition is expected to take effect in July 2026, creating a comprehensive regulatory framework against such tools.

Hoving believes this European action will set a precedent beyond the EU's borders: "Now American search engines like Google and Bing still show those apps in their results," he noted. "That won't be allowed anymore soon. But it's true: the law is a dead letter if it's not enforced. So if companies don't comply with the ban, we really have to go after them."

The case also unfolds against the backdrop of the EU's Digital Services Act (DSA), under which the European Commission opened an investigation into X in January 2026 over risks associated with Grok's rollout, including manipulated sexually explicit images.

## International Reactions and Precedents

The Netherlands is not alone in addressing this issue. Malaysia and Indonesia blocked access to Grok in January 2026, while UK regulators (Ofcom and the Information Commissioner's Office) are investigating X over non-consensual sexual imagery. In the United States, Baltimore became the first major city to sue xAI on March 24, 2026, alleging violations of consumer protection laws, and three teenagers in Tennessee have filed suit after Grok created sexually explicit content depicting them.

Demissionary Dutch Minister of Justice and Security Foort van Oosten had previously called AI strip apps "extremely reprehensible," but acknowledged the complexity of developing an appropriate regulatory response. Involved agencies, including the Dutch Data Protection Authority, the Authority for Consumers & Markets, and the Authority for Online Terrorist and Child Pornographic Material, all described the issue as complex.

## Analysis and Implications

This ruling represents a significant milestone in the regulation of generative AI and the protection of individuals against digital abuse. Several key implications emerge:

1. **Platform Liability**: The decision establishes that AI companies bear responsibility for preventing misuse of their tools, particularly when such misuse causes demonstrable harm. This shifts the burden from individual victims to technology providers.

2. **Cross-border Enforcement**: While technically applying only to the Netherlands, the ruling's practical effect may be global due to Grok's inability to determine user locations. This creates pressure for xAI to implement worldwide restrictions.

3. **Legal Precedent**: As one of the first European court rulings specifically targeting AI-generated non-consensual sexual imagery, this case may influence similar actions in other jurisdictions.

4. **Regulatory Coordination**: The coincidence of national court action with EU-wide legislative measures suggests a coordinated regulatory approach is emerging in Europe, potentially setting global standards for AI ethics and platform accountability.

## What's Next

The immediate question is whether xAI will appeal the ruling or implement the required changes. The company faces daily fines starting immediately, with Grok potentially being blocked from the X platform in the Netherlands during any period of non-compliance.

Looking ahead, the EU-wide ban expected in July 2026 will create a uniform regulatory environment across member states. This may prompt similar legislation in other regions and increase pressure on global technology companies to implement more robust content moderation systems.

As Hoving noted, enforcement will be crucial: "The law is a dead letter if it's not enforced." This case demonstrates that courts and regulators are increasingly willing to intervene when self-regulation proves insufficient, marking a new chapter in the ongoing tension between rapid AI innovation and fundamental rights protection.

The ruling also highlights the need for technical solutions that can effectively prevent AI misuse while preserving legitimate uses, a challenge that will likely shape the next generation of AI development and regulation.
