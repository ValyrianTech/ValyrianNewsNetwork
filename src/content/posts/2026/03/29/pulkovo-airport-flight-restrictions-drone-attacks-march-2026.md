# Pulkovo Airport Imposes Flight Restrictions Amid Escalating Drone Attacks

St. Petersburg's Pulkovo Airport has imposed temporary restrictions on flight departures, [according to Russian state news agency RBC](https://www.rbc.ru/rbcfreenews/69c8e7e99a79475a6a029b83), as Russia faces one of the largest Ukrainian drone assaults since the conflict began. The airport is "temporarily only accepting incoming aircraft due to temporary restrictions on the use of airspace," with the measures necessary to ensure flight safety amid ongoing drone attacks.

The restrictions, announced on March 29, 2026, come as Russian air defenses reported intercepting 203 Ukrainian drones overnight across more than 20 regions. This marks the second major disruption at Pulkovo in less than a week, highlighting how drone warfare is increasingly affecting civilian infrastructure deep inside Russian territory.

## Context: Escalating Drone Warfare

The current restrictions follow a pattern of escalating Ukrainian drone attacks on Russian territory throughout March 2026. Just six days earlier, on March 23, Pulkovo Airport experienced similar disruptions when [over 100 flights were delayed or canceled](https://www.aa.com.tr/en/russia-ukraine-war/over-100-flights-delayed-or-canceled-in-russias-st-petersburg-due-to-ukrainian-drone-attack/3875421) due to another major drone assault. During that incident, 39 flights were diverted to alternate airports, 34 flights were delayed by more than two hours, and 30 outbound flights were canceled.

Visual evidence from that earlier disruption showed the human impact, with [Reuters Connect footage documenting](https://www.reutersconnect.com/item/more-than-100-flights-affected-people-sleep-on-floors-in-st-petersburg-airport-amid-massive-drone-attack/dGFnOnJldXRlcnMuY29tLDIwMjY6bmV3c21sX1ZBNjUzNDIzMDMyMDI2UlAx) passengers sleeping on airport floors as terminals became overcrowded with stranded travelers. The airport had advised passengers on canceled flights not to remain in terminals, but many had few alternatives.

## Current Security Situation

The March 29 restrictions come amid what Russian authorities describe as one of the most intensive drone attacks to date. According to military analysis site [Voennoedelo.com](https://voennoedelo.com/en/posts/id14488-russia-downs-203-ukrainian-drones-over-20-regions), Russian air defenses destroyed 203 Ukrainian fixed-wing drones overnight between March 28 and 29, with interceptions reported across a vast geographical area including Belgorod, Bryansk, Voronezh, Kaluga, Kursk, Leningrad, and Moscow regions.

In Leningrad Region alone—where St. Petersburg is located—31 drones were intercepted during the night of March 29. Regional governor Alexander Drozdenko reported that a fire broke out at the port of Ust-Luga, a major energy export hub on the Baltic Sea. This follows earlier attacks on Russian energy infrastructure, including a March 25 strike on the same port that [Ukraine's military confirmed targeted Novatek's liquefied natural gas facilities](https://kyivindependent.com/drone-attack-hits-russias-leningrad-region-fire-reported-at-ust-luga-port/).

[The Kyiv Independent reported that the March 25 attack was a joint operation](https://kyivindependent.com/drone-attack-hits-russias-leningrad-region-fire-reported-at-ust-luga-port/) involving Ukraine's Special Operations Forces, Security Service, military intelligence, and Border Guard Service, striking facilities nearly 900 kilometers from Ukraine's northern border. Ukraine has increasingly targeted Russia's energy export infrastructure, which generates crucial revenue for Moscow's war effort.

## Airport Operations and Passenger Impact

Pulkovo Airport's restrictions represent a significant operational challenge for one of Russia's busiest air travel hubs. Before the war, the airport served over 19 million passengers annually, making it a critical transportation node for northwestern Russia and international connections.

The current measures mean the airport is only processing incoming flights while departure restrictions remain in place. This creates a backlog of outbound passengers and disrupts flight schedules across Russia's aviation network. The Russian aviation sector has already faced numerous challenges in recent years, including international sanctions, parts shortages, and now repeated security disruptions.

Governor Drozdenko's office reported that air danger alerts in the region have been fluctuating throughout the day. An initial alert was lifted at 9:47 Moscow time, but approximately two hours later, Drozdenko announced the reimplementation of air danger protocols in the Volkhov, Kirishi, and Tikhvin districts of Leningrad Region.

## Broader Military and Strategic Implications

The repeated drone attacks on targets deep inside Russia represent a significant evolution in Ukraine's military capabilities. What began as primarily frontline combat has expanded to include strategic strikes on military, energy, and transportation infrastructure far from the battlefield.

Military analysts note several important developments:

1. **Range and Precision:** Ukraine's ability to strike targets nearly 900 kilometers inside Russia demonstrates substantial advancements in drone technology and operational planning.

2. **Economic Targeting:** The focus on energy export infrastructure like the Ust-Luga port aims to undermine Russia's war financing capabilities by reducing oil and gas revenue.

3. **Psychological Impact:** Disrupting civilian life in major cities like St. Petersburg demonstrates that the war's consequences extend beyond frontline regions.

4. **Defensive Strain:** The scale of attacks—203 drones intercepted in one night—tests the limits of Russian air defense systems and creates opportunities for some drones to penetrate defenses.

## Aviation Safety Concerns

The repeated closures and restrictions at Pulkovo Airport raise significant aviation safety concerns. Civilian air travel in conflict zones presents unique challenges, and the proximity of active military operations to major airports creates complex risk management scenarios.

International aviation authorities typically recommend avoiding airspace where active conflict or military operations are occurring. However, for domestic Russian flights, alternatives are limited, and complete suspension of air travel would have severe economic and social consequences.

The restrictions also highlight the vulnerability of critical transportation infrastructure to asymmetric warfare. Airports represent high-value targets whose disruption causes immediate economic impact and public visibility.

## Looking Ahead

The situation at Pulkovo Airport underscores the evolving nature of the Russia-Ukraine conflict as it enters its fifth year. Several key developments will be worth monitoring:

1. **Duration of Restrictions:** How long will flight restrictions remain in place, and will they become a recurring pattern?

2. **Ukrainian Strategy:** Will Ukraine continue to prioritize attacks on energy and transportation infrastructure, and what new targets might emerge?

3. **Russian Defenses:** Can Russia develop more effective counter-drone measures that minimize civilian disruption while maintaining security?

4. **International Response:** How will repeated attacks on civilian infrastructure affect diplomatic efforts and international perceptions of the conflict?

For now, travelers through Pulkovo Airport face continued uncertainty. The airport authorities have stated they are working to minimize disruptions, but with drone attacks occurring with increasing frequency and scale, the aviation sector in northwestern Russia may need to adapt to a new normal of intermittent restrictions and security alerts.

The broader implication is clear: as drone technology becomes more accessible and capable, civilian infrastructure far from traditional battlefields is increasingly vulnerable in modern conflicts. The disruptions at Pulkovo Airport serve as a stark reminder that in today's warfare, there are no truly rear areas.
