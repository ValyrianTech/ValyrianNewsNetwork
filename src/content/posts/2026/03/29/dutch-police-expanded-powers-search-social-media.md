# Dutch Police Granted Expanded Powers to Search Social Media Platforms

The Dutch government has approved legislation granting police expanded powers to search and collect data from social media and other online platforms, a move aimed at preventing public order disturbances but raising significant privacy concerns. The "Wet gegevensvergaring openbare orde" (Public Order Data Collection Act) was approved by the Council of Ministers on March 27, 2026, and now moves to parliamentary debate.

According to the [official government announcement](https://www.rijksoverheid.nl/actueel/nieuws/2026/03/27/kabinet-versterkt-aanpak-ernstige-verstoringen-openbare-orde-met-nieuwe-politiebevoegdheden), the new law provides police with two key powers: collecting personal data from the public part of the internet, and collecting online data of persons and their public accounts. Minister of Justice and Security David van Weel (VVD) stated that "serious disturbances of public order are increasingly being driven and organized via social media and other online platforms."

## Current Legal Gap and New Authorities

Currently, Dutch police may look at public sources on the internet but cannot store or use information from them. The new legislation creates a legal basis for collecting and using this data. As [RTL Nieuws reported](https://www.rtl.nl/nieuws/politiek/artikel/5583185/politie-krijgt-extra-bevoegdheden-voor-doorzoeken-sociale-media), this change addresses what Minister Van Weel called a "frustrating" limitation for law enforcement.

"With this legislative proposal, the police are being brought into the 21st century," Van Weel told reporters. The legislation specifically targets publicly accessible information and does not apply to private or closed groups, though the cabinet has indicated it wants to extend these powers to private groups in the future.

## Direct Response to Recent Riots

The legislation was directly prompted by the Malieveld riots of September 2025, when an anti-asylum policy demonstration escalated into violent riots where police were attacked and the D66 party office was damaged. The [NOS coverage](https://nos.nl/artikel/2608076-kabinet-wil-politie-extra-bevoegdheden-geven-voor-doorzoeken-sociale-media) notes that similar disturbances on Scheveningen Boulevard in May 2025 also influenced the government's decision.

Minister Van Weel emphasized that the new powers would allow police to intervene more effectively when there are indications that riots are about to break out. "It is therefore essential that the police may collect online information to be able to intervene in a timely manner," he stated in the government announcement.

## Safeguards and Oversight Requirements

The legislation includes several safeguards due to its potential impact on citizens' privacy. Police can only act on a mayor's instructions, who must obtain prior permission from an examining magistrate (rechter-commissaris). Additionally, a strict regime governs the processing of collected personal data.

However, the Dutch Data Protection Authority (Autoriteit Persoonsgegevens, AP) has issued devastating criticism of the concept law. In March 2026, the AP stated that the proposal "lacks essential safeguards" and could lead to "untargeted and disproportionate monitoring of unsuspected citizens." The authority warned that without proper boundaries, systems could theoretically harvest almost the entire publicly accessible internet, including large amounts of non-relevant and sensitive data.

## Critical Perspectives and Privacy Concerns

The Data Protection Authority's criticisms highlight several key concerns:

1. **Insufficient safeguards** against mass surveillance
2. **Risk of discriminatory targeting** where the same groups could be systematically monitored without cause
3. **Algorithmic profiling dangers** from systems based on statistical correlations rather than deep insight
4. **Lack of transparency** in data processing choices
5. **Processing of illegal data** from unlawfully published information leaks

Other critical voices include the Dutch Bar Association, which stated that "legal protection has not or hardly received attention" in the proposal, and the Human Rights College, which expressed concerns about "function creep"—where the law could be used for less serious purposes than intended.

## Legislative Process and Political Landscape

The proposal now goes to the Council of State for advice before parliamentary debate in both the Second Chamber (Tweede Kamer) and First Chamber (Eerste Kamer). The current minority government needs at least nine extra seats for a parliamentary majority, but many opposition parties reportedly support the proposal.

Public consultation on the legislation received dozens of negative or critical responses from citizens, reflecting divided public opinion about balancing security needs with privacy rights.

## Historical Context and Related Issues

The legislation exists within a broader context of government surveillance debates in the Netherlands. Recent controversies include:

- **NCTV Fake Accounts Controversy**: Former minister Grapperhaus stated the National Coordinator for Security and Counterterrorism may have broken the law with fake accounts
- **Police Data Collection Scandals**: AP reports of police intelligence teams unlawfully collecting sensitive citizen data (March 2026)
- **OSINT Legislation Gap**: Broader context of missing nationwide framework for government automated data collection from open sources

## Implications and Forward Look

The short-term implications involve the legislative process through the Council of State and parliament, with potential amendments based on the Data Protection Authority's criticisms. Long-term concerns include the establishment of precedent for government online surveillance powers and the risk of "function creep"—expansion to less serious uses.

As digital platforms become increasingly central to protest organization and public discourse, this legislation represents a significant test case for how democracies balance law enforcement needs with fundamental privacy rights in the digital age. The outcome will likely influence similar debates across Europe and shape the future of digital policing in democratic societies.

The legislation's effectiveness will ultimately depend on both its implementation and the ongoing oversight mechanisms that ensure these new powers are used proportionately and transparently, without unduly infringing on civil liberties that form the foundation of Dutch democracy.
