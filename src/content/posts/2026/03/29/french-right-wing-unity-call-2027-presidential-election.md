# French Right-Wing Unity Call for 2027 Presidential Election

Ninety prominent French right-wing and centrist politicians have issued an urgent appeal for a single presidential candidate for the 2027 election, warning that a fragmented vote could lead to a dangerous runoff between far-left leader Jean-Luc Mélenchon and the far-right Rassemblement National. The call, published in La Tribune Dimanche on March 28, 2026, comes exactly one year before the presidential vote and one week after municipal elections revealed a deeply divided political landscape.

## Growing Momentum for Right-Wing Unity

The appeal represents the most significant coordinated effort yet to unite France's fragmented center-right political forces. According to [Le Figaro](https://www.lefigaro.fr/politique/90-personnalites-de-droite-et-du-centre-dont-maud-bregeon-et-vincent-jeanbrun-appellent-a-une-candidature-unique-pour-2027-20260328), the signatories include government ministers, members of parliament, and senators from across the political spectrum. They warn that without unity, France faces "a probable duel in the second round of the presidential election between Jean-Luc Mélenchon and the Rassemblement National candidate."

"To avoid this one-way ticket to populism, we, elected officials from the central bloc and the right, are speaking out today," the signatories wrote. "We call for a surge of unity to create the conditions for a broad gathering around a single project and a single candidacy in 2027."

## Key Signatories and Political Reach

The list of signatories reveals the breadth of support for unity across traditional party lines. From President Emmanuel Macron's Renaissance party, signatories include government spokesperson and Energy Minister Maud Bregeon, ministers Stéphanie Rist and Mathieu Lefèvre, and former ministers turned parliamentarians Stéphane Travert, Astrid Panosyan-Bouvet, and Marc Ferracci.

From the main opposition Les Républicains (LR), the appeal includes ministers Vincent Jeanbrun and Philippe Tabarot, Senator François-Noël Buffet, and MP Michèle Tabarot. The call also draws support from smaller parties including Edouard Philippe's Horizons party (with Overseas Minister Naïma Moutchou), the centrist MoDem, the center-right UDI, and the independent Liot parliamentary group.

In a subsequent interview with Le Parisien on March 29, Maud Bregeon emphasized the existential stakes: "The issue in 2027 is not to save parties. I don't give a damn about saving parties. If we have two, three, four candidates from the right and center, the probability of disappearing from the political game is obviously very high."

## Electoral Mathematics and Historical Precedents

The unity push reflects growing anxiety about electoral mathematics. Current polling suggests that with multiple candidates from the center-right bloc, none might qualify for the second round, potentially leaving only Mélenchon's La France Insoumise (LFI) and the Rassemblement National (RN) to compete for the presidency.

This fear echoes historical precedents that haunt French politics. In the 2002 presidential election, a divided left allowed far-right leader Jean-Marie Le Pen to reach the second round against Jacques Chirac. More recently, the fragmentation of traditional parties in both 2017 and 2022 enabled Emmanuel Macron's rise and reelection as a centrist outsider.

Senate President Gérard Larcher, an LR figure, added his voice to the unity chorus earlier in the week, calling for a "single candidate" from the right and center "by November at the latest." Meanwhile, centrist senator Hervé Marseille expressed opposition to expensive primaries, suggesting instead that "polls and public opinion" should determine by autumn "those who can compete and those who must withdraw."

## Methodological Challenges and Leadership Dynamics

Despite broad agreement on the need for unity, significant obstacles remain. The appeal notably avoids specifying how a single candidate should be selected, stating only that "methods don't matter, paths don't count." This vagueness reflects deep divisions over process, particularly between those advocating for primaries and those favoring consensus-based selection.

Leadership dynamics present another major challenge. At least seven prominent figures are either declared candidates or frequently mentioned as potential contenders: Bruno Retailleau (LR president), Edouard Philippe (Horizons), Gabriel Attal (former prime minister), Gérald Darmanin (interior minister), Michel Barnier (former minister), Dominique de Villepin (former prime minister), and potentially others.

Bruno Retailleau has already expressed opposition to primaries, warning they could "kill LR." Meanwhile, Edouard Philippe emerges in polls as the only center-right figure consistently beating the RN in hypothetical matchups, making him a potential frontrunner but also a target for rivals.

## The Populist Threat and Political Realignment

The appeal's stark warning about "a one-way ticket to populism" reflects broader European trends where traditional center-right parties struggle against populist movements. In Italy, Giorgia Meloni's Brothers of Italy has governed successfully for over three years, while in Portugal, the recent presidential election saw strong performances from populist candidates.

Maud Bregeon framed the choice bluntly in her interview: "We tell them: listen to each other, because we didn't go into politics to leave the country to Mr. Bardella or Mr. Mélenchon." On Edouard Philippe's potential candidacy, she added: "He has certainly taken a serious option. He is today one of those, perhaps the one, who is best placed among us to carry this fight."

## What Comes Next for French Politics

The coming months will test whether France's political elites can overcome personal ambitions and party loyalties. The signatories have "solemnly asked the leaders of our political families, declared candidates, and those who might participate tomorrow, to dialogue and build this approach," while "exhorting each to overcome the quarrels of ego."

Several key deadlines loom: Gérard Larcher's November target for selecting a single candidate, Hervé Marseille's suggestion that polls should decide by autumn, and the official campaign start in early 2027 ahead of the April first round.

The success or failure of this unity effort could determine not just the 2027 election outcome but the future viability of France's traditional center-right political families. As the appeal warns, failure to unite risks marginalizing established parties in a political landscape increasingly dominated by anti-establishment movements from both left and right.

With exactly one year until voters go to the polls, the pressure for France's fragmented right and center to find common ground has never been greater—or more urgent.
