# Russia Plans Underground Oil Storage for Energy Security

Russian authorities have identified multiple potential locations across the country for constructing underground oil storage facilities, a strategic move aimed at enhancing energy security and logistical flexibility amid ongoing sanctions and volatile global markets. According to [TASS](https://tass.ru/ekonomika/26926023), Oleg Kazanov, head of Rosnedra (Russia's Federal Agency for Mineral Resources), revealed in an interview that companies are studying the possibility of creating such storage from the Irkutsk region to the European part of Russia, though only state-owned Rosneft is currently working on such projects.

## Strategic Context: Sanctions and Energy Resilience

The push for underground oil storage represents a significant shift in Russia's energy infrastructure strategy, driven by geopolitical pressures and market uncertainties. Since 2022, Western sanctions have targeted Russia's energy exports, including price caps on Urals oil and restrictions on shipping and insurance. These measures have forced Moscow to adapt its energy logistics, including building a "shadow" tanker fleet and seeking alternative markets.

Underground storage offers several advantages over traditional surface facilities: enhanced resilience against potential attacks on energy infrastructure, lower operational costs, and greater strategic flexibility. The concept gained traction during the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020 when demand plummeted and prices collapsed, then intensified after the 2022 boycott of Russian crude oil. Experts from the Russian Gas Society previously estimated that optimal strategic reserves should equal 10-20% of annual production—approximately 55-100 million tonnes based on 2019 output levels.

## Key Developments: From Krasnoyarsk to Nationwide Network

Russia's first underground oil storage facility, located in the Krasnoyarsk territory within the Arctic zone, is ready for trial operation according to [Interfax](https://interfax.com/newsroom/top-stories/111428/). Nina Erofeeva, head of Rosnedra's Oil and Gas Geology, Groundwater and Structures Department, noted in May 2025 that this marked an "unusual case" for Russia, which has traditionally relied on pipeline transportation rather than storage facilities.

"Given recent events and the lack of infrastructure in the Arctic zone, oil storage facilities are needed in several regions," Erofeeva explained. "Accordingly, oil will be placed in these oil storage facilities so as not to burn it during pilot development." The Krasnoyarsk facility, approved by Rosnedra's Central Commission in September 2023, is designed for operational storage rather than strategic reserves, with what Igor Shpurov, head of the State Mineral Reserves Commission, described as "not a very large volume."

In his recent TASS interview, Kazanov emphasized that depleted oil fields or salt caverns could serve as natural storage sites. "This is a serious point, because especially in conditions of unstable supplies, a situation arises when you cannot shut down wells, you need to accumulate oil in some intermediate storage facilities," he stated. While acknowledging that only Rosneft is currently active in this area, Kazanov added: "Obviously, companies are studying this issue."

## Technical and Logistical Considerations

The choice between constructing new storage facilities versus repurposing existing geological formations presents different timelines and challenges. Building from scratch would take an estimated 10-12 years, but utilizing depleted oil deposits could reduce this to 3-5 years. Salt caverns, used extensively for natural gas storage in other countries, offer another proven technology that could be adapted for oil.

Geographic distribution is also crucial for strategic resilience. Kazanov's mention of locations "from Irkutsk region to the European part of Russia" suggests a nationwide network that would reduce vulnerability to regional disruptions. This aligns with Russia's broader efforts to develop Arctic resources while maintaining supply flexibility to European and Asian markets.

## Strategic Implications and Economic Impact

The underground storage initiative reflects Moscow's adaptation to what it perceives as a permanently hostile international environment. By enhancing storage capacity, Russia gains several strategic advantages:

1. **Operational Flexibility:** The ability to adjust production without shutting down wells during supply disruptions
2. **Market Influence:** Greater capacity to time exports to maximize revenue during price fluctuations
3. **Infrastructure Resilience:** Protection against potential attacks on above-ground facilities
4. **Arctic Development Support:** Storage solutions for remote regions where pipeline infrastructure is limited

Economically, the projects require significant capital investment at a time when sanctions restrict access to Western technology and financing. However, underground storage typically has lower long-term operational costs than surface tanks, and the strategic benefits may justify the initial expenditure from Moscow's perspective.

## What's Next for Russia's Storage Strategy

The full interview with Rosnedra head Oleg Kazanov is scheduled for publication on March 31, 2026, and may provide additional details about specific locations, timelines, and capacity targets. Key questions remain about which companies beyond Rosneft might participate, how the projects will be financed amid sanctions, and what security measures will protect these strategic assets.

Russia's move toward underground oil storage represents more than just infrastructure development—it signals a fundamental shift in how Moscow approaches energy security in an era of geopolitical confrontation. As Western sanctions continue to evolve and global energy markets remain volatile, these storage facilities could provide Russia with crucial flexibility to navigate uncertain terrain while maintaining its position as a major energy exporter.

The success of the Krasnoyarsk pilot project and subsequent expansion will test Russia's ability to innovate within constraints, potentially setting a precedent for how resource-rich nations adapt to changing global energy dynamics in the coming decade.
