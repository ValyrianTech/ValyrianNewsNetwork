# Reform UK Faces Internal Concerns Over Links to Extreme Figures Like Andrew Tate

Reform UK, the political party led by Nigel Farage, is facing growing internal concerns about its association with controversial figures including misogynist influencer Andrew Tate and convicted criminal Tommy Robinson, according to party insiders who fear these connections could alienate moderate voters and damage electoral prospects. The revelations, reported by [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2026/mar/29/reform-nigel-farage-links-extreme-figures-andrew-tate-young-men-misogyny), highlight tensions within the party as it attempts to broaden its appeal while maintaining connections to online personalities popular with some of its base.

## Context and Background

Reform UK has positioned itself as a right-wing populist party advocating for reduced immigration, Brexit completion, and anti-establishment politics. As the party gains traction in polls and prepares for potential governance, it faces the strategic challenge of transitioning from protest movement to mainstream political force. This shift requires appealing to a broader electorate beyond its core supporters, creating tension between maintaining radical appeal and courting moderate voters.

The controversy centers on two particularly problematic associations: Andrew Tate, the influencer facing 21 charges for crimes including human trafficking if he returns to the UK, and Stephen Yaxley-Lennon (known as Tommy Robinson), a convicted criminal and founder of the English Defence League. Farage has previously called Tate an "important voice" for "emasculated" young men and held back from criticizing his misogynistic views, while Robinson has been a vocal supporter of Reform candidates despite the party's official disavowal.

## Key Developments and Internal Concerns

According to The Guardian's report, Reform insiders are becoming increasingly irritated by the party's association with these extreme online celebrities whose views are considered too toxic for mainstream voters. "Yes, I have advised Nigel and others to be more careful about who we associate with – if we are to win over a broader section of British society, we cannot be linked to unpopular characters or people who have made unsavoury comments about women," one source told the publication.

Another senior Reform source attempted to distance the party from Tate, stating: "I am quite familiar with him and what he's like, I look at his social media. He is nothing to do with Reform. We can't help it if people that we don't particularly want around us endorse us. There have been a few cases of that."

The association with Tommy Robinson presents a similar challenge. Though Reform has stated Robinson "isn't welcome in the party," he recently endorsed Reform candidate Matthew Goodwin for the Gorton and Denton byelection. Goodwin did not disavow the endorsement, highlighting the party's struggle to control its associations with controversial figures on the far right.

## Polling Data Reveals Electoral Risk

Research by polling company More in Common provides quantitative evidence supporting insiders' concerns about these associations. According to director Luke Tryl, there is a significant difference in approval ratings between Reform's 2024 voters and newer supporters when it comes to controversial figures.

Tryl's research found Tommy Robinson has an approval rating of +15 with 2024 Reform voters, but new Reform voters give him a -11 approval rating, which sinks to -15 among women. Andrew Tate has a -34 negative approval rating among 2024 Reform voters, which reduces to -46 among new voters and -51 among women.

"The risk then to Reform is their association with online right figures alienates the wavering female voter who ultimately will decide whether Reform gets into government," Tryl told The Guardian. "The sort of voter who is tempted to roll the dice as the status quo feels so miserable, but is also worried Reform may be too extreme."

## Broader Network of Controversial Associations

Tate and Robinson form just one part of a wider network of controversial figures who have associated themselves with Reform. Lady Victoria Hervey, a socialite increasingly seen at Reform events, has described Jeffrey Epstein's victims as liars and prostitutes and called anyone not in the Epstein files a "loser." She was recently photographed with Farage at Donald Trump's Mar-a-Lago resort in Florida.

Former pop star and soap actor Holly Valance, another Mar-a-Lago guest, has been a vocal Reform supporter and referred to climate campaigner Greta Thunberg as a "demonic little gremlin." She is also an associate of Robinson, attending his Unite the Kingdom march in London and stating she was "very proud and pleased of Tommy – this is his redemption."

Few mainstream celebrities have vocally supported the party, but former model Caprice Bourret has become an enthusiastic backer, even joining the campaign for Laila Cunningham to be Reform's candidate for London mayor.

## Analysis and Implications

The controversy over Reform UK's associations highlights the party's strategic dilemma as it transitions from protest movement to potential governing party. While connections to figures like Tate and Robinson may appeal to certain online communities and the party's core base, they risk alienating the moderate voters, particularly women, that Reform needs to win power.

The internal tensions revealed in the Guardian article suggest ongoing debates within Reform about its direction and public image. Some insiders appear to recognize the electoral danger of these associations, while others seem reluctant to fully disavow figures who maintain popularity with portions of the party's base.

This situation mirrors challenges faced by other right-wing populist movements globally as they attempt to balance maintaining radical appeal with broadening their electoral coalitions. The polling data clearly indicates that newer Reform voters and women are particularly put off by associations with extreme figures, suggesting that continued alignment with these personalities could limit the party's growth potential.

## What's Next

As UK politics becomes increasingly polarized, how Reform navigates these associations will significantly impact its electoral prospects and place in the political landscape. The party faces several immediate challenges:

1. **Official positioning:** Will Reform leadership publicly distance the party from Andrew Tate and other controversial figures, or maintain ambiguous relationships to avoid alienating parts of its base?

2. **Candidate vetting:** How will the party handle future endorsements from controversial figures and ensure candidates disavow problematic associations?

3. **Gender gap:** Can Reform address its apparent gender gap, exacerbated by associations with misogynistic figures, without fundamentally changing its appeal to certain male demographics?

4. **Mainstreaming strategy:** Does Reform have a coherent strategy for appealing to moderate voters while maintaining its anti-establishment credentials?

The coming months will reveal whether Reform can successfully navigate these tensions or whether associations with extreme figures will continue to hinder its transition from protest party to potential governing force. With by-elections approaching and general election speculation increasing, the party's handling of these controversies may prove decisive in determining its electoral ceiling.
