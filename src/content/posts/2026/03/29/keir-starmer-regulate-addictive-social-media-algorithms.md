# Keir Starmer Vows to Regulate Addictive Social Media Algorithms

UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer has announced his strongest intervention yet on social media regulation, vowing to curb addictive features that hook children and young people to digital platforms. In an exclusive interview with the Sunday Mirror, Starmer said addictive algorithms "shouldn't be permitted" and that the government "will have to act" to protect children from harmful design features.

## Context: US Court Ruling Sets Precedent

The announcement comes just days after a landmark US court ruling found Meta and YouTube liable for deliberately designing addictive products that harmed a 20-year-old woman. According to [The Guardian's report on the US trial](https://www.theguardian.com/media/2026/mar/25/jury-verdict-us-first-social-media-addiction-trial-meta-youtube), a Los Angeles jury awarded $6 million in damages after determining the tech giants had engineered features to maximize addiction, particularly among young users.

Starmer referenced this case directly, telling the Sunday Mirror: "This is the platforms trying to get children to stay on for longer, to get addicted. I can't see that there's a case for that, and therefore I can see we're going to have to act."

## Government Consultation Underway

The UK government is currently consulting on a range of potential measures to protect children online. Education Secretary Bridget Phillipson, speaking on BBC's Sunday With Laura Kuenssberg, confirmed the government is examining "addictive features and some of the algorithmically driven content we know can be damaging for our youngest children."

Phillipson told the BBC: "I think as an adult it's hard to escape the conclusion that some of this is designed to get your attention and to keep your attention. Now, that's one thing for an adult, but of course we have to think pretty seriously about what that means for the developing brains of younger children."

## Proposed Regulatory Measures

The consultation, which closes on May 26, 2026, has already received nearly 30,000 responses from parents and children. Potential measures under consideration include:

- **Banning addictive algorithms** for younger users
- **Social media restrictions for under-16s**, following Australia's model
- **Time limits on app usage** and overnight digital curfews
- **Restrictions on specific design features** like 'streaks' (encouraging daily app usage) and infinite scrolling

Starmer said he remains "open-minded" about a complete social media ban for under-16s but emphasized: "We'll go through the consultation, but I think I'll be absolutely clear, things will not stay as they are. This is going to change. I don't think the next generation would forgive us if we didn't act now."

## Pilot Program and Screen Time Guidance

As part of the consultation process, hundreds of UK teenagers will trial social media bans, digital curfews, and time limits on apps in a government pilot program. According to [The Guardian's coverage of the pilot](https://www.theguardian.com/uk-news/2026/mar/25/hundreds-of-uk-teenagers-to-trial-six-week-social-media-curbs-for-major-study), 300 teenagers across all four UK nations will participate, with some having their social apps disabled to "mimic the enforcement of a social media ban at home."

The announcement follows new government screen time guidance published on March 26, which recommends:
- **Under-2s:** Avoid screen time except for shared interactive activities
- **Ages 2-5:** Maximum one hour per day
- **Avoid screens** during bedtimes and mealtimes

[The Guardian reported on this guidance](https://www.theguardian.com/society/2026/mar/26/under-fives-should-have-at-most-hour-a-day-of-screen-time-under-new-uk-advice), noting that 98% of UK children watch screens daily by age 2, with high screen time linked to language development delays.

## International Regulatory Landscape

The UK's move aligns with growing international consensus on digital regulation. Australia has already enacted a social media ban for under-16s, while Spain recently announced similar plans. The European Union is investigating Snapchat over child safety concerns and has implemented the Digital Services Act, which imposes stricter obligations on large platforms.

In the United States, multiple lawsuits are pending against social media companies, with a federal trial scheduled for June 2026. The recent California verdict has created significant legal precedent that could influence regulatory approaches worldwide.

## Analysis: Shifting Political Consensus

Starmer's intervention represents a significant shift in UK digital policy. Previously, governments have been cautious about regulating tech companies, often citing innovation concerns. However, growing evidence of harm to children's mental health—including the 2022 coroner's ruling that social media contributed to 14-year-old Molly Russell's death—has changed the political calculus.

Tech Secretary Liz Kendall has indicated the government is considering annual legislative updates to keep pace with rapidly evolving technology, similar to Australia's approach. This would represent a more agile regulatory framework than traditional legislation.

## Implications for Tech Companies

The proposed regulations could force major redesigns of popular social media features. Platforms might need to:
- Remove or modify infinite scroll and autoplay features for young users
- Implement robust age verification systems
- Redesign engagement metrics to prioritize safety over time-on-platform
- Face increased liability exposure following the US legal precedent

Meta and YouTube have both indicated they plan to appeal the US court decision, with Google stating its platforms are "responsibly built" and include parental controls.

## What's Next

The digital wellbeing consultation will continue until May 26, with the government expected to announce its policy response later in 2026. Key questions remain:

1. How will age verification for under-16s be implemented effectively?
2. What specific regulatory powers will the government seek?
3. How will regulations balance protection with children's rights to digital participation?
4. What international coordination is planned with EU, US, and other jurisdictions?

Starmer's strong language suggests the UK is prepared to take a leadership role in child online protection. As he told the Sunday Mirror: "Things will not stay as they are. This is going to change." The coming months will determine whether the UK can establish a regulatory framework that protects children while maintaining a thriving digital economy.
