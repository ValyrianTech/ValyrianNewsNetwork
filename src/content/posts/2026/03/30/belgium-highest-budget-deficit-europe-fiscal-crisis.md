# Belgium Faces Europe's Highest Budget Deficit Amid Fiscal Crisis

Belgium confronts the highest budget deficit in the eurozone alongside France, with National Bank Governor Pierre Wunsch declaring the country "the worst pupil in the class" on fiscal discipline, according to a [VRT NWS analysis](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/03/29/_we-zijn-de-slechtste-leerling-in-de-europese-begrotingsklas/). The stark assessment comes as Prime Minister Bart De Wever's Arizona coalition government faces mounting pressure to implement at least €5 billion in savings to meet European rules and reassure financial markets, while contending with limited fiscal space and rising energy prices driven by Middle East conflicts.

## Context: Belgium's Fiscal Challenges in European Perspective

Belgium's fiscal position has deteriorated significantly, with the budget deficit reaching 5.3% of GDP in 2025—tying with France for the highest in the eurozone. Public debt stands at approximately 107% of GDP and is projected to continue rising to 121.4% by 2031 without substantial policy changes. These figures place Belgium in clear violation of European Union Stability and Growth Pact rules, which mandate deficits below 3% of GDP and debt below 60% of GDP.

The country's complex federal structure further complicates fiscal management, dividing responsibilities between the federal government, regions (Flanders, Wallonia, Brussels), and communities. This fragmentation has historically challenged coordinated fiscal policy, contributing to Belgium's persistent struggle with high public debt that peaked at over 130% of GDP in the 1990s before being reduced through austerity measures.

## The De Wever-Wunsch Tandem and Fiscal Reality

The relationship between Prime Minister Bart De Wever and National Bank Governor Pierre Wunsch has emerged as a crucial dynamic in Belgium's fiscal crisis. Wunsch's blunt assessment in a recent interview highlighted the severity of the situation: "We have the highest deficit in Europe, together with France. The government has taken serious measures, but the deficit remains the same. In France, the deficit is falling, despite political instability. We are in Europe really the worst pupil in the class."

This partnership echoes historical collaborations between Belgian prime ministers and central bank governors, most notably between Wilfried Martens and former governor Fons Verplaetse. The current tandem faces the challenge of maintaining market confidence while navigating political constraints. Wunsch emphasized that "some politicians think: ha, it is crisis, we are going to spend money. They all come with their wish lists. But no, that's not how it works. There is simply no more money to spend."

Despite this hardline stance, both Wunsch and Budget Minister Vincent Van Peteghem have indicated openness to "targeted measures to protect the most vulnerable" from energy price increases. Van Peteghem defended the government's record while acknowledging inherited challenges: "Why is the deficit so high today? Yes, that has to do with economic parameters, but also with the lack of reforms last legislature."

## Limited Fiscal Space and Required Savings

Belgium's fiscal constraints are particularly acute. Before the federal government can allocate approximately €170 billion in tax revenues (plus €94 billion flowing directly to social security), it must first disburse €81 billion to regions, €50 billion to social security, €12 billion in debt interest, and €9 billion to the EU. This leaves only €17 billion available for federal operations against expenses exceeding €40 billion—a situation described as "concrete policy" that makes any budget exercise extremely difficult.

Wunsch estimates that Belgium needs at least €5 billion in additional savings to meet European rules and reassure financial markets, with €10 billion required for substantial improvement. He identified potential areas for savings in "not always equally effective" business subsidies and the growing healthcare budget. Prime Minister De Wever has announced intentions to make €3-4 billion in structural savings annually, but this falls short of the National Bank's recommendations.

## Political Constraints and Alternative Proposals

The government faces significant political headwinds, with recent polls showing half of Flemish voters supporting N-VA and Vlaams Belang—creating pressure against austerity measures that might alienate the government's base. This political reality complicates efforts to implement the necessary fiscal consolidation.

In response to the crisis, opposition parties have proposed alternative revenue sources. New Green Party chair Aimen Horch advocates for a "Trump tax" on windfall profits of oil and gas companies benefiting from increased energy prices: "While everyone loses in this war, there is one group that makes a profit: oil and gas companies... Introduce a Trump tax, and demand immediately a fair contribution from those large oil and gas companies that make extreme profits." PVDA chair Raoul Hedebouw has launched an identical proposal, extending cooperation to Groen.

However, Budget Minister Van Peteghem reacted pointedly to comparisons with the previous Vivaldi government's performance: "That is a remark that makes me uncomfortable. That is also something the opposition likes to say, that we would have worse figures."

## Analysis: Structural Challenges Beyond Cyclical Factors

Belgium's deficit issues are structural rather than cyclical, persisting even during economic growth periods due to underlying spending pressures from an aging population, rising healthcare costs, and revenue weaknesses. The country's competitiveness within the European single market has also deteriorated, exacerbating fiscal challenges.

The energy crisis adds another layer of complexity, with the ongoing Middle East war driving up prices and creating pressure for government intervention while fiscal constraints limit response options. The debate between spending cuts and alternative revenue sources like windfall profit taxes reflects broader political tensions about how to address Belgium's fiscal sustainability.

## Outlook and Implications

In the short term (2026-2027), Belgium faces pressure to implement €5 billion in savings to meet European obligations while potentially introducing targeted measures to protect vulnerable groups from energy price increases. The tension between fiscal consolidation and political realities will likely continue.

Medium-term projections (2028-2031) suggest continued debt growth to over 120% of GDP without significant policy changes, necessitating structural reforms in pensions, healthcare, and labor markets. Belgium's credit rating and borrowing costs could face downward pressure if fiscal consolidation remains inadequate.

Long-term structural issues, particularly the aging population's impact on pension and healthcare systems, will require coordinated policy responses across federal entities. Belgium's ability to address these challenges will have implications not only for its economic stability but also for its position within the European Union and the broader eurozone.

The close alignment between Prime Minister De Wever and National Bank Governor Wunsch underscores the seriousness of Belgium's fiscal situation. While the government has announced savings measures, both the National Bank and international institutions warn that current plans may be insufficient. The coming months will test whether Belgium can transition from being "the worst pupil in the class" to achieving sustainable fiscal discipline while maintaining social cohesion and economic competitiveness.
