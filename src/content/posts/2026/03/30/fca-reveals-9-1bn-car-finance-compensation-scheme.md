# FCA Reveals £9.1bn Car Finance Compensation Scheme

The Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) has confirmed a £9.1 billion redress scheme that will provide compensation averaging £829 to approximately 12.1 million UK drivers who were mis-sold motor finance agreements between 2007 and 2024. According to [BBC News](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c9vlm2ggrwlo), the regulator's final plan represents a reduction from initial estimates that would have covered 14.2 million agreements at a cost of £11 billion.

## Historical Context of the Mis-selling Scandal

The compensation scheme addresses widespread use of discretionary commission arrangements (DCAs) that allowed car dealers to adjust interest rates charged to customers to earn higher commissions, often without disclosing this practice. The FCA banned these arrangements in 2021 after determining they created incentives for dealers to charge higher interest rates than necessary.

The legal foundation for the mass redress scheme emerged from the **Johnson case** considered by the Supreme Court in August 2025. The Court partially overturned a Court of Appeal ruling, finding that the use of DCAs with outsized commissions constituted unfair treatment. This ruling enabled the FCA to establish compensation based on principles of unfairness rather than strict illegality.

## Eligibility Criteria and Compensation Details

Consumers are eligible for compensation if they weren't told about at least one of three arrangements between lenders and car dealers:

1. **Discretionary Commission Arrangements (DCAs)**: Where dealers could adjust interest rates to earn higher commissions
2. **High Commission Arrangements**: At least 39% of the total cost of credit AND 10% of the loan paid to the dealer (raised from 35% in initial proposals)
3. **Contractual Ties**: Exclusivity or right of first refusal arrangements, except where lenders can prove visible links with manufacturer and dealer

The FCA has established a two-tier compensation system. Approximately 90,000 consumers whose cases closely match the Johnson precedent will receive full commission plus interest. The majority will receive a hybrid remedy averaging estimated loss and commission paid, plus interest.

## Implementation Timeline and Consumer Process

The scheme features staggered implementation deadlines:
- **June 30, 2026** for agreements made between April 2014 and November 2024
- **August 31, 2026** for agreements made between April 2007 and March 2014

Consumers who have already complained will receive compensation determinations within three months of the relevant deadline. Lenders will contact potentially eligible consumers who haven't yet complained within six months of deadlines, with consumers having six months to respond. The final deadline for new complaints is August 31, 2027.

## Stakeholder Reactions and Industry Impact

**FCA CEO Nikhil Rathi** emphasized the scheme's balance: "We've listened to feedback to make sure the scheme is fair for consumers and proportionate for firms. It will put £7.5 billion back into people's pockets." The regulator warned consumers against using claims management companies, noting they could lose over 30% of any compensation received.

The banking industry, which initially contested the FCA's interpretation of the Supreme Court ruling, secured several concessions including the higher commission threshold. **Lloyds Banking Group**, with the largest exposure at £2 billion provision, warned the scheme could take "20 years of profitability" off the car finance industry.

Despite the scandal, the motor finance market continues to function, with record £41 billion lent in 2025—a 6% increase from 2024. New car sales reached a 22-year high in February 2026, suggesting limited impact on the new car finance market.

## Economic Implications and Consumer Impact

The £9.1 billion total cost comprises £7.5 billion in compensation payments and £1.6 billion in administrative costs—significantly reduced from initial estimates of £8.2 billion and £2.8 billion respectively. Major banks have set aside tens of millions in provisions, with share prices increasing 2.1-29.7% following the Supreme Court ruling.

For consumers, the average £829 compensation represents a meaningful injection for millions of households amid ongoing cost of living pressures. The FCA estimates that handling complaints individually would have cost £6 billion more than the centralized scheme.

## Regulatory Precedent and Future Implications

The scheme establishes a significant precedent for mass redress in UK financial services, following the £38 billion PPI mis-selling compensation and £2.4 billion mortgage arrears charges redress. It demonstrates the FCA's attempt to balance consumer protection with market stability while establishing a template for future sector-wide compensation initiatives.

Market evolution is already underway, with permanent shifts away from discretionary commission arrangements toward greater transparency. The FCA has established a dedicated team to monitor compliance and ensure the scheme's smooth implementation.

## What Consumers Should Expect Next

Eligible consumers can expect communication from their lenders according to the established timelines. The FCA emphasizes that there is no need to use claims management companies or law firms, warning that doing so could significantly reduce compensation received.

The regulator expects 75% of eligible consumers to participate in the scheme, with most compensation payments occurring in 2026 and all completed by the end of 2027. While the scheme represents the quickest and most cost-effective resolution according to the FCA, some consumer advocates have criticized the reduction in eligible agreements from 14.2 million to 12.1 million and the increased commission threshold.

As implementation proceeds, attention will focus on lenders' ability to process millions of claims within tight timelines and whether the 75% participation estimate proves accurate. The scheme's success will influence future regulatory approaches to sector-wide consumer redress in UK financial services.
