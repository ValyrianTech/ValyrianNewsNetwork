# Belgium Cannot Fully Compensate Price Hikes, Minister Says

Belgian Finance Minister Vincent Van Peteghem has publicly acknowledged that the federal government lacks the financial means to fully compensate citizens for rising prices, particularly energy costs. The admission comes amid a €4.9 billion budget shortfall identified by government experts and soaring inflation driven by the war in Iran.

"We cannot compensate 100% of price increases," Van Peteghem stated in an interview with Het Laatste Nieuws, as reported by multiple Belgian media outlets including [Le Soir](https://www.lesoir.be) and 7sur7.be. "We must be honest with citizens: we will never compensate the totality of price hikes." The minister's statement represents a significant shift from previous crisis responses and signals tighter fiscal constraints for the De Wever government.

## Budgetary Constraints and Economic Pressures

The stark admission follows a report from the government's Monitoring Committee that identified a €4.9 billion deficit needed to meet European Union spending rules. Of this amount, €1.4 billion in savings or new revenues must be found for the 2026 budget alone.

Van Peteghem, who serves as Vice-Prime Minister and Minister of Budget (CD&V), emphasized the scale of the challenge: "The €4.9 billion is only a minimum objective. The impact of the war in Iran has not yet been taken into account by the Monitoring Committee."

The economic context is particularly challenging. Oil and gas prices have been soaring due to the conflict in Iran, driving inflation across multiple sectors. Economic growth is expected to be disappointing, further complicating the government's fiscal position.

## Shift from Previous Crisis Response

The minister explicitly contrasted the current approach with that of the previous Vivaldi government, which implemented broad support measures during earlier crises. "We should not repeat the error of the Vivaldi government which immediately injected colossal sums to help everyone," Van Peteghem said. "We are still paying for it today."

Instead, the government plans more targeted interventions. "We must help people, certainly, but through intelligent, targeted and temporary measures," he explained. This represents a move away from what he described as "helicopter money"—broad, untargeted financial support for all citizens.

## Political Tensions Within Coalition

The budgetary challenges come amid visible tensions within the "Arizona" coalition government led by Prime Minister Bart De Wever (N-VA). The coalition includes N-VA, CD&V, MR, Vooruit, and Les Engagés.

Van Peteghem criticized MR President Georges-Louis Bouchez for refusing to discuss tax increases, calling the position "simply unrealistic." He also defended his CD&V colleague Annelies Verlinden, who was recently sidelined by N-VA and MR colleagues in what he described as "unacceptable" treatment.

The internal conflicts raise questions about the coalition's ability to make difficult decisions on fiscal policy. Van Peteghem acknowledged the challenges but maintained that solutions must be found by summer 2026.

## Policy Implications and Reform Prospects

### Tax Reform

One proposal under consideration is VAT harmonization—merging the current 6% and 12% rates into a single standard rate. Van Peteghem suggested this could "create budgetary mass and easier implementation," though a previous VAT reform was rejected by the Council of State.

"Not touching taxation is particularly difficult when you see the scale of the effort required," the minister noted, highlighting the need for a combination of spending cuts and revenue increases.

### Labor Market and Indexation

Van Peteghem ruled out freezing wage indexation, calling such a move an "unacceptable transfer of purchasing power from workers to companies." Instead, he indicated the government would focus on addressing energy prices rather than labor costs.

### European Context

Belgium must comply with EU spending norms, though Van Peteghem noted there might be some temporary flexibility. However, he warned that relying on EU exceptions isn't sustainable, referencing countries like Greece, Portugal, and Ireland that were placed under EU/IMF supervision.

## International Dimension: War in Iran Impact

The conflict in Iran represents a major complicating factor for Belgium's economic outlook. Beyond driving energy prices higher, the war creates economic uncertainty and necessitates additional defense spending.

Van Peteghem's acknowledgment that the Monitoring Committee hasn't yet factored in the war's full impact suggests the €4.9 billion deficit figure may be optimistic. The situation underscores how international conflicts can rapidly alter domestic fiscal calculations.

## Timeline and Next Steps

The government faces tight deadlines:
- **Spring 2026**: €1.4 billion solution needed for 2026 budget
- **Summer 2026**: Comprehensive €4.9 billion agreement required

Van Peteghem expressed hope that negotiations would conclude before the summer holidays, but acknowledged the difficulty of the task ahead.

## Outlook and Implications

The minister's admission marks a turning point in Belgium's economic policy approach. After years of expansive crisis response, the government now faces hard constraints that limit its ability to shield citizens from economic shocks.

The shift toward targeted, temporary support represents both a practical necessity given fiscal constraints and a philosophical departure from previous approaches. However, it raises questions about how effectively the government can protect vulnerable populations during periods of high inflation.

Political tensions within the coalition add another layer of complexity. The government's ability to implement difficult measures—whether spending cuts or tax reforms—will test its cohesion and stability.

Internationally, Belgium's situation reflects broader European challenges: balancing fiscal responsibility with social protection amid geopolitical instability and economic uncertainty. How Belgium navigates these challenges may offer lessons for other EU members facing similar pressures.

As Van Peteghem summarized the situation: While Prime Minister De Wever has called the budget a "Titanic," the finance minister prefers the metaphor of the Tour de France—a series of difficult climbs that must be tackled one by one. The coming months will reveal whether the government can successfully navigate these ascents or faces more turbulent waters ahead.
