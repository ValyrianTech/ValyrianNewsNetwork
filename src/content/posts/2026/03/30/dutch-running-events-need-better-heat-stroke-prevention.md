# Experts Call for Better Heat Stroke Prevention at Dutch Running Events

Medical experts are urging running event organizers in the Netherlands to implement better heat stroke prevention measures as climate change increases temperatures and associated risks. Despite established cooling methods being available since 2023, a survey reveals these life-saving techniques are rarely used at smaller events.

According to [NOS](https://nos.nl/artikel/2608351-hardloopevenementen-kunnen-meer-doen-bij-hitteberoertes-zeggen-experts), exercise physiologist Coen Bongers from Radboudumc and HAN University states that ice baths and ice towels should be standard equipment at all running events. "In the Netherlands, in principle no one needs to die from heat stroke," Bongers emphasizes.

## The Gap Between Standards and Practice

Since 2023, ice baths and ice towels have been the recommended standard for event medical care according to the Stichting Veldnorm Evenementenzorg (Field Standard Event Care Foundation). However, research by Omroep Gelderland shows limited implementation. A survey of 120 running event organizers in Gelderland found only 59 responses, with just 2 organizations having ice baths as an option—and never using them. Forty-one of the 59 organizations don't have these cooling methods available at all.

Pim de Ruijter, anesthesiologist-intensivist and chair of Stichting Veldnorm Evenementenzorg, expresses concern: "I can get angry that people still die from heat stroke. That shouldn't happen. I do find it worrying that there are still organizations that do little with cooling methods. The treatment of heat stroke is super simple: just start cooling."

## A Personal Experience Highlights the Risk

Djurre, a 25-year-old from Doesburg, experienced a heat stroke during the 2024 Dam tot Damloop after running nearly 16 kilometers. His body temperature reached 42.5°C (108.5°F). He was wrapped in ice-cold towels on site and survived to tell his story. "I had never heard of heat stroke before I got one myself," he recounted in an Omroep Gelderland documentary.

## Effective Cooling Methods

There are two primary methods for rapidly lowering body temperature: ice baths and ice towels. While ice baths are most effective, they require specialized medical teams and are more expensive, making them challenging for smaller events. Ice towels, however, are inexpensive and easy to deploy—requiring only towels and a cooler with ice.

Bongers notes that ice towels are "second best" but emphasizes: "The body temperature just needs to come down as quickly as possible."

Currently, only five major Dutch running events consistently use ice baths: the Amsterdam Marathon, Utrecht Marathon, Eindhoven Marathon, Tilburg Ten Miles, and Dam tot Damloop. The Almere Challenge uses them only above certain temperatures.

## Scientific Evidence Supports Simple Methods

A 2025 study published in [The Journal of Emergency Medicine](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0736467924003548) provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of ice towel cooling. The retrospective observational study examined 374,534 runners participating in events between 2016-2019. Researchers found 36 cases of exertional heat stroke (9.6 per 100,000 participants).

All patients treated with rotating ice water-soaked towels reached temperatures below 40°C within 30 minutes, with a cooling rate of 0.12°C/min (0.22°F/min). Most importantly, there were zero fatalities among the heat stroke patients.

The study authors concluded that rotating ice water-soaked towels "could provide an effective and practical method of cooling in the prehospital setting," supporting the "cool first, transport later" principle.

## Climate Change Increases Urgency

With climate change leading to warmer temperatures, the risks during running events are increasing. Many organizers mistakenly believe cooling methods aren't needed for events held in late autumn or winter, but Bongers counters this misconception: "Heat strokes also occur at lower temperatures. Additionally, runners then wear more—and often too much—clothing, which means they can lose less heat."

The [Heat-Aware Sports Foundation](https://www.hittebewustsporten.nl/en/events/) advocates for better education and safety policies, noting that heat injuries can occur even at perceived temperatures of 15°C. They recommend using the Wet Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT) metric, which combines temperature, humidity, wind speed, and solar radiation to assess risk.

Scientist Roberts advises against starting marathons when WBGT is above 21°C, while research shows optimal marathon performance occurs around 11°C.

## Comprehensive Heat Safety Recommendations

The Heat-Aware Sports Foundation provides extensive guidelines for event organizers:

**Before events:** Adjust start times to cooler parts of the day, choose shady courses (especially in finish areas and last 5 km), establish clear criteria for shortening or canceling events, and arrange sufficient medical capacity.

**During events:** Provide extra drink stations with mandatory walking time, deploy additional healthcare providers with cooling resources, disable time registration during extreme heat, and have protocols for removing participants from the race.

**After finishing:** Create shady finish areas with drink stations and sprinklers, establish cooling zones with fans, provide large run-out areas, and offer ice baths supervised by healthcare professionals.

## Moving Forward

Experts agree that implementing existing heat protocols could prevent serious medical incidents and save lives. Bongers calls for universal availability: "It's really a lifechanger. I advocate that ice baths and ice towels be used at every running event."

As temperatures continue to rise due to climate change, adapting event safety protocols becomes increasingly urgent. The combination of scientific evidence, expert recommendations, and affordable cooling methods presents a clear path forward for making Dutch running events safer for all participants.

Event organizers now face the challenge of bridging the gap between established standards and practical implementation—a gap that could mean the difference between life and death for participants experiencing heat-related emergencies.
