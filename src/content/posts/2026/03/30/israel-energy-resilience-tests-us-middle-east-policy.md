# Israel's Energy Resilience Creates New Challenges for US Middle East Policy

When Israel attacked Iran's South Pars gas field last week, global energy prices spiked and allies issued condemnations—but within Israel itself, the immediate impact was minimal. This apparent immunity to regional energy shocks stems from a remarkable transformation: Israel has become nearly self-sufficient in energy production, importing only about 7% of its needs compared to nearly 70% for countries like the Netherlands. According to [RTL Nieuws](https://www.rtlnieuws.nl/nieuws/economie/artikel/5583131/israel-leunt-zwaar-op-eigen-gas-en-olie-uit-azerbeidzjan-en-rusland), this energy resilience, born from geopolitical necessity, now presents significant challenges for US Middle East policy coordination at a time when Washington's strategic priorities are shifting.

## From Energy Importer to Regional Power

Israel's energy transformation began in 2000 with the discovery of massive gas reserves in the Mediterranean Sea off its coast. This discovery turned the country from a fossil fuel importer into a gas exporter, with the Leviathan field becoming central to its regional influence. "Israel verraste vriend en vijand met de aanval op het Iraanse deel van het South Pars-gasveld vorige week," noted the RTL Nieuws analysis, highlighting how the attack caused global energy price spikes while having limited immediate impact domestically.

The country's energy self-sufficiency emerged from geopolitical necessity. Surrounded by nations with which it maintains at best lukewarm relations, Israel developed what analysts call a "Super-Sparta" doctrine—an intensely self-reliant war economy aimed at overcoming military and operational bottlenecks. This doctrine extends to energy, where domestic production has reduced traditional vulnerabilities.

## The Anatomy of Energy Resilience

Gas has surpassed oil as Israel's main energy source in recent years, with most electricity plants running on domestically produced gas. Data from the [International Energy Agency](https://www.iea.org/countries/israel/energy-mix) shows Israel's remarkable position, with the country importing only about 7% of its energy needs. The French research bureau [Enerdata](https://www.enerdata.net/estore/energy-market/israel/) reports similar figures, confirming Israel's exceptional energy independence among Middle Eastern nations.

Despite overall self-sufficiency, Israel maintains strategic import dependencies that reveal vulnerabilities. The country relies heavily on Azerbaijan and Kazakhstan for crude oil imports, with nearly 70% of oil imports coming from these two former Soviet states, typically via Turkey or Russia. During the Gaza war, Russia accounted for about 45% of refined petroleum products like gasoline and diesel imported by Israel, according to research by [Oil Change International](https://oilchange.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Behind-the-Barrel-3.pdf).

## Infrastructure Vulnerabilities and Risks

Israel's energy resilience comes with significant vulnerabilities. The gas supply arrives via two pipelines from offshore reservoirs with no storage capacity, and there are only a limited number of gas-to-electricity conversion plants. Researchers at the [Israeli Institute for National Security](https://www.inss.org.il/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/Special-Publication-281225-sp-1.pdf) warn in a recent study that "the dependence on few sources and limited infrastructure forms a growing risk to supply security, especially in emergency situations."

These vulnerabilities were exposed during the 12-day Israeli attack on Iran in June 2025, when the Leviathan field shut down for 13 days, and again more recently when production halted following attacks on Iranian infrastructure. Each shutdown immediately stopped gas flows to Jordan and Egypt, plunging both countries into crisis.

## Regional Dependencies: The Arab Gas Pipeline Network

The war on Iran revealed how dependent Israel's Arab neighbors have become on its gas exports, a dependency that could extend to Syria and Lebanon. As Hisham Bustani, researcher at the Centre for International Studies at Sciences Po, noted in [Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/opinions/2026/3/21/arab-states-should-beware-of-israels-hegemonic-energy-expansion), "When Israeli gas stops, the entire network stops."

Egypt imports about [10 billion cubic meters of gas annually from Israel](https://www.africa-energy.com/news-centre/article/gas-hungry-egypt-ramps-lng-imports) and signed a [$35 billion deal to import Israeli gas from the Leviathan field until 2040](https://www.enerdata.net/publications/daily-energy-news/israel-greenlights-gas-exports-leviathan-egypt.html). Jordan imports approximately [3.6 billion cubic meters per year, mostly from Israel](https://www.enerdata.net/estore/energy-market/jordan/). The Arab Gas Pipeline—once a symbol of joint Arab development projects—has become the primary conduit for exporting Israeli gas to both countries.

This growing energy dominance creates new political dynamics. "The material reality appears to contradict official narratives," Bustani argues. "The gas reaching Syria and, in the future, Lebanon, is, in practical terms, Israeli gas." Even when Israel isn't the immediate supplier, the system depends structurally on Israeli gas, granting Jerusalem considerable political leverage.

## Complications for US Policy Coordination

Israel's energy resilience creates significant challenges for US Middle East policy at a time when Washington's strategic priorities are shifting. As Piotr Smolar, Washington correspondent for Le Monde, observed in analysis of [US strategic myopia in the region](https://www.lemonde.fr/en/international/article/2026/03/13/military-victory-political-stalemate-the-us-s-strategic-myopia-in-the-middle-east_6751405_4.html), "The two allies do not share the same priorities."

American energy independence achieved through the shale revolution has reduced Washington's traditional strategic interest in the Middle East, while Israel's security concerns remain paramount. This divergence complicates coordination on issues ranging from confronting Iranian aggression to maintaining regional energy stability.

Israel's "Super-Sparta" doctrine signals a desire for reduced dependence on US military support, with plans to "taper off" reliance on American aid over the next decade. This growing autonomy, coupled with energy self-sufficiency, reduces leverage the US traditionally exercised through energy security guarantees.

## The Weaponization of Energy Infrastructure

Israel has demonstrated willingness to use energy as a political instrument. During conflicts, it has cut gas supplies to neighbors and threatened to reconsider gas agreements with Egypt over alleged peace treaty violations. More starkly, during military operations in Gaza, electricity, energy, and water infrastructure were systematically targeted and cut off.

This capacity to weaponize essential infrastructure creates what Bustani calls "the cage of dependency" for Arab states. "Once embedded, disengaging from such systems becomes extraordinarily difficult," he warns, "because they govern the essentials of everyday life: electricity, water and energy."

## Alternative Development and Future Scenarios

Syria and Lebanon have substantial untapped energy reserves that could provide alternatives to Israeli-dominated networks. Syria holds an estimated 280 billion cubic meters of onshore gas reserves and possibly 250 billion cubic meters offshore, while Lebanon could have as much as 700 billion cubic meters in offshore reserves. Developing these resources would require significant time, financing, and political will to resist pressure for dependency on Israeli gas.

The emerging regional energy system centered on Israel presents a paradox: while insulating Israel from regional shocks, it creates vulnerabilities for its neighbors and complicates US policy. As the US re-evaluates its Middle East engagement in light of its own energy independence, Israel's unique position will continue to shape regional dynamics.

## Strategic Implications and Forward Look

The situation reveals what analysts describe as strategic myopia in US policy. While America's historic focus on the Middle East was driven by energy security concerns, its newfound independence has created a mismatch with Israel's continued security preoccupations. This divergence is particularly evident in coordination around Iran, where Israel's willingness to target energy infrastructure creates global price volatility that affects US allies differently than it affects Israel itself.

Looking forward, several developments bear watching:

1. **Infrastructure Investment**: Israel's 2026 budget includes significant investments for protection of critical energy infrastructure, suggesting recognition of existing vulnerabilities.

2. **Regional Integration**: The potential extension of the Arab Gas Pipeline network to Syria and Lebanon could draw additional countries into the Israeli-dominated energy system.

3. **Renewable Energy Transition**: Israel consistently fails to meet its renewable energy targets (30% by 2030), hampered by bureaucratic red tape, limited suitable land, and inadequate infrastructure. Cheap natural gas reduces incentives to invest in solar or wind energy.

4. **US-Israel Security Cooperation**: How Israel's "Super-Sparta" doctrine affects long-term military and intelligence cooperation remains uncertain.

## Conclusion: A Double-Edged Sword

Israel's energy resilience represents a double-edged sword in Middle East geopolitics. While insulating the country from regional energy shocks and enhancing its strategic autonomy, this position complicates US policy coordination and creates new dependencies among Arab neighbors. The emerging regional energy system centered on Israel grants it significant political leverage while exposing neighboring countries to supply disruptions during conflicts.

As the US confronts the challenges of maintaining alliance cohesion while pursuing its own strategic reorientation, Israel's unique energy position will continue to test traditional partnership structures. The weaponization of energy infrastructure and growing Arab dependencies create a complex web of interdependencies that may prove difficult to unravel, potentially locking the region into patterns of influence that extend well beyond military considerations into the fundamental infrastructure of daily life.
