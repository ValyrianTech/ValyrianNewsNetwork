# Tories Propose Removing VAT from Energy Bills for 3 Years

The Conservative Party has called for Value Added Tax (VAT) to be removed from household energy bills for three years as part of a broader plan to alleviate cost-of-living pressures on UK families. The proposal, announced by Conservative leader Kemi Badenoch, comes amid rising energy prices driven by the Iran conflict and global market pressures, with the Tories claiming the measure would save the average household £94 per year.

## Context: Rising Energy Costs and Middle East Conflict

Energy bills have become a central political issue as the UK faces what some are calling "Awful April"—a period of expected price increases when the energy price cap is reset in July. The situation has been exacerbated by the Iran conflict, which has effectively blocked the Strait of Hormuz, one of the world's busiest oil shipping channels, leading to soaring wholesale oil and gas prices.

According to [BBC News](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c4gx01d0re1o), the current energy price cap stands at £1,641 for a typical household from April 2026, with bills expected to rise again in July. This follows a period of extreme volatility in energy markets, with prices peaking at £4,059 in January 2023 during the previous energy crisis.

## The Conservative "Cheap Power Plan"

The VAT removal proposal forms the centerpiece of what the Conservatives are calling their "Cheap Power Plan." The party says removing the 5% VAT from energy bills would provide immediate relief while domestic oil and gas production is ramped up. The £94 annual savings estimate is based on energy cost forecasts for July 2026.

However, the VAT removal is just one component of a broader package that the Tories claim would reduce energy bills by £200 per year for the average household. Other elements include:

- Scrapping the carbon tax on electricity bills (£75/year savings)
- Removing wind levies (£22/year savings)
- Ending the Renewable Obligation scheme and other green levies
- Terminating the windfall tax on oil and gas companies
- Maximizing North Sea oil and gas production

The party says the plan would be funded by scrapping renewable energy schemes and green levies, including heat pump subsidies, and by generating increased tax revenue from expanded North Sea drilling—estimated at £25 billion over a decade.

## Political Reactions and Criticism

The proposal has drawn immediate criticism from both the governing Labour Party and opposition parties. Labour's Exchequer Secretary to the Treasury, Dan Tomlinson, dismissed the Conservative plan's central foundation, stating that maximizing North Sea drilling "won't bring bills down."

"Now [Conservative leader Kemi Badenoch] says the Tories would scrap the windfall tax which is helping tackle the cost of living," Tomlinson told BBC News.

Reform UK, which had already promised to scrap VAT and green levies on household energy bills, accused the Conservatives of "copying our policies." The party's Treasury spokesman, Robert Jenrick, noted that the Tories had spent "14 years introducing the very policies they now say they'd abolish."

Conservative leader Kemi Badenoch defended the plan, saying: "I know families and business owners across Britain will be very worried about how the global energy crisis will impact them. That's why I find it appalling that Labour's solution is to tax working people to fund a bailout for those on benefits. By drilling in the North Sea and scrapping [Energy Secretary] Ed Miliband's crazy green taxes, our Cheap Power Plan would reduce bills by £200 for everyone."

## Broader Energy Policy Landscape

The Conservative proposal highlights deepening political divisions on UK energy policy. The Labour government has banned licenses for new oil and gas fields in the North Sea while maintaining that oil and gas will continue to be part of the UK's energy mix in coming years. Chancellor Rachel Reeves has indicated the government would provide support if energy bills spiraled due to the Iran war, though signaling this would be targeted rather than universal support.

Other parties have put forward their own energy proposals:

- **Liberal Democrats**: Plan to break the link between gas prices and electricity costs, aiming to halve bills by 2035
- **Green Party**: Proposed freezing bills in July, funded by hiking taxes on capital gains and tightening existing taxes on energy firms' profits

Prime Minister Sir Keir Starmer is hosting senior leaders from sectors including energy, shipping, finance, and insurance for a Downing Street roundtable to discuss how the government and private sector can work together to reduce the impact of the war on the cost of living. Representatives from Shell, BP, Lloyds of London, shipping giant Maersk, and banks including HSBC and Goldman Sachs are attending the meeting.

## Analysis: Implications and Challenges

The Conservative proposal raises several important questions about energy policy, fiscal responsibility, and environmental commitments:

**Economic Impact**: While £94 per year represents modest relief (approximately £7.83 per month), the removal of VAT would reduce Treasury revenue, requiring alternative funding sources. The Conservatives claim this would come from scrapping green levies and increased North Sea tax revenue, but critics question the economic modeling behind these assumptions.

**Environmental Considerations**: Scrapping renewable energy schemes and green levies could undermine the UK's net-zero commitments and slow the transition to cleaner energy sources. The Renewable Obligation scheme, which the Tories propose ending, was originally introduced by Labour Energy Secretary Ed Miliband and is currently funded 75% by the government until 2028-29 under Chancellor Reeves' budget.

**Energy Security**: The Iran conflict has highlighted the UK's dependence on imported energy, giving political weight to arguments for increased domestic production. However, energy experts note that UK energy prices remain tied to international markets regardless of domestic production levels.

**Political Timing**: The proposal comes ahead of May local elections, where cost-of-living concerns are expected to be a key issue. The Conservatives are positioning themselves as the party offering direct bill relief, while Labour emphasizes environmental sustainability and targeted support.

## What's Next

The energy price debate is likely to intensify in coming weeks as the July price cap reset approaches. Key developments to watch include:

1. **Government Response**: Whether Labour offers alternative measures to address energy costs
2. **Economic Modeling**: Detailed analysis of the Conservative plan's fiscal implications
3. **Environmental Impact Assessments**: How scrapping green levies would affect UK climate commitments
4. **International Developments**: Resolution or escalation of the Iran conflict and its impact on global energy markets

While the Conservative VAT removal proposal offers immediate political appeal to households facing rising bills, it represents just one facet of a complex energy policy debate balancing immediate cost relief against long-term energy security and environmental sustainability. As energy costs continue to dominate political discourse, the coming months will test both the government's response to the crisis and opposition parties' alternative visions for the UK's energy future.
