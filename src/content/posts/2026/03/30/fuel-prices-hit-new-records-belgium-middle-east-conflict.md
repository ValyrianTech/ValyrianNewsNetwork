# Belgium Fuel Prices Hit Records Amid Middle East Conflict

Fuel prices in Belgium have reached unprecedented levels, with diesel setting a new record at 2.333 euros per liter effective Tuesday, March 31, according to official announcements from the Federal Public Service Economy. The 17-cent increase from current maximum prices marks the highest level since Energia began compiling data in 2008, as global oil prices surge amid escalating Middle East tensions.

## Context and Price Increases

The price surge is directly linked to the ongoing conflict involving the United States, Israel, and Iran, which began on February 28 and has since disrupted global oil markets. According to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/liveblog/diesel-benzine-en-olie-opnieuw-duurder-olieprijzen-naar-hoogst~1774343778229/), Brent crude oil reached $115 per barrel on Monday morning—the highest level since the conflict started—representing a more than 50% increase from approximately $70 per barrel on February 28.

Effective March 31, Belgian consumers will face the following maximum prices:
- **Diesel (B7):** 2.333 €/liter (+0.17 €) - NEW RECORD
- **Gasoline 95 (E10):** 1.92 €/liter (+0.057 €)
- **Heating oil (H0/H7):** 1.4347 €/liter (+0.035 €) for orders of at least 2,000 liters

[Le Soir](https://www.lesoir.be/737741/article/2026-03-30/le-prix-du-diesel-sapprete-battre-un-nouveau-record) confirmed the record-breaking diesel price, noting that the increases follow "sharp rises in oil prices on international markets in recent weeks, driven by the conflict in the Middle East."

## Economic Impact and Inflation

The fuel price surge is already affecting Belgium's economy. Inflation rose from 1.45% to 1.65% in March, with motor fuels and package holidays identified as the main drivers. According to data from the federal energy regulator CREG, an average family with a variable energy contract has seen their energy bill increase by €45 since the conflict began—€38 for gas and €7 for electricity.

Belgium's unique position as one of only two EU countries (along with Luxembourg) with petroleum price caps has created cross-border economic effects. Dutch petrol stations have reported turnover declines of 10-20% in recent weeks as consumers cross into Belgium to benefit from lower prices, creating queues at Belgian stations, particularly from trucks leaving motorways.

## Political Debate and Government Response

The price increases have sparked intense political debate in Belgium, with the government taking a cautious approach while opposition parties demand immediate action.

Prime Minister Bart De Wever (N-VA) has emphasized fiscal responsibility, stating in an interview with VRT NWS: "Promising the population that the state is a universal insurer for everything happening now is totally irresponsible." The federal government is awaiting proposals from the European Commission before taking action.

Flemish Minister of Budget Ben Weyts (N-VA) echoed this cautious stance during a debate in the Flemish Parliament, arguing against "knee-jerk measures" and advocating for "calm and reasoned" policy. "Steekvlam-maatregelen zijn niet effectief, dat heeft het verleden ons geleerd" (Knee-jerk measures are not effective, history has taught us that), he stated.

## Opposition Demands and Policy Proposals

Opposition parties have united in calling for immediate government intervention. PVDA's Jos D'Haese demanded measures to lower energy bills by "taking money from war profiteers making profits from misery," while Groen's new chairman Aimen Horch proposed a "Trump tax" on oil company profits to fund investments in green energy.

Vlaams Belang's Chris Janssens criticized the government's inaction: "When is the right time according to you? Because for the Flemings whose interests you are supposed to defend, it's already 5 past 12."

Among the policy proposals circulating is MR president Georges-Louis Bouchez's suggestion for a "reverse cliquet"—an automatic tax reduction mechanism that would activate when fuel prices exceed €2 per liter, with taxes increasing again when prices fall.

## International Context and Energy Security

The Belgian situation reflects broader European energy vulnerability. Dutch gas reserves have fallen to their lowest level in 10 years at just 5.8% capacity, while France has temporarily relaxed diesel quality standards to address supply concerns.

Shell CEO Wael Sawan warned that Europe will soon face the same fuel supply disruptions already affecting Asian countries. "We try to work with governments to warn them about the buttons they need to turn, including the demand side and what they need to do about storage," he stated at an energy conference in Houston.

The Strait of Hormuz—through which approximately 20% of global oil trade passes—has been effectively blocked by Iran, which is now charging ships $2 million for passage unless they have ties to Israel or the United States.

## Expert Analysis and Economic Perspectives

University of Ghent economist Gert Peersman offered a stark assessment of proposed support measures, calling them "huiveringwekkend" (horrifying). He argued that price signals are necessary to reduce consumption when supply is constrained: "If there is 10% less supply, consumption must also fall by 10%. The price will rise until 10% of people leave their car at home or turn their heating down one degree."

Political scientist Carl Devos criticized the political rhetoric surrounding the crisis: "You see politicians every day making proposals to lower energy prices, while they know there is no political consensus and no money for it... which only increases people's disappointment and sows political distrust."

## Sector-Specific Impacts

The transport industry is particularly affected, with Dutch fishermen not sailing this week due to doubled fuel costs. Weekly fuel expenses for fishing boats have increased from €12,500 to €25,000-30,000, making operations economically unviable.

Vietnamese airline Vietnam Airlines has announced it will cancel 23 domestic flights weekly starting in April due to jet fuel shortages, while Australia has halved fuel excise for three months at a cost of €1.52 billion to provide consumer relief.

## Looking Ahead

With the conflict showing no signs of immediate resolution, energy prices are expected to remain elevated. The European Commission has warned that the crisis could drive eurozone inflation above 3% in 2026, while the International Energy Agency has described the situation as "the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market."

As Belgian consumers brace for record fuel prices, the political debate continues between those advocating for immediate consumer relief and those emphasizing long-term fiscal stability and energy transition. The situation underscores Europe's continued dependence on global energy markets and the urgent need for accelerated diversification and green energy investments.
