# Dutch Proxy Voting Rate Highest Among Democracies

One in ten Dutch voters cast their ballots by proxy in recent municipal elections, the highest rate among democracies worldwide, according to a [data analysis by NOS](https://nos.nl/artikel/2608347-nederland-stemt-vaak-per-volmacht-zwakte-in-ons-verkiezingsproces). This widespread practice, originally designed in 1928 for sailors and fishermen unable to vote, has become a significant vulnerability in the Dutch electoral process that international observers have criticized for years.

## Historical Context and Recent Reforms

The Dutch proxy voting system dates back to 1928, when it was created to accommodate sailors and fishermen who were often at sea during elections. Nearly a century later, the practice has evolved from a practical solution for specific professions to a common convenience used by approximately 10% of voters nationwide.

Recent fraud allegations in Gorinchem have brought the system's vulnerabilities into sharp focus. On March 26, 2026, Mayor Reinie Melissant [filed an official report](https://nos.nl/artikel/2607971-gorinchem-doet-aangifte-verkiezingsfraude-na-ronselen-stemmen) of suspected electoral fraud after a candidate visited a polling station five times in one day with different people carrying proxy votes. Approximately 20% of votes at the affected polling station were cast by proxy.

In response to growing concerns, the Netherlands tightened rules against soliciting proxy votes on January 1, 2026. The reforms made it faster to criminalize asking for proxies and increased penalties for violations. However, experts argue these measures address only one aspect of a broader systemic weakness.

## Statistical Analysis Reveals Concerning Patterns

The NOS analysis of municipal election results reveals striking regional disparities in proxy voting usage. In the conservative Christian community of Urk, over 20% of voters used proxies, while in Groningen the rate was just 7%. For comparison, a 7% proxy voting rate in Ninove, Belgium, was considered "alarmingly high" and prompted [national news coverage and additional investigations](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2024/10/17/opvallend-meer-volmachten-in-ninove-dan-in-andere-kleine-steden).

Individual polling stations show even more extreme concentrations. At buurthuis Sam Sam in The Hague's Schilderswijk neighborhood, about 450 voters entered the polling station, but more than 700 votes were cast—with over one-third coming from proxy certificates. The thirteen polling stations with the highest proxy voting percentages are all located in The Hague.

According to the Organization for Security and Co-operation in Europe's Office for Democratic Institutions and Human Rights (OSCE/ODIHR), the highest proxy voting rates occur in "Christian communities like Urk and Volendam and in neighborhoods where the majority have a migration background," based on an [inventory by the Dutch Electoral Council](https://odihr.osce.org/sites/default/files/f/documents/c/6/322761.pdf).

## Expert Analysis and International Criticism

"It will always be a weakness in our electoral process," says John Bijl, director of the Perikles Institute, which advises municipalities on elections. "You never know for sure under what circumstances someone gave up their voting pass, and whether the person voting for you actually votes what you want."

International observers have repeatedly warned about the "disproportionate use" of proxy voting in the Netherlands. OSCE/ODIHR monitors have noted that the practice conflicts with voting secrecy and the principle of one person, one vote. While some democracies allow proxy voting, none approach Dutch levels of usage.

Professor Wim Voermans, a constitutional and administrative law expert at Leiden University, notes in research on the topic that "vote soliciting happens more often. It is very easy in the Netherlands to vote via proxy. The problem is that it is forbidden if the initiative does not lie with the voter themselves, but with someone who asks for the proxy."

## Systemic Vulnerabilities and Reform Proposals

The proxy voting system creates multiple vulnerabilities in electoral integrity:

1. **Compromised Voting Secrecy:** When someone else votes on your behalf, secrecy cannot be guaranteed
2. **Coercion Risk:** Potential for pressure from family members, partners, or political operatives
3. **Misrepresentation Risk:** Proxy voters may not vote as instructed
4. **Regional Targeting:** High concentrations in specific communities raise questions about equal access

Professor Voermans has proposed several reforms in his analysis:
- Reduce the number of proxies per person from two to one
- Require proxy givers to write a short explanation of why they're giving proxy
- Allow proxies only to sworn officials voting in separate polling stations

However, complete abolition presents its own challenges. "That would also cost voters," Bijl notes. "Is it worth it? Turnout in Rotterdam is already low at 40%. If it gets even lower, that hurts my heart."

## The Gorinchem Dilemma and Broader Implications

As of March 30, 2026, the Gorinchem municipal council faces what local politicians describe as a "devil's dilemma." They must decide whether to accept the current election results, call for partial re-elections at affected polling stations, or order completely new municipal elections. The Dutch Electoral Council has advised against holding new elections.

Johan Klein, faction leader of Stadsbelang Gorinchem, expressed the political challenge: "What you decide, there will always be a kind of haze of possible fraud around this council. Every vote that is solicited is one too many."

The broader implications extend beyond Gorinchem. The case has intensified calls for modernization of the 1928 proxy voting law. Pierre Schefferlie, faction leader of Democrats Gorinchem, argues that "the current rules around proxies are no longer of this time."

## Looking Forward

The Dutch proxy voting system represents a unique electoral vulnerability among democracies. While designed nearly a century ago for practical reasons, its widespread modern use creates significant integrity concerns that international observers have highlighted for years.

The Gorinchem case demonstrates how the system can be exploited, while statistical analysis reveals concerning patterns of usage that vary dramatically by region and community. As pressure for reform grows, Dutch authorities face the challenge of balancing electoral accessibility with integrity safeguards—a dilemma that will shape the future of Dutch democracy.

The decisions made in Gorinchem today, along with potential legislative reforms, will determine whether the Netherlands maintains its high proxy voting rates or joins other democracies in implementing stricter controls to protect the fundamental principles of secret ballots and one person, one vote.
