# UK Economic Pessimism Hits Multi-Year Low as Iran War Drives Up Living Costs

Growing economic pessimism is sweeping across the United Kingdom as the Iran war drives up prices for essentials, forcing half of all households to dip into savings, sell possessions, or borrow money just to make ends meet. According to the latest [Which? consumer insight tracker](https://www.theguardian.com/business/2026/mar/30/pessimism-takes-root-uk-shoppers-struggle-essentials-iran-war-prices), confidence in the UK economy has plummeted to its lowest level since the end of 2022, with two-thirds of adults expecting conditions to worsen over the next year.

## Context: A Perfect Storm of Economic Pressures

The UK's economic challenges represent a convergence of long-standing domestic issues and new global shocks. The country has been grappling with a persistent [cost-of-living crisis](https://www.theguardian.com/business/cost-of-living-crisis) since 2021, characterized by high inflation, successive interest rate hikes, and stagnant wage growth. This existing vulnerability has been dramatically exacerbated by the [US-Israel war with Iran](https://www.theguardian.com/world/us-israel-war-on-iran) that began on February 28, 2026, which has sent global energy prices soaring and threatened vital shipping routes through the Strait of Hormuz.

Before the conflict, the UK economy showed tentative signs of stabilization, with inflation expected to fall to the Bank of England's 2% target. However, the war has fundamentally altered this outlook, creating what economists describe as "Trumpflation"—a combination of geopolitical instability and resulting price pressures that threaten to undermine household finances and economic growth.

## Key Developments: Surveys Reveal Deepening Gloom

Multiple economic indicators released in March 2026 paint a concerning picture of UK consumer sentiment:

### Plummeting Confidence Metrics

The Which? survey found confidence in the future of the UK economy dropped 13 points to -56 in the month to March 13—the lowest score recorded since late 2022. This reflects what the consumer organization calls "a deep-seated pessimism across the country," with 67% of UK adults expecting the national economy to worsen over the next 12 months, while only 12% anticipate improvement.

Parallel surveys confirm this trend. The GfK Consumer Confidence Barometer fell two points to -21 in March, with expectations for the general economic situation over the next year dropping six points to -37. Barclays' separate survey of around 2,000 consumers between March 3-6—days after the attack on Iran began—found UK consumer confidence at a four-month low, reversing gains made at the start of the year.

### Household Financial Strain

The most alarming finding is the scale of household financial adjustment. An estimated 14 million UK households—half of the total—are making at least one significant adjustment to cover essential costs. According to the Which? research, 26% of households are regularly dipping into savings to bridge the gap between income and essential expenses, transforming savings from a safety net to a lifeline.

Sue Davies, head of consumer protection policy at Which?, summarized the situation: "Our research shows a concerning shift in consumer sentiment, with confidence in the economy hitting its lowest point in years as households face a daunting combination of rising prices and global instability. Pressure on budgets is becoming unsustainable for many."

### Inflation Expectations Revised Upward

The economic outlook has shifted dramatically since the conflict began. Before the war, UK inflation was expected to fall to the Bank of England's 2% target. Now, the [Bank expects the rate to rise to 3.5%](https://www.theguardian.com/business/2026/mar/19/bank-of-england-holds-interest-rates-iran-war-inflation) and remain more than a percentage point above target throughout 2026. This revision reflects the direct impact of energy price shocks, with Brent crude oil prices increasing around 50% to $110 a barrel since the outbreak of hostilities.

## Analysis: Economic Implications and Sector Impacts

The convergence of these indicators suggests significant economic headwinds for the UK in the coming months:

### Immediate Consumer Behavior Changes

With confidence in future household finances hitting its lowest level since April 2025 (falling five points to -15), consumers are likely to reduce discretionary spending. Retail sales had already fallen 0.4% in February according to Office for National Statistics data, and further declines are expected as households prioritize essentials.

Neil Bellamy of GfK captured the prevailing mood: "A ripple of fear is spreading. People simply do not feel the economy is robust enough to ride out the knock-on effects from the Middle East conflict."

### Energy and Fuel Price Pressures

The conflict's impact on energy markets is particularly acute for the UK, which remains heavily dependent on imported energy. Cornwall Insight predicts average annual energy bills could increase by around £300 from July, while RAC figures show petrol prices up 13% on average and diesel up 25% since the war began.

### Broader Economic Consequences

Ashley Webb, UK economist at Capital Economics, warned: "The decline in GfK consumer confidence in March... is probably the start of a bigger fall and suggests real household spending growth will soften in 2026." Given that consumer spending accounts for approximately 60% of UK GDP, this softening could significantly impact overall economic growth.

The situation presents particular challenges for the Bank of England, which recently held interest rates at 3.75% while signaling possible future increases. The central bank must balance inflation control against supporting economic growth—a difficult task amid geopolitical uncertainty.

## Government Response and Forward Outlook

The Labour government faces mounting pressure to address the economic consequences of the Iran war. Prime Minister Keir Starmer has stated that "every lever" will be explored to ease rising costs, while Chancellor Rachel Reeves is expected to address household finances in her spring forecast.

### Policy Options and Constraints

Government responses are constrained by several factors: limited fiscal space given high public debt, the need to maintain energy security, and the challenge of providing targeted support without exacerbating inflation. Options under consideration include potential profit caps for energy and petrol firms, adjustments to fuel duty, and enhanced support for vulnerable households.

### Long-Term Structural Challenges

Beyond immediate crisis management, the situation highlights deeper structural issues in the UK economy: vulnerability to global energy shocks, inadequate domestic energy storage capacity, and the erosion of household financial resilience through successive economic crises.

## Conclusion: A Critical Juncture for UK Economic Policy

The UK stands at a critical economic juncture. The Iran war has acted as an accelerant on existing cost-of-living pressures, triggering a significant decline in consumer confidence that threatens to undermine economic recovery. With half of households already making financial adjustments and savings being depleted, the path forward requires careful policy balancing.

The coming months will test both the government's ability to provide effective economic support and the Bank of England's capacity to manage inflation amid external shocks. As global conflicts reshape economic landscapes, the UK's response to this latest crisis will have lasting implications for household wellbeing, business confidence, and the country's economic trajectory in an increasingly volatile world.

For consumers struggling with rising costs, Which? advises speaking to providers about bill management and seeking free, independent debt advice—a recommendation that underscores the severity of the financial pressures facing millions of British households as they navigate this latest economic challenge.
