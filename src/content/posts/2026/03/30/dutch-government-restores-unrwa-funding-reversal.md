# Dutch Government Reverses Position, Will Restore UNRWA Funding

The Netherlands will restore full funding to UNRWA, the United Nations agency for Palestinian refugees, committing €19 million annually starting in 2026. This decision marks a significant reversal of earlier cuts and follows weeks of political tension within the Dutch governing coalition. Minister for Development Cooperation Sjoerd Sjoerdsma announced the policy shift in a letter to parliament, citing the "ongoing humanitarian crisis in Gaza and the region" as justification for the move.

## Context: A Controversial Agency

UNRWA (United Nations Relief and Works Agency for Palestinian Refugees) has been a point of international controversy since Israel accused some of its employees of involvement in Hamas' October 7, 2023 attack. Following these allegations, multiple countries including the Netherlands [temporarily suspended their funding](https://www.rtl.nl/nieuws/home-rtl-nieuws/artikel/5447587/kabinet-geeft-voorlopig-geen-geld-aan-de-unrwa) in January 2024 pending investigations.

The agency provides essential services including healthcare, education, and emergency aid to Palestinian refugees across the Middle East. In Gaza specifically, UNRWA has been described as "crucial" and "indispensable" given the current humanitarian crisis. Unlike the UNHCR (which seeks durable solutions for refugees), UNRWA's mandate focuses on providing direct services, a structure that has drawn criticism for potentially perpetuating refugee status across generations.

## Political Maneuvering and Reversal

The funding announcement comes just days after a difficult parliamentary passage of the development cooperation budget. Last week, coalition party D66—Minister Sjoerdsma's own party—withdrew support for a proposal to restore UNRWA funding to secure right-wing support from parties like JA21 and SGP. This created a majority "over rechts" (over the right) and drew sharp criticism from left-wing opposition parties who called the budget cuts "inhumane."

According to RTL Nieuws, the timing has created political confusion. Suzanne Kröger of PRO (the new name for GroenLinks-PvdA) called the sequence of events "incomprehensible": "Last week still a monster cut on development aid and pushing through UNRWA with support from the right, and now this letter."

The decision represents a return to pre-cut funding levels. The Netherlands had previously decided to phase out UNRWA subsidies after a deal with SGP and JA21 parties. The restoration to €19 million annually matches the country's original 2023 contribution before the suspension.

## Strict Conditions and Safeguards

Minister Sjoerdsma emphasized that the restored funding comes with stringent conditions. "The cabinet emphasizes the strict condition that public funds may under no circumstances be used for direct or indirect support of terrorist groups such as Hamas," he stated. Dutch funding will be contingent on UNRWA implementing reforms based on recommendations from international investigations into the agency's operations.

"It is of great importance that aid reaches the people who need it as efficiently as possible," Sjoerdsma wrote in his parliamentary letter. The minister added that if there are any indications Dutch funds might support terrorist organizations, the support would be "reconsidered."

## International Context: Netherlands Joins Majority

The Dutch decision brings the country in line with most Western democracies that have already restored UNRWA funding. According to UN Watch data, by July 2024, most major donors had reinstated their support:

- **European Union**: Restored $114.1 million on March 1, 2024
- **Germany**: Restored $202.1 million on April 24, 2024  
- **Sweden**: Restored $61 million on March 9, 2024
- **France**: Restored $28.9 million on March 28, 2024
- **Canada**: Restored $23.7 million on March 8, 2024

The notable exception remains the United States, whose $343.9 million contribution remains frozen until at least March 2025 by congressional mandate. The Netherlands' restoration comes later than most EU counterparts but follows the broader international trend of resuming support after initial suspensions.

## Political Fallout and Reactions

The announcement has created tension with JA21, the right-wing party that supported the budget specifically because UNRWA funding was excluded. JA21 MP Michiel Hoogeveen told [RTL Nieuws](https://www.rtl.nl/nieuws/politiek/artikel/5584071/kabinet-trekt-toch-portemonnee-voor-unrwa): "In that changed situation, we would not have voted for the budget."

Human rights organizations welcomed the decision. Amnesty International called it "the lifeline for Palestinians, which they cannot do without when it comes to education, care and emergency aid." The organization added, however, that "it is irresponsible that it was previously chosen to suspend the aid" and noted that renewed support "cannot erase the suffering that was then caused."

## Analysis: Coalition Dynamics and Humanitarian Imperatives

The sequence of events reveals complex coalition dynamics within the Dutch government. D66, as a progressive social-liberal party, faces competing pressures: supporting humanitarian aid to Palestinians (a progressive priority) while managing a coalition that requires compromise with right-wing parties.

The strategic maneuvering appears deliberate:
1. **December 2025**: D66 joins left-wing parties in proposing UNRWA restoration
2. **March 24, 2026**: Withdraws support to pass budget with right-wing backing
3. **March 30, 2026**: Announces restoration via ministerial letter

This allows the party to achieve its policy goal while navigating coalition mathematics. The approach, however, risks alienating both left-wing critics who see inconsistency and right-wing partners who feel misled.

## What's Next: Parliamentary Debate and Implementation

Parliament will debate humanitarian aid—including UNRWA support—this coming Wednesday. The discussion is likely to feature heated exchanges given the recent political tensions and the timing of Sjoerdsma's announcement.

Key questions remain:
1. How will the €19 million annual commitment be integrated into a budget that recently excluded UNRWA funding?
2. What specific reform benchmarks will UNRWA need to meet to maintain Dutch support?
3. How will this decision affect Netherlands' relations with Israel, which has been highly critical of UNRWA?

The funding restoration represents both a humanitarian policy shift and a political compromise. While framed as a necessary response to the Gaza crisis, the decision highlights ongoing tensions in Dutch foreign policy between humanitarian imperatives, counter-terrorism concerns, and the practical realities of coalition governance. As the Netherlands joins most EU countries in resuming UNRWA support, attention now turns to remaining holdouts—particularly the United States—and whether they might follow suit.
