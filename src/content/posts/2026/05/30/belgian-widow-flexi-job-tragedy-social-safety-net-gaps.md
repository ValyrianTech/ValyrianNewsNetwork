# Belgian Widow's Flexi-Job Tragedy Exposes Safety Net Gaps

A Belgian widow is facing severe financial hardship after her husband died while working a flexi-job, a case that has laid bare the gaps in the country's social safety net just weeks before the government plans to dramatically expand the flexible work system. Elanur, a mother of two, describes the situation as "onrechtvaardig" — unjust — as the reduced social contributions tied to flexi-job work have left her without adequate survivor benefits.

As reported by [De Morgen](https://www.demorgen.be/politiek/haar-man-stierf-tijdens-zijn-flexi-job-nu-dreigt-financiele-nood-het-systeem-is-onrechtvaardig~b364c585/), the system costs Elanur hundreds of euros per month, threatening financial ruin for her and her two growing children. Her case has become a flashpoint in an already heated national debate over the future of flexi-jobs in Belgium.

## What Is a Flexi-Job?

Introduced by law on November 16, 2015, and taking effect on December 1 of that year, the flexi-job system was designed as a secondary, flexible employment arrangement. Initially limited to the hospitality (horeca) sector, it allowed workers to earn additional income alongside their main job — or as pensioners — under a highly favorable tax regime. Income from flexi-jobs can be fully or partially exempt from taxes, while employers pay a special social security contribution of 28 percent, significantly lower than standard rates.

The system proved popular and was expanded to additional sectors in 2018. But as its reach has grown, so have concerns about its impact on social security funding and worker protections.

## A Human Cost

Elanur's story illustrates the human consequences of these policy choices. When a flexi-job worker dies, survivor benefits — known in Belgium as overlevingspensioen or nabestaandenuitkering — are calculated based on the contributions made during the worker's employment. Because flexi-job contributions are substantially lower than those for regular employment, the resulting benefits are proportionally reduced.

"Het systeem kost Elanur honderden euro's per maand," the De Morgen article notes. "Voor haar en haar twee opgroeiende kinderen dreigt financiële nood." The system costs Elanur hundreds of euros per month, threatening financial hardship for her family.

## Political Timing

The case breaks at a critical political moment. The Arizona coalition government, led by Prime Minister Bart De Wever, is pushing forward with plans to expand flexi-jobs to nearly all sectors starting July 1, 2026. The Council of Ministers approved the draft law on April 30, despite significant opposition and constitutional concerns.

The Council of State (Raad van State) has raised serious constitutional objections, arguing that the expansion discriminates against regular employment and effectively constitutes illegal state aid. Economists have warned that flexi-jobs create "enormous leaks" in social security revenues, undermining the funding base for pensions, healthcare, and survivor benefits.

According to reporting by HLN, approximately 8,000 Belgians currently combine unemployment or sickness benefits with flexi-job work — a practice that critics argue further strains the social security system.

## Competing Visions

The debate over flexi-jobs reflects deeper divisions about the future of Belgium's labor market. Supporters, including the government and employers' organization UNIZO, argue that flexi-jobs provide much-needed flexibility, reduce undeclared work, and help businesses manage peak periods. Managing Director Bart Buysse of UNIZO has been a vocal advocate for expansion.

Opponents — socialists, unions, and a range of economists — counter that flexi-jobs undermine regular employment, create tax avoidance opportunities, and weaken the social security system that protects all workers. The case of Elanur gives their arguments a powerful human face.

## What's at Stake

As the July 1 expansion date approaches, several urgent questions remain unanswered. Are flexi-job workers adequately insured against workplace accidents? Do survivor benefits adequately protect families when a flexi-job worker dies? And should the expansion proceed without first addressing these protection gaps?

The number of flexi-jobs and work-related accidents (arbeidsongevallen) has risen significantly in Belgium as the system has expanded. Yet the regulatory framework has not kept pace with the growing scale of flexible employment.

## The Road Ahead

Elanur's case has the potential to influence the ongoing political debate. Whether it will delay or alter the planned expansion remains to be seen. What is clear is that her story has struck a chord in a country grappling with the tension between labor market flexibility and the preservation of a robust social safety net.

For now, Elanur and her children face an uncertain future — a future made more precarious by a system designed to provide opportunity, but which, in her case, has delivered hardship instead.
