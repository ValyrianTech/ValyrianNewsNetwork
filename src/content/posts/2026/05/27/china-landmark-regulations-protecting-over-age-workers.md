# China Issues Landmark Rules Protecting Over-Age Workers

Five Chinese government departments have jointly issued the nation's first-ever specialized regulations to protect the basic rights of workers who continue to work beyond the statutory retirement age. The "Interim Regulations on the Protection of Basic Rights and Interests of Over-Age Workers," announced on May 25-26, 2026, will take effect on July 1, 2026, marking a historic shift in China's approach to elderly labor rights.

The regulations, issued as Decree No. 56 by the Ministry of Human Resources and Social Security, National Health Commission, Ministry of Emergency Management, State Taxation Administration, and National Healthcare Security Administration, establish a comprehensive framework covering labor remuneration, rest and leave, occupational safety, and work-related injury protection for over-age workers, as [reported by People's Daily](http://society.people.com.cn/n1/2026/0527/c1008-40728179.html).

## Closing a Legal Gray Zone

Prior to these regulations, over-age workers in China existed in a legal gray zone. Under Chinese labor law, once a worker reaches statutory retirement age, their relationship with an employer is typically classified as a civil law relationship rather than a labor relationship, meaning they were not protected by the Labor Contract Law, Labor Law, or work-related injury insurance. Common problems included wage arrears, forced overtime, and injury compensation disputes with no clear legal recourse.

As [China News Service](https://www.chinanews.com.cn/gn/2026/05-25/10628184.shtml) reports, the regulations apply to any employer within China's territory that hires workers who have exceeded the statutory retirement age, as well as workers who retired early and are rehired. Workers who have chosen flexible delayed retirement under the national delayed retirement policy are not covered, as they remain under standard labor law.

## Four Pillars of Protection

The regulations guarantee four fundamental rights for over-age workers. On labor remuneration, employers must pay wages on time and in full, with amounts not falling below the local minimum wage. Regarding rest and leave, employers must comply with statutory working hours and generally cannot require overtime from over-age workers. For occupational safety, employers must assign suitable positions and workloads and provide safety training. On work-related injury insurance, employers are now required to enroll over-age workers in工伤保险 (work injury insurance), a significant change from previous practice.

The regulations also address pension continuity. Over-age workers who have not met minimum contribution periods may continue paying pension contributions individually or through employer agreement, as detailed by [Yicai](https://www.yicai.com/news/103199676.html).

## Expert Perspectives

Legal scholars have praised the regulations as a paradigm shift in Chinese labor law. Lin Jia (林嘉), a professor at Renmin University Law School, told Yicai that "this legislation breaks the inherent concept of 'linking labor relations with labor protection.' It no longer takes the existence of a labor relationship as the sole criterion, but instead precisely protects the basic rights of over-age workers based on the fact of labor, allowing the labor value of over-age workers to be fully respected."

Shen Jianfeng (沈建峰), Director of the Labor Law and Social Security Law Research Center at Central University of Finance and Economics, described the approach as "bottom-line, basic protection." He noted that the rights employers must protect are basic rights concerning the survival and health of over-age workers, including working hours, rest, wages, working conditions, and injury protection, which protects workers while avoiding unreasonable burdens on employers.

Li Wenjing (李文静), a researcher at the Chinese Academy of Labor and Social Security Sciences, told People's Daily that the regulations "provide a clear basis for protecting the rights of over-age workers and compliance standards for employers. They are a direct manifestation of labor security law responding to the national strategy of addressing population aging."

## Demographic Imperative

The regulations come against the backdrop of China's rapidly aging population. By 2025, the population aged 60 and above exceeded 300 million, accounting for over 21% of the total population. The working-age population (16-59) has been declining since 2012, creating labor shortages in many sectors. These demographic pressures have forced China to reconsider its retirement policies.

In September 2024, the Standing Committee of the National People's Congress adopted a landmark decision to implement a progressive delayed retirement age system, as documented by [NPC Observer](https://npcobserver.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/2024-NPCSC-Retiremeng-Age-Decision_Gazette.pdf). The 2025 Supreme People's Court added new independent case categories for over-age worker employment disputes, laying the groundwork for the regulatory framework.

Lin Jia noted that over-age workers include both retired technical experts and industry specialists, as well as ordinary workers in security, cleaning, housekeeping, and logistics. "Over-age workers continue their labor value with years of accumulated experience and diligent dedication, becoming an important force in activating the silver economy and supplementing the labor market," she said.

## Balancing Protection and Costs

The regulations explicitly adopt a balanced approach. Rather than extending full labor law protections, which would impose significant costs on employers, the regulations focus on four essential areas. The Ministry of Human Resources and Social Security stated that the regulations "fill the shortcomings of China's current labor legal system, clarify the rights and obligations of employers and over-age workers, and protect the legitimate rights and interests of workers who have exceeded the statutory retirement age."

## Implementation Challenges Ahead

While the regulations represent significant progress, several implementation challenges remain. China's labor inspection system is already strained, and adding over-age workers to its mandate will require additional resources. Many over-age workers, particularly in rural areas and the gig economy, work informally without written agreements. Implementation may also vary significantly across provinces with different economic conditions and administrative capacities.

## Broader Significance

The regulations represent a broader shift in how Chinese society views aging and work, moving toward recognizing the value and dignity of older workers. Chinese policymakers have increasingly framed the aging population not just as a challenge but as an opportunity, emphasizing the transition from a "demographic dividend" to a "human capital dividend." The regulations are part of a broader strategy to formalize and protect "silver employment" (银发用工), ensuring that China's growing population of older workers can contribute to the economy with dignity and legal protection.

As the July 1 effective date approaches, employers across China will need to update their HR policies, insurance coverage, and workplace safety practices to comply with the new framework. The regulations mark a significant step forward in China's labor rights landscape, setting a precedent for how the world's most populous nation adapts its labor laws to the realities of an aging society.
