# 28 Boy Scouts Rescued From West Virginia River Floodwaters

Twenty-eight Boy Scouts from Fairfax, Virginia, were rescued Saturday after fast-moving floodwaters stranded them along the Cacapon River in Hampshire County, West Virginia. Emergency crews responded with watercraft and safely transported all scouts across the river within approximately 45 minutes, with no serious injuries reported.

## How the Rescue Unfolded

The troop was traveling along the Cacapon River on Saturday afternoon when a storm caused the river's current and water levels to intensify unexpectedly, according to [Fox News](https://www.foxnews.com/us/28-boy-scouts-rescued-from-fast-moving-floodwaters-during-river-trip-west-virginia). What began as a routine river trip quickly turned into a dangerous situation as the scouts were forced to head toward the riverbank.

Micah Campbell, a scout with the troop, described the sudden change in conditions. "We were doing a river trip and the current and the strength of the river turned out to be a little stronger than we thought," Campbell told Fox News Digital. He explained that after some of the boys entered the water, the group made the decision to abort the trip and seek safety on shore. "And now we were on the wrong side of the river, so we needed some help getting across."

Jeffrey West, whose father owns a cabin near where the scouts became stranded, said rescue crews were called to the scene. Watercraft arrived and transported the boys safely across the river. Altogether, the scouts were stranded for roughly 45 minutes.

## Aftermath and Community Response

Following the rescue, the boys gathered at the cabin owned by West's father, where they were given pizza and hot cocoa while waiting for transportation home. West, who had originally planned a "quiet weekend at the cabin," told Fox News Digital with a laugh, "It's been a day."

No serious injuries were reported among the scouts or rescue personnel. Arrangements were made to shuttle the scouts out of the area following the incident.

## Broader Context

The Cacapon River, a tributary of the Potomac River flowing through West Virginia's Eastern Panhandle, is known for its scenic beauty and recreational use. However, the Appalachian region's mountainous topography makes it particularly susceptible to flash flooding after heavy rainfall. West Virginia has experienced devastating floods in recent years, including the 2016 flooding that killed 23 people and the 2025 flooding in Ohio County that claimed at least six lives.

As [Yahoo News](https://www.yahoo.com/news/weather-news/articles/28-boy-scouts-rescued-fast-085832065.html) reported in its syndicated coverage, the incident highlights the unpredictable nature of river conditions following storms, particularly in regions prone to rapid water level changes.

## Analysis

The successful rescue operation underscores the importance of swift emergency response in rural areas. The 45-minute response time demonstrates effective coordination between local emergency services and community members who stepped in to assist. The scouts' decision to abort the river trip when conditions deteriorated likely prevented a more serious outcome.

No official statements from the Boy Scouts of America or Hampshire County emergency services have been released at this time. The reporting relies primarily on eyewitness accounts from those present at the scene, including scouts and local residents.

In an opinion piece published by [The Progressive Voice](https://www.progressivevoice.net/article/progressive-boy-scout-rescue-highlights-need-for-climate-resilience-in-rural-communities-ix86r0), author Dev Kapoor framed the incident within a broader discussion about climate resilience and the need for infrastructure investment in rural communities, noting that extreme weather events are becoming increasingly unpredictable. ## What's Next

The scouts were awaiting transportation back to Fairfax, Virginia, approximately 90 miles east of the Cacapon River area. The incident serves as a reminder of the dangers posed by rapidly changing river conditions, particularly during storm season in the Appalachian region. West Virginia's steep terrain and narrow valleys make it one of the most flood-prone areas in the United States, and officials urge caution for those engaging in water recreation, especially following storms.

Questions remain about which specific emergency services responded to the scene and whether the Boy Scouts of America will issue an official statement regarding the incident. For now, the story stands as a testament to quick-thinking scouts, responsive emergency crews, and a community that came together to ensure everyone made it home safely.
