# Pentagon Pushes Battlefield AI as Leaders Urge Caution

The Trump administration is accelerating the integration of artificial intelligence into U.S. military operations, even as senior commanders warn of the risks of autonomous lethal systems and an escalating dispute with AI company Anthropic raises questions about ethical safeguards in defense contracting.

The push has created a three-way tension: Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth demanding unrestricted AI use, top military leaders urging caution on autonomous weapons, and AI companies resisting what they see as dangerous overreach, according to [AP News](https://apnews.com/article/artificial-intelligence-military-hegseth-anthropic-d5fbaee17ee0bdb9738dbb808ea2d047).

## Military Leaders Sound the Alarm

Adm. Frank Bradley, head of U.S. Special Operations Command, told attendees at a recent special forces conference in Tampa, Florida, that troops "have to be very careful about how we come to AI's employment and its inspiration into the delivery of lethality." Bradley said he can envision a future where AI determines targets but insisted that "we, as humans, have to have the confidence that ... it's going to deliver violence only where we intend it to be delivered."

Bradley's caution stands in contrast to his boss's aggressive posture. Hegseth has insisted the Pentagon be allowed to use AI technology "any legal way it sees fit," telling an audience of SpaceX employees in January that he would reject any AI models "that won't allow you to fight wars" and that his vision was systems operating "without ideological constraints that limit lawful military applications."

## AI Already in Combat

AI is not a future prospect — it is already being used in active combat operations. Lt. Gen. Michael Conley, head of Air Force Special Operations Command, told a congressional committee in May that his troops used AI "bots" to convert top secret intelligence to a secret classification within seconds during the Iran war, enabling faster sharing with drone operators on the ground.

The [Center for Security and Emerging Technology at Georgetown University](https://cset.georgetown.edu/publication/building-the-tech-coalition/) published a case study on how the Army's 18th Airborne Corps used the Maven Smart System to target artillery strikes "just as efficiently as the best unit in recent American history" with 2,000 fewer service members. Helen Toner, interim executive director at CSET and a former OpenAI board member, noted that "the general public often seems to underestimate the caution with which the U.S. military approaches new technologies."

## The Anthropic-Pentagon Showdown

The most dramatic flashpoint has been the public dispute between the Pentagon and Anthropic, the San Francisco-based AI company founded in 2021 by former OpenAI employees. The [Pentagon formally labeled Anthropic a "supply chain risk"](https://apnews.com/article/pentagon-ai-anthropic-claude-dario-amodei-openai-d4608c7dd139245ac8ad94d5427c505a) — the first U.S. company ever to receive this designation — ending a $200 million defense contract after CEO Dario Amodei refused to accept terms allowing AI use for mass domestic surveillance and fully autonomous weapons.

"We cannot in good conscience accede to their request," Amodei said, citing concerns about "mass domestic surveillance" and "fully autonomous weapons," adding: "We will not knowingly provide a product that puts America's warfighters and civilians at risk."

[Anthropic sued the Pentagon](https://apnews.com/article/anthropic-trump-pentagon-hegseth-ai-104c6c39306f1adeea3b637d2c1c601b) in March, claiming the supply chain risk label is illegal retaliation and a First Amendment violation. Microsoft and retired military chiefs filed briefs supporting Anthropic. The designation — a tool designed to protect against foreign adversaries like China or Russia — drew broad bipartisan criticism. Sen. Kirsten Gillibrand (D-NY) called it "a dangerous misuse of a tool meant to address adversary-controlled technology."

## Trump's AI Policy Paradox

President Donald Trump [called off plans to sign a new AI executive order](https://apnews.com/article/trump-ai-executive-order-ee318f35acc8a2c43e47f3ebf26cb459) hours before a scheduled White House ceremony on May 21, over concerns it could dull America's AI edge against China. "We're leading China, we're leading everybody, and I don't want to do anything that's going to get in the way of that lead," Trump told reporters.

The scrapped order would have required frontier AI labs to submit models for 90-day federal review before public release. Trump pulled it after pushback from advisers and tech executives.

## Consumer Backlash and Industry Fallout

The dispute has had unexpected consequences. Anthropic experienced a surge in consumer downloads — over 1 million signups per day — lifting its Claude chatbot past ChatGPT and Gemini as the top AI app in more than 20 countries. Hours after the Pentagon punished Anthropic, OpenAI announced a deal to replace Claude with ChatGPT in classified military environments, though CEO Sam Altman later expressed regret, saying the move "looked opportunistic and sloppy."

## What's Next

The legal battle between Anthropic and the Pentagon continues, with significant implications for how the U.S. military acquires and deploys AI technology. The case raises fundamental questions about whether AI companies can set ethical boundaries on the use of their technology by the military, and whether the government can penalize them for doing so. As the Iran war creates urgency around AI deployment, the tension between speed and safety — between Hegseth's push for unrestricted capability and Bradley's call for human oversight — remains unresolved.
