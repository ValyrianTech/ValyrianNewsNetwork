# New York Times Sues Pentagon Again Over Press Escort Policy

The New York Times filed a second lawsuit against the U.S. Department of Defense on Monday, challenging an interim policy that requires credentialed journalists to be accompanied by a government escort at all times while inside the Pentagon. The lawsuit, filed in U.S. District Court in Washington, D.C., argues that the escort requirement violates the First and Fifth Amendments and represents a retaliatory effort to punish the newspaper for its previous successful legal challenge.

## Background of the Dispute

The new lawsuit is the latest escalation in a growing confrontation between the mainstream press and the Pentagon under the second Trump administration. In September 2025, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth unveiled sweeping restrictions on press access, requiring reporters to sign a pledge that included strict rules on the "solicitation" of information from defense employees. Major news organizations — including CBS News, ABC News, NBC News, CNN, and Fox News — declined to sign and were [forced to vacate their Pentagon workspaces](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/new-york-times-sues-pentagon-second-time-media-restrictions/).

The New York Times filed its first lawsuit in December 2025. In March 2026, U.S. District Judge Paul L. Friedman [struck down key parts of the policy](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/judge-strikes-down-restrictive-pentagon-press-policy-finding-it-violates-first-amendment/), finding they violated the First and Fifth Amendments. Judge Friedman ordered the reinstatement of reporter Julian E. Barnes' credentials, writing that "especially in light of the country's recent incursion into Venezuela and its ongoing war with Iran, it is more important than ever that the public have access to information from a variety of perspectives."

## The Escort Policy at Issue

Shortly after Judge Friedman's ruling, the Pentagon implemented a new "interim" policy requiring journalists to have official escorts at all times inside the building and permanently closed the designated workspace for journalists. Judge Friedman ruled that this new policy violated his March court order, but an [appeals court allowed the escort requirement to remain in place](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/may/18/new-york-times-pentagon-press-restrictions-lawsuit) while the government appeals. That appeal is ongoing.

According to the Times' 50-page complaint, the escort policy has made routine reporting virtually impossible. The lawsuit describes how reporter Julian Barnes and others must now "call or email for an appointment, wait for a response, get an escort, ask their question, and return to the library outside the Pentagon — only to repeat the process for the next source." The complaint notes that for decades, the Pentagon allowed reporters unescorted access in unsecured corridors so they could move between press offices and ask questions on short notice as events unfolded.

## Legal Arguments

The lawsuit, brought by the Times and reporter Julian E. Barnes, names as defendants the Department of Defense, Secretary Pete Hegseth, Chief Pentagon Spokesperson Sean Parnell, and Special Advisor Timothy Parlatore. The complaint argues that the interim policy is "patently retaliatory, utterly unreasonable, and manifestly arbitrary and capricious," alleging that [the Pentagon adopted it to thwart a district court order](https://deadline.com/2026/05/new-york-times-pentagon-pete-hegseth-1236914916/) and punish the Times for its editorial viewpoint and its successful lawsuit.

Theodore J. Boutrous Jr. of Gibson Dunn, representing the Times, said in a statement that the escort requirement is "a blatant effort to thwart independent journalism that violates the First Amendment, defies the district court's earlier injunction, departs from longstanding tradition, and hurts the American people by trying to hide important information from them during wartime."

## Pentagon's Defense

Pentagon Chief Spokesperson Sean Parnell defended the policy on social media, calling the lawsuit "nothing more than an attempt to remove the barriers to them getting their hands on classified information." He argued that journalists "want to roam the halls of the Pentagon freely and without an escort — a privilege that they do not have in any other federal building," and maintained that the policy is "completely lawful and narrowly designed to protect national security information from unlawful criminal disclosure."

## Broader Implications

The lawsuit underscores the escalating tensions between the Trump administration and the press at a time of active U.S. military operations. The complaint notes that the restrictions are "made more urgent by recent events that have exacerbated the importance of independent reporting, including the capture of the President of Venezuela, the Iran war, and Secretary Hegseth's firings of multiple high ranking military officials."

Press freedom advocates have expressed alarm. The National Press Club has called the policies a "direct assault on independent journalism," and the Pentagon Press Association has urged the immediate reinstatement of credentials for all its members.

## What's Next

The new lawsuit seeks a court order forcing the Pentagon to lift the escort restriction. It remains to be seen whether the case will be assigned to Judge Friedman, who has already ruled against the Pentagon twice, or to a different judge. Meanwhile, the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals continues to consider the Pentagon's appeal of Judge Friedman's March ruling, a decision that could shape the legal landscape for press access at the Pentagon for years to come.
