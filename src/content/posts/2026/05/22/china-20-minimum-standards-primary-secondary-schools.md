# China Issues 20 School Standards for Primary and Secondary

Four Chinese government departments have jointly issued a sweeping new set of 20 bottom-line requirements for primary and secondary school operations, mandating minimum standards in safety, mental health support, digital infrastructure, and staffing across all of the country's schools. The policy, released on May 22, 2026, gives schools nationwide a three-year deadline to achieve full compliance.

The [Ministry of Education](https://www.moe.gov.cn/srcsite/A06/s3321/202605/t20260521_1437176.html), together with the National Development and Reform Commission, the Ministry of Finance, and the Ministry of Housing and Urban-Rural Development, signed the document on May 6 under the designation Education Basic Department [2026] No. 1. It replaces and significantly expands a 2014 policy that applied only to impoverished compulsory education areas, extending coverage to every regular primary and secondary school in the country.

## A Significant Expansion from 2014

The original "20 Bottom-Line Requirements" issued in 2014 were part of China's poverty alleviation drive, focused narrowly on rural schools in poor regions. The 2026 version represents a major upgrade, reflecting China's shift from targeted poverty relief toward universal quality education. According to a [Ministry of Education spokesperson Q&A](https://www.moe.gov.cn/jyb_xwfb/s271/202605/t20260521_1437195.html), the revision was driven by "changes in the school-age population" and the need to "adapt to urbanization development."

"This iteration upgrades the original 'Comprehensive Improvement' 20 bottom-line requirements," the spokesperson said. "In addition to basic requirements for school buildings and teaching/living hardware, it places special emphasis on mental health, aesthetic education, and physical education facilities and staffing."

## Key Requirements Across Five Categories

The 20 standards are organized into five categories: school building construction, safety and security, living facilities, teaching equipment, and staff allocation.

**Safety and security** measures include mandatory closed-campus management, one-key alarm devices and anti-collision barriers at school gates, video surveillance in key areas, and compliance with national fire safety standards. Classroom lighting must meet national standards, and all D-grade dangerous buildings must be demolished or sealed.

**Living facility** requirements mandate hygienic drinking water, individual desks and chairs with back support for every student, and adequate sports venues and equipment. Boarding schools must provide cafeterias, dormitories with proper ventilation and guardrails on upper bunks, and adequate toilet and washing facilities.

**Teaching equipment** standards require classrooms to have blackboards or interactive electronic whiteboards, internet bandwidth of at least 100 Mbps, dedicated computer and science laboratories, music and art classrooms, and libraries with sufficient book collections.

Perhaps most notably, the policy mandates the establishment of **student psychological counseling rooms** in every school and requires at least one full-time or part-time mental health education teacher per school — reflecting growing official concern about youth mental health.

**Staffing** requirements also mandate full-time security guards with proper certification, sufficient teachers to offer all nationally mandated courses, and dormitory administrators, with female dormitories specifically requiring female administrators.

## Implementation and Oversight

Local education authorities are required to conduct school-by-school assessments, identifying gaps and creating "one school, one policy" improvement plans. The [Xinhua News Agency](https://education.news.cn/20260522/a5867fb64d7344fea28b47cbb504202a/c.html) reported that the policy aims for all schools to meet the standards within three years, with a dynamic "account cancellation" system to track compliance.

The Ministry of Education has promised a "zero tolerance" approach to violations, with education inspection departments incorporating compliance into their oversight mandates. Development and reform commissions will support qualifying construction projects, while finance departments will coordinate central subsidies and local funds to prioritize school condition improvements.

## Broader Implications

The policy arrives amid significant demographic shifts in China, including declining birth rates and regional population movements that have created challenges for school resource allocation. It aligns with the "Five Educations Simultaneously" framework — emphasizing moral, intellectual, physical, aesthetic, and labor education — and is part of a broader push toward the 2035 goal of establishing China as an "education power."

The inclusion of mental health requirements signals official recognition of a growing crisis among Chinese youth, while digital infrastructure mandates — including the 100 Mbps minimum bandwidth — reflect China's broader digital education strategy. However, implementation challenges remain significant, particularly for poorer inland provinces where funding gaps, teacher shortages, and infrastructure deficits may test the three-year timeline.

## What to Watch For

As local governments begin their school-by-school audits, the key questions will center on funding mechanisms for poorer regions, how the policy interacts with ongoing rural school consolidation, and whether exemptions or extended timelines will be granted for particularly remote schools. The policy's success will be measured not just by compliance rates, but by whether the promised improvements in student welfare and educational quality materialize on the ground.
