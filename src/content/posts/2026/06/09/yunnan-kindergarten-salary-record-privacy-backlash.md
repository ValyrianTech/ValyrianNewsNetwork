# Yunnan Kindergarten Salary Demand Sparks Privacy Backlash

Four public kindergartens in Xiangyun County, Yunnan Province, ignited a national controversy after requiring parents to submit salary records, bank statements, and utility bills as part of their enrollment process — a policy that authorities have since been forced to retract following widespread public outcry over privacy violations and discriminatory practices.

## The Controversial Requirements

The disputed policy was published on June 5, 2026, by the Xiangcheng Town Central School via its official public account, detailing the enrollment requirements for the 2026 fall semester at four public kindergartens: Chengdong Community, Chengxi Community, Chengnan Community, and Xiangcheng Town No. 4 Kindergarten.

According to [Cover News](https://news.qq.com/rain/a/20260606A04EUB00), which first reported the story, the notice divided applicants into categories with escalating documentation demands. Homebuyers were required to submit property purchase contracts alongside property management fee and utility bill receipts. Self-employed individuals had to provide business licenses, storefront photos, and in some cases three months of business financial statements. Migrant workers faced the steepest requirements: employment certificates, salary records showing wage income via bank statements, and rental agreements.

Notably, the notice explicitly exempted government employees and state-owned enterprise workers with permanent positions in Xiangcheng Town from providing these additional materials.

## Public Outcry and Legal Violations

Local parent Ms. Li was the first to raise concerns after discovering the requirements. The story quickly went viral on Chinese social media, with the Weibo hashtag #4PublicKindergartensCheckParentsSalaryRecords amassing over 23 million reads.

Legal experts were swift to condemn the policy. Du Wei, Director of Sichuan Weixu Law Firm, told Cover News that the requirements violated multiple laws and regulations. "The Ministry of Education explicitly stipulated in 2022 that the collection of parents' occupation and income information is strictly prohibited," Du Wei said. "Requiring business financial statements, property management fees, and utility bill receipts has no legal basis and constitutes an illegal addition of registration materials, a violation of privacy, and a disguised barrier to enrollment."

The policy also ran afoul of China's Personal Information Protection Law, the Civil Code, and the Regulations on Promoting the Development of Individual Businesses, all of which restrict the collection of sensitive personal and financial data.

## Official Response and Policy Reversal

Facing mounting public pressure, the Xiangyun County Education and Sports Bureau issued a correction notice on June 7, acknowledging that the requirements contained "non-standard and unnecessary" content. The bureau ordered the school to remove the controversial provisions.

In a detailed response to [The Paper](https://www.thepaper.cn/newsDetail_forward_33339506), an Education Bureau representative explained the rationale behind the original policy. "The main reason for setting these controversial requirements was to verify whether applicants genuinely reside, work, or operate businesses in the urban area," the official said, citing previous instances of parents submitting fraudulent housing contracts and temporary business licenses. However, the representative conceded that "the school's request for parents to provide salary records potentially infringes on privacy and was indeed inadequately considered."

The bureau has since committed to conducting a comprehensive review of enrollment policies across all schools in the county, strengthening legal compliance checks, and implementing a three-tier审核 (review) system for enrollment materials.

## Deeper Issues: Discrimination and Supply Scarcity

The controversy exposed deeper structural problems in China's public education system. The enrollment consultant "Teacher Zhang" admitted to Cover News that the requirements were specifically designed to target migrant workers, stating: "We are mainly targeting migrant workers. Regular employees with permanent positions in the town do not need to provide these materials."

This differential treatment drew sharp criticism from commentators. Feng Haining of [Guangming Online](http://views.ce.cn/view/ent/202606/t20260609_3018435.shtml) wrote: "All public education institutions should follow the principle of fairness. Public kindergartens use land that is allocated free of charge and are funded by fiscal appropriations. Every local taxpayer has contributed to this. Yet, when taxpayers' children enroll, they face various conditional restrictions."

At the heart of the issue is a chronic shortage of public kindergarten slots. Across China, demand for affordable public preschool education far exceeds supply, leading schools to adopt increasingly creative — and legally questionable — screening mechanisms. The Education Bureau acknowledged this, pledging to "expand channels to secure project funds, promote the construction of new and expanded public kindergartens, and alleviate the supply-demand contradiction."

## Broader Implications

This incident is particularly significant given the policy environment in 2026. In April, the Ministry of Education launched the "Sunshine Enrollment Special Action" to standardize enrollment practices nationwide. The Xiangyun case represents a high-profile test of whether local authorities will comply with these directives.

Elephant News, in its public opinion analysis, identified five key themes in online discussion: privacy infringement concerns, education equity and social stratification, differential treatment of migrant workers, broader anxiety about education sector irregularities, and skepticism toward official rectification efforts.

## What's Next

While the immediate policy reversal represents a victory for privacy advocates, fundamental questions remain. Will any individuals face disciplinary action for approving the illegal requirements? How will the adjusted policy be enforced to prevent informal reinstatement of the removed provisions? And most critically, can local governments move quickly enough to expand public kindergarten capacity to meet growing demand?

As Feng Haining noted in his commentary, the incident should prompt deeper reflection: "The local authorities cannot only 'address the issue as it arises' under舆论 (public opinion) pressure. They must also reflect on the deeper problems in their kindergarten enrollment work — whether approval procedures are compliant, whether enrollment requirements are fair, and how to resolve the supply-demand contradiction."
