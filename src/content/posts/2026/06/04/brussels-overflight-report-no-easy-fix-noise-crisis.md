# Brussels Overflight Report: No Easy Fix for Noise Crisis

A comprehensive scientific impact study ordered by the Brussels Court of First Instance has delivered a stark verdict on the decades-long conflict over aircraft noise above the Belgian capital: there is no "magic solution." The report, conducted by the French firm ENV-ISA and delivered to stakeholders on June 1, 2026, concludes that every scenario for reducing noise pollution necessarily comes at the cost of reduced capacity at Brussels Airport.

## The Core Trade-Off: Noise vs. Capacity

The ENV-ISA report analyzed all 196,000 flight movements from 2024 and weather conditions across all 366 days of that year. Researchers virtually modified wind rules and recalculated which runway each aircraft would have used, then overlaid trajectories with demographic data to count, street by street, the number of people awakened or highly annoyed per World Health Organization criteria. As [RTBF reported](https://www.rtbf.be/article/survol-de-bruxelles-un-nouveau-rapport-demontre-qu-il-n-y-a-pas-de-baguette-magique-11734716), the report's central finding is unambiguous: "No scenario eliminates the noise-capacity trade-off."

## Scenario B: Relief for Some, Burden for Others

Only one scenario — Scenario B — showed a significant reduction in the number of Brussels residents overflown. Under this scenario, approximately 12,000 residents of Molenbeek-Saint-Jean would be removed from noisy zones during daytime, while Woluwe-Saint-Lambert, Schaerbeek, and Brussels-City would also see massive relief both day and night.

However, the trade-offs are substantial. The scenario would reduce airport arrival capacity by roughly 10%. The Brussels municipality of Jette would bear the brunt of displaced flights, with Ganshoren partially affected. Further afield, Kortenberg in Flanders would see noise levels explode as flights are redirected.

At the other extreme, a maximum-capacity scenario that forces greater use of preferential runway systems would make Woluwe-Saint-Lambert and Brussels-City the primary daytime victims, while Evere, Brussels-City, and Schaerbeek would be heavily penalized at night.

## A 40-Year Conflict Reaches a Critical Juncture

The report arrives at a moment of heightened tensions in what [Governance.brussels](https://governance.brussels/fr/dossiers/survol-bruxelles) describes as a 40-year conflict involving 670,600 Brussels residents exposed to aircraft noise above WHO thresholds. The institutional complexity is a textbook case of Belgium's federal architecture: aviation and flight routes fall under federal authority (Minister Jean-Luc Crucke), noise and environment are regional competences (Brussels Environment), the airport's environmental permit is controlled by Flanders (annulled in July 2025), and individual communes pursue their own legal recourse.

## The Route Crucke Controversy

The report analyzes 2024 data and does not account for the current situation caused by the RNP-07L approach route — widely known as the "Route Crucke" — activated in summer 2025 as a temporary alternative procedure during airport construction. Unlike previous curved approaches, this satellite-navigation-guided route concentrates 100% of flights on the exact same corridor, with 96% of flights violating Brussels noise standards and over 450,000 residents affected.

## Political and Legal Pressure Mounts

The legal timeline is unforgiving. The Brussels Court of First Instance has ordered the federal government to inform parties of concrete measures to end violations before October 1, 2026, and to justify its choices by December 1, 2026. These deadlines follow five condemnations of the Belgian state, with cumulative penalties reaching approximately €25 million.

On June 3, 2026, the 19 Brussels mayors met with Minister Crucke but reported no progress. As [RTBF reported](https://www.rtbf.be/article/nuisances-des-avions-les-bourgmestres-bruxellois-rencontrent-le-ministre-crucke-sans-obtenir-d-avancee-11734696), Sophie Devos, Mayor of Auderghem and President of the Conference of Brussels Mayors, stated: "There was no progress — the minister came to listen." The mayors are demanding a night flight ban from 23:00 to 07:00, the elimination of the noisiest aircraft, and restrictions on the use of secondary runways 01 and 07.

Minister Crucke responded: "What matters are not announcements of effects but the announcement of effects, which is why I will communicate more fully in due time."

## The Health Dimension

The health stakes are enormous. The Belgian Superior Health Council estimates the health cost of aircraft noise around Brussels Airport at over €1 billion per year, with documented effects including sleep disorders, cardiovascular disease, stress, reduced lung function, and learning difficulties in children. The RNP-07L route alone affects 450,000 residents, and Brussels Environment recorded 4,758 noise violations in just 10 months of 2025.

## What Comes Next

With the RNP-07L authorization set to expire on October 31, 2026, and the court-ordered deadline for concrete measures arriving on October 1, the federal government faces an imminent choice. The ENV-ISA report provides scientific backing for judicial decisions, but political implementation remains uncertain. As the report itself notes, the choice between alternatives "reflects political choices concerning the distribution of advantages and disadvantages between airport operators, airlines, local communities, and the general interest in air connectivity."

For the 670,600 Brussels residents exposed to noise above WHO thresholds, the question is whether Belgium's complex institutional machinery can finally deliver a solution — or whether the courts will continue to be the only avenue for progress.
