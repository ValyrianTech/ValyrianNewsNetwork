# China Launches Communications Technology Test Satellite 26A

China successfully launched the Communications Technology Test Satellite 26A on June 23, 2026, from the Wenchang Spacecraft Launch Site in Hainan Province, marking another milestone in the nation's expanding aerospace capabilities. The satellite was carried into space by a Long March 7A carrier rocket at 10:10 Beijing time and successfully entered its predetermined orbit, according to [Xinhua News Agency](https://www.news.cn/tech/20260623/80dc8e8e584c44c981fdb73cda287357/c.html).

## Mission Overview

The launch was conducted from Wenchang's Launch Complex 2, China's southernmost and most advanced spaceport. The Long March 7A — a three-stage variant of the Long March 7 family featuring a hydrolox third stage — performed nominally throughout the ascent, delivering the satellite to its intended orbit with precision. The mission was declared a complete success shortly after satellite separation.

This flight marked the 653rd mission of the Long March rocket series, underscoring the maturity and increasing cadence of China's space launch program. As [CCTV News](https://news.cctv.com/2026/06/23/ARTIZNsY9B3UaOcwX7x29KJw260623.shtml) reported, the satellite is primarily designed for satellite communications, broadcasting, television, data transmission, and related technology verification.

## The Long March 7A Rocket

The Long March 7A (CZ-7A) is a medium-to-heavy lift launch vehicle developed by the China Academy of Launch Vehicle Technology (CALT). Standing 60.13 meters tall with a mass of 573,000 kilograms, the rocket is capable of delivering up to 7,000 kilograms to geostationary transfer orbit (GTO).

Since its first successful flight in December 2021 — following an initial failure on its maiden voyage in March 2020 — the Long March 7A has established a strong track record. With this latest success, the vehicle has now completed 15 successful launches out of 16 attempts, cementing its reputation as a reliable workhorse for geostationary orbit missions.

The rocket employs a distinctive design: four liquid-fueled boosters powered by YF-100 engines, a first stage with two YF-100 engines, a second stage with four YF-115 engines, and a third stage using two YF-75 hydrolox engines inherited from the Long March 3B family.

## Satellite Capabilities and Purpose

The Communications Technology Test Satellite 26A belongs to China's ongoing series of experimental communications satellites designed to validate new technologies in space. The "26A" designation suggests this is the 26th satellite in the series, with the "A" suffix possibly indicating a specific variant or model iteration.

According to Xinhua, the satellite will support multiple service types including satellite communications, broadcasting, television, and data transmission. Crucially, it will also conduct technology test verifications — experimental procedures that, if successful, could be incorporated into future operational satellite designs.

These test satellites play a vital role in China's broader strategy to develop advanced communications infrastructure, including efforts to improve rural connectivity and lay groundwork for next-generation networks.

## Strategic Significance

This launch carries implications beyond a single mission success. It demonstrates China's continued investment in space technology and its ability to regularly deploy sophisticated payloads for communications technology development.

The Long March 7A's growing reliability record is particularly significant for China's space program. As a vehicle optimized for GTO missions, it is well-suited for launching communications satellites that serve both domestic needs and potential international customers.

Furthermore, the launch frequency of the Long March family — now at 653 missions — reflects a space program operating at an increasingly ambitious tempo. China's space launch cadence has accelerated considerably in recent years, with analysts estimating approximately 80 to 100 launches annually across the Long March family and emerging commercial rocket platforms.

## What's Next

With the satellite now in orbit, attention will turn to the technology verification phase, during which engineers will test the satellite's communications systems and data transmission capabilities. The results of these tests could inform the design of future operational satellites in China's communications constellation.

As China continues to expand its space capabilities, missions like this one serve as building blocks for more ambitious projects, including potential expansion of satellite internet constellations and deep-space communications infrastructure. The successful deployment of Satellite 26A adds another data point to China's steady trajectory of aerospace advancement.
