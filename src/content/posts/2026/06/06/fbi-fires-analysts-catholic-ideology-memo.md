# FBI Fires Analysts Tied to Controversial Catholic Ideology Memo

FBI Director Kash Patel fired at least five intelligence analysts on Friday for their involvement in a disputed 2023 internal memo that characterized traditionalist Catholic beliefs as a potential source of extremism, according to [AP News](https://apnews.com/article/fbi-kash-patel-firings-e9793d06e6310bfcd848b55bf8c47cc6). The terminations mark a significant escalation from the previous disciplinary actions taken under former Director Christopher Wray and represent the latest personnel shake-up under Patel's leadership.

The fired employees — four intelligence analysts and one supervisory analyst — worked in the FBI's Richmond, Virginia field office, where the memo originated. Their lawyer, David Laufman, a former Justice Department official, called the firings "manifestly unjust" and said they "subvert standard FBI policy and procedure."

## Background of the Controversial Memo

The 11-page intelligence assessment, produced on January 23, 2023, was titled "Interest of Racially or Ethnically Motivated Violent Extremists in Radical-Traditionalist Catholic Ideology Almost Certainly Presents New Mitigation Opportunities." It defined "radical traditionalist Catholics" (RTCs) as those characterized by rejection of the Second Vatican Council, disdain for modern popes, and adherence to anti-Semitic, anti-immigrant, anti-LGBTQ, and white supremacist ideology.

The memo stemmed from a domestic terrorism investigation of Xavier Lopez, a Virginia man arrested in November 2022 with Molotov cocktails, smoke bombs, and firearms components who described himself as a "radical traditional Catholic Clerical Fascist." Lopez pleaded guilty to federal weapons charges in March 2024.

## Investigations Found Analytical Failures, Not Bias

The memo was leaked to the public in February 2023 by former FBI Special Agent Kyle Seraphin, sparking immediate backlash. Then-FBI Director Christopher Wray said he was "aghast" and ordered the document withdrawn, while then-Attorney General Merrick Garland called it "appalling."

A 2024 review by the Justice Department's Inspector General found "no evidence of malicious intent or anti-Catholic bias" in the memo's creation, according to the [DOJ Inspector General report](https://oig.justice.gov/sites/default/files/2024-04/4-18-2024-letter.pdf). However, the investigation concluded the memo "failed to adhere to analytic tradecraft standards" and "improperly conflated the religious beliefs of activists with the likelihood they would engage in domestic terrorism."

Under Wray's leadership, all employees involved were formally admonished with notations in their annual performance reviews, but no terminations occurred. The FBI also implemented policy changes, tightening approval requirements for intelligence products discussing religious matters.

## Patel's Escalation

Since becoming FBI director in 2025, Patel — a Trump loyalist who has pushed out dozens of employees perceived as not aligned with the administration's agenda — has taken a harder line. In September 2025, he testified that there had been "terminations" and "resignations" related to the memo, though specific numbers were not disclosed at the time.

Shortly after Friday's firings, Patel posted on social media: "This FBI will never infringe on religious freedom," as [MS NOW reported](https://www.ms.now/news/fbi-fires-analysts-behind-controversial-memo-on-radical-catholic-ideology).

The firings are part of a broader personnel purge under Patel, who has also fired counterintelligence agents involved in investigations of President Donald Trump. A so-called "FBI Support Network" has formed among current and former agents, signaling widespread fallout from his leadership.

## Political Reactions and Implications

The controversy has long been a flashpoint in Washington. House Republicans released a 2023 report titled "The FBI's breach of religious freedom: The weaponization of law enforcement against Catholic Americans," while the [Wikipedia article on the memo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FBI_Richmond_Catholic_memo) documents extensive congressional investigations led by House Judiciary Committee Chairman Jim Jordan.

Catholic organizations, including the United States Conference of Catholic Bishops, condemned the memo while supporting legitimate counterterrorism efforts. Bishop Barry Knestout of Richmond called the memo "troubling and offensive."

The terminations raise significant questions about the politicization of the FBI. Critics argue the firings represent a purge of career professionals for political purposes, while supporters view them as necessary accountability for an agency that overstepped its bounds. David Laufman has indicated the fired analysts may pursue legal action, suggesting the controversy is far from over.

## What's Next

The fired analysts could challenge their terminations through legal channels, potentially testing the limits of Patel's authority. Congress may also investigate the firings themselves, adding another layer to the ongoing debate over the FBI's independence and accountability. The case underscores the enduring tension between religious freedom protections and national security investigations — a balance that remains as contested as ever.
