# China's Y-20 Transport Aircraft Marks 10 Years of Service

China's domestically developed Y-20 large military transport aircraft, affectionately nicknamed "Fat Girl" (胖妞), is marking its 10th anniversary of service with the People's Liberation Army Air Force (PLAAF) this July. Since its commissioning on July 6, 2016, the Y-20 has evolved from a single transport platform into a family of aircraft spanning the Y-20A, Y-20B, and YU-20A aerial refueling tanker variants, with flight tracks reaching over 40 countries across four continents, according to [Xinhua News](https://www.news.cn/politics/20260628/15355321fa1b42bf93f14fd6bdc30f3b/c.html).

## From 'Air Gym' to Digital Cockpit

Before the Y-20's arrival, China's airlift capabilities relied heavily on the turboprop Y-7, Y-8, and Y-9 aircraft, supplemented by a limited fleet of imported Il-76 transports. The transition to the Y-20 represented a generational leap from mechanical to digital aviation.

"We used to call the Y-8 an 'air gym'!" recalled air mechanic Wei Peng, a 27-year veteran who was among the first to convert to the Y-20. "Pure mechanical operation — after one flight we'd often be drenched in sweat."

The Y-20 introduced a fully glass cockpit with highly integrated avionics, consolidating flight parameters, navigation data, engine status, and warning systems onto large display screens. Pilot Yuan Bo described the experience as "a visual shock — not just of 'bigness,' but of technological transformation." The aircraft's fly-by-wire control system, a first for Chinese transport aircraft, delivers precise handling that pilots describe as "silky smooth."

## A Decade of Global Reach

Over the past ten years, the Y-20 has demonstrated its strategic value through a series of high-profile missions. In February 2020, Y-20s led a fleet of 11 aircraft delivering 2,600 military medical personnel and supplies to Wuhan during the COVID-19 pandemic — the aircraft's first non-combat mission. In 2022, six Y-20s conducted a landmark strategic airlift to Serbia, delivering FK-3 surface-to-air missile systems, while other aircraft delivered humanitarian aid to Tonga following a volcanic eruption and to Afghanistan after a devastating earthquake.

The aircraft has also played a solemn role in national remembrance, repatriating the remains of Chinese soldiers killed in the Korean War. "Today, we fly the Kunpeng across the Yalu River to bring our heroes home with the highest honors."

## The Y-20B Era

The most significant advancement came with the Y-20B variant, powered by four indigenous Shenyang WS-20 high-bypass turbofan engines. Entering service in late 2024, the Y-20B achieves a maximum takeoff weight of 220 tons, a payload capacity of 66 tons, and an extended range of approximately 10,290 kilometers — putting it in the same class as strategic airlifters like the Boeing C-17 Globemaster III.

On June 28, 2026, just days before the official anniversary, 10 Y-20B aircraft conducted an "elephant walk" formation — the largest public display of the type to date — signaling the PLAAF's accelerating strategic airlift capabilities. Production is estimated at 40-50 aircraft per year, with total deliveries exceeding 100 units, making the Y-20B the only large military transport aircraft in active high-rate production globally, as the US C-17 production line closed in 2015.

## A Family of Variants

The Y-20 has spawned multiple variants serving different roles. The YU-20A (also designated YY-20) aerial refueling tanker entered service in 2022, capable of carrying approximately 90 tons of fuel and extending the range of fighter aircraft like the Chengdu J-20. An airborne early warning and control variant, reportedly designated KJ-3000, was spotted flight testing in December 2024, suggesting the Y-20 platform will continue to expand its mission profile.

## Generational Shift

The anniversary also highlights a changing of the guard within the PLAAF. Young pilot Liu Jiajun, born in 2001 and graduated from aviation university just two years ago, has already flown nine overseas missions across six countries. Asked to describe the Y-20 in three words, he replied: "Advanced. Enormous. Future."

Chief Designer Tang Changhong, an academician of the Chinese Academy of Engineering who led the Y-20 program from its inception, reflected on the journey: "Ten years ago, with anxiety and barely contained excitement, I sent 'Fat Girl' to the troops. I imagined a fleet of Y-20s soaring through the skies — how magnificent that would be. You did it. We did it. The motherland did it."

## What's Next

As the Y-20 enters its second decade of service, the focus is on deepening operational capabilities. The PLAAF is expected to continue expanding its Y-20 fleet, with analysts projecting over 100 transport aircraft and 75 tanker variants by 2032. Chief Designer Tang Changhong's parting words to the troops captured the spirit of the next phase: "Fly better, fly steadier, fly higher, fly farther."

The Y-20's journey from a single prototype to a multi-role strategic platform mirrors China's broader ambitions in aerospace and defense — a transition from regional territorial defense to global power projection, one mission at a time.
