# Fujian Shoe Factory Fire Kills 28; Investigation Launched

A catastrophic fire at a shoe factory in Jinjiang, Fujian Province, has claimed 28 lives, prompting provincial authorities to establish an investigation team and detain company executives as questions mount over workplace safety failures. The blaze broke out around noon on July 9 at Fujian Huiteng Shoe Industry Co., Ltd. in Chendai Town, a densely packed manufacturing hub known as "China's Shoe Capital."

## The Fire and Rescue Effort

The fire began shortly before midday on the first-floor stamping workshop, where highly flammable materials such as shoe fabrics, sponges, adhesives, and cardboard were stored. According to [CCTV News](https://news.cctv.com/2026/07/10/ARTI6RVWPg3AjKSvsQAqJCfJ260710.shtml), the Quanzhou Fire Rescue Command Center received the alarm at approximately noon and dispatched 183 firefighters with 35 fire trucks, supported by helicopter aerial surveillance. Despite the rapid response, visible flames were not largely extinguished until late afternoon.

At the time of the fire, 237 employees and two external visitors were inside the five-story reinforced concrete building. Rescue teams successfully evacuated 213 people, but two later died in hospital, and 26 others were found deceased during search operations. Firefighters reported that corridors piled with debris and shoe materials significantly hampered rescue efforts, according to the [China News Service](http://www.fj.chinanews.com.cn/news/2026/2026-07-10/587350.html).

## Official Response and Accountability

President Xi Jinping issued instructions on the evening of July 9, demanding a thorough search and rescue operation, a swift investigation into the cause, and legal accountability, as reported by [Xinhua News Agency](https://www1.xinhuanet.com/politics/20260711/a2b5e8f4e922495498549b3f271b0227/c.html). Premier Li Qiang also issued directives on the incident.

On July 10, the Fujian Provincial Government announced the formation of an official investigation team. At a press conference that morning, Quanzhou Mayor Cai Zhansheng publicly apologized to the victims' families and society, vowing to fully investigate and hold those responsible to account, according to [Lianhe Zaobao](https://www.zaobao.com.sg/news/china/story20260710-9343565). Company executives have been detained, and the factory's accounts have been frozen.

## Safety Violations and Systemic Failures

Disturbing details have emerged about safety conditions at the factory. Just two days before the fire, on July 7, a routine fire safety inspection identified two hazards: debris blocking the first-floor corridor and an electric fan placed near an electrical distribution panel. These violations were not rectified in time.

The [BBC Chinese](https://www.bbc.com/zhongwen/articles/cdr47vzk81no/trad) reported that workers on the fourth-floor sewing workshop, who were paid on a piece-rate basis, remained at their stations during the lunch break to earn more, leaving them trapped when the fire spread rapidly. A village official told The Beijing News that "some people wanted to do more work, so they didn't rush to clock out."

Firefighters noted that the factory's roof, typically considered a safe refuge, was cluttered with shoe lasts and other materials, with a hole allowing smoke to rise from below, creating deadly conditions for those who sought shelter there.

## Broader Implications

The tragedy has cast a spotlight on persistent workplace safety issues in China's manufacturing sector, particularly in the shoe industry where flammable materials, blocked exits, and piece-rate wage systems create dangerous conditions. Chendai Town, home to over 7,000 shoe enterprises producing more than one billion pairs of sports shoes annually, has experienced multiple factory fires in recent years.

Questions remain about compensation for victims' families. According to [QQ News](https://news.qq.com/rain/a/20260710A07TT300), only 12 of the factory's 200-plus employees were registered for social insurance, raising concerns about financial support for the families of the deceased. The investigation is ongoing, with authorities promising a thorough accounting of the incident and potential regulatory reforms to prevent future tragedies.

## What's Next

The Fujian Provincial investigation team is expected to release its findings in the coming weeks. Meanwhile, Quanzhou and Jinjiang authorities have launched city-wide safety inspections targeting similar manufacturing facilities. The case has drawn comparisons to the November 2025 Hong Kong Wang Fuk Court fire that killed 168 people, which prompted a nationwide high-rise fire prevention campaign. Observers will be watching closely to see whether this latest disaster leads to meaningful regulatory change in China's industrial safety framework.
