# Hawaii Raises Truck Bed-Riding Age to 16 as Roads Get Faster

Hawaii has raised the minimum legal age for riding in pickup truck beds from 12 to 16, signing into law a measure that balances growing safety concerns with a deeply rooted island tradition, [AP News reported](https://apnews.com/article/truck-beds-riding-hawaii-law-b658910e78c9d44a99b0315ac1cdea45). The law, signed by Democratic Gov. Josh Green in June and effective July 2026, comes as Hawaii's roads have become faster and more urbanized, particularly on Oahu. The change represents a carefully calibrated compromise between an outright ban — which many residents have long resisted as an attack on local identity — and maintaining the status quo, which safety advocates say has led to preventable deaths and injuries.

## A Tradition Rooted in Plantation History

Riding in pickup truck beds is woven into the fabric of Hawaii's local identity. The practice traces back to the plantation era, when workers from the Philippines, China, Puerto Rico, Portugal, Japan and other countries piled into truck beds to navigate sugar and pineapple fields. Over generations, what began as a necessity became a cultural hallmark.

"It's not Hawaiian culture. It's Hawaii culture," said Kimeona Kane, a Native Hawaiian and Waimanalo resident who grew up riding in truck beds to get to baseball and football practice. "It takes us back to even the plantation days."

In modern Hawaii, the practice remains common in rural areas, where many families rely on a single pickup truck as their primary vehicle. A popular cover of John Denver's "Take Me Home, Country Roads" has become an island anthem, romanticizing the image of riding through coastal highways fully exposed to the sun and saltwater air.

## What the New Law Does

The new age limit was included in a broader motorist safety bill (HB 1692) that also significantly expanded Hawaii's move-over law. Under the truck bed provisions, those over 16 may ride in the bed only if there are no available seats in the truck's cab, and the tailgate and side racks are securely closed. Passengers must be seated on the floor and may not attempt to unlash cargo.

The law includes exemptions for life-threatening emergencies, parades, employees on duty, farm workers, first responders, and military personnel.

Separately, the expanded move-over law now requires drivers to slow down and change lanes for any stationary vehicle displaying hazard lights or warning signals — previously this only applied to emergency vehicles. Hawaii Department of Transportation Director Ed Sniffen noted that three people were killed in the past year after being hit by vehicles while stopped on the side of the road, [KHON2 reported](https://www.yahoo.com/news/us/articles/hawaii-raises-truck-bed-age-072406712.html).

## The Push for Balance

State Rep. Darius Kila, who chairs the House Transportation Committee and sponsored the bill, said he initially sought more restrictions, including banning truck bed riding on certain highways. But past attempts to restrict the practice faced fierce opposition.

"Any time that legislation has been proposed of restricting or removing it, people have viciously fought back because I think for identity and place, that's something they associate with Hawaii," Kila said.

The compromise — raising the age rather than an outright ban — was intentional. "Somebody who's 16 is a little bit older and maybe has a little bit more discretion of safety," Kila said.

Gov. Green, a physician, brought personal experience to the issue. In 2023, he was traveling behind a pickup when a 25-year-old man was ejected from the truck bed. Green stopped to render medical aid and later stated: "I would prefer that nobody ride in a truck bed, because people need to be belted in."

## A National Context

Hawaii joins a growing number of states tightening restrictions on truck bed riding. According to the [Insurance Institute for Highway Safety](https://www.iihs.org/research-areas/seat-belts/cargo-area-restrictions-laws), 29 states and Washington D.C. have some restrictions on riding in pickup truck beds, though laws vary widely. Hawaii's new 16-year-old minimum aligns it with states like Connecticut, Maryland, North Carolina, Ohio, Rhode Island, and Virginia.

David Kidd, vice president of vehicle research at IIHS, likened resistance to regulating truck bed riding to the historical opposition faced by seat belt and motorcycle helmet laws. "Riding in truck beds is extremely dangerous because they lack any safety mechanisms," he said.

## Voices from Both Sides

Reaction to the law reflects the tension between safety and tradition. Richard Landford Jr., an 80-year-old lifelong Waianae resident who remembers skipping school to hitch rides in farmers' pickups, said he still believes truck beds should remain an option for transporting passengers. "You drive to almost any church out here in the rural area, you'll see people in the back of trucks," he said. "I wish there was a simple answer."

Dean Okimoto, chief of operations for Nalo Farms, takes a harder line. His farm has already switched to passenger vans with seat belts. "I wouldn't chance putting people in an open truck," he said. "They should ban it completely."

## What's Next

Kila predicted that Hawaii will eventually ban truck bed riding altogether. "And it will probably be met with resistance, and then 20 years later after the fact, it will just be like, wow, we should have done that sooner," he said.

For now, the new law represents a step forward for safety advocates while preserving a practice that many in Hawaii consider inseparable from island life. How the law is enforced — particularly in rural areas where truck bed riding remains most common — and whether public education campaigns accompany the change remain open questions as the Aloha State navigates the delicate balance between protecting its people and preserving its culture.
