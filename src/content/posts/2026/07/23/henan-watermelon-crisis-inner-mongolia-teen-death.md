# Henan Watermelon Crisis and Inner Mongolia Teen Death Case

Two unrelated but significant societal stories are drawing attention across China this week: a deepening agricultural crisis in Henan Province where watermelon farmers face ruin from oversupply, and the release of an official police investigation into the death of a 16-year-old in Inner Mongolia that has sparked nationwide debate about vigilantism and proportionality of force.

## Xuchang Watermelon Farmers Plead for Help

In JinYing Village, Jian'an District, Xuchang, Henan Province, two farming households are urgently seeking buyers for approximately 140,000 jin (70 tons) of unsold watermelons. The farmers, one of whom is a first-time watermelon grower, have watched their crop reach 95% maturity with sugar content of 12.5 to 12.6 degrees, leaving a sales window of at most one week before the fruit becomes susceptible to cracking and rotting, according to [The Paper](https://www.thepaper.cn/newsDetail_forward_33637251).

"If it rains, the watermelons might burst open," Mr. Jin, a truck driver whose relatives are the affected farmers, told The Paper. "The sales window for this batch is at most about one week."

The farmers planted approximately 40 mu (about 6.6 acres) of the "Shuanghong Jinchengwu" watermelon variety, with production costs of approximately 1,060 yuan per mu. The field purchase price has fallen to just 0.2 yuan per jin (approximately $0.03 USD per pound), far below the break-even point.

"This is a loss-making business, barely able to recover part of the cost or reduce losses, unable to cover labor costs and inputs," Mr. Jin explained.

Farmer Mr. Cao added that if the watermelons cannot be sold before August, the land cannot be tilled for the next crop, missing the planting window entirely.

### A Province-Wide Crisis

The Xuchang farmers' plight is part of a much larger crisis. Henan is China's largest watermelon-producing province, with 3.7 million mu planted in 2026 and an estimated output of 14 million tons. Since July, watermelons have been widely unsold across the province, with field prices plummeting to between 0.1 and 0.3 yuan per jin, as [Sina Finance](https://cj.sina.com.cn/articles/view/7880068208/1d5b04c7006801l0mu) reported.

The crash stems from multiple factors: expanded planting area driven by good prices in previous years, early maturation due to higher temperatures, greenhouse and open-field watermelons ripening simultaneously, and multiple northern provinces hitting peak production at the same time. On the demand side, extreme weather including hail and thunderstorms damaged fruit quality, while continuous heavy rain in southern China suppressed consumer demand.

### Emergency Response and Civil Aid

In response, agricultural and urban management departments have set up over 1,000 temporary sales points and launched a "Watermelon Map" app to connect farmers with buyers. Cross-province aid efforts have shown remarkable results: a Guangzhou team sold approximately 550,000 jin in 20 days, a Hangzhou team cleared 100 tons in 7 hours, and Beijing street vendors moved 18 tons in 1.5 days at 0.8 yuan per jin. Tourist attractions including Luoyang Chongdugou and Kaifeng Qingming Riverside Park have bulk-purchased watermelons for free distribution to visitors.

However, the farmers face a critical constraint: due to the high ripeness of their crop, long-distance transport is not feasible, limiting their sales to Xuchang and surrounding areas. They are currently selling to local supermarkets and retailing by the roadside in Zhengzhou, transporting about 10,000 jin on July 22 alone.

## Inner Mongolia Police Release Teen Death Investigation Findings

In a separate development, police in Urad Front Banner, Bayannur City, Inner Mongolia, have released a detailed investigation report on the death of a 16-year-old male, Zhang Ruifeng, a high school student and sports champion, addressing widespread online speculation and rumors, as reported by [Xinhua News](https://www.news.cn/local/20260722/589aa15338104db991a22f3ade6bf65b/c.html).

The incident occurred in the early hours of May 24, 2026. According to the police report, the victim and nine companions went to a residential area believing there was prostitution activity there. They engaged in provocative behavior including pressing electric bicycle horns, whistling, and kicking doors.

The suspect, identified as Zhang, a 36-year-old unemployed local resident, was temporarily staying at a friend's first-floor apartment approximately 50 meters from the house the victim kicked. A confrontation ensued, and the victim was stabbed once in the lung, leading to his death.

The suspect was criminally detained on May 24, approved for arrest on June 5, and transferred for prosecution on July 3, 2026.

### Clarifying Rumors

The police investigation explicitly addressed two major rumors that had circulated online. First, the incident location is not a sex-work venue — it was a long-vacant bungalow in a residential area. Second, the suspect is not a security guard; he was unemployed and staying at a friend's place. Police noted that there had been prostitution-related activity in the area historically, with a case investigated in May 2024 that resulted in two arrests, and the area has been under regular police surveillance.

The victim's mother, Ms. Shi, acknowledged her son's behavior was wrong but stated, "The mistake does not warrant death" (错不致死), as reported by China News Weekly. Her statement has resonated widely online, sparking intense debate about proportionality of force, vigilantism, and the ethics of what locals call "disturbing prostitution" (扰嫖) — a phenomenon where citizens harass suspected prostitution locations.

### Broader Implications

The watermelon crisis highlights persistent structural challenges in Chinese agriculture, particularly the vulnerability of smallholder farmers who lack market intelligence, storage infrastructure, and bargaining power. The phenomenon of "bumper harvests but no income" (增产不增收) remains a critical issue, with climate variability adding further uncertainty.

The Inner Mongolia case, meanwhile, has ignited public discourse on multiple sensitive fronts: the boundaries of self-defense, the dangers of vigilante justice, and the ethical complexities surrounding informal social enforcement. Legal experts suggest the case could influence how similar incidents involving "disturbing prostitution" are treated in the future.

## What to Watch For

For the Xuchang farmers, the coming week is critical. Whether the remaining 70 tons of watermelons can be sold before the crop is lost will depend on continued government support and civil society mobilization. The broader question remains whether this crisis will prompt structural reforms in agricultural market regulation.
In the Inner Mongolia case, all eyes are on the forthcoming prosecution and trial, which will test how Chinese courts balance public outrage, the rights of the victim, and the legal boundaries of self-defense in a case that has captured the nation's attention.
