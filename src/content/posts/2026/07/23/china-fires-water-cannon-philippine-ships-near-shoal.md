# China Fires Water Cannon at Philippine Ships Near Shoal

The China Coast Guard (CCG) fired a water cannon at Philippine government vessels near Scarborough Shoal (Huangyan Island) on Thursday, the latest in a series of escalating confrontations in the South China Sea that has raised fears of a broader regional crisis.

According to [Rappler](https://www.rappler.com/philippines/china-scarborough-shoal-encounter-july-23-2026/), the Philippine Coast Guard (PCG) reported that a CCG vessel began sailing close to the Bureau of Fisheries and Aquatic Resources (BFAR) ship BRP Datu Dumangsil in the early morning, while another Chinese vessel conducted dangerous maneuvers near the BRP Datu Paiburong. Approximately 30 minutes later, a CCG ship fired a water cannon at the Datu Dumangsil for about two minutes, though the vessel was not hit.

Both Philippine ships were on a humanitarian mission delivering fuel subsidies to Filipino fisherfolk in the area. The PCG confirmed that despite the confrontation, both vessels completed their mission without injuries or damage.

## Competing Narratives

The incident has produced sharply contrasting accounts from Beijing and Manila. The [Xinhua News Agency](https://www.news.cn/world/20260723/0127d2fb8a514aa2a82def8bfda86ec5/c.html) published a statement from CCG Spokesperson Jiang Lue, who said that Philippine vessels 3012 and 3018 "ignored repeated dissuasions and warnings" and "deliberately intruded into China's jurisdictional waters around Huangyan Island." Jiang described the CCG's response as "professional, standardized, and lawful," reaffirming that "Huangyan Island is China's inherent territory."

By contrast, the Philippine Coast Guard characterized the Chinese actions as disproportionate, noting that the vessels were on a routine supply mission within the Philippines' exclusive economic zone. The PCG said the CCG used "dangerous maneuvers" including crossing 500 meters across the bow of the Datu Paiburong.

## Diplomatic Context

Thursday's confrontation unfolded against a backdrop of heightened diplomatic tension. Just one day earlier, Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi met with Philippine Foreign Secretary Ma. Theresa Lazaro on the sidelines of the ASEAN Foreign Ministers' Meeting in Manila. According to the [South China Morning Post](https://www.scmp.com/news/china/diplomacy/article/3361523/after-latest-clash-sea-china-warns-philippines-bitter-consequences), Wang Yi lodged a "strong protest" over a July 20 incident at Ayungin (Second Thomas) Shoal, where CCG personnel boarded Philippine Navy rubber dinghies and struck a Filipino sailor on the head with a wooden baton. Wang warned that the Philippines would "bear the bitter consequences" if it provoked Beijing with the support of outside powers.

US Secretary of State Marco Rubio also held talks with Wang Yi in Manila during the ASEAN meetings, underscoring the growing involvement of external powers in the dispute.

## Escalating Pattern

The July 23 Scarborough Shoal incident marks the third major confrontation in the South China Sea within a week. The July 20 Ayungin Shoal clash, which left a Filipino sailor injured, drew condemnation from the United States and Australia. The Philippines summoned China's ambassador in response.

Scarborough Shoal, located approximately 220 kilometers west of the Philippine island of Luzon, has been under effective Chinese control since a tense standoff in 2012. The 2016 Permanent Court of Arbitration ruling — which China rejects — determined the shoal to be a traditional fishing ground shared by Filipino, Chinese, and Vietnamese fisherfolk without assigning sovereignty.

## Analysis and Implications

The rapid succession of incidents — Ayungin on July 20, the ASEAN diplomatic exchanges on July 22, and Scarborough on July 23 — suggests either deliberate coordination or rapidly deteriorating conditions in the contested waterway. Analysts point to an escalation pattern that has moved from blocking maneuvers to water cannon use and, most recently, to physical confrontations resulting in injury.

The Trump administration has assured Philippine President Ferdinand Marcos Jr. that Manila's South China Sea concerns would be "taken up" with Beijing, signaling continued US commitment under the Mutual Defense Treaty. However, the treaty's applicability to disputed maritime features remains a subject of legal debate.

## What to Watch

With ASEAN foreign ministers still meeting in Manila, the incidents may test the bloc's ability to maintain a unified position on the South China Sea. The long-stalled Code of Conduct negotiations between China and ASEAN face renewed pressure, while questions remain about what structures China is reportedly building on Scarborough Shoal and whether the Philippines will adjust its resupply strategy for the Sierra Madre garrison at Ayungin Shoal following the July 20 injury.

For now, the competing narratives from Beijing and Manila leave little common ground — and the waters of the South China Sea grow more contested by the day.
