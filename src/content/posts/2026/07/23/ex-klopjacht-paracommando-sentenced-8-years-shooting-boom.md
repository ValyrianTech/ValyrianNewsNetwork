# Ex-Klopjacht Paracommando Gets 8 Years for Boom Shooting

The Antwerp Criminal Court has sentenced Yahya D. (30), a former paracommando who appeared on the popular Flemish television program *Klopjacht* (Manhunt), and his accomplice Simon J. (20) to eight years in prison for attempted murder. The verdict, delivered on July 23, 2026, brings a measure of closure to a case that shocked the community of Boom and drew widespread media attention due to the defendant’s television fame.

According to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/07/23/klopjacht-ex-paracommando-moord-cel-boom/), the court followed the prosecution’s demand for both men, finding them guilty of attempted murder in connection with a shooting that took place on August 1, 2024, in the Gevaertlaan neighborhood of Boom.

## The Incident

On the evening of August 1, 2024, victim Ali T. (32) was shot in the chest at his doorstep in Boom. The bullet passed completely through his body. The court noted that his survival was “pure coincidence.” Ali T., who had prior convictions for drug trafficking, had told his wife shortly before the shooting that he was “in very bad papers.”

One hour after the shooting, police stopped Yahya D. in Wilrijk while he was driving a white van used in the attack. Gunshot residue was found on his hands, chest, and face, though forensic experts determined the traces were too small to identify him as the shooter—only that he was in close proximity to the incident.

Simon J. was arrested in February 2026 after his DNA was found on a discarded blue medical glove near the crime scene. No gunshot residue was detected on the glove after two separate analyses.

## The Court’s Reasoning

The judges emphasized that the intent to kill was evident from the use of a firearm. As [HLN](https://www.hln.be/antwerpen/klopjacht-kandidaat-30-kent-zijn-straf-voor-moordpoging-op-drugscrimineel-in-boom-hij-belde-om-te-vragen-of-er-nog-jobkes-waren~a62ec0d3/) reported, the presiding judge stated: “The intent to kill is evident from the use of a firearm. That the bullet did not pierce the victim’s chest appears to be pure coincidence. These facts clearly fit within a drug-related criminal organization that does not shy away from intimidation, threats, and violence.”

The court did not accept the defense’s claim that the two men first attempted to kidnap the victim before shooting.

## Defense Arguments

Both defendants attempted to minimize their roles. Yahya D.’s lawyer, Cédric Monheim, argued that his client acted under duress, claiming the order-giver threatened him and his family. He requested acquittal or reclassification to attempted kidnapping with a discharged weapon, with a maximum sentence of five years with probation.

Simon J.’s lawyer, Christian Clement, argued that his client could not be the shooter since no gunshot residue was found on the gloves. He pointed to Yahya D. as the initiator, noting that Yahya D. sent a message asking if there were “any jobs” and deleted incriminating chat messages during his flight.

“Yahya D. sent my client a message asking if there were any jobs,” Clement told the court, as quoted by HLN. “He had financial stress and deleted all incriminating chat messages during his flight.”

## The Victim’s Condition

Ali T. survived the shooting but remains deeply affected. His attorney, Brecht Pyppe, described his condition: “Physically he has recovered well, but mentally he is incapacitated. He regularly suffers panic attacks.” Pyppe requested further examination for permanent injuries.

## The Third Person Mystery

It remains unclear whether a third person was present during the shooting. Simon J. refused to provide information, citing threats to his family. “Excuse me, I cannot give you names because I am being threatened in prison,” he told the court, as reported by HLN. “People send me the addresses of my family. I cannot talk.”

## Reality TV Meets Real Crime

The case attracted significant media attention because of Yahya D.’s participation in *Klopjacht*, a Belgian television program based on the British format “Hunted,” broadcast on Play since 2021. According to [Wikipedia](https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klopjacht_(televisieprogramma)), the show features ordinary citizens who must evade a team of trackers from police, military, and private sectors for 400 hours (17 days).

Season 4 of *Klopjacht*, recorded in May 2024, was postponed for nearly two years after Yahya D. became involved in the criminal investigation. The channel ultimately decided to air the season in full from April 6 to 16, 2026, with the consent of the other participants. The irony was not lost on the media: a man who demonstrated his skills as a paracommando and freerunner on television was now convicted for a real-life violent crime.

## Analysis and Implications

The case highlights the intersection of reality television and real criminality, raising questions about the glorification of evasion skills in entertainment media. The Antwerp region, particularly its port area, has been a significant hub for drug trafficking in Europe, and drug-related violence has been a recurring issue. This verdict sends a clear message that the courts will treat such violence with the full weight of the law.

As [Belga News Agency](https://www.msn.com/nl-be/nieuws/other/rechtbank-antwerpen-ex-paracommando-en-kompaan-krijgen-acht-jaar-cel-voor-poging-tot-moord/ar-AA28vNNH) reported, the court found that the crime was part of a drug-related criminal organization that does not hesitate to use intimidation, threats, and violence.

## What’s Next

It is not yet known whether either defendant will appeal the verdict. The question of who ordered the attack remains unanswered, as does the possible involvement of a third person. For the victim, Ali T., the physical wounds have healed, but the psychological scars remain. The case serves as a stark reminder that the skills showcased on reality television can have dangerous real-world applications.
