# Zelensky Fires Army Chief After Protests Over Defense Shakeup

Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky has fired his Commander-in-Chief General Oleksandr Syrsky and offered a "prominent position" to the popular Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov, whom he had dismissed just days earlier, in a dramatic reshuffle that has triggered the largest political crisis in Ukraine since Russia's full-scale invasion began in 2022. The shakeup, which unfolded over ten days in July 2026, has sparked nationwide protests, exposed a bitter generational divide within Ukraine's military leadership, and raised urgent questions about the country's wartime strategy and political direction.

## The Reshuffle Unfolds

The crisis began on July 12, when Zelensky announced a broader government overhaul, forcing Prime Minister Yulia Svyrydenko to resign and nominating Sergii Koretskyi, head of the state energy giant Naftogaz, as her replacement, according to [RTBF](https://www.rtbf.be/article/ukraine-les-dessous-d-un-remaniement-douloureux-a-la-defense-pourquoi-volodymyr-zelensky-a-t-il-limoge-son-ministre-de-la-defense-avant-de-se-debarrasser-de-son-chef-d-etat-major-11761185). By July 14, the entire government had resigned, setting the stage for a more consequential confrontation.

On July 15, Zelensky dismissed Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov, a 35-year-old reformist technocrat widely credited with revolutionizing Ukraine's drone warfare capabilities and modernizing defense procurement. Fedorov confirmed his dismissal on social media, listing 22 major accomplishments during his tenure. The move triggered immediate and widespread backlash.

## Nationwide Protests

Within hours, thousands of Ukrainians took to the streets in Kyiv, Kharkiv, Lviv, and Odesa, chanting "Bring back Fedorov!" and demanding Syrsky's resignation. The protests, described by [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2026/jul/21/zelenskyy-replaces-military-leader-and-brings-back-defence-minister-he-sacked) as one of the biggest political crises since the 2022 invasion, continued for over a week. Protesters gave a deadline for Syrsky's removal and promised "indefinite protests" if their demands were ignored.

Fedorov, who had served as Minister of Digital Transformation before taking the defense portfolio, publicly accused Syrsky of blocking military reforms. He revealed that he had proposed dismissing both Syrsky and Chief of General Staff Andrii Hnatov before his own dismissal — a request Zelensky refused.

## The Reformist vs. Soviet-Era Divide

At the heart of the crisis lies a fundamental conflict between two visions for Ukraine's military. Fedorov represented a new generation of technology-driven, transparent, NATO-standard reform. He introduced open tenders for defense procurement, strict audits, and stopped automatically approving General Staff requests — saving over $100 million on 155mm shells alone, according to RTBF. He also famously blocked Russian military access to Starlink satellite internet and intensified drone strikes on Russian oil infrastructure.

Syrsky, 60, was born in Russia and trained in Moscow. Nicknamed "the butcher" for his rigid Soviet-era command methods, he was credited with leading the defense of Kyiv in 2022 and launching the Kursk counter-invasion in 2024. But his traditional approach clashed sharply with Fedorov's modernization agenda.

As Yuriy Hudymenko, a veteran and member of Ukraine's anti-corruption council, told the [Kyiv Independent](https://kyivindependent.com/fedorov-admits-trying-to-replace-syrskyi-before-his-dismissal-as-defense-minister/): "This is a conflict between a young technocrat and a general from a largely post-Soviet military school."

Zelensky himself acknowledged the rift, saying: "They live in two different worlds. Misha wants to digitize everything... The military just wants to be heard. I can't allow the Defense Ministry and the General Staff to be at war with each other in a country that's at war."

## Syrsky's Dismissal and a New Era

On July 21, after more than a week of escalating protests, Zelensky delivered a late-night televised address announcing Syrsky's dismissal. He appointed Major General Mykhailo Drapatyi, a popular and innovative commander, as the new head of Ukraine's armed forces. Drapatyi, viewed as an ally of Fedorov and a reformer, was welcomed by protesters as "a breath of fresh air."

Zelensky also offered Fedorov a "prominent position" within the government, though it remains unclear whether the former defense minister will accept a role outside his old portfolio. Yevhen Khmara, a former special operations commander, was named as the new Defense Minister.

## Analysis and Implications

The reshuffle carries significant risks for Ukraine. Changing military command in the middle of a war is inherently destabilizing, and Russian pro-war bloggers have celebrated Fedorov's dismissal as a sign of internal discord. The Financial Times called Fedorov "the most successful Ukrainian minister of the past four and a half years," and his departure has alarmed Western partners.

EU Commissioner Andrius Kubilius expressed concern about the developments. Meanwhile, on July 22, Zelensky held calls with Trump envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner, signaling the high stakes of the political crisis as Ukraine navigates both battlefield challenges and complex diplomatic negotiations.

For Zelensky, the crisis represents a serious erosion of political capital. Critics accuse him of prioritizing his own political survival over military effectiveness, and the protests have revealed deep public attachment to reformist governance. The crisis also echoes a similar confrontation in 2025, when citizens mobilized to protect anti-corruption agencies from presidential overreach.

## What's Next

The key questions now are whether Drapatyi can succeed where Fedorov failed in implementing reforms, whether Fedorov will accept a non-defense role or become a political rival, and how the command change will affect Ukraine's battlefield performance. Civilian casualties have already increased 37% in the first half of 2026, according to the UN, adding urgency to the need for effective military leadership.

As Ukraine faces a critical phase in its war with Russia, the resolution of this leadership crisis — and the unity it restores or fails to restore — may prove as consequential as any battlefield development.
