# Cyclospora Outbreak Tied to Taylor Farms Lettuce

A massive Cyclospora outbreak linked to iceberg lettuce from Taylor Farms de Mexico has triggered a national food safety crisis, sickening thousands of Americans and sending shockwaves through the restaurant industry. As of July 23, the CDC has reported 4,173 laboratory-confirmed cases connected to a five-state outbreak cluster, with more than 7,400 additional cases still under investigation.

## The Scope of the Outbreak

The outbreak, which began in May 2026, has primarily affected Indiana, Kentucky, Michigan, Ohio, and West Virginia. Michigan has emerged as the epicenter, reporting 7,171 cases according to state health officials. Nationally, cases have been identified in 34 states, with 94 hospitalizations reported in the outbreak cluster and 308 hospitalizations nationwide, according to the [CDC](https://www.cdc.gov/cyclosporiasis/php/surveillance/index.html). No deaths have been reported.

The FDA's traceback investigation identified Taylor Farms de Mexico — a subsidiary of Taylor Fresh Foods based in Salinas, California — as the single supplier of contaminated iceberg lettuce to Taco Bell locations where ill people ate, as [AP News reported](https://apnews.com/article/cyclospora-lettuce-taco-bell-cdc-fda-13d9e9ebdc46a4d05a58da2ae8e8d0de). Patients fell ill between May 13 and July 13, 2026.

## Recalls and Industry Response

On July 17, Taylor Farms announced a voluntary recall of all iceberg lettuce sourced from central Mexico. The recalled products include Marketside-brand iceberg salads and shredded lettuce sold at Walmart stores in 28 states, with Best if Used By dates ranging from July 18 to August 3, 2026. Taco Bell removed the affected lettuce from its supply chain nationwide within 24 hours and has committed to using a different supplier.

In a statement, Taylor Farms expressed concern for those affected: "As a family owned and operated company, we are deeply concerned for those who became ill, their families, and the many Americans whose trust in the safety of their fresh produce has been shaken."

## FDA False Positive Incident

The investigation took an unusual turn when the FDA initially reported a positive Cyclospora test on a Taylor Farms lettuce sample on July 18, only to retract it the following day, calling it a false positive. Despite this, the FDA continues to consider Taylor Farms lettuce the source based on epidemiological and traceback evidence, according to [Food Safety Magazine](https://www.food-safety.com/articles/11639-taylor-farms-lettuce-named-as-source-of-us-cyclospora-outbreak).

Alex Sundermann, Dr.P.H., Assistant Professor of Epidemiology at the University of Pittsburgh, explained that the false positive does not undermine the broader investigation: "Outbreak investigations rely on a toolkit, and epidemiology and environmental sampling are two different tools that answer two different questions. The epidemiologic and traceback evidence haven't changed."

## Economic Fallout

The outbreak has had significant economic consequences. Sweetgreen shares declined more than 20% since July 10, and salad chains across the country report dramatic drops in foot traffic. Consumer confidence in lettuce and salad products has been severely shaken, with even the so-called "Salad Queen" reportedly avoiding greens.

## A Record-Breaking Year for Cyclospora

This outbreak has surpassed all previous records. Cyclospora cases in the U.S. have been rising dramatically over the past decade — from 537 cases in 2016 to approximately 4,700 in 2019, the previous record year. Experts attribute the increase to climate change, which favors the heat-loving parasite, and improved diagnostic testing.

Cyclospora cayetanensis is a microscopic parasite that causes cyclosporiasis, an intestinal illness characterized by watery diarrhea, loss of appetite, and fatigue. It is transmitted through consumption of contaminated food or water and cannot be cultured in a lab, making detection challenging.

## What Consumers Should Know

The CDC advises consumers to wash hands and fresh produce thoroughly under running water before eating. Cooking kills the parasite — heating food to 158°F (70°C) or higher is effective. Dr. Gwen Biggerstaff of the CDC told [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/07/17/nx-s1-5897509/lettuce-taco-bell-mexico-cyclospora-outbreak) that people with symptoms should stay well-hydrated and contact their healthcare providers for specific Cyclospora testing, as routine stool tests often do not include it.

## Looking Ahead

Senators have written to Health Secretary Kennedy urging the restoration of Cyclospora surveillance to the FoodNet program, highlighting strains on the national foodborne illness surveillance system. With a six-week reporting lag between illness onset and case confirmation, health officials anticipate case counts will continue to rise. The outbreak has exposed vulnerabilities in the concentrated produce supply chain and raised urgent questions about food safety protocols, testing methods, and the resources dedicated to tracking emerging foodborne pathogens.
