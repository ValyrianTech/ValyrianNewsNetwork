# Madison Police Shooting: Wisconsin Gov. Seeks Transparency After Officer Fatally Shoots Man With Knife

Wisconsin Governor Tony Evers is demanding transparency and accountability after a Madison police officer fatally shot a man armed with a knife on Wednesday afternoon, an incident that has sparked protests, reignited debates over police use of force, and highlighted the absence of body cameras in the city's police department.

The shooting occurred at approximately 1:35 p.m. CT on July 22 at the intersection of Williamson Street and S. Baldwin Street in Madison's Marquette neighborhood, according to [AP News](https://apnews.com/article/police-shooting-madison-knife-protests-listening-session-f4efad72ed0dc5cad6005bb6e4a326dd). Police had responded to reports of a man checking vehicle doors and stealing bicycles. When officers located the suspect, he fled on a bicycle through backyards before being confronted at the intersection.

## The Incident

Madison Police Chief John Patterson said at a news conference that the man either fell off or was pulled off his bicycle during the confrontation. A struggle ensued, during which the man produced a fixed-blade knife and injured an officer. Another officer deployed a Taser, but it was unsuccessful. The same officer who was injured by the knife then fired his weapon, striking the man multiple times. The man was transported to a hospital, where he was pronounced dead.

"This is not a small folding knife. It's a large fixed blade knife that was there and present," Patterson said, as reported by [Wisconsin Public Radio](https://www.wpr.org/news/shooting-police-madison-east-side-protest-concerts-on-the-square). He described the officer who fired as a veteran male officer, but his name has not been released. All four officers at the scene have been placed on administrative leave.

## No Body Cameras

None of the four officers involved were wearing body cameras. Madison police do not widely use the devices; a 90-day pilot program was conducted in 2024, and Chief Patterson has cited funding as an obstacle, calling body cameras an "essential tool" for modern policing, according to [NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/us-news/madison-wisconsin-police-fatal-shooting-rcna588818). The absence of body camera footage has become a central point of criticism as the community seeks answers.

## Conflicting Accounts

Cellphone videos recorded by bystanders circulated widely on social media, showing the confrontation and the shooting. The videos have generated sharply conflicting accounts of what transpired.

Richard Hegwood, who recorded one video, told reporters he "never saw a weapon" and that the man "never brandished a weapon." Michael Doubek, who recorded from a closer vantage point, said the man was "nearly handcuffed" and "already tased" before being shot, calling it "an execution," according to [CBS News](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/madison-wisconsin-police-shooting/).

Chief Patterson cautioned against drawing conclusions from the cellphone footage alone. "I caution everyone — this is one perspective," he said. "It is not a full overview of everything that was visible from different angles, certainly visible from the officers."

## Protests and Political Reaction

Hundreds of protesters gathered at the scene Wednesday evening, marching to the state Capitol and disrupting a Concerts on the Square event, which was ultimately canceled. Some protesters remained at the scene overnight, holding a candlelight vigil and demanding the officer's name and accountability.

Gov. Tony Evers called for transparency in a social media post. "There must be transparency and accountability in any investigation moving forward," Evers said. "We will continue to keep the loved ones and family of the individual who was killed in our hearts and prayers. His life mattered."

Mayor Satya Rhodes-Conway said the shooting must be "rigorously investigated" and pledged that the community "deserves full transparency and accountability." The Wisconsin Department of Criminal Investigation is conducting an independent probe, assisted by the Dane County Sheriff's Office, Wisconsin State Patrol, and Sun Prairie Police Department.

## Historical Context and Unanswered Questions

The shooting occurred on the same street where, in 2015, a white Madison police officer shot and killed Tony Robinson, a 19-year-old biracial man. That officer was exonerated, and the city paid a $3.35 million settlement to Robinson's family in 2017.

Key questions remain unanswered: Police have not released the name or race of the victim, though Black advocacy groups say they believe the man was Black. The officer who fired the fatal shots has also not been identified. Witness Michael Doubek's claim that he saw police toss a knife to the side after the shooting will be a central focus of the investigation.

## What's Next

The DCI investigation and subsequent district attorney review will determine whether charges are filed against the officer. The shooting has also become a flashpoint in Wisconsin's governor's race, with voting already underway in the August 11 primary. Meanwhile, the absence of body cameras is expected to intensify pressure on the city to fund the devices for all officers, as Madison grapples with yet another police shooting that has deeply strained community trust.
