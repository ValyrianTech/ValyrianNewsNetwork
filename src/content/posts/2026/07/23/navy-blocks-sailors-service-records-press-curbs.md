# Navy Blocks Sailors' Service Records in New Press Curbs

The U.S. Navy announced it will no longer release sailors' service history records to the public, marking the latest and most sweeping restriction on press access under the Trump administration and Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth. The policy — which covers duty assignments, awards, and unit histories — eliminates access to information that has been publicly available for decades and is considered critical for reporting on service members involved in major news events.

According to [AP News](https://apnews.com/article/media-navy-press-policy-access-pentagon-b4d5d0f60709692e107e3331370422cd), the move was initially targeted at commanding officers. Last Thursday, Navy top spokesman Rear Admiral John Robinson issued a policy (NAVADMIN 26170) mandating the removal of all public-facing information about commanding officers. But Capt. Candice Tresch, spokeswoman for the Chief of Naval Personnel, announced the policy was expanded to deny access to any sailor's service information "in the interest of equality."

## A Pattern of Secrecy

The Navy's decision is not an isolated event but part of a coordinated campaign by the Trump administration and Hegseth to restrict media access and public oversight of the military. This summer, the Pentagon declared its press office a classified space, inaccessible to journalists. A policy requiring journalists to be accompanied by official escorts on Pentagon grounds was temporarily blocked by a federal judge on June 30 but later reinstated pending appeal.

As [Deadline](https://deadline.com/2026/06/pentagon-press-escort-judge-pete-hegseth-1236971550/) reported, U.S. District Judge Paul Friedman called the Pentagon's rationale for the escort policy "facially dubious," questioning why the timing of a journalist's question would increase the likelihood of a classified disclosure. "Is the implication that a Department official is more likely to divulge such information while, say, in line at Starbucks?" the judge wrote. "Based on what? The Department offers no answer to these questions."

Last year, most major news outlets turned in their Pentagon access badges rather than agree to new restrictive rules — a policy that is now being litigated in federal court.

## Legal and Constitutional Concerns

The Pentagon's own governing document (DoD Manual 5400.11-R) categorizes service members' rank, duty assignments, awards, and service status as information "normally releasable" that "may be disclosed without a clearly unwarranted invasion of personal privacy." Despite this, the Navy will now only confirm whether someone is a sailor if it deems the request "of high public interest," and no other details will be offered until what the Navy calls an "undefined threat has passed."

First Amendment attorney Floyd Abrams criticized the policy sharply. "That effort at suppression of speech is flatly inconsistent with the First Amendment," Abrams told AP News. He added that there is "no reason to think that this is anything less than an effort to prevent 'bad' press about the conduct of the administration with respect to our service people."

## Broader Transparency Threats

Beyond the Navy's policy, the Pentagon has asked Congress for authority to exempt "Controlled Unclassified Information" (CUI) from Freedom of Information Act requests. As [CBS News](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/pentagon-secrecy-power-unclassified-records-foia/) reported, this legislative proposal could shield millions of unclassified records from public disclosure permanently — with no expiration date. Even classified information is generally subject to release after 25 years.

The Pentagon's own inspector general has found the CUI system is inconsistently applied and overused, with the prevailing attitude being "when in doubt, mark it CUI." Critics argue it is arbitrarily used to keep embarrassing information from being made public.

## Implications for Military Reporting

The policy's impact was immediately felt. AP News reported that when it requested the biography of a Blue Angels pilot following a controversial low-altitude maneuver over a crowded beach, the Navy initially refused under the new policy. Tresch only provided the biography after AP began inquiring about the policy itself.

Information about sailors' service history is critical for providing context when service members are involved in accidents, misconduct, or heroic acts. Abrams noted the policy could make reporting on wounded or injured sailors much more challenging.

## What's Next

The restrictions come as the U.S. is engaged in a war with Iran, with the Pentagon requesting $67.1 billion in additional emergency funding. The [Military Times](https://www.militarytimes.com/news/pentagon-congress/2026/07/21/cost-of-iran-war-rises-to-375-billion-pentagon-requests-671-billion-more/) reported the war has cost $37.5 billion to date, with reports that the Pentagon is excluding wounded American troops from official casualty tolls.

Legal challenges to the Navy's policy are widely expected, given that federal judges have already ruled against similar Pentagon restrictions on First Amendment grounds. If the Navy's policy stands, it could set a precedent for the Army, Air Force, Marine Corps, and Space Force to implement similar restrictions — further eroding military transparency at a time when public oversight is most needed.
