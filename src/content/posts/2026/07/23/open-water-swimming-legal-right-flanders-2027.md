# Open-Water Swimming to Become a Legal Right in Flanders by June 2027

Flanders is set to fundamentally reverse its approach to open-water swimming, granting residents a legal right to swim in natural water bodies by June 1, 2027 at the latest. The landmark policy shift, spearheaded by Flemish Environment Minister Jo Brouns (CD&V), will replace the region's longstanding default prohibition with a presumption of access, according to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/en/2026/07/22/open-water-swimming-to-be-permitted-across-flanders-from-next-su/).

Currently, swimming in Flanders' rivers, lakes, and ponds is prohibited unless explicitly designated as a swimming zone. Under the new rules, the reverse will apply: swimming will be permitted in all public waterways unless water managers impose specific bans for safety, pollution, or navigational reasons.

## A Historic Reversal of Policy

The reform fulfills a promise enshrined in the September 2024 coalition agreement of the Diependaele government. It represents a dramatic departure from Flanders' historically cautious approach, which has long prioritized liability prevention and safety over recreational access.

Minister Brouns estimates the change could unlock approximately 140 new swimming locations across the Flemish region. "We want to give Flemings more opportunities to swim," Brouns told VRT NWS. "The demand is increasing year after year. It's high time for a catch-up movement in Flanders."

## How the New Rules Will Work

While the default shifts to permission, water managers — including the Flemish Environment Agency, De Vlaamse Waterweg, the Agency for Nature and Forests, and local municipalities — retain the authority to prohibit swimming where justified. Valid grounds include heavy shipping traffic, dangerous currents, pollution, and hazardous infrastructure. Any such prohibition must be communicated clearly, preferably through digital channels, as [RTBF](https://www.rtbf.be/article/nager-en-eau-libre-deviendra-un-droit-en-flandre-au-plus-tard-le-1er-juin-2027-11761120) reports.

Crucially, swimming outside official zones will be entirely at one's own risk. No lifeguards or supervisors will be present at the new locations. "That is impractical and financially unfeasible," Brouns acknowledged. "Moreover, we will never be able to eliminate every risk."

The rules on the North Sea coast remain unchanged, with swimming outside supervised beach zones still strictly prohibited, according to the [Belga News Agency](https://www.belganewsagency.eu/press-releases/35055/).

## Water Quality Challenges

A significant hurdle remains water quality. According to recent research cited by VRT NWS, only half of Flanders' major waterways currently meet swimming water quality standards. The Flemish Environment Agency (VMM) plans to test water at swimming locations every two weeks, mirroring the current regime for official swimming zones.

Brouns expressed hope that the policy change would create public pressure for cleaner waterways. "By now encouraging open-water swimming, we hope that the call for clean waterways will be louder and water quality will rise higher on the social agenda," he said.

## Mixed Reactions from Stakeholders

Ann De Ceulaer of Outdoor Swimming Belgium, which has advocated for regulatory change for six years, welcomed the announcement. "It's good that more open-water swimming spots will be accessible to everyone," she told VRT NWS, while cautioning swimmers to "do enough research before you jump into the water."

However, Karel Logghe of the Flemish Rescue Federation urged caution. "We may not throw all risks overboard in the water. Rules remain necessary," he said, pointing to dangers near locks and infrastructure. Logghe also raised concerns about litter and nuisance at unsupervised locations.

Some local mayors have expressed hesitation, viewing the new responsibility as a "poisoned gift." Brouns acknowledged "koudwatervrees" (cold feet) among certain municipalities concerned about liability.

## Learning from the Netherlands

Brouns pointed to the Netherlands as a model, noting that the country has nearly 1,000 official swimming locations for its 18 million inhabitants — yet proportionally far fewer incidents occur there. "By creating swimming opportunities throughout Flanders, you spread the crowds and limit the nuisance," he argued.

## What Comes Next

The new regulations must be in force by June 1, 2027, though they could take effect as early as mid-2027. The reform builds on earlier groundwork laid in February 2024, when then-Environment Minister Zuhal Demir approved VLAREM amendments that relaxed requirements for establishing swimming zones and introduced a new category of "free swimming zones" without supervision.

As Flanders prepares for this transformation, key questions remain: Will municipalities embrace the new freedom or impose blanket bans? Can water quality monitoring scale up to cover 140 new locations? And will the necessary infrastructure — access points, signage, basic facilities — receive adequate funding?

For now, the message from the Flemish government is clear: come summer 2027, Flanders' lakes and rivers will be open for business — at the swimmer's own risk.
