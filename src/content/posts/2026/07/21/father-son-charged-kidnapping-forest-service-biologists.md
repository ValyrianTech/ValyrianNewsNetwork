# Father and Son Charged with Kidnapping Forest Biologists at Gunpoint

A father and his adult son have been charged with kidnapping two U.S. Forest Service biologists at gunpoint in a remote Northern California forest, sparking a 15-hour standoff that ended with the hostages rescued unharmed and the suspects in custody, federal authorities announced Monday.

Joseph Charles Henrichsen, 49, and his son Phoenix Henrichsen, 23, appeared in federal court in Sacramento on July 20 but did not enter pleas to charges of kidnapping a federal employee and aiding and abetting, according to [NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/us-news/father-son-charged-kidnapping-forest-service-biologists-gunpoint-rcna588463). If convicted, they face a maximum penalty of life in prison and a $250,000 fine.

## The Kidnapping

On the morning of July 16, two Forest Service staff biologists were conducting research on frog habitats near Gumboot Lake Campground in Shasta-Trinity National Forest, approximately 290 miles north of San Francisco, when Joseph Henrichsen allegedly approached them carrying an AR-15-style rifle. According to a federal criminal affidavit, he ordered the researchers to turn around, zip-tied their hands behind their backs, and forced them into a nearby camping trailer.

Phoenix Henrichsen emerged from the woods and, at his father’s direction, cleared space inside the trailer before the victims were ordered inside, the affidavit states.

## The Voicemail and Standoff

Joseph Henrichsen used one victim’s phone to leave a voicemail for a federal forest ranger, stating: “I’ve taken two fed biologists hostage from the Forest Service… I’ve got live rounds ready.” He requested an FBI investigator to “talk things through,” according to court documents cited by [NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/us-news/two-us-forest-service-employees-rescued-zip-tied-held-hostage-gunpoint-rcna588112).

The Siskiyou County Sheriff’s Office was notified at 10:55 a.m., and deputies arrived around noon, deploying drones to locate the trailer. The FBI’s Sacramento field office was contacted at 12:20 p.m., and agents from Redding were deployed along with a crisis negotiation team and SWAT personnel.

Negotiations began around 4 p.m. and continued through the night. At one point, Joseph Henrichsen claimed to have a grenade and threatened to use it if officers moved closer, the affidavit alleges. Phoenix allegedly alerted his father when he saw people moving through the trees — believed to be law enforcement surrounding the trailer.

The standoff drew a massive multi-agency response. The FBI flew in its elite Hostage Rescue Team from Quantico, Virginia, on a Boeing 757. The White House, FBI Director Kash Patel, and Agriculture Secretary Brooke Rollins, who oversees the Forest Service, monitored the situation as it unfolded, according to [AOL / The Independent](https://www.aol.com/articles/forest-employees-headed-field-were-213012000.html).

## Resolution and Charges

Around 1:50 a.m. on July 17, the first hostage was released alone, followed by the second approximately 15 minutes later. At 2:30 a.m., Joseph and Phoenix Henrichsen exited the trailer and surrendered. Both were arrested without further incident.

“I want to say how profoundly grateful and relieved that we are that our two employees were released safely,” Forest Service Chief Tom Schultz said at a news conference. “Their wellbeing continues to be our highest priority.”

FBI Acting Special Agent Brian Tosh praised the outcome, noting, “Crisis situations like this don’t often result in everyone leaving the scene safely.”

The two biologists, who have not been publicly identified, were released unharmed. One victim was described as a woman. Officials said both are receiving support and will need time to process the experience.

## Suspects’ Background

Court records reveal that Joseph Henrichsen was charged in 2022 with a hate crime in Whatcom County, Washington, for allegedly throwing fireworks at a neighbor’s home whose landlords were of Russian and Ukrainian descent. He left homemade handcuffs and a note reading “Assassinate Vlad Putin” on the front door. A psychologist determined Henrichsen was not competent to stand trial, and the case was dismissed without prejudice after months of waiting for a hospital bed.

Henrichsen identified himself to FBI negotiators as “Charles Perry,” an alias he has used since 2020. Property records show he lived in Orange County, approximately 580 miles from the incident location.

## Motive and Broader Concerns

Charging documents do not list a possible motive. However, Shasta County Sheriff Michael L. Johnson told The New York Times that the workers were targeted “specifically because of their affiliation with the government.” Johnson added that the elder Henrichsen had been known for years to make intense complaints about the federal government.

The incident has raised concerns about the safety of federal employees working in remote areas, particularly in regions where anti-government sentiment has flourished. The Shasta region has seen some residents push to secede from California and form a new state, citing grievances with leaders in Washington and Sacramento.

A preliminary examination for the Henrichsens has been scheduled for August 3.
