# Man, 61, Dies After Speed Pedelec Fall in Veldegem, Belgium

A 61-year-old man from Veldegem, West Flanders, has died after falling from his speed pedelec early Tuesday morning, in an incident that has renewed concerns about the safety of high-speed electric bicycles in Belgium. The accident occurred shortly after 6:00 AM on the Zuidveldstraat, a quiet residential street in the municipality of Zedelgem.

According to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/07/21/veldegem-fietser-gestorven-val-pedelec/), the man was on his way home when he fell and collided with a pole. Emergency services arrived promptly and attempted resuscitation, but he succumbed to his injuries at the scene.

## No Third-Party Involvement

Initial findings from the West Flanders public prosecutor's office indicate there were no traces of a collision with another vehicle or involvement of third parties. "The man was on his way home with his speed pedelec and apparently had a very unfortunate fall," said Griet De Prest, spokesperson for the public prosecutor's office, as reported by [Het Laatste Nieuws](https://www.hln.be/zedelgem/man-61-overleden-na-val-met-speedpedelec-in-zedelgem~ab935377/).

A forensic doctor has been appointed to investigate the precise circumstances of the death. The fire department set up a tent to screen the victim, and the Zuidveldstraat was closed to traffic for a considerable time while authorities conducted their investigation.

## Speed Pedelec Safety Under Scrutiny

The tragedy adds to a growing body of evidence highlighting the risks associated with speed pedelecs — electric bicycles that provide pedal assistance up to 45 km/h (28 mph) and are legally classified as mopeds in Belgium. Unlike standard e-bikes, which are limited to 25 km/h, speed pedelecs require a license plate, mandatory helmet, insurance, and at least a class AM driving license.

According to the [VIAS Institute](https://www.vias.be/nl/newsroom/in-meer-dan-de-helft-van-de-dodelijke-ongevallen-met-een-automobilist-is-er-geen-tegenpartij/), single-vehicle accidents — those involving no other party — account for 17% of all traffic victims but 37% of traffic fatalities, making them disproportionately deadly. This is directly relevant to the Veldegem incident, where no third-party involvement was found.

Commuting accidents involving e-bikes and speed pedelecs are rising sharply across Belgium. In the Antwerp port area alone, bicycle commuting accidents increased by 66% over four years, with speed pedelecs identified as a key factor, according to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/02/antwerpen-haven-ongevallen-speedpedelec-voka/) reporting on a campaign by Voka Antwerpen-Waasland. The risk of permanent injury from a speed pedelec accident is twice as high as from a standard e-bike, said Pieter Leuridan, spokesperson for Voka Antwerpen-Waasland.

## Infrastructure and Regulatory Challenges

Speed pedelecs have surged in popularity in Belgium, driven by commuting incentives, company leasing programs, and a broader shift away from car travel. However, infrastructure has not kept pace. Bike paths designed for cyclists traveling at 15–25 km/h are now shared with vehicles capable of 45 km/h, creating significant speed differentials that increase risk.

Belgian regulations for speed pedelecs are comprehensive on paper. Riders must be at least 16 years old, hold a valid driving license, wear a helmet, carry insurance, and display an SP license plate, as detailed by [Joule.be](https://www.joule.be/blog/alle-speedpedelec-regels-op-een-rijtje). Yet enforcement and infrastructure adaptation remain ongoing challenges.

## Public Debate Intensifies

The incident has reignited public debate about speed pedelec regulation. Reader comments on HLN reflect a polarized public: some describe speed pedelecs as "murder machines" requiring urgent regulation, while experienced riders argue that responsible use is possible and the problem lies with a minority of reckless users.

One commenter, Chris Ysebaert, a 67-year-old experienced speed pedelec rider with over 52,000 km accident-free, noted: "I follow all the rules perfectly... Not everyone rides like crazy with those bikes, but I do have questions when I see how some people race around on them."

## What's Next

The forensic investigation will seek to determine the exact cause of the fall — whether it was due to road surface conditions, a mechanical failure, a medical event, or rider error. The outcome may inform future policy discussions around speed limiter requirements, mandatory training for speed pedelec riders, and investments in separate cycling infrastructure for high-speed bicycles.

As Belgium grapples with the tension between promoting sustainable mobility and ensuring road safety, this tragic incident serves as a stark reminder that the rules of the road must evolve alongside the vehicles that use them.
