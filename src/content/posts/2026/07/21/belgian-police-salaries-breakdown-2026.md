# Belgian Police Salaries: From €2,800 Gross to €6,000 Net

Belgian police officers start at around €2,800 gross per month, but some experienced personnel can earn up to €6,000 net — a disparity that reflects a complex web of premiums for night shifts, weekend work, language skills, and specialized duties. The salary breakdown, published by [Het Laatste Nieuws](https://www.hln.be/binnenland/agent-start-met-2-800-euro-bruto-terwijl-enkelen-tot-6-000-euro-netto-verdienen-zo-zit-het-loon-van-de-belgische-politie-in-elkaar~ab46a484/), comes as the federal government approves €60 million in additional funding to make police compensation more attractive amid mounting recruitment challenges.

## The Base Salary: A Starting Point

On paper, the salary structure appears straightforward. According to the official pay scales of the SSGPI (the social secretariat of the police), a starting agent earns approximately €2,816 gross per month as of September 2026. An inspector begins at around €3,044 gross, a chief inspector at €3,592, and a commissioner at €3,981.

But these figures tell only part of the story. Without any premiums or dependents, a starting inspector takes home roughly €1,800 net per month, according to PolBru, the Brussels-Capital-Elsene police zone. A starting agent nets closer to €1,700.

## Where Premiums Make the Difference

The Belgian police operate 24 hours a day, seven days a week, and that is where the real earning potential lies. Intervention teams work in cyclical rosters — three ten-hour shifts followed by two rest days — with shifts starting at 7 a.m., 10 a.m., noon, or 9 p.m. for night duty.

An intervention inspector with a few years of seniority in a continuous shift system typically earns around €4,000 gross per month, netting between €2,800 and €3,100. At the other end of the spectrum, a neighborhood inspector in a smaller local zone — mostly day shifts, few weekends — stays closer to the official scale at around €3,000 gross and €2,250 to €2,400 net.

## The Brussels Factor

Brussels police zones, which face severe staffing shortages, offer additional financial incentives. A Brussels intervention inspector with ten years of seniority and a bilingual certificate can earn around €5,000 gross per month, netting approximately €3,500 or more. Language premiums for passing exams through Werkenvoor.be (formerly Selor) add roughly €100 gross per month for a basic second-language level.

"At the same time, it's no gift to work in Brussels," sources in police circles told HLN. "You have to be able to take a hit, literally." Daily, two officers in Brussels are victims of physical violence.

## Top Earners and Specialized Units

At the upper end of the scale, specialized federal units — including motorcycle officers, dog handlers, detectives, air support, dive teams, explosives units, and the DSU special intervention unit — can combine multiple operational allowances. In exceptional cases, chief commissioners with extensive seniority and leadership responsibilities can reach total gross compensation of €9,000 to €10,000 per month, netting €5,000 to €6,000.

However, unions caution that these figures are not representative. Many older premium schemes have been reformed or phased out for new personnel, creating a generational divide within the force.

## The Hidden Costs

Police unions are quick to point out that premium pay comes with significant trade-offs. "It has already been proven that officers who stay in service for a long time have a significantly lower life expectancy," union representatives told HLN.

There are also financial offsets. According to the VSOA Politie union, premiums are taxed at 54 percent. Many officers who work in Brussels but live elsewhere receive no compensation for commuting costs, which can amount to hundreds of euros per month in fuel and maintenance.

## The €60 Million Question

On 18 July 2026, the federal government's core cabinet approved a €60 million envelope to improve police compensation, as [La Libre Belgique](https://www.lalibre.be/dernieres-depeches/2026/07/18/une-enveloppe-de-60-millions-deuros-pour-augmenter-le-salaire-des-policiers-SMBOEX3PJZGABDOJPN4JM2CY2U/) reported. The funding will implement the second phase of the sectoral agreement, with negotiations ongoing between the government and police unions.

"Security is a priority for this government, and our officers play a crucial role," said Minister of Security and Interior Bernard Quintin (MR). "That's why appropriate compensation is essential."

The package was part of a broader social agreement that also included a deal for Defense personnel, five additional days of birth leave, and annualized working hours, as [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/07/18/akkoord-kernkabinet-overzicht/) detailed.

## What Lies Ahead

The salary breakdown has generated significant public engagement — 163 comments on HLN alone, making it the most-read and most-shared article on the platform. Public reaction is mixed: some express envy at top-end salaries, while others defend the compensation given the risks and working conditions.

Several structural questions remain unanswered. How will the €60 million be distributed across ranks and zones? Will the new funding address the complexity of the premium system, or simply add another layer? And crucially, can increased pay alone solve the recruitment crisis, or are broader working condition reforms needed?

For now, one thing is clear: a Belgian police officer's salary is far more complex — and sometimes far higher — than most Belgians realize. But as unions emphasize, those premiums are not "free money." They are compensation for a job that demands flexibility, resilience, and increasingly, a willingness to put one's safety on the line.
