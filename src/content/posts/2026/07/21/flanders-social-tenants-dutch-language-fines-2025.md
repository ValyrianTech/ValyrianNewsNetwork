# Only 41 Fined in Flanders Dutch Language Enforcement Gap

Just 41 social housing tenants in Flanders received fines in 2025 for failing to meet Dutch language requirements — a strikingly low figure given that roughly 150,000 tenants are subject to the policy. The data, obtained by Flemish Parliament member Gijs Degrande (N-VA) from Housing Minister Melissa Depraetere (Vooruit), has exposed a significant gap between policy ambition and on-the-ground enforcement, as reported by [Het Laatste Nieuws](https://www.hln.be/binnenland/amper-41-sociale-huurders-krijgen-boete-omdat-ze-geen-nederlands-spreken-andere-prioriteiten~a818913a/).

## Context: The Language Requirement

Since November 2017, social housing tenants in Flanders have been required to learn Dutch as a condition of their tenancy. The policy is structured as a "tenant obligation" rather than an entry requirement — tenants are not required to speak Dutch before receiving a home but must reach the required level within two years. According to the [Flemish government's official guidelines](https://www.vlaanderen.be/een-sociale-woning-huren-bij-een-woonmaatschappij/taalkennisvereiste-bij-een-sociale-woning), tenants who moved in before 2023 must achieve A1 level (basic phrases), while those who moved in after January 1, 2023, must reach A2 level. From the end of 2027, the threshold will rise to B1 oral level, representing fluent participation in Flemish society.

## The Numbers: Warnings and Fines

In 2025, 78 social tenants received formal warnings (*ingebrekestelling*), of whom 41 ultimately received financial fines totaling €9,325 — an average of approximately €212 per fine. This represents a decline from 2024, when 107 warnings were issued and 66 fines imposed. Critically, tenants cannot be evicted for failing the language requirement; they can only be fined repeatedly until compliance is achieved.

## The Enforcement Gap

The low number of fines does not necessarily mean that nearly all tenants are compliant. As [VRT NWS reported](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2024/10/16/taalvereiste-sociale-huurders-melissa-depraetere/), the Flemish government itself admits it lacks a centralized overview of how many tenants actually meet the requirement. Enforcement is handled independently by 41 local housing companies, each operating its own system with no central IT infrastructure to aggregate results.

An audit of 19 housing companies revealed significant procedural failures: four had no procedure at all for monitoring language compliance, one had a procedure that was not yet approved, and two had written procedures but did not apply them in practice, citing high workload and "other priorities." Only 12 of the 19 audited companies had their affairs in order.

"The government rightly imposes an obligation, but at the same time cannot demonstrate who does not comply," Degrande told HLN. "That is a fundamental problem for monitoring and the credibility of the policy."

## Political Reactions

Minister Depraetere defended the policy's intent while acknowledging implementation challenges. "As a government, we invest massively in social housing. Then we may also ask something in return," she said. "Knowledge of Dutch is also simply very important, if only to chat with the neighbors or to communicate smoothly with your landlord."

Her cabinet emphasized that the 41 housing companies are obliged to enforce the language requirement and that a supervisor monitors their compliance. A Central Tenant Register (*Centraal Huurdersregister*) is planned for 2028, which will enable centralized tracking of language compliance across all housing companies for the first time.

## Broader Housing Context

The language enforcement debate unfolds against a backdrop of severe housing shortage. As [VRT NWS reported](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2025/04/09/nooit-eerder-zoveel-mensen-op-wachtlijst-voor-sociale-woning/), a record 199,085 people were on the waiting list for a social home as of April 2025. Compounding the shortage, over 11,000 social homes stood empty for more than six months in 2025, according to [related HLN reporting](https://www.hln.be/binnenland/meer-dan-11-000-sociale-woningen-staan-leeg-terwijl-duizenden-vlamingen-op-de-wachtlijst-staan-hoe-komt-dat~aa768e5a/).

## Analysis and Outlook

The story of "only 41 fines" reveals a fundamental disconnect between policy and practice. While the language requirement exists on paper with clear rules and escalating penalties, the enforcement infrastructure is fragmented, under-resourced, and poorly monitored. The planned Central Tenant Register for 2028 may address the data gap, but its effectiveness remains to be seen.

As the language threshold is set to rise to B1 by the end of 2027, the question of whether the enforcement system can keep pace becomes increasingly urgent. Critics argue that the government's focus on language enforcement may be misplaced when the fundamental problem is a lack of affordable housing — nearly 200,000 households waiting for a home they may never receive.

"The same rules must be followed everywhere in Flanders," Degrande insisted. Whether the administrative machinery can deliver on that principle remains an open question.
