# Rubio and Wang Yi Plan Xi Jinping's Washington Visit

U.S. Secretary of State Marco Rubio met with Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi in Manila on Wednesday for a 90-minute discussion focused on preparing for Chinese President Xi Jinping's planned state visit to Washington in September, according to [Channel News Asia](https://www.channelnewsasia.com/world/rubio-wang-yi-xi-jinping-us-visit-6270711). The meeting, held on the sidelines of the 59th ASEAN Foreign Ministers' Meeting, represents the highest-level direct engagement between the two powers since President Donald Trump's state visit to Beijing in May.

## A Fragile Diplomatic Path

Rubio confirmed that Xi's visit remains "on track" and emphasized the need to identify areas of cooperation to ensure a productive summit. "The work we need to do now in September is to identify what those areas are, so that we can lay the groundwork for a very positive visit," Rubio told reporters after the meeting. He acknowledged the deep divisions between the two nations, stating, "There are areas of great differences. Both sides will acknowledge that. We'll have to work through those, and I think these differences will exist for the foreseeable future."

The meeting unfolded against the backdrop of a fragile truce between the world's two largest economies. Trump and Xi reached a [temporary trade accord](https://www.channelnewsasia.com/world/us-and-china-agree-trade-framework-ahead-leaders-meeting-5425546) in October 2025, and Trump made a reciprocal state visit to Beijing in May 2026, where Xi agreed to a return visit to the United States in the autumn. However, just days before the Rubio-Wang meeting, Trump accused China of meddling in U.S. elections — claims that Beijing has dismissed as "pure fabrication." Notably, Rubio stated that these allegations were not discussed with Wang.

## Wang Yi's Call for Strategic Stability

Wang Yi described the meeting as "pragmatic, positive and constructive," according to an official Chinese summary reported by [Al-Monitor](https://www.al-monitor.com/originals/2026/07/rubio-meets-chinas-wang-regional-gathering-where-iran-war-looms-large). He urged both sides to pursue "strategic stability" and "peaceful coexistence," while demanding that the United States "respect China's core interests ... effectively manage differences and disagreements, and address China's legitimate concerns." Wang and Rubio agreed to "make sound preparations for the next stage of high-level exchanges," China's foreign ministry said in a statement.

Rubio struck a pragmatic tone, telling reporters, "It would frankly be reckless and irresponsible for the US and China not to have a relationship, given the impact our countries have on the global economy and on the world." He added that while both sides would continue to defend their national interests, there remain "some areas of potential cooperation."

## South China Sea Tensions Loom

The diplomatic discussions were complicated by a recent escalation in the South China Sea. On July 20, China's coast guard and Philippine navy personnel clashed at Second Thomas Shoal, an incident Rubio described as "very negative" and "benefiting no one." The altercation represented a significant escalation in the long-running dispute over Beijing's expansive maritime claims, which overlap with the exclusive economic zones of several ASEAN nations.

Rubio reiterated the United States' commitment to its defense treaty with the Philippines, stating, "We intend to live up to our treaty obligations, and I think we've done so, and will continue to do so." The U.S. has increased foreign military financing to the Philippines to $100 million for fiscal year 2026 and is providing an additional $9 million to expand a Philippine Coast Guard pier on Palawan.

## Broader Geopolitical Context

The ASEAN gathering in Manila was dominated by multiple crises. Rubio also joined counterparts from India, Australia, and Japan for a meeting of the Quad grouping, which Beijing has criticized as "Cold War-like." Additionally, Russian Foreign Minister Sergei Lavrov confirmed he would meet Rubio in Manila on Thursday to discuss the Ukraine war, adding another layer of geopolitical complexity to the regional gathering.

## Analysis and Outlook

The Rubio-Wang meeting signals that both Washington and Beijing see strategic value in maintaining high-level engagement despite deep and persistent differences. The decision to move forward with Xi's September visit suggests a mutual interest in stabilizing the bilateral relationship, even as tensions simmer over trade, the South China Sea, election interference allegations, and the ongoing Iran conflict.

Key questions remain: whether Trump's election interference rhetoric will escalate or subside ahead of Xi's arrival, whether further South China Sea incidents could derail diplomatic progress, and what specific deliverables — from trade agreements to crisis communication mechanisms — might emerge from the September summit.

For now, the diplomatic machinery is in motion. As Rubio put it, the task ahead is to manage differences while identifying areas where the world's two most powerful nations can find common ground.
