# Tomorrowland Hit by Hours-Long Communication Blackout

The world-famous Tomorrowland festival in Boom, Belgium suffered a severe communication breakdown lasting several hours on Sunday evening, July 19, raising serious safety concerns at the electronic music festival. According to a report from [Het Laatste Nieuws](https://www.hln.be/boom/tomorrowland-kampte-urenlang-met-communicatieproblemen-zelfs-voor-een-gewonde-bezoeker-konden-we-niemand-bereiken~ae2eef9a/), both mobile internet, calling, and SMS services were down for many Belgian visitors and volunteers during the final night of the first festival weekend.

## Context

Tomorrowland, one of the world's largest and most famous electronic dance music festivals, is held annually in De Schorre provincial park in Boom, Belgium. The 2026 edition marks the festival's 20th anniversary, attracting hundreds of thousands of visitors from around the globe across two weekends. The festival's first weekend ran from July 17 to July 19, with the second weekend scheduled for July 24 to July 26.

## Key Developments

According to an anonymous festival volunteer who spoke to HLN, the communication problems began while Belgian techno DJ Charlotte de Witte was performing on the mainstage. "It was really not normal. Everyone was watching the Spain-Argentina match and streaming it. No one could send messages anymore," the volunteer said. "I couldn't send SMS to my girlfriends and we couldn't reach our supervisor either."

The volunteer reported that the network failure had serious consequences when an attendee was injured. "At a certain point, someone got injured and we needed first aid. Normally I would just put a message in the group chat asking to send someone to the front zone or contact via walkie-talkie," she explained. Unable to reach emergency services through any communication channel, she had to physically walk to another group to find help for the injured person.

The World Cup final between Spain and Argentina was being streamed by many attendees during the same period, which may have contributed to extraordinary network congestion. The volunteer noted that foreign visitors appeared to have fewer problems — a friend from Egypt with a Lycamobile prepaid SIM card experienced only minor delays, suggesting the issue may have been specific to certain Belgian network operators.

The volunteer described the situation as extreme: "Everything was down. I couldn't do anything at all." Even sending a simple SMS to her daughter, who needed a Netflix verification code, proved impossible. The email with the code arrived, but she could not forward it due to the network failure.

Both Proximus and Telenet, Belgium's major telecom providers, confirmed that extra antennas are typically placed at large events like Tomorrowland to handle increased demand. However, both companies stated that their teams had no confirmation of a network outage. This raises questions about network monitoring at major events and whether the scale of the problem was fully understood by the providers.

## Analysis

The inability to contact emergency services for an injured person represents a critical safety failure at a festival hosting tens of thousands of people. The differential impact between Belgian users and foreign users with prepaid SIM cards suggests the issue may relate to network prioritization, roaming agreements, or specific Belgian provider configurations rather than a total network failure.

The incident highlights the growing strain on mobile networks during major events, particularly when combined with high-bandwidth activities like live-streaming a World Cup final. The fact that a World Cup final coincided with the festival's closing night created a perfect storm of data demand, overwhelming the network capacity that telecom providers had deployed.

Notably, some festivalgoers on different networks reported fewer issues. A reader comment on the HLN article from an Orange subscriber noted they experienced only minor delays and could communicate normally throughout the evening, suggesting the problem may have been concentrated on specific provider networks rather than being a universal failure.

As festivals continue to grow in scale and attendees increasingly rely on mobile connectivity for communication, navigation, and social media, the need for robust and redundant communication infrastructure becomes ever more critical. The 2025 edition of Tomorrowland was already marred by a major fire that destroyed the mainstage during setup, making this latest incident another significant safety concern for the festival.

## What's Next

The second weekend of Tomorrowland 2026 is scheduled to begin on July 24. Questions remain about whether the festival organizers or telecom providers will implement additional measures to prevent a recurrence. Neither Proximus nor Telenet has issued a follow-up statement since the HLN article was published, and it remains unclear whether Tomorrowland has backup communication systems such as satellite phones or dedicated radio networks for emergency services.

This incident may lead to increased scrutiny of communication infrastructure at large public events in Belgium and could prompt festivals to invest in dedicated communication networks or distributed antenna systems to ensure reliable coverage in the future.
