# French DJ Barbara Butch Harassed Off Stage by LFI and Pro-Palestinian Activists

French DJ and LGBTQ+ icon Barbara Butch was forced to interrupt her performance at the Cabaret Frappé festival in Grenoble on July 18 after approximately 150–200 pro-Palestinian activists, including members of the far-left La France Insoumise (LFI), stormed the venue with chants, whistles, and projectiles. The artist, who has been a repeated target of online harassment since her appearance at the 2024 Paris Olympics opening ceremony, told [La Libre Belgique](https://www.lalibre.be/culture/musique/2026/07/22/vive-polemique-en-france-autour-dune-artiste-harcelee-par-des-militants-lfi-et-propalestiniens-avant-meme-de-commencer-jetais-en-larmes-6ZZAXJFDDRFZ3ICUFOLWYIOBLY/) that she was "in tears before I even began."

## Background of the Controversy

The protest did not emerge spontaneously. Since May 2026, the Grenoble branch of La France Insoumise, led by local elected official Allan Brunon, had been organizing a boycott of Butch's scheduled performance. A flyer distributed by LFI activists described the DJ as an "artist complicit in genocide."

The boycott was triggered by Butch's decision to sign a March 2026 tribute supporting the proposed Yadan law, which aimed to "fight against new forms of antisemitism" by expanding the offense of apology for terrorism. The law proved deeply controversial — opponents, including LFI, argued it conflated antisemitism with legitimate criticism of Israel — and was ultimately withdrawn due to parliamentary scheduling conflicts.

Butch, 45, is a prominent figure in Parisian queer nightlife and a Jewish woman who has been a persistent target of lesbophobic, fatphobic, and antisemitic attacks since her celebrated performance alongside drag queens at the 2024 Paris Olympics. Four men were previously convicted for cyber-harassing her after that event.

## The Incident at Cabaret Frappé

On the evening of July 18, Butch's concert began at approximately 22:20. Within roughly 20 minutes, activists who had gathered at the venue began chanting, whistling, waving Palestinian flags, and throwing glass bottles and other projectiles at the stage. Electrical installations were also reportedly sabotaged. Forced to abandon her set, Butch left the stage as the sound system played John Lennon's "Imagine."

City officials, including Mayor Laurence Ruffin, confirmed that glass bottles and projectiles were "deliberately aimed at the stage." Allan Brunon, however, denied that any projectiles were thrown, characterizing the event as a "peaceful demonstration" and stating he was "extremely proud" of the protest.

## Political Fallout

The incident has drawn condemnation from across the French political spectrum. Interior Minister Laurent Nuñez condemned the violence "with the greatest firmness." Socialist Party leader Olivier Faure declared that "these methods do not help the Palestinians in any way." Assembly President Yaël Braun-Pivet expressed indignation, and Culture Minister Catherine Pégard announced she would receive Butch "in the coming days." Far-right leader Marine Le Pen denounced what she called "the antisemitism of the far left, its recurrent violence."

LFI's national coordinator, Manuel Bompard, offered a notably qualified response. He condemned insults "if there were any" while defending the right to "peaceful protest" — a position that drew sharp criticism from former Prime Minister Gabriel Attal, who said "the antisemitism of LFI is anything but residual."

Clémentine Autain, an ecologist politician and former LFI member, criticized the targeting of Butch, noting her long history of LGBTQ+ and anti-fatphobia activism.

## Legal Proceedings

The Grenoble public prosecutor's office opened an investigation on July 21 for "concerted obstruction of freedoms" (délit d'entrave concerté aux libertés), an offense punishable by up to one year imprisonment. The city of Grenoble also announced it would file a complaint.

Butch, represented by lawyer Audrey Msellati, has given interviews expressing both fear and defiance. "I was afraid… but I will go back on stage," she wrote on Instagram.

## Analysis: A Deeper Divide

The incident has reignited a fierce debate in France about the boundaries between political protest, antisemitism, and artistic freedom. Butch and her lawyer argue that "the anti-Zionism invoked was merely the clothing of antisemitism." The League Against Racism and Antisemitism (LICRA) denounced what it called "an antisemitic amalgam consisting of assigning Jewish people to the policy of the State of Israel."

LFI, meanwhile, maintains that opposition to the Yadan law and criticism of Israeli policy is legitimate political expression. The party, officially classified as "far left" by the Interior Ministry for upcoming municipal elections, has faced repeated accusations of tolerating antisemitism within its ranks — accusations that leader Jean-Luc Mélenchon has dismissed, notably stating in 2024 that "antisemitism remains residual in France."

## What's Next

As the Grenoble prosecutor's investigation proceeds, the incident has further isolated LFI on the political spectrum, drawing condemnation not only from the right and center but also from other left-wing parties. The case raises unresolved questions about the security of artists targeted by political boycotts and the limits of protest in democratic societies.

For Barbara Butch, the immediate future remains uncertain. But her message is clear: she intends to return to the stage, despite the threats. "A frontier has been crossed," she said, "but I will not be silenced."
