# US House Passes Funding Bill to Avert Government Shutdown

The Republican-controlled US House of Representatives has passed a short-term funding bill to extend federal government operations through December 4, aiming to prevent a government shutdown just months before the November midterm elections. The measure, H.R. 9770 — the Continuing Appropriations Act, 2027 — passed by a vote of 220-205 on July 21, with six Democrats joining all but one Republican in support.

The continuing resolution (CR) extends federal funding at current levels past the September 30 end of the fiscal year, pushing the next funding deadline beyond Election Day. The legislation now heads to the Senate, where its path remains uncertain amid partisan negotiations over spending and immigration enforcement.

## Context: A History of Shutdowns

This Congress has already experienced two record-setting government shutdowns. In 2025, Democrats forced a 45-day total government shutdown in a failed attempt to extend federal healthcare subsidies — at the time, the longest such shutdown in US history, according to [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/07/21/nx-s1-5902245/government-funding-house-vote-continuing-resolution).

A 76-day Department of Homeland Security shutdown followed in early 2026 after immigration enforcement personnel killed two American citizens in Minnesota. Democrats refused to fund DHS unless Republicans agreed to reforms, including restrictions on face coverings and body cameras for immigration officers. Republicans ultimately used the reconciliation process to fund the agency on a party-line vote through the end of President Trump's term.

## The House Vote and Key Arguments

Speaker Mike Johnson (R-LA) championed the CR as a clean extension devoid of controversial provisions. "The CR is as clean as they come. There are no poison pills. There's no maneuvers. There's nothing other than a simple extension of current funding levels," Johnson said, as reported by [CBS News](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/house-government-funding-short-term-resolution-vote-december-4/).

House Appropriations Committee Chairman Tom Cole (R-OK) argued the bill was necessary to prevent a third shutdown. "We want to take away the possibility of a shutdown between now and the election. We've seen that happen twice in this Congress already, generated by the Senate, and I'm not willing to risk a third time if we can avoid that," Cole said.

Democratic leaders opposed the measure, arguing it gives the Trump administration unchecked power over the federal budget and fails to restrict funding for immigration enforcement. In a joint statement, Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries (D-NY), Minority Whip Katherine Clark (D-MA), and Caucus Chair Pete Aguilar (D-CA) called the bill "a premature and desperate attempt by Republicans to abdicate their responsibility to govern."

Rep. Rosa DeLauro (D-CT), the ranking Democrat on the House Appropriations Committee, said the bill would allow the Trump administration to redirect billions to Border Patrol without reforms. "It's a clear line in the sand that Democrats have drawn for months," DeLauro said, as quoted by [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/jul/22/house-passes-spending-bill).

## Senate Path Uncertain

The House is set to leave Washington for its five-week August recess days after the vote. The Senate, however, remains in session through the first week of August. Senate Majority Leader John Thune (R-SD) said he intends to bring a funding plan to the floor before the break.

"That is something that I intend to bring to the floor for a vote before the August break," Thune said, according to CBS News. He also indicated that reconciliation — which would allow Republicans to pass funding with a simple majority — is a backup option, though he added, "I hope that's not necessary."

The House has passed only 3 of the 12 annual appropriations bills for fiscal year 2027, while the Senate has passed none. The lack of progress underscores the deep partisan divisions that have characterized budget negotiations throughout this Congress.

## Analysis: Midterm Election Dynamics

The funding fight is unfolding against the backdrop of the November 2026 midterm elections, where all 435 House seats and roughly one-third of Senate seats are up for grabs. Democrats hope to retake control of the House and potentially the Senate, where they need at least three seats in states that backed President Trump in 2024.

Both parties are maneuvering to avoid blame for a potential shutdown while positioning themselves on key issues. Republicans present the CR as responsible governance, while Democrats highlight immigration enforcement concerns — a wedge issue that energizes their base. The near-unanimous Republican support (only one defection) suggests party unity on avoiding a pre-election shutdown.

## What's Next

The Senate must now act before the August recess. If the House-passed CR fails to clear the 60-vote threshold, Republicans may turn to reconciliation to bypass Democratic opposition. With the September 30 funding deadline looming and Congress largely absent for August, the compressed timeline sets the stage for intense negotiations this fall.

The December 4 expiration date also means that the next funding fight will occur during the lame-duck session after the midterm elections, potentially reshaping the political dynamics depending on the election results.

As [Anadolu Agency](https://www.aa.com.tr/en/americas/us-house-passes-short-term-funding-bill-to-avoid-government-shutdown/4005297) reported, Johnson accused Democrats of using the funding fight for political purposes, claiming they believe a shutdown could benefit them at the ballot box. "They do this to try to score political points. I think they're making the wrong calculation here," Johnson said.
