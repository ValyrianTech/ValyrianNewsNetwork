# Rubio and China's Wang Yi Plan Xi Jinping's Washington Visit

US Secretary of State Marco Rubio met with Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi in Manila on Wednesday for a 90-minute discussion focused on laying the groundwork for Chinese President Xi Jinping's planned state visit to Washington in September 2026. The meeting, held on the sidelines of the ASEAN Foreign Ministers' Meeting, signals a concerted effort by both nations to stabilize a relationship strained by trade disputes, South China Sea tensions, and allegations of election interference.

## Diplomatic Breakthrough Amid Tensions

According to [Channel NewsAsia](https://www.channelnewsasia.com/world/rubio-wang-yi-xi-jinping-us-visit-6270711), Rubio told reporters after the meeting that both sides discussed identifying areas of cooperation to ensure a "very positive visit." The top US diplomat acknowledged significant differences between the two nations but emphasized the importance of managing them responsibly.

"The work we need to do now in September is to identify what those areas are, so that we can lay the groundwork for a very positive visit," Rubio said. "There are areas of great differences. Both sides will acknowledge that. We'll have to work through those, and I think these differences will exist for the foreseeable future."

Rubio further stressed that maintaining communication between the world's two largest economies was essential, stating: "It would frankly be reckless and irresponsible for the US and China not to have a relationship, given the impact our countries have on the global economy and on the world."

## A Fragile Truce

The meeting came amid a fragile truce between Washington and Beijing, stabilized by a temporary trade accord reached by President Donald Trump and President Xi in October 2025. The two leaders previously met in Beijing in May 2026, during which China announced that Xi would make a state visit to the United States in the autumn.

However, the diplomatic calendar has been complicated by multiple flashpoints. Just two days before the Rubio-Wang meeting, a clash erupted between the China Coast Guard and Philippine Navy personnel at Ayungin (Second Thomas) Shoal in the South China Sea, where a Filipino sailor was reportedly injured. The incident sharpened regional tensions and cast a shadow over the ASEAN gatherings.

Additionally, President Trump had recently accused China of interfering in US elections — allegations that Beijing dismissed as "pure fabrication." Rubio confirmed that the election interference claims were not discussed during his meeting with Wang.

## China's Position

Wang Yi described the meeting as "pragmatic, positive, and constructive," according to a Chinese foreign ministry statement. He called on the United States to "respect China's core interests" and "effectively manage differences and disagreements." On the South China Sea, Wang said the region "should not become an obstacle to China-ASEAN relations."

Chinese Foreign Ministry Spokesperson Lin Jian confirmed that both sides had "maintained communication regarding the arrangements for the heads of state's interactions later this year," underscoring that "head-of-state diplomacy provides irreplaceable strategic guidance for US-China relations."

## Broader Diplomatic Context

Rubio's Manila agenda extended beyond the bilateral meeting with Wang. He also met with Philippine President Ferdinand Marcos Jr., participated in the ASEAN Post-Ministerial Conference with the US, and held talks with Quad counterparts from India, Australia, and Japan — a grouping that Beijing has criticized as "Cold War-like."

The US has also increased foreign military financing to the Philippines to $100 million for fiscal year 2026 and provided an additional $9 million to expand a Philippine Coast Guard pier on Palawan island, underscoring Washington's commitment to its treaty ally amid the South China Sea disputes.

Rubio is also scheduled to meet Russian Foreign Minister Sergei Lavrov on July 23 to discuss the war in Ukraine, adding another layer of diplomatic activity to his visit.

## Analysis and Implications

The Xi visit to Washington in September appears to be on track despite multiple points of tension. Both Washington and Beijing have strong incentives to ensure the summit proceeds: for the United States, stabilizing relations with China allows focus on other pressing priorities including the ongoing US-Iran conflict; for Beijing, a successful state visit to Washington would be a significant diplomatic achievement, projecting an image of normalcy and continued great-power engagement.

However, the success of the summit will ultimately depend on whether concrete outcomes emerge on trade, technology, Taiwan, and South China Sea issues. The structural tensions underlying US-China strategic competition are unlikely to be resolved by any single meeting, but maintaining open communication channels remains critical for crisis management.

## What to Watch For

As September approaches, several questions remain: Will the Ayungin Shoal incident further complicate the US-China-Philippines dynamic? Could Trump's election interference allegations resurface and disrupt summit preparations? And how will the broader US-Iran conflict shape the context for what promises to be one of the most consequential diplomatic meetings of the year?

For now, both sides appear committed to ensuring that Xi's Washington visit proceeds as planned — a development that, if realized, would mark a significant moment in the ongoing effort to manage the world's most important bilateral relationship.
