# Hitler's Birthplace Becomes Police Station After Years of Controversy

Austria officially inaugurated a police station on Wednesday in the 17th-century townhouse where Adolf Hitler was born, ending a decades-long controversy over how to handle the site at Salzburger Vorstadt 15 in Braunau am Inn, Upper Austria. The transformation, which cost €20 million (approximately $21.7 million), aims to prevent the location from becoming a neo-Nazi pilgrimage destination while ensuring Austria confronts its Nazi past.

## Context and Background

The building, where Hitler was born on April 20, 1889, has been a source of contention for years. Hitler lived there only a few weeks before his family moved to another address in Braunau, leaving the town for good when he was three years old. During the Nazi era, the building was used as an art museum under the "Führer birth house" cult. After the war, from 1972 to 2011, it housed a day center and workshop for people with special needs run by the Lebenshilfe charity.

The controversy escalated in 2011 when the owner, Gerlinde Pommer, whose family had owned the building since 1912, blocked renovations, forcing the charity to move out. According to [BBC News](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cy5d36e752yo), Pommer repeatedly refused to sell the property to the government despite generous rent payments. In 2016, the Austrian parliament passed a special law allowing expropriation, and the state paid €812,000 in compensation — a sum Pommer challenged in court but ultimately lost.

## Key Developments

The inauguration ceremony, attended by about 100 people including 30 uniformed officers, opened to the sound of a police orchestra. The building has been substantially refurbished by the architecture firm Marte.Marte Architekten, which won an EU-wide competition. The old yellow plaster has been replaced with a sober white facade, the building was raised, and a police logo now sits above the entrance.

As [La Libre Belgique](https://www.lalibre.be/international/europe/2026/07/22/la-maison-natale-dadolf-hitler-devient-un-commissariat-de-police-en-autriche-OVDTC5C5BRETJK43N5SURXZOPY/) reported, Interior Minister Gerhard Karner declared at the ceremony: "Now, this house will become a place of democracy, the rule of law, security, freedom and human dignity." He added that "the memory does not disappear, nothing is concealed," noting that a memorial stele made from granite from the former Mauthausen concentration camp remains in place on the sidewalk.

Mayor Johannes Waidbacher said the inauguration "shows that the city of Braunau confronts its history, that it assumes its responsibilities." He expressed hope that it would bring "a little serenity to the residents of Braunau."

A memorial stone inscribed "For peace, freedom and democracy. Never again fascism. Millions of dead remind us" remains outside the building, and security cameras have been installed to monitor potential neo-Nazi pilgrims.

## Analysis and Implications

The decision to convert the site into a police station was not without alternatives. Demolition was proposed by former Interior Minister Wolfgang Sobotka in 2016 but rejected by historians who argued it would be tantamount to denying Austria's Nazi past. A memorial or educational center was also ruled out by an expert commission to avoid creating a neo-Nazi magnet. The Lebenshilfe charity declined to return, fearing it would be used as a "fig leaf" for political debate.

However, the conversion has drawn criticism. Robert Eiter of the Mauthausen Committee Austria argued that "the transformation will not discourage a single neo-Nazi tempted by pilgrimage," adding that "the world won't forget where the worst mass murderer in history was born." Local historian Florian Kotanko noted that "Braunau will always be associated with the birthplace of Adolf Hitler in the world. This house is a link in the culture of remembrance and this is not the end of the story."

The conversion comes at a politically sensitive time. According to a Kronen Zeitung poll published on July 19, Austria's far-right Freedom Party (FPÖ) — founded by former Nazis — had 38% of voting intentions, having won 28.9% in the September 2024 parliamentary elections. This context adds significance to the government's decision to repurpose the site as a symbol of democratic state authority.

## What's Next

While the police station is now operational, questions remain about whether it will actually deter neo-Nazi pilgrimages or simply shift them elsewhere. The building lacks any explicit historical signage inside about Hitler's crimes, a compromise that satisfies neither critics who want more education nor those who prefer the site be depoliticized entirely. As Florian Kotanko told the BBC: "You have to deal with it, and so we all try to deal with it in an open and correct way. It's not a matter of being proud, but it's a matter of dealing with the fact."

The conversion may serve as an international precedent for how countries grapple with sites associated with dictators and atrocities, but whether it truly resolves the tension between remembrance and prevention remains an open question.
