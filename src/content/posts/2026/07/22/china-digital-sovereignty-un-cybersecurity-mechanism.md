# China Unveils Digital Sovereignty Doctrine at Inaugural UN Cyber Meeting

China has introduced the principle of **digital sovereignty** in a landmark position paper submitted to the inaugural meeting of the UN Global Cybersecurity Permanent Mechanism, marking a significant expansion of Beijing's vision for international digital governance. The document, submitted by the Chinese delegation on July 21 at UN Headquarters in New York, calls for multilateral cooperation and explicitly rejects coercion in technology choices, according to [Xinhua News Agency](https://www.news.cn/world/20260722/8611b2f9c47540959640845b2cceef32/c.html).

## A New Forum, A New Doctrine

The UN Global Cybersecurity Permanent Mechanism, convening for its first session from July 20 to 24, represents a significant institutional step in formalizing international cybersecurity dialogue. The establishment of a permanent platform for member states to negotiate cybersecurity norms and rules has been long anticipated, and China's decision to submit a comprehensive position paper at this inaugural gathering signals its intent to play a leading role in shaping the body's agenda from the outset.

The centerpiece of the Chinese submission is the introduction of **digital sovereignty** (数字主权) as a governing principle for the digital intelligence era. This builds upon China's long-standing doctrine of cyber sovereignty (网络主权), which President Xi Jinping first prominently articulated at the Second World Internet Conference in Wuzhen in 2015. Where cyber sovereignty focused primarily on internet governance and content control within national borders, digital sovereignty expands the framework to encompass artificial intelligence, data governance, and broader digital infrastructure.

## Core Principles: Sovereignty, Multilateralism, Non-Coercion

The position paper articulates three interconnected principles. First, it calls on all nations to abide by the purposes and principles of the UN Charter in cyberspace to maintain peace and stability. Second, it advocates for respect of digital sovereignty and inclusive development, asserting that each country has the right to independently choose its path for digital intelligence technology development — including AI — based on national conditions. Third, it reaffirms adherence to multilateralism to address risks posed by emerging technologies.

A particularly pointed element of the document is its explicit stance against technological coercion. The position paper states that countries "should not be coerced into taking sides" in their choice of AI technology, products, and services. This language is widely interpreted as a direct reference to US-led efforts to restrict Chinese technology, including bans on Huawei 5G equipment, semiconductor export controls, and limitations on AI technology transfers. By positioning itself as a defender of technological autonomy, China is appealing directly to developing nations wary of being caught in the crossfire of US-China strategic competition.

## Sophisticated Diplomatic Choreography

The submission of the position paper is the latest move in a carefully orchestrated sequence of Chinese diplomatic initiatives. Just days earlier, on July 16, the **World Artificial Intelligence Cooperation Organization (WAICO)** was formally established in Shanghai with 29 founding member states, as documented in the [WAIC Chair Statement](https://www.mfa.gov.cn/web/ziliao_674904/1179_674909/202607/t20260717_11984707.shtml) published by China's Ministry of Foreign Affairs. This was followed by the 2026 World AI Conference (WAIC) from July 17 to 20, where President Xi Jinping delivered a keynote speech calling for a "fair and reasonable global AI governance system" and declaring that "AI development should not be a solo performance by one country, but a symphony of global cooperation," as [recorded by中国政府网](https://www.gov.cn/yaowen/liebiao/202607/content_7075874.htm).

The close temporal sequencing of these events — WAICO founding, the WAIC conference, and the UN mechanism submission — demonstrates sophisticated diplomatic coordination. China is simultaneously advancing its agenda on multiple fronts: establishing a new international organization, hosting a major global conference, and engaging with existing UN mechanisms.

## From Cyber Sovereignty to Digital Sovereignty: An Evolving Framework

The conceptual shift from cyber sovereignty to digital sovereignty is more than semantic. It reflects China's recognition that the digital landscape has fundamentally changed. The term "digital intelligence era" (数智时代), which features prominently in the position paper's title, combines digitalization (数字化) and intelligentization (智能化) — acknowledging that AI and data governance are now inseparable from traditional cybersecurity concerns.

This expanded framework allows China to bridge its AI governance initiatives — including the Global AI Governance Initiative proposed by Xi in 2023, the AI Capacity Building普惠 Plan, and the newly established WAICO — with traditional cybersecurity discussions at the UN. The position paper effectively serves as a conceptual bridge between these forums, proposing a unified approach to digital governance that spans both cybersecurity and artificial intelligence.

## Implications and Reactions

The position paper's emphasis on digital sovereignty and non-coercion is likely to resonate with many developing countries in the Global South who are seeking to navigate the intensifying US-China technology competition without sacrificing their policy autonomy. China's broader strategy of positioning itself as a champion of developing country interests in the digital domain has found tangible expression in initiatives such as its pledge to provide 5,000 AI training opportunities for developing countries over the next five years, as announced by Xi at the WAIC.

However, the concept of digital sovereignty remains undefined in international law, and its reception among Western nations is uncertain. The United States and its allies may view the doctrine as a justification for internet censorship and digital protectionism, potentially creating new fault lines in international digital governance debates. The coming months will test whether digital sovereignty can serve as a basis for consensus-building or whether it becomes another point of contention in an increasingly fragmented global technology landscape.

## What to Watch For

As the UN Global Cybersecurity Permanent Mechanism continues its inaugural session through July 24, several questions remain. How other member states respond to China's position paper will be a critical indicator of the document's diplomatic impact. The full text of the position paper has not yet been publicly released, and its detailed provisions may reveal additional nuances. Perhaps most significantly, the relationship between the newly established WAICO and the UN mechanism remains to be defined — and China's position paper may serve as an early attempt to shape that relationship on its own terms.

For now, one thing is clear: with the introduction of digital sovereignty, China has laid down a marker in the evolving architecture of global digital governance. The debate over its meaning and implications is only just beginning.
