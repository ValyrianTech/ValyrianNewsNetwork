# Iran War Escalates: Trump Threatens Infrastructure Strikes as Costs Mount

The conflict between the United States and Iran intensified dramatically on July 22, 2026, as President Donald Trump threatened to bomb Iranian civilian infrastructure for every attack on shipping in the Strait of Hormuz, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth revealed the war has cost $37.5 billion, and Trump attended a dignified transfer ceremony for four US service members killed in the Middle East. Secretary of State Marco Rubio separately accused Iran of flying members of its Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) into Yemen to escalate Houthi attacks.

## A Dangerous New Escalation

In a post on Truth Social, Trump declared that "from this point forward, any time the Islamic Republic of Iran shoots at a ship in the Strait of Hormuz, the United States will bomb and destroy ONE BRIDGE OR POWER PLANT, including those located next to, or in, the Capital City of Tehran." The threat marks a significant departure from the US strategy of targeting only military assets, raising the specter of direct attacks on Iranian civilian infrastructure.

According to [AP News](https://apnews.com/article/dignified-transfer-iran-war-trump-8a79b27e10cedd202596d57f743ca569), Trump made the comments hours before departing for Dover Air Force Base to attend the dignified transfer of four service members. Speaking to reporters, he called it "one of the hardest things to do as a president," adding that his message to grieving families would be "we love you, we love your child."

The four fallen soldiers were identified as Tyler James Feehan, 25, of Ewa Beach, Hawaii; Isabella Gonzales, 19, of Carrollton, Texas; Angel S. Rampersad, 28, of Ozone Park, New York; and Michael Emmanuel Swinton, 30, of Spring Lake, North Carolina. Their deaths bring the total number of US service members killed since the war began on February 28 to 18.

## War Costs Surge Past $37 Billion

At a fiery Senate Appropriations Committee hearing, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth estimated the Iran war has cost $37.5 billion so far, up sharply from the roughly $29 billion estimated in mid-May. As [AP News](https://apnews.com/article/iran-war-hegseth-military-deaths-95-billion-7d158708c3e5385683930f8b839d0344) reported, Hegseth defended a request for $67 billion in supplemental funding, calling it an "urgent, necessary" injection of money, while Republicans prepare a $95 billion package to fund the military along with other White House priorities.

The hearing grew combative as Democratic senators pressed Hegseth on the administration's strategy. Sen. Patty Murray warned of "another forever war," stating, "This war is now spiraling out of control again." Sen. Gary Peters told Hegseth directly, "You, sir, are the failure. You don't have a strategy. You don't have a long-term plan to actually win this war." Hegseth shot back, accusing Democrats of having "Trump Derangement Syndrome."

Gen. Dan Caine, chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, acknowledged the uncertainty when asked if the requested funds would be sufficient, stating, "I can't answer the question on what it will cost, because the enemy has a vote."

## Regional Conflict Spreads to Yemen

Secretary of State Marco Rubio accused Iran of flying IRGC personnel directly into Yemen, escalating the Houthi threat against Saudi Arabia. As [Fox News](https://www.foxnews.com/world/rubio-accuses-iran-flying-irgc-personnel-yemen-houthi-threat-escalates) reported, Rubio told reporters in Manila, "They decided to send direct flights from Tehran into Yemen, carrying IRGC elements and others into the country."

The Houthis have declared a blockade targeting Saudi-linked vessels in the Bab el-Mandeb Strait, a critical chokepoint through which approximately 12% of global trade and one-quarter of worldwide container traffic normally passes. Saudi Arabia has vowed to keep the waterway open, while the Houthis launched missiles at Abha International Airport.

Rubio warned that allowing Iran to control international waterways and charge tolls would set a "very dangerous precedent which will repeat itself in other parts of the world." The Houthis, Hezbollah, militias in Iraq, and Hamas represent "what Iran spends its money on," Rubio said. "It's not on its people."

## Military Operations Continue

The US military launched its 11th consecutive night of strikes against Iranian targets, with CENTCOM targeting aircraft hangars, drone storage facilities, and military logistics infrastructure. According to [Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/liveblog/2026/7/22/iran-war-live-us-launches-new-attacks-hegseth-says-war-has-cost-37-5bn), Iran's military spokesman Abolfazl Shekarchi claimed that "missiles are moving directly from production to operational use while replenishing our stockpiles," asserting that the US has been unable to locate Iran's missile production sites.

The CIA estimated in May that Iran retained 70% of its pre-war missile stockpile, far more than US strategists anticipated. Meanwhile, at least 53 Iranian civilians, including three children, have been killed in US strikes since fighting flared again on June 27, according to Iran's Health Ministry.

## Analysis: A Strategic Stalemate

Analysts characterize the situation as a dangerous escalatory spiral with no clear endgame. Mehran Kamrava of Georgetown University in Qatar told Al Jazeera, "What we are seeing from the US is a series of military tactics but no strategy whatsoever." He warned that "a wounded Iran is a dangerous Iran," noting that the US appears undecided between waging a full-scale war or limiting attacks surgically.

Diplomatic prospects remain bleak. Trump stated the US has "no interest" in talks with Iran for now, while Pakistan's Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif continues efforts to salvage the collapsed interim deal brokered in June. As [Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/2026/07/22/trump-says-us-has-no-interest-in-iran-talks-as-cost-of-war-increases) reported, Iran's Interior Minister met with Pakistan's army chief in Rawalpindi, but no breakthrough has emerged.

## What to Watch For

The coming days will reveal whether Trump follows through on his threat to bomb Iranian civilian infrastructure, a move that could trigger Iranian retaliation against US allies in the region. Iran has already vowed to strike civilian infrastructure in the Middle East if the US attacks its bridges or power plants. With midterm elections approaching and public opposition to the war growing — even within Trump's MAGA base, where support for the war's economic costs has dropped from 50% to 35% — the political calculus for the administration is becoming increasingly precarious.
