# Bad Bunny Brings Controversy and Defiance to Brussels

Global pop superstar Bad Bunny performed at Brussels' King Baudouin Stadium on Wednesday, closing out the European leg of his "Debí Tirar Más Fotos" World Tour at the center of intersecting controversies — from his outspoken defiance of Donald Trump to a growing backlash over his "La Casita" stage design. The Puerto Rican artist, who made history this year as the first Spanish-language act to win the Grammy Award for Album of the Year, has become both a symbol of Latino cultural power and a lightning rod for criticism.

## A Political Voice on the World Stage

Bad Bunny's political activism has been central to his 2026 trajectory. In February, he headlined the [Super Bowl LX halftime show](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Super_Bowl_LX_halftime_show) entirely in Spanish, drawing both acclaim and criticism from conservative American figures. Weeks later, at the Grammy Awards, he made headlines with a blistering anti-ICE speech after winning Album of the Year for "Debí Tirar Más Fotos."

"Before thanking God, I want to say: ICE out," Bad Bunny declared in French during his acceptance speech, according to [La Libre Belgique](https://www.lalibre.be/culture/2026/07/22/bad-bunny-licone-engagee-UT2BK3EQ2BCAHJTKM5XUSCZLDU/). "We are not savages, animals, or aliens. We are human beings. And we are Americans."

Donald Trump responded by calling the Grammy ceremony "garbage," continuing a feud that has defined much of Bad Bunny's relationship with American conservative politics. The artist's music has long addressed Puerto Rico's colonial status as a U.S. territory — whose residents are citizens but cannot vote in presidential elections — and his album "Debí Tirar Más Fotos" tackles themes of gentrification, colonialism, and Puerto Rican identity.

## The "Casita" Controversy

But while Bad Bunny's political stances have earned him widespread admiration, his stage design has sparked a very different kind of debate. "La Casita" — a replica of a 1950s Puerto Rican home from Humacao — was conceived as a tribute to the island's working-class neighborhoods and cultural identity. During the Super Bowl halftime show, celebrities like Jessica Alba, Pedro Pascal, and Cardi B danced on its porch, making it an instant cultural landmark.

However, as [La Libre Belgique reports](https://www.lalibre.be/culture/musique/2026/07/22/la-casita-de-bad-bunny-fait-polemique-on-veut-des-grosses-TKCPN3VQ3JHBTJZWCH2HUFWP6Y/), the pink house has become a source of indignation during the European tour. Critics allege that the porch has become an exclusive VIP area, accessible mainly to celebrities, influencers, and conventionally attractive women — contradicting Bad Bunny's carefully cultivated progressive, anti-machismo image.

Spanish feminist groups have been particularly vocal. As [watson.ch notes](https://www.watson.ch/fr/divertissement/people/249538856-pourquoi-la-casita-de-bad-bunny-fait-polemique), critics argue that the women selected to appear on the porch overwhelmingly conform to thin, young, conventionally attractive standards — a stark contrast to an artist who built his reputation on breaking reggaeton's hyper-masculine mold by performing in drag and advocating for LGBTQ+ rights and women's autonomy.

Spanish music critic Nando Cruz captured the shift in sentiment: "Bad Bunny continues to break records, but now with a bad reputation. He arrived in Spain as a hero, he will leave as a villain."

## Elitism and Gentrification Concerns

The controversy runs deeper than body image. The Spanish daily El Mundo, quoted by [Le Dauphiné Libéré](https://www.ledauphine.com/culture-loisirs/2026/07/03/elitiste-gentrifiee-reservee-aux-vip-pourquoi-la-casita-de-bad-bunny-divise-les-fans), described the situation bluntly: "What was meant to be a sanctuary for diaspora identity and community pride has turned, under the relentless logic of the entertainment industry, into an aristocratic den for international celebrities."

Among the VIP guests spotted on "La Casita" during the European tour were footballers Lamine Yamal and Robert Lewandowski, Spanish actress Ester Expósito, and Marta Ortega — the daughter of Inditex (Zara) founder Amancio Ortega. Bad Bunny has a close commercial partnership with Zara, which recently launched a 150-piece "Benito Antonio" collection, raising questions about the intersection of art, activism, and commerce.

Adding a legal dimension to the controversy, an 84-year-old Puerto Rican homeowner has filed a lawsuit against Bad Bunny and his production companies. The man, whose home in Humacao inspired the "La Casita" design, claims his property was used without proper authorization and that he received only a token payment.

## What's Next for Bad Bunny?

As Bad Bunny's European tour concludes in Brussels, the controversies are unlikely to fade. The lawsuit in Puerto Rico could set precedents for how artists use real people's property as stage designs, while the ongoing debate over "La Casita" may force his team to reconsider the VIP selection process for future tour legs.

For an artist who has built his brand on challenging power structures — from Trump to machismo to the music industry itself — the "casita" controversy represents a fundamental tension between progressive messaging and the commercial machinery of global entertainment. Whether Bad Bunny addresses the criticism directly or lets his music speak for itself remains an open question as he prepares for the next chapter of his historic career.
