# Third Death Confirmed in Manhattan Legionnaires' Disease Outbreak

A third person has died in connection with the ongoing Legionnaires' disease outbreak on Manhattan's Upper East Side, bringing the total number of confirmed cases to at least 74, according to the New York City Department of Health and Mental Hygiene. Health officials say the outbreak, which has hospitalized 59 people since late June, appears to be subsiding after an aggressive remediation campaign.

## Outbreak Details

The outbreak is concentrated in the Carnegie Hill and Yorkville neighborhoods of Manhattan's Upper East Side, affecting ZIP codes 10028, 10128, and 10075. As of July 19, eight patients remain hospitalized while 51 have been discharged, according to [Spectrum News NY1](https://ny1.com/nyc/all-boroughs/health/2026/07/19/second-person-dies-in-upper-east-side-legionnaires--disease-outbreak).

Health Commissioner Dr. Alister F. Martin announced the third death on Sunday evening, expressing condolences while declining to release identifying information about the victim. "It is with great sadness that I report a third person has died in connection with the Legionnaires' disease community cluster on the Upper East Side," Martin said in a statement. "Our hearts are with their family and friends as they mourn their loss."

The first death was reported on Friday, July 17, followed by a second on Saturday and the third on Sunday, according to [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/jul/19/legionnaires-disease-deaths-new-york-city). None of the victims have been publicly identified.

## Source and Containment

Health officials have traced the outbreak to *Legionella* bacteria found in cooling towers on 76 buildings across the Upper East Side, including the Guggenheim Museum, the Metropolitan Museum of Art, and The Spence School. One cooling tower on the Upper West Side also tested positive. All 76 buildings have completed cleaning and disinfection of their cooling towers as of July 16.

The aggressive response appears to be working. New cases dropped from an average of more than eight per day between July 2 and July 8 to fewer than one per day between July 10 and July 17. Dr. Martin described the data as "encouraging," suggesting that "an aggressive strategy of testing, enforcement and remediation" has likely stopped the source of exposure, as [reported by the New York Post](https://nypost.com/2026/07/19/us-news/nyc-legionnaires-clusters-third-death-reported-from-upper-east-side-outbreak/).

No new symptoms have been identified for more than a week, and officials have not pinpointed a single specific building as the definitive source of the outbreak.

## What Is Legionnaires' Disease?

Legionnaires' disease is a severe form of pneumonia caused by *Legionella* bacteria, which grows in warm water and can proliferate in cooling towers, hot tubs, and showerheads. People contract the disease by inhaling tiny droplets of contaminated water vapor. It is not contagious and cannot be spread from person to person.

According to the [USA Today](https://www.usatoday.com/story/news/2026/07/06/nyc-reports-legionnaires-disease-cluster-in-manhattan-is-it-fatal/90819389007/), symptoms include headaches, muscle aches, fever, cough, shortness of breath, chest pain, nausea, and confusion. The incubation period ranges from two to ten days after exposure. The disease is treatable with antibiotics if caught early, but the CDC reports a mortality rate of approximately 10%, with higher risk for people aged 50 and over, smokers, and those with chronic lung disease or weakened immune systems.

The NYC Health Department has assured residents that tap water remains safe to drink, bathe, and cook with.

## Systemic Concerns and Regulatory Failures

This outbreak is the second major Legionnaires' event in New York City in two years, following a 2025 Harlem outbreak that killed seven people and sickened more than 100. The recurrence has raised serious questions about the city's regulatory framework and enforcement.

A [Bisnow analysis](https://www.bisnow.com/new-york/news/property-management/one-in-ten-cooling-tower-inspections-find-public-health-hazards-related-to-legionella-135457) of Health Department data found that 70% of inspections of the city's 5,000 registered cooling towers since 2017 resulted in violations, with nearly 10% classified as public health hazards related to *Legionella*. 

Additionally, the [New York Post reported](https://nypost.com/2026/07/16/us-news/hundreds-of-nyc-cooling-towers-issued-legionella-testing-violations-since-last-year-as-plumbers-union-calls-for-new-regulations/) that more than 250 cooling towers citywide have been cited for Legionella testing violations since 2025, with over half located in Manhattan. In the affected outbreak area, 73% of cooling towers had provided required compliance information, according to the Health Department.

These compliance failures come despite the passage of Local Law 159 of 2025, which introduced stricter testing requirements, and new citywide regulations that took effect on May 8, 2026 — just weeks before the first cases emerged. The regulations require more frequent testing than anywhere else in the country, yet enforcement gaps remain significant.

## What's Next

Health officials will continue monitoring for new cases, though the two-to-ten-day incubation period means additional cases from before the remediation may still emerge. Dr. Martin has vowed to "continue inspecting every cooling tower that tested positive" and to "hold accountable any owner who fails to comply with our public health laws."

The Plumbers Local 1 union has called for even more comprehensive testing requirements, arguing that the current approach of waiting for people to get sick before testing is "a failed, reactive strategy." The union is urging the city to mandate comprehensive building water testing for facilities over a certain size every three to six months.

As New York City confronts its second major Legionnaires' outbreak in as many years, the central question remains: whether stricter regulations alone can prevent future outbreaks, or whether fundamental changes in enforcement and infrastructure maintenance are needed to protect public health.
