# Walloon Tourism Off to a Strong Start This Summer Season

Wallonia's tourism sector is reporting a broadly positive start to the summer 2026 holiday season, according to a mid-July assessment published by [RTBF](https://www.rtbf.be/article/tourisme-en-wallonie-durant-les-vacances-d-ete-un-premier-bilan-globalement-positif-11760254). Early indicators show strong visitor numbers across the region's attractions and holiday destinations, driven largely by sunny weather conditions — though the accompanying heatwave has created a mixed impact on different segments of the tourism industry.

## A Tale of Two Seasons: Winners and Losers Under the Sun

The warm weather has been a clear boon for water-based activities. The Île d’Yvoir beach and restaurant, located along the Meuse River, has seen strong attendance since the start of summer. “We are very satisfied,” said Arnaud Baudoin, manager of the site, in an interview with RTBF. “With the coolness of the water around the island, the temperature drops a few degrees. Our terrace has worked very well. And the water activities too, obviously, with the Meuse being quite warm.”

Chloé Dive, Director of the Explore Meuse Tourism Office in Dinant, confirmed the trend. “Anything related to water and nature reserves, like Furfooz, has been popular,” she told RTBF. However, she noted that when temperatures exceed 36 or 37 degrees Celsius, even forest hikes become unappealing. Museums with air conditioning, she added, have seen increased foot traffic as visitors seek refuge from the heat.

The hospitality sector has experienced more mixed results. In the Fagnes region near the Signal de Botrange, some restaurants report steady business with a notable shift in customer habits. “It was concentrated, given the temperatures, a bit more on the evenings,” said Carole Greoli, room manager at Brasserie Peak. “The afternoons were quieter. People settled on the terraces to enjoy the coolness around 6 PM.”

Other establishments, however, have struggled. Some hospitality operators told RTBF that extreme heat drove customers away from outdoor terraces toward air-conditioned indoor spaces — a luxury not all restaurants can afford.

## Building on a Strong Spring

The positive summer follows an encouraging spring season. In May, Walloon Tourism Minister Valérie Lescrenier (Les Engagés) published a spring holiday assessment showing a 1.2% overall growth in tourist volume compared to spring 2025, as [La Libre](https://www.lalibre.be/dernieres-depeches/2026/05/08/un-premier-bilan-touristique-positif-a-lissue-des-vacances-de-printemps-en-wallonie-F47JDQL3ZVDPPI34LVEFPQYDZI/) reported. Camping reservation rates exceeded 60%, continuing a strong trajectory that saw 25% growth in 2025.

“These first trends of spring 2026 confirm the solidity of Walloon tourism and the importance of proximity tourism, complemented by a diversification of foreign clienteles,” Minister Lescrenier said at the time, as reported by [La Libre](https://www.lalibre.be/economie/conjoncture/2026/05/08/le-secteur-poursuit-son-evolution-positive-bilan-encourageant-des-vacances-de-printemps-pour-le-tourisme-en-wallonie-6C4ZHOJCQZFNXALCCKP4CK46O4/).

A notable trend from the spring assessment was a decrease in Dutch tourist numbers, traditionally a major source market for Walloon tourism. This decline was compensated by growth in domestic travel and diversification of international visitors.

## Structural Trends and Policy Context

Several structural trends continue to shape Walloon tourism. The shift toward proximity tourism — domestic and short-haul travel — remains strong even as international travel has fully resumed. Last-minute booking culture is increasingly prevalent, giving operators greater flexibility but also introducing uncertainty.

Minister Lescrenier has been active in promoting the region’s offerings. In June, she launched the “Patrimoine en Spectacle” summer heritage initiative, showcasing Walloon cultural sites through performances and events, as [L’Avenir](https://www.lavenir.net/actu/belgique/2026/06/08/cet-ete-le-patrimoine-wallon-se-donne-en-spectacle-PZ6KQNSCNRG4LBZQXUY2QM4ECY/) reported. The region is also set to host the Université du Tourisme Durable (Sustainable Tourism University) in Liège in 2027, marking the first time the event leaves France.

## What’s Next

The current assessment is described as a “premier bilan” (first assessment) at mid-July, meaning comprehensive data on occupancy rates and revenue figures are not yet available. The findings are based on qualitative reports from tourism operators rather than hard statistics.

As the summer season progresses, key questions remain: How will the summer 2026 performance compare numerically to summer 2025? What impact has the heatwave had on tourism revenue across different sectors? And has the decline in Dutch tourists continued into the peak season?

For now, the early signs are encouraging. Wallonia’s tourism sector appears to be navigating the challenges of an unusually hot summer with resilience, adapting to changing weather patterns and visitor preferences alike. A more complete picture will emerge as the season unfolds and comprehensive data becomes available.
