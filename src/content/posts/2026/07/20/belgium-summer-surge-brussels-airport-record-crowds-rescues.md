# Belgium's Summer Surge: Record Airport Crowds and Rescues

Belgium is experiencing the full force of summer on Monday as a convergence of holidays, heat, and travel creates both opportunity and risk across the country. Brussels Airport is handling its busiest day of the year with 92,000 passengers, while on the coast, a dramatic rescue and a positive tourism outlook paint a mixed picture of the season.

## Brussels Airport: A Perfect Storm of Travelers

Brussels Airport is facing its busiest day of the summer, with approximately 92,000 travelers expected—about 41,000 departing and 51,000 arriving. According to [HLN](https://www.hln.be/zaventem/ik-heb-het-hier-nog-nooit-zo-druk-gezien-lange-wachtrijen-op-brussels-airport-waar-vandaag-92-000-reizigers-verwacht-worden~aa7e36f0/), the surge is the result of multiple factors converging: the construction holiday period, summer vacations, a bridge day leading into Belgium's National Day on July 21, and the return of Tomorrowland festival-goers.

"Today we expect about 92,000 passengers," said spokesperson Jeffrey Franssens. "The combination of the construction holiday, summer vacation, the bridge day, and returning Tomorrowland visitors brings all these travelers together today."

Long queues formed at check-in counters and baggage drop-off areas, though security and passport control for departing passengers reportedly ran smoothly. Travelers arriving from outside the European Union face approximately 45-minute waits at passport control due to the new European Entry/Exit System (EES), while Belgian and other European travelers can use automatic e-gates with minimal delay.

Passengers interviewed at the airport reported arriving three hours early, with most experiencing manageable wait times. Josée, a traveler from Breda in the Netherlands, told reporters she had "never seen it so busy here," while noting that Brussels Airport remains her preferred choice over Schiphol due to proximity.

## Drowning Rescue on the Belgian Coast

On Sunday afternoon, a potentially tragic incident was averted on the beach at Ostend. Three boys aged 10, 11, and 16 were rescued from drowning by beach lifeguards at the height of the Royal Galleries, according to [La Libre Belgique](https://www.lalibre.be/belgique/societe/2026/07/20/trois-garcons-sauves-de-la-noyade-sur-la-cote-belge-ils-ont-ete-transportes-a-lhopital-IIYOIIETNBAWVA2TYDLPOUFV2Q/).

The boys were swimming in a supervised bathing zone when lifeguards spotted them in difficulty and intervened quickly. All three were brought to shore and taken to hospital for observation. The youngest, aged 10, suffered mild hypothermia.

"In accordance with standard procedure, they were all transported to hospital," said Eline Goeminne, spokesperson for the Ostend police zone.

The incident highlights ongoing safety concerns on the Belgian coast during peak summer, when crowded beaches and warm weather can create dangerous swimming conditions.

## Wallonia Tourism: A Positive Start

Despite the challenges, Wallonia's tourism sector is reporting a promising start to the summer season. As [RTBF](https://www.rtbf.be/article/tourisme-en-wallonie-durant-les-vacances-d-ete-un-premier-bilan-globalement-positif-11760254) reports, water-based activities have benefited most from the warm weather.

At Île d'Yvoir, manager Arnaud Baudoin expressed satisfaction: "We are very satisfied. With the cool water around the island, the temperature drops a few degrees. Our terrace has done very well. And the water activities too, obviously, with the Meuse being quite warm."

However, the extreme heat has had mixed effects. While nature reserves like Furfooz remain popular, temperatures exceeding 36-37°C have deterred hikers. Museums with air conditioning have seen increased visitors, and the hospitality sector reports a shift toward evening dining as customers seek cooler hours.

"It has concentrated, given the temperatures, a bit more on the evenings," noted Carole Greoli, restaurant manager at Brasserie Peak near the Signal de Botrange. "Afternoons have been quieter. People settled on the terraces to enjoy the coolness around 6 PM."

## National Day Fireworks Canceled Due to Drought

Belgium's National Day on July 21 will look different this year, as many municipalities have canceled their traditional fireworks displays due to persistent drought and fire risk. According to [RTBF](https://www.rtbf.be/article/pas-de-feux-d-artifice-pour-la-fete-nationale-dans-de-nombreuses-communes-11759409), multiple towns in Hainaut province—including Tournai, Dour, Comines-Warneton, and Jurbise—have called off their displays.

Hainaut Governor Tommy Leclercq issued a decree banning ground fires, later extended to July 31. The Province of Namur has also extended its fireworks ban until July 27, with the City of Namur canceling its July 20 display. In Wallonia Picardy, fire services recommend complete cancellation of fireworks, sky lanterns, and festive fires until July 24.

"I understand the disappointment this cancellation may cause among the population, but current conditions require us to act with prudence and responsibility," said Marie Christine Marghem, Mayor of Tournai.

## What This Means for Belgium

These four stories, while distinct, are woven together by the summer season and extreme weather. The heatwave has boosted water-based tourism but created fire risks that forced fireworks cancellations. It has contributed to dangerous swimming conditions on the coast while driving record numbers of travelers through Brussels Airport.

As Belgium celebrates its National Day on July 21, the country finds itself navigating the familiar joys of summer—travel, beach days, and celebration—alongside the growing challenges of a warming climate. With drought conditions persisting and temperatures remaining high, the question of how to balance tradition with safety will only become more pressing in the weeks ahead.
