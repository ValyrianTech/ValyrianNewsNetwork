# Maine Democrats Turn Chaos Into Coronation for Troy Jackson

What began as a chaotic scramble for a replacement nominee has become a decisive coronation. Troy Jackson, a former Maine Senate president and logger from Allagash, has emerged as the presumptive Democratic nominee for U.S. Senate after securing at least 456 of 500 pledged delegates during county caucuses held July 18-19. The consolidation of support behind Jackson follows the dramatic implosion of Graham Platner's campaign after sexual assault allegations forced him to withdraw, setting off an unprecedented nominating process that now appears all but settled.

## From Chaos to Clarity

The Democratic primary in Maine took a stunning turn on July 6, when [Politico reported](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/07/06/graham-platner-sexual-assault-allegation-00987737) that a woman who had previously dated Platner accused him of raping her while he was intoxicated nearly five years prior. Platner denied the accusation, but the report triggered a cascade of additional allegations and a swift collapse of endorsements from virtually every Democrat who had once backed him. By July 9, Platner formally suspended his campaign.

The Maine Democratic Party then initiated an unprecedented process: 16 county parties elected slates of 601 total delegates, who will vote to select the new nominee at a July 25 convention in Bangor. What followed was a compressed, high-stakes organizing battle that Jackson's labor-backed machine won decisively.

## Jackson's Dominant Delegate Sweep

Over the weekend of July 18-19, Jackson won at least 456 of 500 delegates awarded during county caucuses, powered by a relentless organizing push backed by labor unions. According to the [Bangor Daily News](https://www.bangordailynews.com/2026/07/20/politics/elections/inside-troy-jacksons-delegate-sweep/), Jackson's supporters reached out via phone, text, email, WhatsApp, and social media, building a network that proved far more effective than any competitor's.

"We followed every rule. We just organized," said Lori Poland, a Jackson delegate from Windham, in response to social media comments suggesting new Senate candidates were trying to "steal" the election.

Jackson's dominance was so complete that his top competitors — Secretary of State Shenna Bellows, former public health chief Nirav Shah, Maine Beer Co. co-founder Dan Kleban, and former congressional candidate Jordan Wood — all withdrew from the race on July 19, clearing his path to the nomination.

## A Progressive With Labor Credentials

Jackson, 58, served as Maine Senate president from 2018 to 2024 and has represented parts of northern Aroostook County for much of his legislative career. His background as a fifth-generation logger from rural Allagash gives him a working-class credibility that supporters believe will resonate in a general election.

"When session would get over, he would always stay and work," Joanne Twomey, a former state representative and former mayor of Biddeford, told the Bangor Daily News. "He wasn't born with a silver spoon in his mouth. He has a different perspective on really representing working people."

Jason Shedlock, president of the Maine Building and Construction Trades Council, described the organizing effort behind Jackson's sweep: "When labor groups get knocked down, they're going to come back. I might ride home with my Bluetooth on and talk to people."

## The General Election: Jackson vs. Collins

Jackson is now poised to face Republican incumbent Susan Collins, who is seeking a sixth term. Collins is the only Republican senator representing a state that Donald Trump did not win in any of his three presidential campaigns, making this one of the most competitive Senate races in 2026. Democrats have not won a Senate election in Maine since 1988, when George J. Mitchell was re-elected.

According to [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_United_States_Senate_election_in_Maine), Collins has raised $14.9 million and has $9.7 million cash on hand as of May 20, 2026. Conservative groups have already invested tens of millions of dollars in ads supporting her.

Ethan Standel, a Jackson supporter from Saco, told the Bangor Daily News: "Troy has a history of winning in deep red districts. I think that just makes him the most likely candidate to win against Republican Sen. Susan Collins."

## What's Next

The Maine Democratic Party has until July 27 to formally declare a replacement nominee with the secretary of state. The nominating convention is scheduled for July 25 in Bangor, where Jackson is expected to be officially selected. With roughly 100 days until the November 3 general election, Jackson will need to quickly scale up his campaign infrastructure and fundraising operation to compete with Collins's well-funded machine.

As [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/jul/20/troy-jackson-maine-senate-race-graham-platner) noted, Jackson's campaign said on Monday it was turning its "full focus" to defeating Collins after the weekend's surge of delegate support. The race is widely viewed as critical to Democrats' hopes of winning back the Senate, with Maine representing one of the party's few genuine pickup opportunities.

Jackson himself summed up the moment with characteristic understatement: "I guess they got what they wanted," he said, laughing, referring to supporters who urged him to run for federal office.

Now he has to prove they made the right choice.
