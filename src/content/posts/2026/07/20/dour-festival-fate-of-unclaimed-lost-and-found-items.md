# Dour Festival: The Fate of Unclaimed Lost and Found Items

As the 36th edition of the Dour Festival concluded on July 19, 2026, organizers are now dealing with an inevitable aftermath: hundreds of unclaimed lost items left behind by festival-goers. With approximately 220,000 to 230,000 attendees over five days, the festival's lost and found stand — managed by the National Lottery — has collected everything from mobile phones and wallets to tents and car keys. But what happens to the items that are never reclaimed?

## A Well-Organized Operation

The lost and found stand at Dour is no small operation. Managed by the National Lottery as a festival partner, the stand operates daily from 10:00 to 20:00 throughout the event. According to [RTBF](https://www.rtbf.be/article/dour-festival-que-deviennent-les-objets-trouves-non-reclames-11760471), between 500 and 1,000 items are collected and returned to their owners during the festival itself.

Benjamin D'Agresta, Partnership Manager at the National Lottery, describes the meticulous process: "The first thing we do is we have shared Excel spreadsheets on different computers. We inventory everything. We really catalog each object we find and note all the distinctive signs of these objects to be able to search in this table later. The absolute rule is to be super well organized, to have an inventory that is clear and up to date."

## What Gets Lost — and What Gets Returned

Mobile phones top the list of most frequently lost and recovered items, followed closely by identity cards, wallets, bank cards, and keys — both car and house keys. Toiletry bags left at campsite showers are also a common find.

D'Agresta notes the range of items that pass through their hands: "We find everything, since everything festival-goers can bring, they're likely to lose it. What we return most obviously are phones, and then identity cards, wallets, bank cards, many as well."

Some discoveries are more personal than others. "Sometimes people bring us bags with absolutely people's lives inside!" he adds.

## The Festival-Goer Who Lost Everything

Among the more unusual stories, D'Agresta recalls a festival-goer from the previous year who lost his tent, suitcase, and food bag on the very first day. "He spent the 4 days of Dour without his gear, and he came to retrieve it on the last day," D'Agresta says with amusement. "I don't know how he lived during those 4 days of festival, but for him in terms of value, he had a great experience for 4 days without his gear!"

## What Happens After the Festival?

Once the festival ends, a selective transfer process begins. From Wednesday, July 22, 2026, unclaimed valuable items — specifically mobile phones and official documents such as identity cards and driver's licenses — are transferred to the Commune of Dour. According to the [Commune de Dour's official notice](https://www.communedour.be/actualites/objets-trouves-au-dour-festival-uniquement-les?u=8680e6a2519849c090c2c81967452435), all other items — including tents, clothing, bags, and camping gear — are not retained by the municipality.

Festival-goers who believe they have lost a phone or official document must email `objetstrouves@communedour.be` with their name, phone number, and phone brand or model. No phone hotline is available due to the high volume of recovered phones. To verify ownership, claimants must provide the unlock code or SIM card PIN.

## Theft Versus Loss

Not every lost item is simply misplaced. D'Agresta acknowledges that some items reported as lost are in fact stolen and are never recovered. The lost and found system, while efficient, cannot address theft — a separate security concern that remains a challenge for large-scale festivals.

## Sentimental Value Matters

While practical items like receipts are thrown away, the team takes care with objects that may carry sentimental meaning. "Sometimes we have small objects that may have sentimental value, a cheap little ring, whatever, but we keep it anyway," D'Agresta explains. "You never know if it has value for someone."

## Looking Ahead

As Dour Festival continues to grow — with nearly a quarter-million attendees in 2026 — the logistical challenge of managing lost property will only increase. The partnership with the National Lottery provides dedicated resources, and the structured cataloging system ensures efficiency. But for the hundreds of items that remain unclaimed each year, the message is clear: if you lose something valuable at Dour, act quickly. After July 22, only phones and official documents will be waiting — and only at the town hall.

For those who lost non-valuable items like tents or clothing, the festival offers a different kind of lesson: sometimes, the best memories are made with nothing at all.
