# Andy Burnham Sworn In as UK PM, Faces Daunting In-Tray

Andy Burnham was formally appointed as the 59th Prime Minister of the United Kingdom on Monday, succeeding Sir Keir Starmer after a rapid political transition that underscores a decade of extraordinary instability at the top of British government. Burnham, 56, met King Charles III at Buckingham Palace before delivering his first address from 10 Downing Street, declaring: "I'm ready to be prime minister. Change begins now."

The former Mayor of Greater Manchester becomes the seventh UK prime minister in a decade, following David Cameron, Theresa May, Boris Johnson, Liz Truss, Rishi Sunak, and Starmer. He takes office at a moment of profound national challenge, inheriting an economy beset by weak growth, high public debt, and a lingering cost-of-living crisis, alongside strained public services and a volatile international landscape.

## The Road to Number 10

Burnham's ascent was unusually swift. After Starmer resigned on 22 June 2026 — triggered by heavy Labour losses in local elections as [Nigel Farage's Reform UK party made major gains](https://www.usatoday.com/story/news/world/2026/07/20/andy-burnham-uk-prime-minister/90965227007/) — Burnham returned to Parliament by winning the Makerfield by-election on 18 June. He was confirmed as Labour leader unopposed at a special party conference on 17 July, having secured over 90% of Labour MP nominations.

[According to the BBC](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/c992mg1202eo), Burnham was sworn in as an MP to loud cheers from Labour colleagues, with the mood described as "beyond celebratory" in Westminster Hall. The new prime minister had previously served as Health Secretary, Culture Secretary, and Chief Secretary to the Treasury under Tony Blair and Gordon Brown, before spending nine years as Mayor of Greater Manchester.

## An Economy Under Pressure

Burnham's most immediate challenge is fiscal. The UK has experienced one of the largest increases in government debt of any advanced economy over the past two decades, and the Office for Budget Responsibility has warned of an "unsustainable and ever-rising path" for debt levels. [The Independent reported](https://www.independent.co.uk/news/business/obr-warning-burnham-uk-economy-b3010922.html) that under the OBR's baseline scenario, UK debt could soar from around 95% of GDP to roughly 300% by 2075-2076 if left unchecked.

The International Monetary Fund has warned Burnham against higher public spending, urging focus on deficit reduction, while the OECD has advised Britain to maintain budget discipline and tackle high pension spending. Yet the new prime minister faces simultaneous demands for increased investment in defense, healthcare, social care, and public infrastructure — all with limited fiscal headroom.

## A Leader of Contrasts

Burnham presents a sharp stylistic departure from his predecessor. Where Starmer was a polished, lawyerly technocrat who struggled to connect with voters, Burnham is an emotive communicator who earned the nickname "King of the North" during the COVID-19 pandemic for challenging Boris Johnson's government over regional lockdown restrictions.

[Writing in The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2026/jun/22/burnham-prepares-power-emotional-starmer-uk-prime-minister-resignation), political correspondents noted that Burnham is expected to begin setting out his policies with a series of speeches demonstrating a symbolic shift from Starmer's government, starting with the economy and devolution. Ed Miliband, the former Labour leader, is widely tipped to become Chancellor of the Exchequer — a move that would signal a leftward shift on economic policy.

Matt Beech, a political scientist at the University of Hull, described Burnham as "much more electable than Starmer" who "speaks human" and "has charisma." But Beech cautioned: "Let's be clear: Andy Burnham is not Tony Blair."

## Foreign Policy Challenges

On the international stage, Burnham must navigate a relationship with US President Donald Trump, who has described the new prime minister as "extremely liberal." Starmer had built a working relationship with Trump and secured lower US tariff rates for Britain. According to a senior British official who spoke to USA TODAY, Burnham is a "seasoned political operator" who knows how to adjust his style to work effectively with almost any world leader — especially Trump.

Reports indicate Burnham may announce new North Sea drilling licenses, a move seen as placating Trump that would break a Labour campaign pledge. The new prime minister also faces pressure over UK policy on the Israel-Palestine conflict, continued support for Ukraine, and strengthening ties with the European Union post-Brexit.

## What Comes Next

With Parliament in recess until September, Burnham has a summer "honeymoon period" to set his agenda without immediate parliamentary scrutiny. He has promised a "listening tour" across the UK, including visits to Port Talbot in Wales and Aberdeen in Scotland, and plans to establish a Prime Ministerial base in Manchester under his "No 10 North" initiative.

Timothy Bale, Professor of Politics at Queen Mary, University of London, summarized the central questions facing the new premier: "How to get the economy growing faster, how to improve vital areas like health and defense when there's no money around, how to signal that the government's getting a grip on irregular migration — and how to deal with an erratic U.S. president in Donald Trump."

As the seventh prime minister in a decade, Burnham's ability to provide stable, effective leadership will be closely watched. The next general election is scheduled no later than August 2029, giving him approximately three years to deliver visible improvements — or risk becoming another short-lived occupant of Number 10.
