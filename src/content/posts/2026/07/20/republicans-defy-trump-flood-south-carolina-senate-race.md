# Republicans Defy Trump, Flood South Carolina Senate Race

President Donald Trump's endorsement of Sen. Darline Graham Nordone for a full six-year term in the South Carolina Senate seat left vacant by the death of her brother, Lindsey Graham, has failed to clear the Republican field. Multiple candidates have jumped into the August 11 special primary, openly defying the president's pick and signaling a potential erosion of his iron grip on the GOP as the 2026 midterms approach.

According to [Fox News](https://www.foxnews.com/politics/republicans-defy-trump-jump-south-carolina-senate-race-despite-his-handpicked-successor), Reps. Russell Fry and Ralph Norman, along with businessman Mark Lynch, have all entered the race despite Trump's "complete and total endorsement" of Graham Nordone. The special primary was triggered by Lindsey Graham's sudden death from cardiac arrest on July 11 at age 71.

## Background: A Rare Open Seat

Lindsey Graham had just won the June 9 Republican primary with 56.8% of the vote when he died unexpectedly. Under South Carolina law, because Graham was the Republican nominee, the state GOP must hold a special primary to select a replacement candidate for the November general election.

On July 13, Trump publicly recommended that Gov. Henry McMaster appoint Darline Graham Nordone — Lindsey Graham's 62-year-old sister — to serve the remainder of his term. McMaster did so, and she was sworn in on July 14 as South Carolina's first-ever woman U.S. Senator. Graham Nordone had never held elected office before; she previously served on the South Carolina Commission for the Blind and worked with people with disabilities.

Trump announced his endorsement on July 17, writing on Truth Social: "I asked Darline, for the Good of our Nation, to run for the U.S. Senate in the Special Republican Primary on Tuesday, August 11, 2026. I hope Darline does this, in that there would be nobody better to honor the legacy of her beloved brother, Lindsey."

As of July 20, Graham Nordone has not committed to running for a full term, though she released an open letter saying she "did not seek this moment, but it's here" and asking for prayers.

## The Rebellion

Rep. Ralph Norman, a Freedom Caucus member representing South Carolina's 5th District, announced his candidacy on July 18. As [Townhall](https://townhall.com/tipsheet/josephchalfant/2026/07/18/ralph-norman-jumps-into-sc-senate-race-after-trump-backs-darline-graham-n2679704) reported, Norman told Fox News' Kayleigh McEnany: "He decided to go with Darline, who's a nice person, and he decided to go with her and I respect that, but that doesn't deter my plans. I'm in this to win." Norman has been endorsed by Sens. Rick Scott (R-FL) and Mike Lee (R-UT).

Rep. Russell Fry, who represents South Carolina's 7th District, announced his entry on July 20. According to [Newsmax](https://www.newsmax.com/john-gizzi/russell-fry-lindsey-graham-darline-graham-nardine/2026/07/20/id/1263409/), Fry had been considered a potential Trump favorite before the Darline Graham endorsement, and had passed three bills in his first four years in Congress. In his campaign announcement, Fry framed his candidacy around honoring Graham's legacy by fighting "alongside the president just as steadfastly as he did."

Mark Lynch, an appliance store owner who finished second in the June 9 primary with 29% of the vote, is also in the race, having run as a pro-Trump, anti-establishment candidate.

## A Warning Sign for Trump?

The rebellion comes on the heels of another setback for Trump in South Carolina. In the June 2026 gubernatorial runoff, Trump-endorsed Lt. Gov. Pamela Evette lost decisively to Attorney General Alan Wilson — a sign that the president's endorsement power may be waning in the state.

South Carolina-based Republican consultant Dave Wilson told Fox News Digital: "South Carolina voters want to have a choice and not be told who they need to be voting for. The president's quick endorsement of Darline Graham was very symbolic. We're seeing in South Carolina that his endorsement didn't carry as much weight as people thought it was going to."

Veteran Republican strategist Ryan Williams added: "If you're an aspiring House member in South Carolina, this is your time to run regardless of whom the president is supporting. There's no downside for running. They're all going to run as Trump Republicans, and they're all going to flatter the president on the campaign trail."

## What's Next

Candidate filing opens July 21 and closes July 28 — a compressed timeline for such a consequential race. The August 11 special primary requires a majority to win; otherwise, a runoff follows on August 25. With at least three serious candidates and potentially more — former Gov. Mark Sanford has signaled interest by changing his campaign committee name to "Sanford for Senate" — a runoff is highly likely.

The eventual Republican nominee will face Dr. Annie Andrews, a pediatrician who won the Democratic primary with 61.5% of the vote. An Impact Research poll conducted before Lindsey Graham's death showed Andrews trailing Graham by only 3 percentage points, suggesting the general election could be more competitive than expected in a state where Democrats have not won a Senate race since 1998.

Graham Nordone's decision on whether to run remains the single most consequential unknown. If she declines, Trump would lose his preferred candidate and would need to pivot — potentially losing face in a state he won by 18 points in 2024. If she runs and loses, it would mark the most significant defeat of Trump's endorsement power in the 2026 cycle, with implications for Republican primaries nationwide.

This race is a bellwether for Trump's influence heading into the midterms. If his endorsed candidate falters in deep-red South Carolina, it could reshape GOP primary dynamics across the country.
