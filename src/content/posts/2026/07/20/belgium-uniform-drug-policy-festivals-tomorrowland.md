# Belgium Debates Uniform Drug Policy for Festival Season

Belgian authorities are debating whether to implement a uniform drug policy across the country's major festivals, including the world-renowned Tomorrowland, as experts caution that no single measure can fully address the complex challenge of drug use at large-scale events. The discussion, reported by [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/07/16/drugs-festivals-uniforme-aanpak-tomorrowland/), seeks to balance public safety with harm reduction while respecting the unique character of each festival.

## The Current Patchwork

All Belgian festivals currently operate a zero-tolerance policy. Festival-goers caught with drugs for personal use must surrender them and pay an immediate fine, while dealers face criminal prosecution. At Reggae Geel, for instance, fines are set at €150 for soft drugs, €300 for hard drugs, and €450 for possession of both. However, enforcement varies significantly between festivals and police zones, creating confusion—particularly for international visitors.

"Those who travel via Amsterdam to Tomorrowland often see it as one area with the same rules," said Ine Van Wymersch, Belgium's National Drug Commissioner. "But if you can't buy cannabis at the festival, it's not legal."

## The Scale of the Problem

Despite high-profile incidents, the actual proportion of drug-related medical cases at festivals remains low. In 2024, only 1.6% of medical interventions at Tomorrowland were drug-related—approximately 29 cases out of thousands of treatments. In 2022, the most common reason for first-aid visits across both festival weekends was sprained ankles, not drugs.

Still, enforcement figures tell a significant story. In 2025, 382 people were caught with user-quantity drugs at Tomorrowland, and 67 were arrested, including 23 dealers. In November 2025, [19 dealers were sentenced](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2025/11/26/vonnis-drugs-tomorrowland-drugsdealers-coke-werkstraf-celstraf-b/) to prison terms of up to 40 months and fines reaching €24,000. At [Rock Werchter 2026](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/07/06/128-mensen-betrapt-met-drugs-tijdens-rock-werchter/), 128 festival-goers were caught with drugs, including 9 crew members caught during backstage checks.

## Uniformity vs. Flexibility

The central debate pits those seeking consistent rules against those who argue each festival requires a tailored approach. Lies Gremeaux, Head of the Drugs Unit at Sciensano, argues that a minimum set of rules would reduce "gray zones" and interpretation gaps. "In the past, everyone worked on festivals in silos," she said. "Some festivals have already grown in that regard, but there is still room for improvement."

Conversely, Van Wymersch stated that "a uniform approach is not necessarily desirable," while Patrice Degobert, Chief of Police at Dour Festival, emphasized that "each festival is unique." Dour's visitor numbers are nearly three times the population of the village that hosts it, creating unique logistical challenges.

Charlotte Colman, Belgium's Interfederal Drug Coordinator, noted that police zones operate within a general legal framework but can set local priorities—a principle known as the "opportunity principle." The National Drug Commissariat and the General Cell Drug Policy (ACD) are working on an advisory framework to provide more guidance without imposing rigid uniformity.

## Harm Reduction vs. Zero Tolerance

A key tension exists between Belgium's official zero-tolerance stance and emerging harm reduction approaches gaining ground across Europe. While the Netherlands allows drug testing at 70 locations nationwide, Belgium does not permit user-side testing, as experts say it creates a "false sense of security."

"White powders are becoming increasingly complex," warned Gremeaux. "These days you really don't know what's in them anymore." The National Institute for Criminalistics and Criminology (NICC) tests confiscated drugs on-site at major festivals, but this information is used for monitoring, not individual harm reduction.

Mercy bins—voluntary drug disposal points at festival entrances—exist at Tomorrowland, Dour, and Paradise City, but Colman cautioned that "without context, that doesn't work. It's often just used as a trash can."

## The Tomorrowland Factor

Tomorrowland's unique position as a global festival—attracting approximately 400,000 visitors annually across two weekends—creates specific challenges. International visitors bring expectations shaped by their home countries' drug laws, and the concentration of global visitors on a small site creates what Colman described as "a doubling of risks."

Over 11 years, six people have died on Tomorrowland grounds, including a 35-year-old Canadian woman in 2025 and two individuals in 2023. Despite these tragedies, authorities stress that drug enforcement is just one aspect of festival safety.

"A safe festival is about so much more: crowd management, safe traffic, what if severe weather strikes?" Van Wymersch said.

## What's Next

After the 2026 festival season, the National Drug Commissariat and ACD plan to compile "good practices" from all festivals, aiming to create a more coherent framework without mandating a one-size-fits-all approach. As Gremeaux concluded: "No single measure is the holy grail. A lot has already changed, but many people know it can be even better and are working hard on that behind the scenes."

The outcome of this debate could reshape how Belgium—and potentially other European nations—approach drug policy at major cultural events, balancing enforcement with the practical realities of large-scale festival environments.
