# Trust Amid Floodwaters: 25 Car Keys in a Rescue Effort

When Typhoon Bavi unleashed a "once-in-a-century" rainstorm on the northeastern Chinese city of Shenyang on July 13, 2026, the streets of the Cuita Community in Shenhe District quickly became rivers. But amid the rising waters, an extraordinary act of trust unfolded that has since captured hearts across China: 25 stranded vehicle owners entrusted their car keys to community volunteers, and every single key was returned safely.

## The Storm That Struck

Typhoon Bavi, the ninth named storm of the 2026 Pacific typhoon season, made its presence felt with devastating force. Between midnight and late afternoon on July 13, Shenyang recorded an average precipitation of 129.5 mm, with a maximum hourly rainfall of 74.4 mm — equivalent to 30 tons of water per basketball court every hour, according to meteorological analysis by [Zhejiang Online](https://zjnews.zjol.com.cn/yc/qmt/202607/t20260714_31786894.shtml). The city activated its highest-level (Level 1) flood emergency response, suspending subway operations, closing roads, and mandating work-from-home arrangements.

## A Community Rallies

As floodwaters rose to waist level on the road in front of the Cuita Community center, Community Party Secretary Zhang Yi and her 15 staff members, wearing red volunteer vests, began directing traffic and warning drivers to turn back. Despite their efforts, some vehicles attempting to cross the flooded road became stranded.

The first to get stuck was resident Gao Yang's SUV. "Everyone, let's help together!" Zhang called out, organizing resident volunteers, property security guards, and nearby police officers to push the vehicle to higher ground. What started as a single rescue quickly multiplied. As [People's Daily](http://society.people.com.cn/n1/2026/0720/c1008-40763722.html) reported, Zhang mobilized Party members in the vicinity, and soon over 50 people were working together to push stranded vehicles to safety. In total, 25 cars were rescued.

## The Keys of Trust

With tow trucks unable to reach them through the flooded streets, vehicle owners faced a dilemma: they needed to attend to urgent personal matters, but couldn't wait indefinitely with their cars. Gao Yang was the first to ask Zhang an unusual question: "Secretary Zhang, can I leave my car keys with you for safekeeping? When the tow truck comes, you can hand them over."

Zhang admitted to initial hesitation. "What if the car gets scratched or the key gets lost while I'm holding it?" she thought. But then, as she later told reporters, she reminded herself: "I'm a Party member. Solving the people's difficulties — I can't be afraid of taking responsibility." She agreed.

Other owners followed suit. One by one, 25 sets of car keys — representing 25 acts of trust — were placed in a small box at the community service counter.

## Every Key Accounted For

When the rain finally stopped, Zhang's phone began ringing with tow truck drivers calling to retrieve vehicles. She verified each driver's identity by calling the vehicle owner before releasing the keys. The first tow truck arrived that same afternoon; the last one came on July 16. Zhang's own water-damaged car was the final vehicle to be towed from the parking lot.

Not a single key was lost or mismatched. One tow truck driver, upon seeing that the community had been safeguarding residents' car keys, remarked: "This community service is so thoughtful!"

## Broader Context: Grassroots Response to Crisis

The story from Cuita Community is part of a larger narrative of community-level disaster response across Shenyang. According to the [CPC Member Network](https://www.12371.cn/2026/07/14/ARTI1783989390847800.shtml), over 3,500 flood control personnel and 16,849 four-level flood response officials were deployed across the city. Across Liaoning Province, more than 36,000 people were evacuated as 14 rivers exceeded warning levels.

## A Symbol of Mutual Trust

"Solving the urgent and difficult problems of the masses, delivering services to the hearts of the people — this is the duty of our community Party committee," Zhang said. The story, published in People's Daily on July 20, has been widely shared across Chinese media as a powerful example of mutual trust between residents and community officials during natural disasters.

As Zhang and her team turned their attention to repairing the flower beds in front of the community center on July 19 — "This is the scenery at the residents' doorstep — it must be repaired well," she explained — the 25 keys in that small box stand as a quiet testament to what communities can achieve when trust flows in both directions.

## What to Watch For

As China's flood season continues, the story from Cuita Community raises important questions about the role of grassroots organizations in disaster response. With extreme weather events becoming more frequent, the social capital built through community-level trust may prove as vital as any physical infrastructure in weathering future storms.
