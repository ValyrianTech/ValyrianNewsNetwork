# 300 Young Footballers Take Patro MM to Court Over License

In an unprecedented legal action that has shaken Belgian youth football, nearly 300 children and teenagers from Patro Eisden Maasmechelen (Patro MM) and their parents have taken the club’s case to the civil court in Brussels after the Pro League denied the club an elite youth license. The young players, some as young as seven, now face losing their top sports status, their places at specialized schools, and potentially their dreams of becoming professional footballers.

“Mama, they’re taking my dream away,” 14-year-old Brandon Domi, an attacker from Dilsen-Stokkem, told [HLN](https://www.hln.be/binnenland/ongezien-300-voetballertjes-patro-mm-en-hun-ouders-naar-rechter-omdat-club-niet-meer-bij-elitejeugd-mag-spelen-mama-ze-pakken-mijn-droom-af~a790295d/). “If Patro Eisden can’t play in elite, I immediately lose my top sports status and possibly my school.”

## Why the License Was Denied

The Pro League evaluates youth academies annually on infrastructure, staff, coaching, and player progression. Patro MM received two failing grades: the club allegedly lacked a qualified doctor or physiotherapist at every home match, and its administrative manager was not receiving a minimum wage.

During the court hearing, the Pro League’s lawyers, Anton Bloemen and Mathieu Baert, raised a more serious allegation. “There are serious grounds not to grant the license, including forged documents regarding the presence of a doctor or physiotherapist,” they stated. “That is the whole essence.”

The club’s lawyer, Walter Van Steenbrugge, unsuccessfully appealed the decision at the Belgian Arbitration Court for Sport (BAS) before turning to the civil court, as [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/07/20/patro-eisden-300-jeugdspelers-licentie-elitecompetitie/) reported.

## A Crisis for Families

For the young players, the stakes extend far beyond football. Many hold a “topsportstatuut” (top sports status) that allows them to attend specialized schools with adapted training schedules. Losing elite club status means losing that status — and potentially their school placement.

“Don’t underestimate this,” Van Steenbrugge told the court. “Some children have a top sports status and attend a top sports school. If they can’t play in an elite competition, they immediately lose their top sports status and possibly their school. For them, this is a gigantic drama.”

Brandon’s mother, Mimoza Domi, described the family’s anguish: “As a family, we have organized ourselves around Brandon’s football career for years. You wouldn’t believe how many kilometers we’ve driven to take him to and from football. Brandon is naturally worried. He panics and fears he will lose everything. My mother’s heart is breaking.”

Damian Awuah, also 14, faces the same uncertainty. His father Frank told reporters the family pays around €400 per month for Damian’s boarding at the top sports school in Genk. “We’re worried,” he said. “This uncertainty weighs on my son and the whole family.”

## A Race Against the Clock

The timing of the license denial has compounded the crisis. The transfer deadline for youth players passed before the decision was made, leaving families with no alternative clubs. The competition format has already been published — without Patro MM.

Van Steenbrugge is asking the civil court to provisionally allow Patro MM to participate in the elite competition, arguing that waiting for a full trial would make the situation irreversible once the season starts. He is also demanding a penalty of €25,000 per day if the Pro League does not respect the court’s decision.

“Once the season is fully underway, it’s too late and the situation is irreversible,” Van Steenbrugge argued. “These children would lose an entire year. Careers will be lost.”

## The Broader Implications

The case highlights a fundamental tension between regulatory compliance and human impact. The Pro League insists on upholding standards to maintain the integrity of youth development, while the club and families argue that the licensing calendar is fundamentally flawed, penalizing children for administrative shortcomings they had no control over.

[Patro Eisden Maasmechelen](https://patroeisden.com/), based in Limburg province and playing in the Challenger Pro League (Belgium’s second tier), has invested significantly in its youth academy since being acquired by the American Common Group in 2022. The club argues that its overall scoring on the youth evaluation was sufficient and that the alleged deficiencies are disputed.

## What’s Next

The civil court is expected to rule by July 30, 2026. If the court grants provisional participation, Patro MM’s youth teams would be allowed to compete at the elite level while the full case proceeds. If not, hundreds of young players will be forced to seek alternatives — or abandon their elite football aspirations altogether.

For Brandon Domi, the wait is agonizing. “I lie awake at night and worry myself sick,” he said. “I’ve wanted to be a professional footballer my whole life and I do everything for it. They’re taking my dream away.”

The case could set a precedent for how Belgian football handles licensing disputes — and whether the system can be reformed to prevent children from becoming collateral damage in administrative battles.
