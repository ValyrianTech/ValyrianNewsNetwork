# Beijing Zoo Opens State-of-the-Art Giant Panda Hall

Beijing Zoo officially opened its new giant panda hall to the public on July 16, 2026, unveiling a 13,000-square-meter facility that sets a new benchmark for animal enclosure design in China. The new hall, a centerpiece of the zoo's 120th-anniversary celebrations, features China's first Green Building Three-Star rated animal enclosure and houses five giant pandas, including the internet-famous "Meng Lan."

## A Historic Milestone for a Century-Old Zoo

Founded in 1906 during the Qing Dynasty as China's first public zoo, the Beijing Zoo has been at the forefront of giant panda conservation for decades. The zoo achieved the world's first successful captive breeding of giant pandas in 1963 and pioneered artificial insemination techniques in 1978. Today, it houses 10 giant pandas, with 9 on public display.

The new panda hall represents the most significant upgrade to the zoo's panda facilities since the Olympic Panda Hall was built for the 2008 Beijing Games. According to [Xinhua News Agency](https://www.news.cn/local/20260716/68e42109a274403e9e7eef200b4ee472/c.html), the facility was constructed on the site of the former Pheasant Garden, east of the old panda hall, and features a classical garden layout with corridors, courtyards, and rain gardens.

## Green Building Innovation and Smart Technology

The new hall is China's first animal enclosure to achieve the Green Building Three-Star standard, the highest rating in the country's green building evaluation system. The facility incorporates eco-friendly bamboo-pattern concrete panels made from recycled materials and utilizes BIM digital technology for ongoing smart maintenance.

A "Smart Animal House Management System" provides intelligent security monitoring, health tracking, and automated environmental control. [People's Daily](http://society.people.com.cn/n1/2026/0717/c1008-40762560.html) reported that a customized misting system offers "cooling SPA" sessions during summer heat, while advanced temperature control and fresh air systems maintain optimal conditions year-round.

## Spacious New Homes for Beloved Pandas

The facility includes four indoor exhibition halls, five animal rooms, and five outdoor exercise yards, all connected through an indoor-outdoor design that allows pandas to move freely between spaces. The first residents — Meng Lan, Bai Tian, Ji Nian, Fu Jiang, and Fu Xing — have all adapted well to their new environment.

Beijing Zoo Deputy Director Mou Ningning told [China News Service](https://www.chinanews.com/tp/2026/07-15/10659865.shtml) that Meng Lan's new space includes over 200 square meters indoors and more than 300 square meters outdoors. Rockeries and trees create visual blind spots to reduce pressure from visitor sightlines, while specially designed anti-climb walls prevent the panda's famous "escape attempts."

Deputy Director Lu Yanping noted that the hall is equipped with fixed climbing frames and movable enrichment items such as tires and balls, designed to restore the natural living conditions of giant pandas as much as possible.

## Enhanced Visitor Experience

The new hall features a circular tour route that separates visitor flow from logistics operations, improving safety and reducing congestion during peak periods. Beijing Zoo, which receives over 6 million visitors annually, has also introduced limited-edition commemorative tickets featuring traditional Chinese panda illustrations and upgraded food and merchandise offerings.

A venue rotation system will be implemented, meaning the new hall and the adjacent Asian Games Hall will serve as the main exhibition areas, while the Olympic Hall will temporarily function as a backstage rest area. According to Lu Yanping, this rotation system means visitors may see different pandas on each visit.

## A Symbol of Conservation Progress

The opening of the new panda hall reflects China's growing emphasis on animal welfare in zoological settings, moving beyond simple exhibition toward sophisticated habitat simulation. Giant pandas remain a powerful national symbol, and the facility's debut during the zoo's 120th anniversary year reinforces their cultural significance.

As Beijing Zoo looks toward its next century, the new panda hall stands as a testament to the evolution of wildlife conservation in China — from a Qing-era menagerie to a modern facility where technology and nature work in harmony to protect one of the world's most beloved species.
