# China Housing Market Revives as Shanghai Leads Urban Renewal

China's housing market is showing clear signs of revival in the first half of 2026, with improved housing demand accelerating across multiple cities and "good housing" projects reporting strong sales, according to data from the Ministry of Housing and Urban-Rural Development and the National Bureau of Statistics [reported by CCTV News](https://news.cctv.com/2026/07/17/ARTIiQZKWjw3WvtbYtjXWEgQ260717.shtml). The recovery, concentrated in tier-1 cities, comes alongside a pioneering urban renewal initiative in Shanghai that is transforming aging city-center homes through an innovative "original demolition and reconstruction" model.

## Housing Market Data Signals Recovery

National real estate markets maintained stability in H1 2026, with the second-hand market particularly active and destocking proving effective. In tier-1 cities, second-hand home prices have risen for four consecutive months since March, with June prices up 0.3% month-on-month.

Beijing recorded 16,618 second-hand home transactions in June, a 10% year-on-year increase, while its H1 total of 93,583 units represented a 5.7% rise over the same period in 2025 and a five-year high. Shanghai's market was even more robust, with June transactions reaching 25,158 units for the fourth consecutive month above 25,000, pushing the H1 total past 147,000 units — also a five-year high.

Guangzhou saw June sales area reach 918,200 square meters, up 3.7% year-on-year, driven by the "Sui Eight Measures" policy easing. Huang Liping, a Guangzhou real estate agency manager, told CCTV that "the 'Sui Eight Measures', provident fund limit increases, and 'sell old buy new' exchanges attracted many rigid-demand buyers." Shenzhen recorded 6,168 units in June, an 11.2% year-on-year increase.

## The Rise of 'Good Housing'

A structural shift is underway in China's property market. Data from 30 key cities shows that homes larger than 120 square meters accounted for 48% of new sales from January to May 2026, up 2.3 percentage points from 2025, while homes under 90 square meters fell to just 15% of sales. This trend reflects the growing dominance of improved housing demand — buyers upgrading from smaller, older homes — over first-time buyer demand.

Wuhan exemplifies this shift: H1 new housing sales reached 45,600 units, up 2.8% year-on-year, while the share of fourth-generation housing rose from 22% in January to 48% in May. "Real estate enterprises focusing on product innovation, quality upgrades, and community services are expected to continuously enhance homebuyers' confidence," said Pan Shibing, President of the Hubei Real Estate Association.

## Shanghai's Urban Renewal Breakthrough

At the heart of this broader narrative is a remarkable transformation story from Shanghai's Huangpu District. The Shimin Xincun project at 211 Baotun Road has turned four dilapidated buildings constructed in 1957 — among Shanghai's earliest "small beam and thin slab" structures — into modern homes with independent kitchens, bathrooms, and elevators, as [detailed by The Paper](https://www.thepaper.cn/newsDetail_forward_33601398).

Before renovation, seven families shared a single kitchen and two toilets. "Most inconvenient was no bathroom — seven families shared two toilets, we had to empty chamber pots," recalled Zhang Lanying, a 69-year-old resident of 40 years. Yu Weiwei, 81, who has lived there since her marriage, described the transformation: "Now it's completely different — clean and good. Small but tidy, with independent kitchen and bathroom, and a balcony. Best of all, there's an elevator for us elderly."

The project, launched in October 2022 and implemented by Nanfang Group, involved over 300 households with more than 40 layout types. Yang Xilin, Party Branch Secretary of Nanfang Group, noted that "changes to one household's plan could affect the entire floor's plan," requiring 22 major revisions. Crucially, residents paid nothing and received incentive payments, rental subsidies, and moving subsidies during the transition period.

## Xi Jinping's Endorsement

President Xi Jinping visited Shimin Xincun on July 15, providing high-profile political endorsement of the model. According to [Xinhua News Agency](http://www3.xinhuanet.com/politics/leaders/20260715/b291748948564a4ca1562d010d635d3a/c.html), Xi emphasized that "the people's lives are family affairs, but even more, they are national affairs. The Communist Party exists to serve the people." He called for communities to be strengthened as "the basic units of the urban governance system," urging resources and services to be pushed down to the grassroots level.

## Party Service Centers as Community Hubs

Complementing the housing revival, Shanghai's 12,000+ Party and Mass Service Centers have gained popularity as multi-functional community hubs, as [reported by CCTV News](https://news.cctv.com/2026/07/17/ARTIFmsbWQEsP3L5y5P7B9ih260717.shtml). Originating from a 2019 CPC Central Committee directive, these centers offer community cafeterias, clinics, reading rooms, night schools, and even live-streaming studios for content creators.

"I come every day, three times a day," one local resident told CCTV. He Hanqing, director of the Butterfly Bay Center in Jing'an District, said: "We open every public space to the citizens." The centers represent a tangible shift from purely administrative party offices to service-oriented community hubs integrated with Shanghai's "15-minute community life circle" concept.

## Analysis and Outlook

The coordinated narrative emerging from these developments is significant. The housing data demonstrates macro-level success for government stimulus measures, while the Shimin Xincun project provides a human-interest case study of urban renewal that preserves social networks — residents kept their neighbors and central-city location. The party service centers illustrate the governance infrastructure supporting these changes.

Shanghai aims to complete 200,000 square meters of old housing renovation in 2026, and the "original demolition and reconstruction" model could offer a template for other Chinese cities grappling with aging housing stock. However, questions remain about the model's replicability at scale and whether the housing market recovery can be sustained beyond short-term stimulus measures.
