# Trump Declassifies Intel Alleging China Compromised 18 State Voter Rolls

President Donald Trump delivered a primetime address from the East Room of the White House on Thursday evening, announcing the declassification of intelligence documents that he claims reveal "shocking vulnerabilities" in U.S. election infrastructure. The release — posted to WhiteHouse.gov during the speech — alleges that China compromised at least 18 state voter rolls, obtaining approximately 220 million voter records, and that over 278,000 noncitizens may be registered on state voter rolls. Trump accused the intelligence community of covering up these findings from the public, Congress, and even from himself during his first term.

## Background and Context

The speech comes as Trump has made election integrity a central theme of his political identity since the 2020 election. The address was attended by Vice President JD Vance, FBI Director Kash Patel, Secretary of State Marco Rubio, Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche, and DHS Secretary Markwayne Mullin. Trump stated that the documents were compiled by the White House Government Transparency Taskforce and the President's Intelligence Advisory Board, with authentication by top intelligence agency chiefs.

According to [Fox News](https://www.foxnews.com/politics/trump-releases-declassified-election-intelligence-says-reveals-shocking-vulnerabilities), Trump did not claim that China changed votes or altered election results. Instead, he argued that Beijing engaged in an influence campaign aimed at shaping U.S. public perceptions. The [Washington Times](https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2026/jul/16/trump-declassifies-evidence-says-exposes-shocking-vulnerabilities-us/) reported that some of the information had been previously public, though not widely covered.

## Key Allegations in the Declassified Documents

The White House released documents covering four main areas: alleged vulnerabilities in electronic voting systems, China's acquisition of American voter data, a Michigan voter-registration investigation, and noncitizens on state voter rolls.

### China and Voter Data

According to [Breitbart](https://www.breitbart.com/politics/2026/07/16/declassified-intelligence-records-china-compromised-least-18-state-voter-rolls/), the declassified intelligence document names 15 states and the District of Columbia as having had their voter rolls compromised by China: Alaska, Arkansas, Colorado, Connecticut, Florida, Georgia, Iowa, Kansas, Maryland, Michigan, New York, North Carolina, Ohio, Oklahoma, and Rhode Island. Three additional states were not named. The document states that China obtained names, addresses, phone numbers, and political affiliations of American voters.

### Noncitizens on Voter Rolls

The Department of Homeland Security identified approximately 278,000 potential noncitizens on voter rolls across California, New Jersey, Nevada, and Pennsylvania, according to letters obtained by Fox News. However, having names on the rolls does not automatically mean these individuals voted, and Democrats argue that voter fraud is "extraordinarily rare."

### Alleged Cover-Up

Trump claimed that reports from the CIA and National Security Agency about China's activities were kept out of his daily presidential briefings and that Congress was also not notified. He called on the director of national intelligence, the Department of Justice, the FBI, and the CIA to investigate and potentially prosecute those involved in the alleged cover-up.

## Reactions and Responses

### Supporters

Election law expert Hans von Spakovsky of Advancing American Freedom told Fox News Digital that Trump "should be commended for declassifying and releasing the reports that support his claims so they can be reviewed in depth." He added that if the intrusions were covered up, "everyone involved in that coverup should be investigated and prosecuted."

RNC Chairman Joe Gruters praised Trump's efforts, stating that "Americans deserve elections we can trust" and calling on Congress to pass the SAVE America Act.

### Democrats

Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer declared the SAVE Act "dead on arrival in the United States Senate," accusing Trump of "undermining the 2026 election before a single vote has been cast." Rep. Ayanna Pressley characterized the legislation as an effort to "make it harder to vote for millions of women, veterans, rural folks, & voters of color."

Sen. Mark Warner, vice chair of the Senate Intelligence Committee, dismissed the China allegations as "totally bogus," noting that a 2021 intelligence community assessment found no evidence that any foreign actor altered any technical aspect of the 2020 election.

### China

Chinese Embassy spokesperson Liu Chang denied the allegations, stating that "China has never and will never interfere in the presidential elections of the U.S." and warning against "vilifying China."

## Analysis and Implications

The declassification comes at a politically sensitive time, ahead of the 2026 midterm elections where Republicans face potential losses. [Deutsche Welle](https://www.dw.com/en/donald-trump-accuses-china-of-meddling-in-us-elections/a-77996793) noted that Trump did not mention Russian interference in the 2016 and 2024 elections — elections he won. The 2021 intelligence community assessment, conducted under John Ratcliffe (now CIA director), found that Russia carried out influence operations in 2020, while China did not attempt to alter votes.

Critics argue that obtaining voter registration data — which is often publicly available or commercially accessible — is fundamentally different from altering votes or changing election outcomes. Trump himself did not claim that votes were changed.

## What's Next

Trump has ordered DHS to notify every state about noncitizens on their voter rolls and renewed his push for the SAVE America Act, which would require photo ID and proof of citizenship for federal elections. The legislation has stalled in Congress, with Trump attempting to attach it to defense spending and housing bills. Meanwhile, ABC and NBC chose not to air the speech live on their main broadcast channels, a decision Trump criticized as being "part of a plot."

The coming days will likely see further debate over the substance of the declassified documents, potential investigations into intelligence officials, and the impact on US-China relations ahead of planned trade talks.
