# China Reaffirms Unification Pledge in Sharp Rebuke to Taiwan's Lai

BEIJING — China's State Council Taiwan Affairs Office issued a forceful statement on Thursday dismissing recent remarks by Taiwanese President Lai Ching-te, declaring that no amount of rhetoric from his administration can alter the trajectory toward national unification. The statement, delivered by spokesperson Chen Binhua, comes amid escalating cross-strait tensions over Beijing's newly implemented Ethnic Unity and Progress Promotion Law.

According to [Xinhua News](https://www.news.cn/20260716/fa8431e982db4800941bc7a912d3d13e/c.html), Chen was responding to Lai's criticism of China's Anti-Secession Law and the Ethnic Unity and Progress Promotion Law at a Democratic Progressive Party (DPP) Central Standing Committee meeting. Lai had alleged that the laws create "extraterritorial jurisdiction" and a "chilling effect" on democratic societies.

## Context of the Dispute

The exchange marks the latest flashpoint in a rapidly deteriorating relationship between Beijing and Taipei. Lai Ching-te, whom Beijing views as a dangerous separatist, won Taiwan's presidency in January 2024 and has since promoted a "democracy versus autocracy" framing that China rejects.

The trigger for the current confrontation is China's Law on Promoting Ethnic Unity and Progress, which took effect on July 1 after being passed by the National People's Congress in March. As [BBC Chinese](https://www.bbc.com/zhongwen/articles/cg4we27y99xo/trad) reported, the law includes provisions that allow Chinese authorities to hold organizations and individuals outside China's territory legally responsible for acts deemed to undermine ethnic unity or promote ethnic division.

Chen Binhua defended both laws, stating that the Anti-Secession Law safeguards national sovereignty and territorial integrity while curbing "Taiwan independence" separatist activities. He described the Ethnic Unity Law as a "promotional law" that protects the fundamental interests of the Chinese nation.

## Accusations and Counter-Accusations

Chen accused the Lai administration of "spreading rumors and smears" and "deliberately confusing the public" by fabricating risks to intimidate Taiwanese people. He charged that since taking office, Lai has continuously sought "Taiwan independence," escalated cross-strait confrontation, obstructed exchanges, and engaged in what Chen called "green terror" on the island.

"No matter what the Lai Ching-te administration says or does, it cannot change the basic pattern of cross-strait relations, nor can it stop the historical trend that the motherland will ultimately and inevitably be unified," Chen said, as reported by [China News Service](https://www.chinanews.com.cn/gn/2026/07-16/10660817.shtml).

Taiwan's Executive Yuan has taken a different view. Premier Cho Jung-tai warned in early July that Beijing has built an expanding network of laws with extraterritorial reach. "When law becomes a tool of authoritarian rule, democracy must become the front line of freedom," Cho said, according to [Asia Times](https://asiatimes.com/2026/07/chinas-ethnic-unity-law-extends-legal-reach-to-taiwan-diaspora/).

## Broader Implications

The Ethnic Unity Law has drawn international concern. The United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights warned the law "could overly restrict freedoms of expression, belief and assembly," while the European Parliament passed a resolution in April condemning China's assimilation policies. The US State Department called the law "problematic" for imposing "sweeping obligations" on individuals and organizations outside China.

Analysts note that the law creates a comprehensive legal framework — alongside the 2005 Anti-Secession Law and 2024 opinions on punishing "Taiwan independence" separatists — that Beijing can deploy at strategic, enforcement, and governance levels. Hung Pu-chao, deputy head of Tunghai University's Center for Mainland China, told BBC Chinese that the law's broad and undefined terms leave significant room for interpretation, potentially causing self-censorship among Taiwanese academics, businesspeople, and media professionals who frequently travel to China.

## Legal Framework and Its Reach

The Ethnic Unity Law's Article 63, which allows Chinese authorities to pursue legal responsibility against overseas organizations or individuals accused of undermining "ethnic unity," has been a particular point of contention. Taiwan's Judicial Reform Foundation is studying whether the island's existing laws adequately protect citizens from cross-border jurisdiction, with a report expected by the end of the year.

Chuang Chia-ying, an associate professor of political science at the National University of Singapore, told BBC Chinese that the law "rationalizes and legalizes" extraterritorial enforcement while expanding the scope of restricted behavior, likely creating a chilling effect on individuals outside China.

## What to Watch

Taiwan will hold local elections on November 28, widely seen as a bellwether for the 2028 presidential election. Beijing's intensified rhetoric may be aimed at influencing the political environment ahead of that vote. The elections will test whether the DPP's stance on cross-strait relations resonates with voters or whether Beijing's pressure campaign shifts public opinion.

Meanwhile, Taiwan's government has established a cross-agency platform to counter what it describes as transnational repression and is expanding cooperation with allied democracies. The coming months will test how China enforces the Ethnic Unity Law's extraterritorial provisions abroad — a question that will shape not only cross-strait relations but also Beijing's standing with the international community.

For now, the fundamental positions remain entrenched. Beijing insists unification is inevitable and accuses Lai of recklessly escalating tensions. Taipei warns of creeping legal overreach and calls on democratic allies for support. The gap between the two sides appears wider than at any point in recent years, with no clear path toward de-escalation on the horizon.
