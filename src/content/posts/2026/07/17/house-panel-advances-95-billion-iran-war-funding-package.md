# House Panel Advances $95 Billion Iran War Funding Package

WASHINGTON — The House Budget Committee voted 20-14 along party lines on Thursday to advance a $95 billion budget resolution that would fund continued U.S. military operations in Iran, provide farm aid, and enact strict new voter ID requirements, sending the package to the full House for what is expected to be a contentious floor fight. The bulk of the funding — approximately $60 billion — is earmarked for the Iran war, which began on February 28 and has already cost U.S. taxpayers an estimated $113.3 billion as of June, according to [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Iran_war).

## What the Package Contains

The resolution, which represents the third budget reconciliation effort by House Republicans this session, directs four congressional committees to draft legislation. The House Armed Services Committee would oversee $60 billion for defense, covering Iran war operations, replenishing stockpiles, and military readiness. The Select Committee on Intelligence would manage $13 billion, the Agriculture Committee $12 billion for farm aid, and the House Administration Committee $10 billion for voter ID and election law enforcement grants tied to the SAVE America Act.

As [AP News](https://apnews.com/article/iran-war-house-republican-budget-47056fcf87e5937bd1bb3f9ae0388d37) reported, the proposal contains no offsets to pay for the new spending, a point of contention among deficit hawks in both parties.

## Partisan Divide on Display

Budget Committee Chairman Jodey Arrington (R-TX) framed the proposal as a final push to deliver for voters ahead of the November midterm elections. "We are rallying to finish what we started when the American people sent us here," Arrington said. On the war funding, he noted the money covers "just the bombs, bullets and battlefield readiness for our men and women in uniform to finish the fight successfully and return home safely."

Democrats pushed back forcefully. Ranking Democrat Brendan Boyle (D-PA) called the package a giveaway to special interests. "I'm going to fight like hell to make sure taxpayer dollars are being used to lower costs and make life better for American families, not to bankroll Trump's giveaways to billionaires and endless wars overseas," Boyle said, as [reported by AP News](https://apnews.com/article/house-republicans-budget-iran-war-farmers-elections-6cce8d8070151748b470a07c0e7d734d).

Rep. Becca Balint (D-VT) described the resolution as "a poorly planned, poorly drafted backdoor deal to pay for President Trump's decision to start a war with Iran," according to [Reuters via the Jerusalem Post](https://www.jpost.com/american-politics/article-902809).

## Republican Unity Under Strain

Despite advancing through committee, the package faces significant headwinds within the Republican conference. Rep. Chip Roy (R-TX), a key Freedom Caucus member who has expressed reservations about the package, did not vote. Senate Majority Leader John Thune (R-SD) expressed skepticism, telling reporters: "It's a very uneven path. We'll see what the House can execute on. I can't make any guarantees over here."

The tension reflects a broader divide between defense hawks who want more spending and deficit hawks who demand offsets. Sen. Ron Johnson (R-WI), expected to take over the Senate Budget Committee, has been a leading voice on fiscal restraint.

## The SAVE Act Complication

Including $10 billion for voter ID requirements — provisions of the SAVE America Act — is a strategic move by Republicans to pass a priority that cannot clear the 60-vote Senate filibuster threshold on its own. Budget reconciliation allows passage with a simple majority of 51 votes. However, the strategy carries risks: some senators worry the provisions may not meet the Byrd Rule requirements for reconciliation in the Senate, potentially unraveling the entire package.

## War Powers Conflict

The funding push comes just weeks after the Senate approved a war powers resolution on June 23 by a 50-48 vote directing President Trump to end hostilities in Iran or seek congressional authorization — the first time the Senate has passed such a measure, as [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/jun/23/trump-iran-war-powers-resolution) reported. The administration has continued military operations, and Congress is now being asked to fund the very war it voted to restrain, creating a constitutional tension between Congress's power of the purse and the president's authority as commander-in-chief.

## What Comes Next

A full House vote is expected as early as the week of July 20. Speaker Mike Johnson can afford to lose only a few Republican votes given the party's narrow majority. House committees will work on bill text over the August recess, with a final floor vote expected in the fall. The path in the Senate remains highly uncertain.

With the November midterm elections looming, the war's economic toll — including elevated fuel and fertilizer prices — is shaping up to be a central campaign issue. Democrats are hammering the message that the conflict is driving up costs for American families, while Republicans are framing the package as a "calling card" for voters who want a strong national defense and election integrity.
