# Trump Uses Government Power to Promote False Election Claims

President Donald Trump delivered a primetime address from the White House on Thursday, deploying the full weight of the federal government to promote unsubstantiated claims about election integrity, less than four months before the November 2026 midterm elections. The 25-minute speech, in which Trump ordered the declassification of hundreds of intelligence documents, has been widely characterized by critics as an attempt to undermine confidence in American democracy.

## Context

Trump's address marks a significant escalation in his long-running campaign to challenge the legitimacy of U.S. elections. Since losing the 2020 presidential election to Joe Biden, Trump has repeatedly alleged widespread fraud — claims that have been rejected by dozens of courts, multiple audits, and fact-checking organizations. The U.S. Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency previously called the 2020 election "the most secure in American history."

According to the [BBC](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cx2k9wvv5wyo), the speech came as a new Washington Post-Ipsos poll showed Trump's approval rating had dropped to 37%, with voters expressing deep pessimism about the cost of living and the ongoing war with Iran.

## Key Developments

Trump's central allegation was that China had carried out "the largest compromise of election data in history," claiming Beijing acquired 220 million U.S. voter files from 18 states. However, as [Al Jazeera reported](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/7/17/key-takeaways-from-donald-trumps-speech-on-election-security), such voter information is already publicly available in many states, and Trump did not present evidence that China used this data to alter voting systems or influence election outcomes.

China's foreign ministry strongly denied the allegations. Spokesperson Lin Jian called them "entirely fabricated" and "malicious smears" that have "long been proven to be groundless," the BBC noted.

The president's claims directly contradict the March 2021 U.S. intelligence community assessment, which concluded with "high confidence" that China did not deploy interference efforts intended to change the outcome of the 2020 election. The assessment was declassified and made public years ago, undercutting Trump's narrative of a "deep state" cover-up.

Trump also cited a DHS analysis identifying approximately 278,000 non-citizens on state voter rolls. However, the DHS SAVE database is known for producing false positives, particularly involving naturalized citizens, and being on voter rolls does not mean these individuals actually voted.

In a moment that drew particular scrutiny, Trump rehashed a Muskegon, Michigan case involving fraudulent voter registration forms. Officials detected and voided the questionable applications before Election Day, and no votes were cast as a result of the incident.

## Network Coverage Decisions

The speech prompted unprecedented decisions from major television networks. ABC, NBC, and CNN chose not to air Trump's address live on their main broadcast channels, instead streaming it online. CBS and Fox News aired it live. Trump responded by calling for the FCC to revoke broadcast licenses for ABC and NBC, a threat that drew sharp criticism from press freedom advocates.

## Analysis

Critics across the political spectrum — including former Trump administration officials, intelligence experts, and Democratic leaders — have characterized the address as laying the groundwork for challenging the 2026 midterm results. Rep. Jim Himes (D-CT), the ranking member of the House Intelligence Committee, warned that Trump was "setting the basis for him to turn around, at midnight on Election Day and say, I warned you."

Sue Gordon, former principal deputy director of national intelligence under Trump, offered a measured assessment: "Intent is not activity. Activity is not impact. And impact is not outcome."

Even White House adviser John Solomon acknowledged there was zero evidence that a foreign power had flipped a single vote in the 2020, 2022, or 2024 elections, as the BBC reported.

## The SAVE America Act

Trump used the speech to call for passage of the SAVE America Act, which would ban most mail voting, require proof of citizenship for voter registration, and mandate photo ID to cast a ballot. Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer declared the legislation "dead on arrival" in the Senate.

## What's Next

The long-term implications of Trump's address for American democracy remain uncertain. With the midterm elections approaching in November, analysts across the political spectrum have expressed concern about the potential for further erosion of public trust in electoral institutions. The declassified documents released by the White House have been described by independent experts as containing no major revelations, with much of the information either previously public or heavily redacted.

As Sen. Raphael Warnock (D-GA) put it: "This really isn't about 2020. It's about 2026. He is trying to sow doubt on the integrity of our elections in Georgia so that he can create the pretext to interfere in 2026."
