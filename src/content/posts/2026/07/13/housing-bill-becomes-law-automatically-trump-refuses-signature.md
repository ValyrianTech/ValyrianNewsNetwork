# Landmark Housing Bill Becomes Law Without Trump's Signature

The largest housing affordability bill in decades became law at midnight on July 11, 2026 — not with a presidential signature, but without one. President Donald Trump allowed the 10-day signing window to expire without signing or vetoing the 21st Century ROAD (Revitalizing Opportunities for Affordable Development) to Housing Act, triggering an automatic enactment under Article I, Section 7 of the Constitution.

As [NPR reported](https://www.npr.org/2026/07/10/nx-s1-5885027/housing-bill-without-trump-signature), the legislation cleared both chambers of Congress with overwhelming bipartisan support — 358-32 in the House and 85-5 in the Senate — making it the most significant federal housing legislation since the Cranston-Gonzalez National Affordable Housing Act of 1990.

## A Bill Caught in a Political Standoff

Trump's refusal to sign had little to do with the housing bill itself. Instead, he demanded that Congress first pass the SAVE (Safeguard American Voter Eligibility) America Act, a strict voter ID bill requiring proof of citizenship to register and photo identification to cast a ballot. The SAVE Act passed the House but has stalled in the Senate, lacking the 60 votes needed to overcome a filibuster.

"I will not sign the Housing Bill, which has been fully approved by Congress and sent to the White House, in PROTEST over the fact that the United States Senate is not capable of passing THE SAVE AMERICA ACT," Trump posted on Truth Social. He had previously called the housing legislation "a big yawn" and "of minor importance" compared to the voting bill.

The standoff created an unusual dynamic. House Speaker Mike Johnson (R-LA) delivered the bill to Trump on June 29, triggering the 10-day constitutional clock. Despite supporting Trump's refusal, Johnson had earlier indicated the president would sign within the window. Some Republicans criticized Trump's decision to tie the two unrelated bills together, noting the House had already passed the SAVE Act and considered it the Senate's responsibility.

## What the Law Actually Does

The 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act contains more than 40 provisions addressing housing supply, affordability, and regulation. Among the most significant:

**Corporate Landlord Restrictions (Title 10):** Large institutional investors controlling 350 or more single-family homes are prohibited from purchasing new ones. Exceptions exist for build-to-rent developments, renovate-to-rent programs, senior living communities, and boost home ownership programs. Notably, large investors own only about 3% of the single-family rental market nationally, though their share is significantly higher in some cities.

**Streamlined Environmental Reviews (Title 2):** The law expands categorical exclusions under the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) for housing projects, creates competitive grants for zoning and planning updates, and allows HUD to delegate environmental reviews to states and localities. Developers can even skip environmental review if a house is built between two already-reviewed buildings.

**Manufactured Housing Reform (Title 3):** The permanent chassis requirement for manufactured homes is eliminated, potentially saving $5,000 to $10,000 per home. HUD gains primary authority over energy-efficiency standards for these homes.

**Community Development Banking (Title 9):** Public-welfare investment caps for community development banks are raised to 20%, boosting local affordable housing financing.

Other provisions include grant programs for "pattern books" — preapproved housing designs needing fewer approvals — and incentives that direct more existing federal funding to communities that build more homes. The law also includes a four-year ban on the Federal Reserve establishing a central bank digital currency.

## The Housing Crisis That Demanded Action

The legislation arrives against a backdrop of severe housing affordability challenges. According to the [BBC](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cn8qwj611qxo), the median existing home price hit an all-time high of $440,600 in June 2026. A household needs approximately $117,000 annually to afford an average home, yet the median U.S. household income is roughly $80,000 — leaving millions of families priced out of homeownership.

A Bipartisan Policy Center survey found that 89% of voters across the political spectrum wanted congressional action on housing affordability. "This bill becoming law is a genuine milestone — and I don't use that word lightly," said Dennis Shea of the Bipartisan Policy Center. "Getting Congress to move on housing supply and affordability has been a long time coming."

## Limits and Forward Look

Despite its historic scope, experts caution that the law has significant limitations. It does not change local zoning rules, which can slow or block construction. Homebuilders remain pessimistic after three years of high material and labor costs. Congress does not control mortgage rates, which hover around 6.5% for a 30-year fixed mortgage. And critically, the law adds no new funding to the federal budget — it redirects existing resources.

"Moving forward in 2028 and beyond, I don't think anyone can run for public office without having a perspective of how housing needs to be prioritized," said Sarah Brundage, president of the National Association of Affordable Housing Lenders, who called the law a "needed first step."

Any meaningful improvements in affordability will take years to materialize. A single housing development can take longer than an elected official's term to go from planning to completion. But as [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/jul/10/housing-bill-becomes-law-without-trump-veto) noted, the bill's unusual path to enactment — without the president's signature — highlights deep divisions within the Republican Party and sets the stage for housing affordability to remain a central issue in the 2026 midterm elections.

House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries (D-NY) captured the political stakes succinctly: Republicans "would rather make it harder to vote than easier to afford a home." With both parties now claiming credit for the legislation, the true test will be whether the law delivers tangible relief to the millions of Americans struggling with the highest housing costs in a generation.
