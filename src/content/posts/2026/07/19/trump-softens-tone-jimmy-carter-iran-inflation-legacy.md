# Trump Softens Tone on Jimmy Carter Amid Iran, Inflation

President Donald Trump has notably softened his rhetoric toward former President Jimmy Carter, offering more measured and wistful comments as he confronts a war with Iran, rising inflation, and mounting questions about his own historical legacy — challenges that eerily mirror those that defined Carter's single term in office.

Once one of Trump's favorite political foils, Carter is now the subject of a strikingly different tone from the president. According to [AP News](https://apnews.com/article/trump-carter-iran-inflation-71fab887b88febc073c89a8c8db595f3), Trump's sentiments have become increasingly reflective as the parallels between the two presidencies grow harder to ignore.

## The Carter Comparison Haunts Trump

The comparison appears to be on Trump's mind. Asked last month why he did not dispatch U.S. Special Forces into Iran to forcibly remove enriched uranium, Trump responded: "I didn't feel like being Jimmy Carter" — a pointed reference to the failed 1980 Operation Eagle Claw hostage rescue that killed eight American servicemen. Trump also remarked in March that the unsuccessful mission "cost them the election" against Ronald Reagan, signaling an awareness of political consequences he rarely displayed when using Carter as a punchline.

"I think it is dawning on him — it's getting through even his thick skull — that he's kicked over a hornet's nest and his presidency might be remembered for some of the same things Jimmy Carter's presidency is," said Jonathan Alter, author of "His Very Best: Jimmy Carter, A Life," as reported by AP News.

## Parallel Crises: Iran, Inflation, and the Strait of Hormuz

Both presidents faced — or are facing — strikingly similar challenges. Carter's presidency was defined by the 444-day Iran hostage crisis, during which 52 Americans were held captive. Trump launched a war with Iran on February 28, 2026, in coordination with Israel, a conflict that has now dragged on for nearly five months with no end in sight.

Inflation plagued both administrations. Carter faced a peak rate of 14.7% in April 1980. Consumer prices under Trump rose 4.2% in May 2026 from a year earlier — a three-year high — and though they fell sharply from May to June, that decline was fueled by lower gas prices from a now-collapsed U.S.-Iran ceasefire. The [Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2026/jul/18/trump-iran-war-political-midterm-risk) reports that oil prices are rising again as hostilities resume.

Both presidents were also bedeviled by the Strait of Hormuz, through which 20% of the world's energy exports once passed. Carter declared during his 1980 State of the Union address that the situation "demands the participation of all those who rely on oil from the Middle East." Trump now faces an Iran that has weaponized the strait as its key bargaining chip, blockading the waterway and attacking commercial vessels.

## A Critical Difference: War vs. Restraint

Perhaps the most significant divergence between the two presidents lies in their approach to military action. Carter considered attacking Iran's Kharg Island — the hub of its oil exports — but ultimately chose restraint to avoid jeopardizing American hostages. Trump, by contrast, ordered strikes on Kharg Island early in the war and has threatened it again.

"While the Carter administration gave serious consideration to attacking Kharg Island, the reason they didn't do it was they didn't want to be at war with Iran," noted Kori Schake, a former member of George W. Bush's National Security Council, as quoted by AP News. "And we're already at war with Iran."

## Political Stakes Ahead of the Midterms

The political implications are enormous. With the November 2026 midterm elections approaching, Democrats are seeking to recapture both houses of Congress. The unpopular war and persistent inflation are key vulnerabilities for Republicans.

"There's basically no timeline in which this makes any sense for preserving [Republicans'] midterm performance," said Curt Mills, executive editor of The American Conservative, as reported by the Guardian. "I think it's a total loser. It's evidence that Trump doesn't really care about the midterms."

## Legacy and the Weight of History

Trump's shift in tone coincides with an increased focus on his personal legacy. He has installed a "Walk of Fame" along the White House Colonnade listing past presidents' accomplishments — including Carter's — and has name-checked predecessors from William McKinley to Franklin Delano Roosevelt.

"Trump is thinking of his legacy, and he might have thought that attacking Iran would have enhanced it. But, in fact, it will seriously hurt," said James P. Pfiffner, a professor emeritus at George Mason University, via email to AP News. "If he had learned anything from Carter's experience — and listened to predictions about the Strait of Hormuz — he would have hesitated. But he ignored lessons of history and geography."

Despite the parallels, the two men remain profoundly different. Carter placed his peanut business in a blind trust, was married for 77 years, and won the Nobel Peace Prize. Trump earned nearly $1.2 billion from crypto businesses while in office, is twice divorced, and has not won a Nobel — despite declaring himself more deserving than any past recipient.

## What to Watch

As the Iran war enters its fifth month and the ceasefire remains in tatters, the key questions are whether Trump can avoid the political fate that befell Carter and whether the historical parallels will deepen. With the Strait of Hormuz crisis unresolved, inflation ticking upward, and midterm elections looming, the president faces a path that looks increasingly familiar — and increasingly perilous.
