# Russia Strikes Kyiv With 31 Ballistic Missiles in Major Overnight Attack

Russia launched a large-scale ballistic missile attack on Ukraine's capital, Kyiv, in the early hours of July 19, firing at least 31 ballistic missiles and eight Zircon hypersonic missiles at the city, according to [Anadolu Agency](https://www.aa.com.tr/en/russia-ukraine-war/at-least-7-injured-as-russian-ballistic-missile-barrage-strikes-kyiv-ukraine/4002568). The attack struck five districts across the capital, killing one person, injuring eight others, and causing widespread fires and structural damage.

## The Attack

The assault began in the early hours of the morning when the Ukrainian Air Force warned that Russian ballistic missiles were approaching from the north, east, and southeast directions, as [Xinhua News Agency](https://www.news.cn/world/20260719/ef0cda4e3b2248728215b581f6bb0f07/c.html) reported. Three intense rounds of explosions shook the city over the course of roughly an hour, with additional blasts continuing into the early morning.

Monitoring channels reported that 31 ballistic missiles and at least eight Zircon hypersonic missiles were directed at the capital. The missiles were launched from Russia's Bryansk and Kursk regions, according to the Ukrainian military.

## Damage Across Five Districts

Kyiv Mayor Vitali Klitschko reported strikes and fires across five districts of the capital. In the **Solomianskyi district**, a multi-story residential building took a direct hit, and a fire broke out on the roof of a supermarket and an adjacent residential building. Four people were rescued from a burning five-story apartment building, and debris shattered windows of a nearby nine-story building.

In the **Shevchenkivskyi district**, the three-story wing of an administrative building caught fire from falling debris, and more than ten parked cars were damaged. One fatality was discovered at this location.

The **Desnyanskyi district** saw a strike on the grounds of a commercial property, with vehicle fires and a warehouse fire reported. In the **Dniprovskyi district**, falling debris ignited a fire in a dormitory, and smoke was seen near a shopping center. The **Sviatoshynskyi district** reported a direct hit on a private residential building, with four residents rescued.

The Lukyanivska Metro station was temporarily closed due to blast wave damage to its above-ground vestibule, according to TSN.

## Casualties and Emergency Response

The State Emergency Service of Ukraine (DSNS) confirmed one fatality and eight injuries, with six people hospitalized and two treated at the scene. Multiple rescues were conducted across the affected districts, including four people rescued from a burning building in the Solomianskyi district.

## Strategic Context: Exploiting an Air Defense Gap

This attack marks the seventh ballistic missile strike on Kyiv in just 16 days during July 2026, according to the [Eastern Herald](https://easternherald.com/2026/07/17/russia-kyiv-ballistic-missiles-two-killed/). The intensifying pattern reflects a deliberate Russian strategy to exploit a critical vulnerability in Ukraine's air defenses.

As the [Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/ukrainealert/putin-escalates-ballistic-missile-attacks-as-patriot-shortages-leave-ukraine-defenseless/) analyzed, only US-made Patriot systems can effectively intercept ballistic missiles, and Ukraine's stockpile of Patriot PAC-3 interceptor missiles is severely depleted or exhausted. Ballistic missiles now account for a growing share of Russia's increasingly deadly attacks on Ukrainian cities.

The [Kyiv Independent](https://kyivindependent.com/russia-hits-kyiv-with-ballistic-missiles-as-ukraine-reels-from-latest-political-shock/) reported that in July alone, Russia has attacked Kyiv with ballistic missiles seven times, including a devastating attack on July 2 that killed over 30 people and injured more than 100.

## Broader Implications

The attack comes amid a period of significant political turmoil in Ukraine, with protests following the dismissal of Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov and the resignation of Prime Minister Yulia Svyrydenko's government. On the same night, Russia also struck the Odesa region, killing a teenager and injuring seven, and hit a university building in Zaporizhzhia.

Ukraine's ability to defend against ballistic missile attacks remains critically constrained. While President Donald Trump [reportedly](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/07/08/world/europe/a-license-to-make-patriot-defense-systems-may-be-a-big-boost-for-ukraine.html) gave preliminary approval for Ukraine to produce Patriot interceptors domestically in early July, establishing new production lines would take years.

## Broader Escalation

The July 19 attack is part of a wider escalation across Ukraine. On the same night, Russian forces struck the Odesa region, killing a teenager and injuring seven others, while also hitting a university building in Zaporizhzhia. Concurrently, Ukrainian drone attacks on Russian territory reportedly killed eight people and injured dozens in the Tambov and Moscow regions, according to Russian authorities.

Ukraine was also experiencing significant internal political turmoil. Protests erupted following the dismissal of Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov, a popular figure credited with improving missile interception rates, and the resignation of Prime Minister Yulia Svyrydenko's government. This internal crisis has compounded the challenges facing Ukrainian leadership as it confronts an intensifying Russian military campaign.

## The Patriot Missile Crisis

Ukraine's inability to effectively counter ballistic missile attacks stems from a critical shortage of Patriot PAC-3 interceptor missiles. The [Associated Press](https://apnews.com/article/russia-ukraine-ballistic-missiles-patriot-attacks-kyiv-f38e07c64d4beba79ca145c3b4bb9510) reported that Ukrainian air defenses likely used Patriot systems to intercept five ballistic missiles on July 14, but such successful interceptions have become increasingly rare as stockpiles dwindle.

The Atlantic Council noted that during the first week of July, Ukraine's air defenses intercepted just four of 49 incoming ballistic missiles, as two large-scale Russian attacks on the Kyiv region killed at least 57 civilians and injured hundreds more. The US-Israeli war in Iran has further exacerbated the supply crisis by consuming as many as 1,430 Patriot munitions in just 39 days.

While President Donald Trump said after the G7 summit that he was open to American companies manufacturing Patriot interceptors under license in Ukraine, establishing new production lines is a complex undertaking. Each PAC-3 missile currently takes more than two years to build, relying on a supply chain of more than four hundred companies.

## What to Watch

With Ukraine's Patriot interceptor stockpiles critically low and Russia showing no signs of relenting in its ballistic missile campaign, Kyiv faces a dangerous summer. The frequency and scale of ballistic missile attacks on Kyiv have increased dramatically in July 2026, suggesting a deliberate strategic decision by Russian command to exploit Ukraine's most significant air defense gap. The international community's response to this escalating pattern of attacks, and whether additional air defense systems can be rushed to Ukraine before winter, remain pressing open questions.
