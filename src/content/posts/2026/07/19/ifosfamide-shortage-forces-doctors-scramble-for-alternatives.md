# Chemotherapy Drug Shortage Forces Doctors to Scramble for Alternatives

A nationwide shortage of ifosfamide — a critical chemotherapy drug used to treat testicular cancer, sarcomas, lymphomas, and other cancers — is forcing doctors across the United States to ration supplies and make agonizing decisions about which patients receive treatment, according to [NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/health/health-news/key-chemo-drug-short-supply-forcing-doctors-scramble-rcna587713). The drug is one of 22 cancer medications currently in short supply in the U.S., underscoring deep and persistent vulnerabilities in the nation's pharmaceutical supply chain.

## The Scope of the Shortage

Ifosfamide, a nitrogen mustard alkylating agent that interferes with DNA replication in cancer cells, is considered a mainstay of treatment for multiple malignancies, including testicular germ cell tumors, soft tissue sarcomas, and certain lymphomas. But Premier, a group purchasing organization that negotiates drug discounts for hospitals and cancer clinics, reports that only **38% of ifosfamide orders** are currently being filled. Fill rates for cisplatin, another backbone chemotherapy drug, have fallen to approximately 66% — below the 80% threshold that experts consider the "danger zone," as [Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/07/01/cancer-centers-ration-chemo) reported.

## Root Cause: A Manufacturing Disruption at Baxter

The shortage stems from a technical problem at Baxter's contract manufacturing site — the primary global supplier of ifosfamide. A regulatory inspection in September 2025 identified issues requiring site improvements, and when combined with a subsequent technical disruption, production and product release were paused. Although manufacturing has since resumed, the backlog and ongoing constraints mean supplies remain severely limited.

The [European Medicines Agency](https://www.ema.europa.eu/en/medicines/human/shortages/ifosfamide) confirmed the shortage on February 13, 2026, warning that ifosfamide supplies across the EU and European Economic Area would remain severely constrained until at least Q1 2027. The affected medicines include Holoxan, Tronoxal, and Mitoxana — all manufactured by Baxter.

## Doctors Forced to Make Impossible Choices

The human toll of the shortage is becoming increasingly apparent. Dr. Andrew Shuman, a head and neck cancer surgeon in Michigan, described the situation to The New York Times as devastating for all involved.

> "There are some individuals who won't be able to receive this drug, even if it is the best option for them, which puts pharmacists, doctors, patients and families in a horrific situation."

The [Yahoo News report](https://uk.news.yahoo.com/supplies-essential-chemotherapy-drugs-running-143946708.html) detailed how doctors are prioritizing younger patients with a full chance of recovery for the limited remaining supplies. Some medical practices have started spacing out the time between doses to stretch their inventory, while others are scheduling patients in consecutive appointments to ensure no medication is wasted.

## FDA Turns to Indian Manufacturers

In response to the crisis, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration has reached out to the Indian Drug Manufacturers' Association (IDMA) to identify manufacturers capable of supplying ifosfamide injection in 1g and 3g strengths, as [Business Today](https://www.businesstoday.in/industry/pharma/story/pharma-industry-body-scouts-for-indian-suppliers-as-us-faces-cancer-drug-shortage-538805-2026-06-23) reported.

Dr. Viranchi Shah, National Spokesperson and Immediate Past President of IDMA, confirmed: "The government has asked IDMA and other similar industry bodies to identify manufacturers who can supply ifosfamide."

The FDA is seeking information on manufacturing locations, regulatory approvals, and available capacity. Preference is being given to products manufactured at U.S. FDA-registered facilities, though the agency has also expressed interest in facilities with a demonstrated history of satisfactory regulatory compliance.

## A Broader Systemic Crisis

The ifosfamide shortage is not an isolated event — it is part of a larger, systemic crisis in generic drug manufacturing. A [STAT News](https://www.statnews.com/pharmalot/2026/06/09/drug-shortages-systemic-problem-analysis/) analysis by U.S. Pharmacopeia found that 75 drugs were in short supply in 2025, spanning 130 therapeutic categories. The average drug shortage lasted 5.3 years — up from 4.3 years in 2024 and far exceeding the two-year average in 2019. Nearly two-thirds of out-of-stock medicines were unavailable for more than three years.

Almost half of all drug shortages involve products with a single supplier. Generic sterile injectables — particularly older, lower-profit-margin drugs — are especially vulnerable. When one plant goes offline, there are often few or no alternative suppliers to fill the gap.

This pattern is recurring. In 2023, carboplatin and cisplatin — two other backbone chemotherapy drugs — experienced months-long shortages, prompting the FDA to allow temporary importation of foreign-approved versions. Dr. Lucio Gordan, president of the Florida Cancer Specialists and Research Institute, told The New York Times that "these three drugs remain the backbone of many cancer therapies."

## What's Being Done

The Department of Health and Human Services has acknowledged the severity of the situation. An HHS spokesperson told The Independent that the agency is "actively working with manufacturers and other stakeholders to help mitigate the impact of the shortage," including exploring "potential temporary importation, where appropriate, to help bridge supply gaps."

However, experts caution that any relief from Indian manufacturers will depend on manufacturing capacity, regulatory clearances, and the timeline for approval. The EMA has warned that the shortage is expected to persist until at least the first quarter of 2027.

## What to Watch For

As the shortage continues, several key questions remain unanswered: Which Indian manufacturers will be identified as potential suppliers, and how quickly can they ramp up production? Will the FDA authorize temporary importation of foreign-approved ifosfamide, as it did for cisplatin in 2023? And perhaps most importantly, what legislative or regulatory action will be taken to prevent future shortages of critical cancer medications?

The Mark Cuban Cost Plus Drug Company recently began manufacturing carboplatin and methotrexate — a model that some experts believe could be expanded to address the systemic vulnerabilities in the generic drug market. For now, however, doctors, patients, and families across the country continue to navigate a "horrific situation" with no immediate end in sight.
