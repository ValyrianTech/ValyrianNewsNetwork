# Trump $1 Coin Faces Legal Scrutiny Over Federal Laws

The U.S. Treasury Department has begun minting a $1 commemorative gold-colored coin featuring President Donald Trump's portrait, sparking sharp criticism from legal experts and bipartisan lawmakers who argue it violates multiple federal laws governing currency and executive ethics. The U.S. Mint in Philadelphia began pressing the coins on July 15, with distribution expected in fall 2026 as part of America's 250th anniversary celebrations, according to [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/07/17/nx-s1-5897380/trump-coin-face-laws-treasury-250).

## The Legal Challenge

At the heart of the controversy are two federal statutes. The Thayer Amendment of 1866, codified at 31 U.S.C. § 5114, states that "only the portrait of a deceased individual may appear on United States currency and securities." The Presidential $1 Coin Act of 2005 further requires presidents to be deceased for at least two years before appearing on coins. Legal experts say the Trump coin runs afoul of both.

"The law prohibits, currently, on currency, the likeness of any person — not just the president — who is alive," said Jeremy Paul, a professor and former dean at Northeastern University School of Law, as reported by NPR.

## Treasury's Defense

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has defended the coin's legality, citing the Circulating Collectible Coin Redesign Act of 2020, which authorizes the Treasury Secretary to issue $1 coins "with designs emblematic of the United States Semiquincentennial" during a one-year period beginning January 1, 2026. Bessent also pointed to the 1926 Calvin Coolidge half-dollar as precedent, as [Business Insider](https://www.businessinsider.com/us-mint-trump-portrait-dollar-gold-coin-legality-2026-7) reported.

However, critics note that the same 2020 Act restricts living persons on coin designs, and the Coolidge precedent is weak — over 859,000 of the 1 million Coolidge half-dollars minted were ultimately returned and melted.

## Bipartisan Opposition

The coin has drawn criticism from both sides of the aisle. Rep. Thomas Massie (R-KY) posted on X: "Congratulations, we've entered the end stages. Eliminate the penny, plug the nickel, and make some commemorative gold coins nobody can afford." Rep. Brad Schneider (D-IL) accused the administration of disregarding the burdens on American families, according to Business Insider.

Rep. Joyce Beatty (D-OH) highlighted the irony, tweeting: "They can put Trump on a $1 coin, but can't keep a decades-old promise to put Harriet Tubman on the $20 bill??? This Treasury is funding presidential vanity projects."

## Committee Review Bypassed

The Citizens Coinage Advisory Committee (CCAC), a nonpartisan federal group created by Congress to advise on coin designs, was never asked to review the Trump coin. Donald Scarinci, a CCAC member, stated that production would be illegal, telling Business Insider: "No one other than Congress has the power to legislate or to enact coin designs in America. And Congress has not passed legislation authorizing this coin."

## Broader Pattern of Presidential Imagery

The coin is part of a broader effort by the Trump administration to place the president's image on federal symbols. New U.S. passports feature Trump's image, national park passes include his picture, all future paper currency will bear his signature, and the Treasury has prepared designs for a $250 bill featuring Trump's face. House Democrats have accused Trump of "hijacking" America's 250th birthday celebrations for personal and political gain, as [USA Today](https://www.usatoday.com/story/money/2026/07/17/us-treasury-mints-trump-coin-federal-law-faces-on-currency/90959018007/) reported.

## Historical Context

The Thayer Amendment was enacted in 1866 after a Treasury official placed his own portrait on a banknote intended to honor the late explorer William Clark. George Washington actively refused to have his image on currency to avoid appearing monarchical. Richard Painter, former White House ethics lawyer for President George W. Bush, told NPR: "One of the reasons for Congress wanting to prohibit this is that we don't want presidents acting like kings."

## Lawsuit Dismissed on Standing

In March, James Rickher, a retired lawyer in Portland, Oregon, sued to block the coin, arguing it would harm him as a coin collector. A federal judge dismissed the lawsuit in June on standing grounds — ruling that Rickher had failed to demonstrate concrete injury — without addressing the underlying legal question.

## What's Next

The coin is set for distribution in fall 2026, though legal challenges remain possible if a plaintiff can establish standing. The "Change Corruption Act," introduced by Sens. Jeff Merkley (D-OR) and Catherine Cortez Masto (D-NV) in December 2025 to block living or sitting presidents from appearing on currency, has not yet passed Congress. As the 250th anniversary celebrations continue, the legal and political debate over the Trump coin — and the broader pattern of presidential branding on federal symbols — shows no signs of resolution.
