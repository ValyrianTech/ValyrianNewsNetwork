# New Details in ICE Killing of Houston Father Lorenzo Salgado

A federal prosecutor in Texas has released new details that significantly complicate the government's account of how Lorenzo Salgado Araujo, a 52-year-old Mexican national and father of three, was fatally shot by a U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement officer on July 7 in Houston. The disclosure has intensified scrutiny of the Trump administration's immigration enforcement campaign, which has now left at least eight people dead at the hands of federal agents.

According to [AP News](https://apnews.com/article/immigration-enforcement-houston-trump-lorenzo-salgado-araujo-fab45a4f012ff844c3e93749245eb454), U.S. Attorney Aaron Reitz revealed on July 16 that ICE officers were not targeting Salgado Araujo at all. Instead, they were pursuing two Guatemalan men believed to be subject to deportation, and mistakenly identified Salgado Araujo's white van as matching the description of their target vehicle.

## The Shooting and Its Aftermath

Salgado Araujo, a homebuilder who had lived in the United States for 35 years and was reportedly close to obtaining legal status, was driving his construction crew to a job site in Houston's Magnolia Park neighborhood at approximately 6:50 AM when the incident unfolded. His brother was among the three co-workers in the van.

Reitz's statement described a sequence of events that began when four officers in two unmarked vehicles attempted to pull over Salgado Araujo's van. He made a U-turn and drove over a median to evade the first stop attempt. When officers encountered the van again later that morning, they surrounded the vehicle. Two agents got out and ordered Salgado Araujo to put the vehicle in park. Just before the shooting, Reitz said, one agent was "partially inside the van or immediately next to it" when Salgado Araujo tried to reverse and then drive forward.

Crucially, the prosecutor's account omitted any mention of a collision between Salgado Araujo's van and a law enforcement vehicle, directly contradicting the Department of Homeland Security's initial claim that he had "weaponized his vehicle" by ramming an ICE vehicle. The statement also did not explicitly say the officer feared for his life, according to [AP News](https://apnews.com/article/ice-shooting-salgado-araujo-houston-7f8b3218b97c63388fc016b3da9718ee).

## Witness Accounts Contradict Official Narrative

The three detained passengers have told their attorney, Hugo Balderas-Ibarra, a starkly different version of events. According to [AP News](https://apnews.com/article/ice-shooting-houston-lorenzo-salgado-araugo-10cf77f29d4559f0f3796342b946031a), the witnesses said the officer shot Salgado Araujo through the passenger window and that no officer was ever in front of the van or in danger. Images of the van after the shooting reportedly show no damage, further undermining the self-defense claim.

"After speaking with these men, I have no doubt that what they're saying is the truth. I know that these agents — the agency — is going to try to cover it up," Balderas-Ibarra said during a news conference.

Adding to the controversy, Reitz noted that officers saw "several small bags of a white, crystal-like substance" in plain view inside the van, which the FBI later investigated with a search warrant. Salgado Araujo's brother's attorney said the substance was simply a salt mixture used by manual laborers as electrolytes to stay hydrated in the Texas heat.

## A Pattern of Deadly Encounters

Salgado Araujo's death is not an isolated incident. Since President Donald Trump took office, federal immigration agents have opened fire on at least 30 people, killing eight. No immigration officers have been charged in any of the killings, and video footage in several previous shootings has contradicted the accounts of federal officers.

The killings have occurred against a backdrop of dramatically expanded enforcement capabilities. In June 2026, Congress approved $70 billion in funding for ICE and Customs and Border Protection, with $38 billion specifically allocated for ICE to expand arrests, detention, and deportation operations. The administration has recruited thousands of new agents, many without proper vetting.

Just days after Salgado Araujo's death, Joan Sebastian Duran Guerrero, 26, was killed by ICE in Maine — the second fatal shooting in less than a week. The spate of deadly encounters prompted ICE to briefly pause vehicle stops, but President Trump overturned that pause, demanding agents resume operations.

## The Body Camera Gap

A recurring issue in these cases is the absence of body cameras. Despite promises from DHS to equip all agents with body cameras following the killings of U.S. citizens Renee Good and Alex Pretti in Minneapolis earlier this year, implementation has not been completed. The officers involved in both the Houston and Maine shootings were not wearing body cameras, leaving critical questions about what happened dependent on conflicting accounts.

Houston Mayor John Whitmire did not mince words in his assessment. "We recognize that it is a federal police agency that was out of control Tuesday morning," he said, according to AP News.

## Diplomatic Fallout and the Search for Accountability

The case has escalated beyond domestic politics. Mexican President Claudia Sheinbaum announced that Mexico will request criminal charges in U.S. courts over the alleged killing of three Mexicans during ICE operations and the deaths of another 14 in ICE custody. "We are going to do everything in our power, because we cannot stand silent" in the face of the deaths of Mexicans "whose only crime is working honestly in the United States," Sheinbaum said.

Locally, Harris County District Attorney Sean Teare is leading an investigation, but federal authorities are tightly controlling evidence. DHS has refused to release the officer's name, citing potential threats, and has not confirmed whether the officer has been placed on leave.

Meanwhile, the three detained witnesses remain in ICE custody, and their attorney has alleged they are being pressured to self-deport — a move that would severely hinder their ability to testify. DHS has called those allegations "categorically false."

## What's Next

Several critical questions remain unanswered. The officer's identity has not been released. The FBI's search warrant analysis of the white substance found in the van has not been made public. And the body camera program that could provide future accountability remains unimplemented.

For Salgado Araujo's family, the search for justice continues. His eldest son, Ronaldo Salgado, has emerged as a powerful voice demanding transparency. "That's how I want the world to know my father," he said. "Not as someone who got shot and killed, but as a family man, a man who understood that good things come to those who put in hard work."

The case has become a flashpoint in the broader debate over immigration enforcement in America — a debate that shows no signs of resolution as the death toll continues to rise.
