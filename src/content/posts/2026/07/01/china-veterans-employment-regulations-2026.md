# China Issues Landmark Regulations for Veterans Employment

China has officially promulgated a comprehensive set of regulations aimed at strengthening employment and entrepreneurship opportunities for the nation's military veterans, establishing a unified legal framework to support their transition to civilian life. Premier Li Qiang signed State Council Order No. 840 on June 26, promulgating the Regulations on Promoting Employment and Entrepreneurship for Veterans, which [Xinhua News Agency](https://www.news.cn/politics/20260630/375bd4b2a3484ef3a37fafbeef0b70c6/c.html) published in full on June 30. The 48-article regulation will take effect on August 1, 2026, coinciding with the 99th anniversary of the founding of the People's Liberation Army.

## Context and Legal Foundation

The new regulations build upon China's Veterans Protection Law, which took effect in June 2021 and established the foundational rights and protections for the country's approximately 57 million veterans. The regulations are also derived from the Employment Promotion Law and represent the culmination of years of policy development. During the 14th Five-Year Plan period (2021–2025), China resettled over 250,000 demobilized officers and arranged-work veterans, held more than 50,000 job fairs reaching 1.4 million veterans, and established nearly 2,000 entrepreneurship incubation bases nationwide, according to a [State Council Information Office press conference](http://www.mod.gov.cn/gfbw/qwfb/yw_214049/16400002.html) in July 2025.

In 2025 alone, 870,000 veterans were helped into employment, and 187,000 veteran-owned micro-enterprises and farmer cooperatives received loan support, as [CCTV News](https://news.cctv.cn/2026/01/10/ARTItA3tFK5shWwbleqvVJzf260110.shtml) reported in January 2026. The new regulations codify and strengthen these scattered policy measures into a single, enforceable legal instrument.

## Key Provisions of the Regulation

The regulation is structured across six chapters covering general principles, education and training, employment support, entrepreneurship facilitation, legal responsibilities, and supplementary provisions.

### Education and Training (Articles 9–25)

The regulations mandate that veterans retain their enrollment or student status for two years after discharge and enjoy preferential policies in national examinations, including tuition and stipend support. Free vocational skills training is guaranteed for self-employed veterans under retirement age, with training subsidies available. Military-acquired vocational qualification certificates are recognized nationwide after discharge, and the regulations integrate veterans training into China's national lifelong vocational training system.

### Employment Support (Articles 26–34)

Government organs, state-owned enterprises, and public institutions may relax age and education requirements for veterans and must give them priority under equal conditions. Specific grassroots civil service positions are reserved for veterans with five or more years of service. The regulations also mandate quotas for veterans in national comprehensive fire rescue teams and prioritize them for police auxiliary staff positions. Military service is counted as grassroots work experience, and service years are calculated toward seniority and pension benefits.

### Entrepreneurship Support (Articles 35–42)

Self-employed veterans enjoy tax preferential policies and access to guaranteed entrepreneurship loans with interest subsidies. Government-built entrepreneurship bases are required to provide dedicated zones for veterans, and the regulations encourage veterans to engage in new technologies, industries, and business models. Dedicated service windows and green channels at government service centers are mandated for veteran entrepreneurs.

## Legal Accountability

For the first time, the regulations establish clear legal consequences for government officials who falsify documents, misappropriate funds, or engage in dereliction of duty in veterans employment work (Chapter 5). Veterans who fraudulently obtain benefits face cancellation of those benefits and recovery of illegal gains. The [Chinese government website](https://www.gov.cn/yaowen/liebiao/202606/content_7073794.htm) emphasized that these accountability measures represent a significant step toward ensuring effective implementation.

## Analysis and Implications

The regulations represent a major advancement in China's approach to veteran transition, moving from scattered policy documents to a consolidated legal framework with enforcement mechanisms. "The Regulations aim to promote employment and entrepreneurship for veterans, leverage the role of veterans as human resources, safeguard their legitimate rights and interests, and make military service a profession respected by the whole society," the [Xinhua News Agency summary](https://www.xinhuanet.com/politics/20260630/d8515ddaaa8c43d0a2856d46ca613cc7/c.html) stated.

Sheng Baochen, Head of the Employment and Entrepreneurship Division at the Ministry of Veterans Affairs, had previously outlined plans to promote the "training first, job placement follow-up" model and tap into job resources in new industry sectors. The regulations formalize this approach while also supporting China's broader strategy of military-civilian integration.

## What to Watch For

As the regulations take effect on August 1, several key questions remain. Local governments, particularly in less-developed regions, will need to secure funding for implementation. The quota systems for veterans in civil service and state-owned enterprises may face scrutiny from other job seekers in a competitive labor market. Additionally, the effectiveness of training programs and the mechanisms for veterans to appeal rights violations will be critical to the regulations' real-world impact. The [People's Daily](http://paper.people.com.cn/rmrb/pc/content/202607/01/content_30165869.html) featured the regulations on its front page on July 1, underscoring the political significance Beijing attaches to this policy initiative.

With China facing broader economic transition and youth unemployment pressures, the successful integration of veterans into the civilian workforce remains a priority for social stability and military morale. The new regulations provide the legal architecture to meet this challenge, but their implementation will determine whether they deliver on their promise.
