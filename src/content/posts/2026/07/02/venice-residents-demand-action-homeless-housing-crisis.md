# Venice Residents Demand Action Over Homeless Housing in Iconic Beach Community

More than 100 residents of Venice, California — the iconic Los Angeles beach community — packed a heated community meeting on Monday night to confront leaders of two supportive housing facilities, describing a neighborhood pushed to its breaking point by crime, drug activity, and deteriorating public safety. The confrontation at St. Mark Parish Hall has reignited a fierce debate over how Los Angeles balances the urgent need for homeless housing with community safety concerns.

## A Community at the Breaking Point

The facilities at the center of the controversy — The Journey Program, operated by [Venice Community Housing](https://la.urbanize.city/post/supportive-housing-opens-2467-lincoln-blvd-venice), and Safe Place for Youth (SPY) — occupy a four-story building at 2467-2471 Lincoln Boulevard. The 40-unit complex, which opened approximately 18 months ago, provides supportive housing for homeless adults, families, transition-age youth, and persons with disabilities. But residents say the promised stability has come at an unbearable cost.

"We're finding dead bodies as we're doing our daily s--- and p--- cleanup every morning. And trash," one resident told the meeting, according to [Fox News](https://www.foxnews.com/us/furious-residents-confront-homeless-housing-leaders-popular-beach-community-hits-breaking-point). Another parent said, "I don't feel safe sending my teenage kids out to walk the dogs on their own, and that's ridiculous."

Residents described finding needles on sidewalks, human feces, open drug use, trespassing, theft, broken car windows, and public masturbation. The litany of complaints, detailed by [FOX 11 Los Angeles](https://www.foxla.com/news/venice-residents-demand-answers-over-safety-concerns-near-supportive-housing-programs), painted a picture of a neighborhood in crisis.

## Church and Community Caught in the Crossfire

St. Mark's Church, a Venice institution since 1949 that shares the block with the facilities, has been forced to hire private security and can no longer keep its doors open to the public. Pastor Albert van der Woerd described the situation bluntly: "There is an explosion of bad behavior, and we need to get the law enforced," he said, as reported by [Yo! Venice!](https://yovenice.com/2026/07/01/venice-shorts-residents-confront-officials-over-ongoing-crisis-at-lincoln-blvd-housing-site/).

Cody Helfand, a local business owner, told the crowd, "I see kids smoking meth, they carry weapons, and there is no consequence for these actions." Another resident simply pleaded, "I don't want to pick up human feces any longer."

## Official Response and Funding Threats

Los Angeles City Councilmember Traci Park, whose district includes Venice and who lives in the neighborhood herself, attended the meeting and acknowledged the severity of the situation. "I don't just represent this neighborhood; I live here too. I see it every day in my travels," Park said. "The escalation of crime and theft and drugs and violence. No neighborhood should have to put up with that anywhere in Los Angeles."

Park warned that future city funding for the facilities could be jeopardized if conditions fail to improve. "We are going to continue to fix this and hold these people responsible for providing their own security, for ensuring that the neighborhood around these facilities remains safe," she said. "And you know what? Then we will start talking about walking back their future funding."

She has already drafted a council motion, dated June 20, that would study conditioning city funding for supportive housing on background checks, comprehensive behavioral-health assessments, and screening out individuals with violent criminal histories — a significant departure from the "housing first" model that typically minimizes barriers to entry.

## Provider Response and Resident Skepticism

Allison Riley, Co-Executive Director of Venice Community Housing, declined an on-camera interview after the meeting. When pressed about accountability, she responded, "We're trying to give that to the community, and we will keep working on that." On its website, VCH states that "providing supportive services in conjunction with stable housing is the proven long-term solution to end homelessness."

Residents left the meeting deeply skeptical. "This feels like a lot of lip service and not a lot of action," one said. Another business owner declared, "It can't just be an open door. Actions need to start having consequences."

## Broader Implications

The Venice controversy reflects a growing tension across California and other states between the humanitarian imperative to house homeless individuals and the concerns of existing residents about public safety and neighborhood character. Similar battles have played out in San Francisco, Oakland, Seattle, and Portland.

Park's motion to require background checks and risk assessments for supportive housing tenants could set a precedent for how Los Angeles oversees its multibillion-dollar homeless housing investments. With local elections approaching, the issue is likely to become a rallying point for candidates who prioritize public safety.

## What's Next

A follow-up meeting has been promised to present concrete solutions, but residents remain unconvinced that meaningful change will follow. Councilmember Park's motion is expected to move through the City Council in the coming weeks, and the outcome could reshape Los Angeles's approach to supportive housing oversight for years to come.
