# China Unveils Landmark Guidelines to Strengthen Social Work

BEIJING — China has issued a sweeping new policy framework to overhaul its social work system, with the Communist Party of China (CPC) Central Committee and the State Council jointly publishing the "Opinions on Strengthening Social Work in the New Era" on July 23, 2026. The guidelines represent the highest-level policy directive on social governance since the establishment of the Central Social Work Department in 2023, signaling an intensified push to modernize community-level governance, extend Party organization into emerging economic sectors, and professionalize social services nationwide.

According to [Xinhua News](https://english.news.cn/20260723/f4d39181da424cc186fd79d4cf99cc2e/c.html), the document declares that "social work is an important component of the work of the Party and the state, bearing on the Party's long-term governance and the country's enduring peace and stability." The guidelines were published on the front page of People's Daily on July 24 and carried by all major Chinese state media outlets.

## Context: Building on Institutional Reform

The guidelines arrive three years after the creation of the Central Social Work Department, a central Party organ established during the 2023 Party and State Institutional Reform Plan. The department consolidated functions previously scattered across multiple ministries, including petition work coordination, public suggestion collection, grassroots governance oversight, and Party building in new economic and social organizations.

As [China Daily](https://global.chinadaily.com.cn/a/202607/24/WS6a62c623a310986e2b467167.html) reported, the new guidelines emphasize that "the Party's overall leadership over social work must be upheld and enhanced and work must remain people-centered." The document builds on a precursor policy from October 2025 that focused specifically on service management for new employment groups in the gig economy.

## Five Pillars of the New Framework

The guidelines are organized around five major areas, each addressing a critical dimension of China's evolving social governance landscape.

**Party Leadership Mechanisms:** The document establishes a comprehensive framework requiring centralized, unified leadership by the Party Central Committee. It calls for incorporating Xi Jinping's expositions on social work into Party school training and creating a work pattern of "unified Party committee leadership, coordinated by Party social work departments, coordinated by relevant departments, and widely participated by social forces."

**Emerging Sector Party Building:** One of the most significant sections targets new economic organizations, new social organizations, and new employment groups — encompassing internet platform companies, tech startups, gig workers, delivery drivers, and freelancers. The guidelines specifically mention "algorithm governance" of internet platforms, indicating a regulatory push to address working conditions in the platform economy.

**Grassroots Governance:** The document calls for streamlining township and subdistrict management systems, reducing burdens on grassroots cadres through "responsibility lists," and consolidating multiple governance grids into unified systems. It explicitly references the "Fengqiao Experience" — a community-level conflict resolution model originating from Zhejiang province — as a guiding principle for resolving disputes at the local level.

**Public Service and Mass Work:** Key measures include reforming the petition system and legalizing the process, establishing systematic channels for collecting public suggestions, expanding volunteer service systems, and using Party members, mass organizations, and legislative deputies as conduits for public engagement.

**Foundational Support:** The guidelines emphasize building a comprehensive legal framework for social work, advancing digital transformation through "smart communities" and "digital villages," professionalizing the social work workforce, and developing a dedicated social work cadre.

## Analysis: A Strategic Priority

The issuance of these guidelines reflects the Party's strategic priority on maintaining social stability amid rapid economic transformation. China's platform economy and gig economy have created new categories of workers outside traditional employment structures, presenting both governance challenges and opportunities for organizational outreach.

According to the [Chinese Government website](https://www.gov.cn/yaowen/liebiao/202607/content_7076427.htm), the guidelines aim to "promote positive interaction between high-quality development and high-level security through high-efficiency governance." This framing ties social work directly to the Party's broader agenda of "common prosperity" and social stability as prerequisites for continued development.

The document's emphasis on digital transformation is particularly noteworthy. By calling for the advancement of smart communities, digital villages, and the "digital-intelligent transformation" of social work, the guidelines signal an intent to leverage technology for more efficient service delivery and governance — a approach consistent with China's broader push for digital governance modernization.

## What to Watch For

While the guidelines establish a comprehensive policy framework, several questions remain unanswered. The document does not specify a concrete implementation timeline, leaving local governments to develop their own schedules. Resource allocation — including budgetary commitments and personnel deployment — will be critical to the guidelines' success, particularly in less developed interior provinces where social service infrastructure is thinner.

The mention of "algorithm governance" for internet platforms is notable but lacks specific regulatory detail. Observers will be watching for follow-up regulations that could reshape how platform companies manage working conditions for millions of gig economy workers.

As China moves to implement these guidelines, the world's largest governing party is attempting nothing less than a systematic modernization of how it engages with and serves a rapidly changing society — from village committees to tech campuses, from community mediation centers to digital governance platforms.
