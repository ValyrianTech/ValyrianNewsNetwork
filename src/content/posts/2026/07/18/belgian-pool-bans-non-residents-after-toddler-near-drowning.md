# Belgian Pool Bans Non-Residents After Toddler's Near-Drowning

A public outdoor pool in Wetteren, East Flanders, has taken the unprecedented step of restricting access to only residents of the province following the near-drowning of a five-year-old boy earlier this week. The measure, announced on July 17 by Acting Mayor Bram De Winne, has ignited a fierce debate in Belgium about regional discrimination, public safety, and the limits of local authority.

## The Incident

On July 13, five-year-old Moussa from Roubaix, France, nearly drowned at the De Warande outdoor pool in the afternoon. According to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/07/13/warande-wetteren-kleuter-levensgevaar-zwembad/), he was rescued by a visitor, resuscitated by a lifeguard using an AED, and transported to hospital in life-threatening condition. As of July 18, his condition remains critical.

The incident was further complicated by a burqini controversy. According to multiple sources, Moussa's mother was denied entry to the pool because she was wearing a burqini (full-body swimsuit). Sports Alderman Peter Blancquaert confirmed to [HLN](https://www.hln.be/wetteren/verdronk-moussa-5-bijna-nadat-mama-afgeleid-werd-ze-mocht-het-water-niet-in-omdat-ze-boerkini-aanhad~ad024da3/) that "the lady in question was indeed informed that she could not enter the pool with a burqini. The clothing regulations must be respected." The family allegedly ignored the restriction and entered anyway, with the mother reportedly distracted as Moussa ran toward the water unsupervised.

## The Access Restriction

On July 17, Acting Mayor Bram De Winne issued a mayoral decree restricting access to East Flanders residents only, based on police advice. The measure takes effect July 20 and will remain in place until the pool closes for the season on August 30.

"All the calamities that have occurred in recent weeks are often attributable to people from outside the province," De Winne told [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/07/17/buitenzwembad-warande-wetteren-enkel-oost-vlamingen-toegelaten/). "We are doing this to guarantee peace and safety for people who really want to use the pool for what it's meant for: recreation and having fun."

Additional security measures include separate entrance and exit gates, identity checks at the entrance, a permanent police presence, and doubling the number of external stewards from two to four. A new reservation system will cap the morning block at 500 visitors and the afternoon block at 700.

## A Broader Pattern of Tensions

The near-drowning was the third incident in recent weeks at the 74-year-old pool, which is operating its final summer season before a new indoor facility opens. According to [HLN](https://www.hln.be/wetteren/na-bijna-verdrinking-moussa-5-laat-buitenzwembad-de-warande-in-wetteren-alleen-nog-oost-vlamingen-toe~af361cd7/), two other children had been rescued from the water previously, though with less severe outcomes. The local government reports that existing rules no longer suffice to maintain order, citing verbal and physical intimidation of staff, visitors climbing over fences without paying, and disturbances at the reception desk.

Wetteren is not alone in facing these challenges. Similar issues have been reported at other Flemish pools: Kluisbergen closed due to nuisance from Northern French visitors, while Kortrijk introduced passport checks.

## Divided Reactions

The restriction has drawn sharp criticism. Eva Peeters, a VTM NIEUWS journalist, called the measure "simply discrimination" in comments reported by [HLN](https://www.hln.be/video/productie/wat-de-gemeente-wetteren-doet-is-gewoon-discriminatie-13788450). Critics argue that restricting access based on provincial residency is a proxy for ethnic discrimination and amounts to collective punishment of all non-East Flanders residents.

On the other side, Flemish nationalist politician Bob De Brabandere of Vlaams Belang framed the incident as evidence of failed multiculturalism, stating: "The result of imposed multiculturalism is that we all become less free."

The family of Moussa has expressed anger at the response time of emergency services. A cousin told HLN: "The lifeguards took too long to act. Why did you wait 20 minutes to intervene before the ambulance arrived, when you could see his heart wasn't beating anymore?" The municipality has defended its lifeguards, with Blancquaert insisting they are trained professionals who acted correctly.

## Legal and Political Questions

The mayoral decree raises significant questions under Belgian anti-discrimination law and the Belgian Constitution, which guarantees freedom of movement. The municipality argues the measure is not discriminatory because it is based on domicile rather than ethnicity or religion, and is justified by public order and safety concerns.

Legal challenges from human rights organizations are widely anticipated. The outcome may set a precedent for how other municipalities handle similar tensions between local residents and visitors from outside the province.

## What's Next

As the pool prepares to reopen on Monday under the new restrictions, several questions remain unanswered. Will the restriction face legal challenges? How will it be enforced for families with mixed residency? And most urgently, what is the current condition of five-year-old Moussa, who remains in critical condition? The public prosecutor's investigation into the incident is ongoing.

The story intersects with multiple sensitive issues in Belgian society: immigration from neighboring France, religious attire regulations, public safety, local autonomy, and the limits of acceptable security measures. The coming weeks will reveal whether Wetteren's approach becomes a model for other municipalities or a cautionary tale about the boundaries of local authority.
