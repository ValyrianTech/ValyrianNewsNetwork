# UK's Incoming PM Burnham Rejects Thatcher-Era Economic Model

Incoming UK Prime Minister Andy Burnham has explicitly rejected the Thatcher-era economic model in his first address as Labour leader, signaling the most significant leftward shift in British politics in decades. The former Greater Manchester mayor, who will be sworn in by King Charles III on Monday, condemned the neoliberal policies established in the 1980s and promised greater public control of essential services.

## A Break from Four Decades of Economic Orthodoxy

Speaking at a special Labour Party conference in London on July 17, Burnham declared that Britain had taken "a series of wrong turns in the 1980s," when political power was centralized and economic power was privatized, according to [Fox News](https://www.foxnews.com/world/incoming-uk-pm-andy-burnham-rejects-thatcher-era-policies-signals-leftward-shift). He argued that four decades of free-market policy had "not been kind" to working-class communities that traditionally supported Labour.

"The country surrendered control of the essentials — housing, water, energy, transport — and left people exposed to higher costs," Burnham said during the speech. He described his ascent as "the most significant change moment in our politics for 40 years."

Burnham succeeds Sir Keir Starmer, who resigned after devastating local election losses in May 2026 and a Labour revolt. The new leader received 379 nominations from Labour MPs out of 403, making him the sole candidate in an uncontested leadership race that has drawn criticism from opponents.

## Five Commitments and a "Distinctively Labour" Direction

In his address, Burnham outlined five key commitments: party unity, a "problem-solving" approach to politics, a "distinctively Labour" direction, governing for the whole UK, and devolving power away from London. As [BBC News](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c2lq12z1xdlo) reported, he promised to be a leader "for the north and the south, for Scotland, for Wales and for Northern Ireland, and for every town and every city."

Burnham said Labour would "not try to out-Green the Greens or out-Reform Reform," signaling a break from both far-left and right-wing populist strategies. "We win by being us boldly, confidently, authentically us — Labour," he declared, as [Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/7/17/burnham-confirmed-as-leader-of-uks-governing-labour-party-headed-for-pm) reported.

He has pledged a "No. 10 North" unit in Manchester to devolve power to regional governments and promised "the biggest rebalancing" of power away from Westminster in modern British history.

## Critics Question Substance and Economic Credibility

The speech has drawn sharp reactions from free-market analysts and conservative figures. Alan Mendoza, executive director of the Henry Jackson Society, told Fox News that Burnham's address offered "a lot of light and heat, but not much actual substance." He questioned whether Burnham's vision represented "socialism of a past kind" or "some form of statism."

Emma Schubart, a research fellow at the Henry Jackson Society who previously worked at the Adam Smith Institute, was more pointed, telling Fox News Digital that Burnham "comes across as pretty economically illiterate." She argued his message was contradictory, presenting his leadership as a national renewal while proposing to dismantle Thatcher-era reforms.

The Adam Smith Institute published a lengthy defense of the Thatcher era in response, highlighting reductions in tax rates, rising homeownership from 55% to 67%, and a dramatic decline in working days lost to strikes.

Even within his own party, questions remain. Graham Stringer, one of 24 Labour MPs who did not nominate Burnham, told BBC Radio 4's Today programme that "a whole series of major policies are still a mystery," adding that nominating him would have meant "giving a blank cheque."

## The Challenges Ahead

Burnham takes over with Labour trailing Reform UK in opinion polls for almost 18 months. His strategy appears to be winning back working-class voters lost to Nigel Farage's party by offering a more authentic Labour message rather than trying to outflank the right.

The incoming prime minister faces significant economic headwinds. The International Monetary Fund has warned his government to avoid increasing public spending, urging a "cautious approach to new fiscal pressures." Financial markets may react negatively to anti-business rhetoric, though Burnham has insisted he will be "pro-business" and fiscally disciplined.

Mendoza warned that Burnham's leftward shift could complicate relations with the Trump administration. "The government could most definitely clash with the United States under Burnham's vision, because the voters he is trying to bring back into his tent include many of those who are deeply hostile to America," he told Fox News.

As [Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2026/07/17/andy-burnham-set-to-become-labour-leader-before-taking-over-as-uk-pm) noted, Burnham has previously called for the UK to rejoin the European Union but sought to soften his stance during his Makerfield campaign, saying: "My view is that Brexit has been damaging. But I also believe the last thing we should do right now is rerun those arguments."

## What to Watch For

Burnham is expected to announce his cabinet on Monday after being formally appointed prime minister. Home Secretary Shabana Mahmood is widely tipped as his choice for Chancellor of the Exchequer. The coming weeks will reveal whether his rhetorical shift translates into concrete policy changes — or whether, as his critics suggest, the substance fails to match the ambition.

The [Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2026/jul/17/andy-burnham-what-first-speech-as-labour-leader-tells-us) described his approach as "borrowing from populism, albeit lightly, and infused with togetherness rather than Nigel Farage's division and anger." Whether that formula can reverse Labour's polling fortunes and navigate the economic and geopolitical challenges ahead remains the defining question of Britain's new political era.
