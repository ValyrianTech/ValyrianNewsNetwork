# US and Iran Exchange Strikes in Major Middle East Escalation

The United States and Iran exchanged strikes targeting critical infrastructure and military positions on Saturday, July 18, as Iran formally announced it was suspending its commitments under the Islamabad Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) — the interim peace deal brokered by Pakistan just one month ago. The escalation marks a significant deterioration in the months-long conflict and threatens to draw the entire Gulf region deeper into the war.

Iranian Deputy Foreign Minister Kazem Gharibabadi told state television that the United States had violated its commitments under the agreement, adding that Tehran was "no longer implementing them." Speaking to Iran's Fars news agency, Gharibabadi stated: "We have also suspended our commitments, we are not implementing them, and we are busy defending the country," as [reported by the Associated Press](https://apnews.com/article/iran-us-hormuz-strait-war-july-18-2026-5adfef67554652580963684d9a663af2).

## Background: The Islamabad MoU

The Islamabad Memorandum of Understanding was a 14-point agreement signed on June 17, 2026, between the United States and Iran, with Pakistan's Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif serving as mediator. The deal established a 60-day ceasefire extension, provisions for reopening the Strait of Hormuz, and a framework for discussions on Iran's nuclear program. Its collapse on Saturday represents a complete breakdown of the most significant diplomatic effort to end the war since it began on February 28.

## Military Developments

U.S. Central Command (CENTCOM) confirmed its seventh consecutive night of airstrikes against Iran, hitting "surveillance sites, military logistics infrastructure, underground weapons storage, and maritime capabilities" using fighter aircraft, aerial drones, and warships. CENTCOM stated that it continues to "hold Iran accountable at the Commander in Chief's direction while fully enforcing a naval blockade against Iranian ports," with more than 50,000 American service members operating across the Middle East, according to [Fox News](https://www.foxnews.com/live-news/iran-war-trump-israel-hormuz-oil-july-18-2026).

On the Iranian side, U.S. airstrikes destroyed the Bonji desalination plant in Hormozgan province, cutting off water supplies to approximately 10,000 people. A desalination plant on strategic Qeshm Island inside the Strait of Hormuz was also damaged. Iranian state media reported that three bridges were hit on Saturday, including one on a route to Bandar Abbas, Iran's main port near the narrowest point of the strait. Iran's Energy Ministry issued a call for civilians in southern provinces experiencing extreme heat to reduce power consumption after strikes on electricity infrastructure, as [Al Jazeera reported](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/7/18/iran-accuses-us-of-striking-critical-infrastructure-as-war-intensifies).

## Regional Spillover: Iran Strikes Gulf States

Iran retaliated by launching waves of drones and missiles at multiple Gulf states. Kuwait suffered the most significant damage, with a water desalination plant and an oil facility hit. The Kuwait Petroleum Corporation described the attacks as "repeated, brutal Iranian attacks, resulting in severe material losses along with some injuries." Several firefighters and workers were injured while battling blazes sparked by the strikes. Kuwait briefly closed its airspace, and Kuwait Airways rescheduled most flights.

Bahrain's military said it "confronted, intercepted, and destroyed a number of treacherous Iranian aerial attacks," while Jordan's air defense systems downed 10 Iranian missiles and the Royal Jordanian Air Force shot down four unmanned drones. Air sirens also sounded in Saudi Arabia. Qatar's Foreign Ministry condemned the attacks in the strongest terms, stating that "targeting electricity and water desalination plants in the State of Kuwait crosses all red lines and constitutes an egregious violation of UN Security Council Resolution 2817."

## The Strait of Hormuz Crisis

At the heart of the conflict remains control of the Strait of Hormuz, the narrow waterway through which approximately one-fifth of the world's oil supply previously transited. Iran effectively closed the strait to shipping after the war began on February 28, sending oil prices soaring. Tehran has demanded sole control of the waterway and fees from vessels, while the United States and the international community consider it an international waterway.

Crossings through the strait fell to a three-week low on Saturday. While a growing amount of regional energy is being shipped through pipelines, it is not nearly enough to offset the decline in shipping. The [United Nations](https://news.un.org/en/story/2026/07/1167898) has warned that renewed attacks risk "catastrophic consequences for the peoples of the region, for international peace and security, and for the global economy as a whole."

## Casualties and Human Impact

Iranian authorities reported that at least 50 people have been killed and more than 500 wounded in U.S. strikes over the past three weeks, including eight killed in a bridge strike on Friday. U.S. officials acknowledged 13 additional service members had been injured since Monday, bringing total U.S. casualties since the war began to 14 killed and 427 wounded. Independent estimates from MilitarySpend.org place total casualties across all sides between 8,966 and 18,300.

## Analysis and Outlook

The suspension of the Islamabad MoU represents a catastrophic failure of diplomacy. The deal, signed only one month ago, was the first formal agreement between the US and Iran since the conflict began. Both sides have now escalated to explicitly targeting civilian infrastructure — water desalination plants, power stations, bridges, and oil facilities — directly threatening civilian populations across the region.

President Trump faces mounting political pressure to end a war he campaigned against, while Iran shows no signs of returning to negotiations. With the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed, global energy markets face severe disruption, and the humanitarian toll — particularly in Kuwait, which depends on desalination for 90% of its drinking water — continues to mount. The UN has called for de-escalation, but with the diplomatic track in ruins and both sides intensifying their military campaigns, a resolution appears increasingly distant.
