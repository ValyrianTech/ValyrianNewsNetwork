# Father and Son Arrested After Taking Forest Workers Hostage in California

A father and son have been arrested in California after allegedly kidnapping two U.S. Forest Service employees at gunpoint, zip-tying them, and holding them hostage inside a trailer for approximately 15 hours in the remote Shasta-Trinity National Forest. The hostages were released unharmed early Friday morning following a massive multi-agency law enforcement response that included the FBI's elite Hostage Rescue Team.

## The Incident

The ordeal began around 10:55 a.m. on Thursday, July 16, when a Forest Service law enforcement officer reported that two employees conducting routine seasonal fieldwork at Gumboot Lake Campground had been taken hostage. According to [Fox News](https://www.foxnews.com/us/us-forest-service-workers-kidnapped-ziptied-hours-father-son-duo-california-ag), the suspect — later identified as Joseph Charles Henrichsen, 49 — was armed with an AR-15 and knives, and claimed to have grenades.

Henrichsen and his son, Phoenix Henrichsen, 23, held the two workers inside a trailer near Mount Shasta, at the border of Siskiyou and Shasta counties. The elder Henrichsen demanded to speak with the FBI, authorities said.

Negotiations began around 4:00 p.m., and the situation drew one of the largest law enforcement responses in the region's recent history. The FBI's Hostage Rescue Team was flown in from Quantico, Virginia, on a Boeing 757, joining local sheriff's offices, SWAT teams, snipers, bomb units, drones, California Highway Patrol, CAL FIRE, and Homeland Security Investigations.

## The Resolution

The first hostage was released at approximately 1:50 a.m. on Friday, July 17, and the second followed 15 minutes later. The Henrichsens surrendered at 2:30 a.m. and were taken into custody without further incident.

"I'm grateful beyond words that both of our Forest Service employees taken hostage on the Shasta-Trinity National Forest are home safe," U.S. Forest Service Chief Tom Schultz said in a statement, as reported by the [Redding Record Searchlight](https://www.redding.com/story/news/local/2026/07/17/forest-service-employees-kidnapped-released-in-california/90956011007/). "This was a frightening experience for everyone involved. Their well-being continues to be our highest priority."

U.S. Attorney Eric Grant announced that both men face federal charges of kidnapping a federal employee under Title 18, U.S. Code, Section 1201(a)(5).

FBI Acting Special Agent in Charge Brian Tosh noted the rarity of such a positive outcome. "Crisis situations like this don't often result in everyone leaving the scene safely," he said.

## A Troubling Background

A critical detail has emerged about Joseph Henrichsen's history. In 2022, he was charged with a hate crime in Washington state for allegedly harassing his landlords. According to [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/jul/17/kidnapped-us-forest-service-employees-released), a judge ruled Henrichsen incompetent to stand trial, but the case was dismissed due to delays in admitting him to a state hospital. This means a man previously deemed unfit for trial by the justice system was free and able to carry out a 15-hour hostage situation on federal land.

Shasta County Sheriff Michael Johnson stated that Henrichsen targeted the workers "specifically because of their affiliation with the government" and had expressed intense complaints about the federal government for years, as reported by [The Dissenter](https://dissenter.com/border/flagged-as-incompetent-man-held-forest-service-workers-hostage-15-hour). The FBI has not disclosed the specific content of negotiations or the full motive.

## Systemic Questions

The case has raised significant concerns about the safety of federal employees working in remote areas and the gaps in the justice system for managing individuals deemed mentally unfit for trial. Siskiyou County Sheriff Jeremiah LaRue said his office had no prior interactions with Henrichsen, despite his documented history in Washington state.

"Federal land is increasingly lawless territory," The Dissenter noted in its analysis, questioning how many similar threats remain unaddressed.

## What's Next

Both Joseph and Phoenix Henrichsen remain in custody facing federal kidnapping charges. The investigation is ongoing, and authorities have not disclosed whether additional charges may be filed. The Forest Service said the two employees are resting and receiving support. As the case proceeds, questions about the competency ruling dismissal and the broader implications for federal worker safety are likely to persist.
