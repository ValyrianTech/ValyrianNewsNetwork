# Trump ICE Cases Collapse as Courts Reject Charges

The Trump administration has lost or abandoned nearly half of more than 400 resolved criminal cases against ICE protesters and immigrants, according to a major investigation published Saturday by [The New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/07/18/us/ice-assaults-protesters.html). The findings reveal an extraordinary failure rate in the federal justice system, where the Department of Justice typically secures convictions or guilty pleas in over 90% of cases.

The investigation found that the administration filed assault charges against more than 550 people under 18 U.S.C. 111 — a statute prohibiting assaulting, resisting, or impeding federal officers — far exceeding previously known figures. Of the more than 400 cases that have been resolved, nearly half have unraveled through acquittals, dismissals, or prosecutorial withdrawals.

## A Pattern of Collapse

The legal setbacks span multiple jurisdictions, including Los Angeles, Chicago, Minneapolis, Charlotte, and Boston. A [ProPublica and FRONTLINE investigation](https://www.propublica.org/article/caught-in-crackdown-ice-cbp-doj-trump-arrests-convictions) published in April documented more than 300 arrests, with over a third of cases collapsing under scrutiny — often because statements made by arresting officers were debunked by video footage.

"What's happening now is not comparable to anything that's happened in the past," Cuauhtémoc Ortega, the chief federal defender for the Central District of California, told ProPublica. "We've never had a situation where it seems like you arrest first and then try to justify the reasons for the arrests later."

Christy Lopez, a former Justice Department attorney who spent years investigating law enforcement misconduct, described the agents' behavior as "on par with the worst protest policing and just law enforcement that I've seen from any department, even in their worst days."

## The Bovino Campaign

At the center of the crackdown was Border Patrol Chief Gregory Bovino, who led immigration sweeps across multiple cities beginning in June 2025. Bovino encouraged aggressive arrest tactics, telling agents, "Arrest as many people that touch you as you want to," according to body camera footage reviewed by ProPublica.

Bovino's forces moved from Southern California to Illinois, then North Carolina, and finally to Minneapolis, where two activists — Renee Good and Alex Pretti — were shot and killed by immigration agents in January 2026. Bovino was demoted shortly after and later retired.

In Chicago, federal agents arrested more than a hundred American citizens during the fall of 2025. Yet as [The Guardian reported](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/feb/21/doj-protesters-federal-agents-cases), of 92 people arrested for assaulting or impeding officers, 74 cases resulted in no charges, 13 were filed and dismissed, and only five remained pending — with zero convictions as of late January.

## Prosecutorial Misconduct and Judicial Rebuke

Courts have documented extensive misconduct throughout the cases. In Boston, a judge held ICE agent Brian Sullivan in contempt of court after the defendant, Wilson Martell Lebron, was seized by ICE agents during his ongoing jury trial. The judge dismissed the case with prejudice, and defense attorney Murat Erkan called it "a plan to stop the defendant, to disappear him during his ongoing jury trial."

In Los Angeles, Trump-appointed U.S. Attorney Bill Essayli — who has never been Senate-confirmed — brought more than 100 cases. Yet federal public defenders have won every case that went to trial. Jimmy L. Arce, a former federal prosecutor in Chicago, told the Times there appears to be "a pattern of charges being filed without any merit."

## A Chilling Effect

Even unsuccessful prosecutions carry heavy costs. Defendants face legal fees, job loss, and emotional trauma. Their mugshots are blasted across government social media channels, and many spend weeks or months in jail before charges are dropped.

"If the goal of the Trump administration is to keep people out of the streets, then it doesn't matter if the people are getting convicted," Jared Fishman, a former DOJ Civil Rights prosecutor, told ProPublica. "I'm sure it's having a chilling effect."

Rebecca Ringstrom, a Minnesota activist who followed ICE vehicles as part of a monitoring group, was charged with interfering with a federal agent. Though her charge was a misdemeanor, she lost her job "likely due to the ongoing coverage" of her arrest.

## Broader Implications

The collapse of these cases raises serious constitutional questions about the criminalization of protest. Courts across the country have issued more than 10,000 rulings against Trump administration immigration policies, according to a Politico analysis. A [USA Today report](https://www.usatoday.com/story/news/politics/2026/06/18/leaders-condem-charges-against-15-minnesota-protesters-in-conspiracy-case/90611079007/) noted that 50 progressive groups condemned the federal indictment of 15 Minnesota protesters, accusing the administration of weaponizing the Justice Department.

Meanwhile, a report by Physicians for Human Rights and the University of California, Berkeley, documented 412 verified incidents of crowd-control weapon misuse against protesters, resulting in 203 injuries including blindings and traumatic brain injuries, as [The Guardian reported](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/jul/14/misuse-crowd-control-weapons-ice-protesters-blindings-traumatic-brain-injuries-report).

## What to Watch

The NYT investigation raises urgent questions about accountability. Will any federal agents or prosecutors face consequences for documented misconduct? How will the administration respond to the findings? And will Congress conduct oversight hearings into DOJ and DHS practices?

As legal experts note, the pattern of failed prosecutions may force the administration to recalibrate its enforcement strategy. But for the hundreds of Americans caught in the dragnet, the damage — financial, reputational, and emotional — has already been done.
