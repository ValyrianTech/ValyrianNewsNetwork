# CD&V Proposes Festival Ban for Staff Caught with Drugs

Belgian federal MP Franky Demon (CD&V) has proposed a sweeping measure targeting drug use at festivals: any staff member caught with drugs on festival grounds would face immediate removal and a temporary ban from working at any festival in the country. The proposal, reported by [Het Laatste Nieuws](https://www.hln.be/binnenland/cdenv-vraagt-festivalverbod-voor-personeel-dat-wordt-betrapt-met-drugs~afa84ecf/), comes as the 21st edition of Tomorrowland, one of the world's largest electronic music festivals, gets underway in Boom, Belgium.

## Context

Demon's proposal targets all festival personnel — from security guards to hospitality workers — arguing that those responsible for visitor safety must be held to a higher standard. "The bar must be set higher," Demon told HLN. "Whoever has drugs on them or uses them temporarily loses the right to work at a festival. That applies to everyone who performs a task on the grounds."

The MP cited a series of drug-related fatalities at Tomorrowland in recent years as justification for the measure. A Canadian woman died on the festival grounds from drug-related causes in 2025, while both a visitor and a staff member died in 2023. Earlier editions also saw fatal consequences from drug abuse.

## Key Developments

Under the proposed framework, staff caught with drugs would be immediately removed from the site and issued a temporary festival ban, effectively losing the right to work at any festival for the duration of the penalty. To enforce this, staff would need to demonstrate they are not subject to such a ban before starting work on festival grounds.

Demon also highlighted the role of organized crime in festival drug supply chains. "Sometimes criminal networks exploit festival staff to handle the logistics of their supply," he stated, suggesting that the measure would disrupt these networks by removing potential facilitators from festival environments.

The proposal is the latest in a series of punitive drug policy initiatives from Demon and CD&V. In March 2026, Demon proposed introducing GAS fines (municipal administrative sanctions) of €50 to €500 for drug possession — a plan that drew criticism from then-Justice Minister Paul Van Tigchelt and the public prosecutor's office. In November 2025, Demon and party leader Sammy Mahdi proposed a "nuisance law" allowing mayors to forcibly commit drug addicts to care facilities for up to one month.

The broader context includes a string of drug-related incidents at Belgian festivals. Just weeks before this proposal, 128 festivalgoers were caught with drugs at Rock Werchter 2026, underscoring the scale of the challenge facing organizers and law enforcement.

## Analysis

The proposal raises several questions about enforcement and proportionality. Critics are likely to ask how a centralized ban would be enforced across Belgium's numerous festivals, and whether a temporary ban for possession of small amounts of drugs — such as cannabis — constitutes a proportionate response that could cost workers their livelihoods.

Privacy concerns also loom: a verification system would require some form of registry or database to track banned individuals, raising questions about data protection and employment rights. Labor unions representing festival workers have not yet publicly responded to the proposal.

Demon's consistent push for stricter drug enforcement measures suggests a strategic positioning on law-and-order issues within the coalition government. CD&V, which holds the Interior Ministry through Minister Annelies Verlinden, appears to be using drug policy as a distinguishing issue ahead of future electoral contests.

## What's Next

The proposal would require legislative action to become law. The response from other political parties — including Groen, Vooruit, N-VA, and coalition partners — will be crucial in determining its fate. Festival organizers such as Tomorrowland, which faces significant operational implications, have yet to comment publicly.

As Belgium's festival season reaches its peak, the debate over drug policy, personal responsibility, and public safety is likely to intensify. The question remains whether punitive measures alone can address the complex social and public health challenges posed by drug use at large-scale events.
