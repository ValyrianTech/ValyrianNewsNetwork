# Belgium Halves Asylum Shelter Places Amid Fears of 'Dramas on the Streets'

Belgium's federal government has cut emergency asylum shelter capacity in Brussels by half, reducing the number of temporary places from 2,000 to 1,000 in a move that humanitarian organizations warn will leave vulnerable people sleeping on the streets. Critics describe the policy as a deliberate "rotting strategy" designed to force asylum seekers out of the country.

Minister of Asylum and Migration Anneleen Van Bossuyt (N-VA) announced the second phase of cuts on July 16, 2026, scrapping an additional 700 shelter places after eliminating 300 in late June. The shelters were part of the so-called "Brussels Deal" — a temporary agreement signed in late 2022 that provided €42 million annually from the federal government to the Brussels-Capital Region for 2,000 emergency accommodation spots when the regular Fedasil reception network became overwhelmed.

## Context: The Brussels Deal and Its Demise

The Brussels Deal was born out of crisis. In late 2022, Belgium's regular asylum reception system was buckling under pressure, with thousands of single men on waiting lists. The federal government struck an emergency arrangement with the Brussels Region: €42 million per year in exchange for 2,000 temporary shelter places, often in hotels and other makeshift facilities.

Nearly four years later, Minister Van Bossuyt argues the arrangement has outlived its purpose. According to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/07/16/minister-van-bossuyt-asiel-en-migratie-schrapt-opvangplaatsen-br/), the "throughput list" — a registry of asylum seekers waiting to move from emergency to regular reception — now stands at approximately 1,000 single men, half of whom find shelter with family or friends.

"Tax money can only be spent once," Van Bossuyt told VRT NWS. "If there are still about 1,000 people on the throughput list, I'm not going to keep paying for 2,000 places." She emphasized that the federal government's responsibility extends only to those legally entitled to asylum reception: "It is not our task to co-pay for Brussels homeless shelters."

The minister plans to fully phase out the Brussels Deal by the end of 2026, integrating remaining individuals into the regular Fedasil network. She also noted that the European Commission has criticized Belgium for the arrangement.

## 'Tents Will Sprout Like Mushrooms'

The cuts have triggered a firestorm of criticism from Brussels regional politicians, humanitarian organizations, and opposition parties. Speaking to [BRUZZ](https://www.bruzz.be/actua/samenleving/van-bossuyt-schrapt-opvangplekken-voor-asielzoekers-tentjes-zullen-massaal-terugkomen-2026-07-16), Mehdi Kassou, director of the citizen platform BelRefugees, warned of dire consequences: "Tents and sleeping bags will sprout like mushrooms from the ground. Buildings will be squatted. In eleven years, I have never seen this."

Brussels Mayor Philippe Close (PS) accused the minister of acting without consultation. "This was decided without consultation with the affected municipalities," Close said. "Does Ms. Van Bossuyt want new refugee camps in the parks? This will only lead to a worsening of the situation."

Ahmed Laaouej, Brussels Minister of Social Affairs (PS), described the decision as "completely irresponsible and inhumane," noting it would put hundreds of people, including children, on the street in the middle of a heatwave. He called it "a direct attack on Brussels."

## Accusations of Data Manipulation

Dirk De Smedt, Brussels Minister of Finance (Anders), went further, accusing Van Bossuyt of manipulating figures to justify the cuts. As reported by [BRUZZ](https://www.bruzz.be/actua/samenleving/van-bossuyt-schrapt-nog-verder-brussels-deal-2026-07-16), De Smedt claimed the minister has been "regularly adjusting the conditions for registration on the waiting list, only to then claim that the list is getting smaller." He warned that the decision "will lead to a social drama."

Benjamin Dalle, VGC Council Chair (CD&V), argued that the problem runs deeper than the Brussels Deal itself. "Tens of thousands of people without legal residence are staying in Brussels because federal migration policy is failing," Dalle said. "They don't disappear just because they disappear from a waiting list; they end up in informal labor, exploitation, or crime, and the bill disproportionately lands with Brussels." He called for a new Brussels Deal.

## A Pattern of Legal Setbacks

The shelter cuts are the latest flashpoint in a broader conflict over Belgium's asylum policy under Van Bossuyt. The minister has pursued a restrictive agenda that includes limiting material aid to asylum seekers with protection status in other EU member states and tightening family reunification rules — both of which have faced legal reversals.

On March 27, 2026, the Belgian Council of State suspended Van Bossuyt's instruction to Fedasil to limit aid to so-called "M-status holders," ruling it unlawful. The Council of State again suspended a related decision on July 8, 2026, as [VRT NWS reported](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/07/08/raad-van-state-schorst-beperking-materiele-hulp-aan-asielzoekers/). The legality of her policies is now expected to be tested before the European Court of Justice.

## Analysis: A Political Fault Line

The conflict over asylum shelter exposes a deep political fault line in Belgium. The federal government, which includes the Flemish nationalist N-VA, is pursuing a restrictive migration agenda. The Brussels-Capital Region, governed by a different coalition led by Francophone socialists, argues it bears a disproportionate burden.

The term "verrottingsstrategie" (rotting strategy) — used by the newspaper De Morgen and echoed by critics — captures the accusation at the heart of the dispute: that by cutting shelter places without providing alternatives, the government is deliberately allowing conditions to deteriorate so badly that people leave voluntarily.

## What's Next

Van Bossuyt aims to complete the phase-out of the Brussels Deal by the end of 2026. Meanwhile, the federal government has reportedly submitted a proposal for a new cooperation agreement to the cabinet of Brussels Minister Laaouej, though no response has been received. With legal challenges mounting and political tensions escalating, the fate of hundreds of asylum seekers — and the broader question of how Belgium manages migration — remains uncertain.

For now, aid organizations are bracing for what they fear will be a humanitarian crisis on the streets of Brussels.
