# Chen Gang, Co-Composer of Iconic 'Butterfly Lovers' Violin Concerto, Dies at 91

Chen Gang, the renowned Chinese composer who co-created the world-famous "Butterfly Lovers" Violin Concerto, passed away in the early hours of July 18, 2026, in Shanghai at the age of 91, according to [The Paper](https://www.thepaper.cn/newsDetail_forward_33611282). A professor at the Shanghai Conservatory of Music, Chen leaves behind a legacy that shaped Chinese classical music for nearly seven decades.

## A Musical Prodigy Forged in Turbulent Times

Born in Shanghai in 1935 into a musical family — his father was the celebrated composer Chen Gexin, known for classics like "The Night of Shanghai" — Chen Gang joined the People's Liberation Army at age 14 in 1949. He wrote his first composition, "We Are the Iron Army Defending Peace," at just 15. In 1955, he entered the Shanghai Conservatory of Music, studying under Dean Ding Shande and Soviet expert Alzamazov.

## The Birth of a Masterpiece

In 1958, the Shanghai Conservatory formed the "Chinese Violin National School Experimental Group" with the slogan "Let the Violin Speak Chinese." He Zhanhao, the group leader and a Yue Opera expert, recruited Chen Gang — then a senior student — to co-create a symphonic work based on the tragic folk tale of Liang Shanbo and Zhu Yingtai, often called the "Chinese Romeo and Juliet."

The "Butterfly Lovers" Violin Concerto premiered on May 27, 1959, at the Lixin Theatre in Shanghai, performed by violinist Yu Lina. As [Guancha.cn](https://www.guancha.cn/politics/2026_07_18_824190.shtml) reported, the piece fused Yue Opera melodies with Western sonata form, creating a landmark in Chinese orchestral music. It remains the most frequently performed Chinese orchestral work on international stages.

Yu Lina recalled the intensive creative process in an interview: "During the creation phase, they would write a section, and a few of us classmates would hand-copy it and sight-read it on the spot — the entire process was done while writing and polishing simultaneously."

## A Career of Remarkable Range

Chen Gang's creativity extended far beyond his most famous work. In the 1970s, he composed the "Red Violin" series, including "The Golden Furnace," "Morning of the Miao Mountains," and "Sunshine Shines on Tashkurgan." The 1980s brought his violin concerto "Wang Zhaojun," considered a companion piece to "Butterfly Lovers."

Even in his later years, Chen remained remarkably productive. In his 80s, he composed the symphonic poem "Love Tragedy — The Song of Yang Guifei" and ten "Chinese Sound Paintings" for violin and string quartet. In October 2025, at age 90, he released "Symphonic Overture — Blossoms," demonstrating undiminished creative vitality.

Chen was also a prolific writer, publishing essay collections including "Black Romance," "Listening to Music with Three Ears," and "Butterflies Are Free." As [CCTV](https://news.cnr.cn/rebang/20260718/t20260718_527716523.shtml) reported, his 2022 collection "Years of Fragrance — Chen Gang Capriccio" was his fifth published work of essays.

## A Philosophy of Emotion

"From the 'Butterfly Lovers' that took flight and resonated worldwide, to the 'Symphonic Overture — Blossoms' written at age 90 — the character 'emotion' runs through Chen Gang's entire creative vein," Guancha.cn noted in its tribute. "Creation cannot be separated from emotion; without emotion there is no music, without emotion there is no writing."

Chen himself once reflected on the unique circumstances that produced his magnum opus: "'Butterfly Lovers' is a 'youthful' work from our student days in the 1950s. It has indeed become a classic — it was the product of that special era and special creative environment. After finishing it, I never changed a single note."

## Legacy and Forward Look

Chen Gang's passing marks the end of an era for Chinese classical music. The "Butterfly Lovers" Violin Concerto remains a cultural touchstone — a work that bridged Eastern and Western musical traditions and introduced Chinese orchestral music to global audiences. His career spanned from the revolutionary era through the Cultural Revolution to China's modern cultural renaissance, and his continued creativity into his 90s demonstrated remarkable artistic vitality.

He Zhanhao, Chen's longtime collaborator who is now 93, survives him. Questions remain about Chen's unpublished manuscripts and whether the "Butterfly Lovers" concerto will see renewed international attention following his death. For now, the music endures — a testament to a composer who, as he once said, pursued four dreams in his life: literature, revolution, music, and Shanghai-style culture.
