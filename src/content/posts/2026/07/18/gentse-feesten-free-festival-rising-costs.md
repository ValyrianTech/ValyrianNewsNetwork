# Can Gentse Feesten Stay Free? Rising Costs Threaten Event

GHENT, Belgium — For ten days each July, the medieval heart of Ghent transforms into a sprawling, open-air celebration of music, theater, and street performance. The Gentse Feesten — one of Europe's largest free street festivals — attracts nearly 1.6 million visitors annually. But behind the revelry, a pressing question is growing louder: how much longer can the party stay free?

Organizers say the festival's unique financial model is under increasing strain. "We are completely dependent on the goodwill of the partygoers," one organizer told [De Morgen](https://www.demorgen.be/nieuws/we-zijn-volledig-afhankelijk-van-de-goodwill-van-de-feestvierders-hoelang-kunnen-de-gentse-feesten-nog-gratis-blijven~b81fe213/), capturing the delicate balance upon which the event depends.

## A Model Built on Beer Sales

Unlike most major festivals, the Gentse Feesten has no central box office. Instead, the city of Ghent provides subsidies and infrastructure, while individual "square organizers" (pleinorganisatoren) take responsibility for programming entertainment on their respective squares. These organizers pay for artists and acts primarily through revenue from drink sales at their bars.

As [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2025/06/17/gentse-feesten-nachtwinkels-geen-bijdrage-oneerlijke-concurrenti/) explained, "The festival is largely free, the square organizers are responsible for programming and pay the artists and acts with income from consumption. That model is under pressure, especially because the city has also reduced subsidies in recent years and costs have risen."

The math is stark: according to reporting from De Standaard, organizers need each visitor to buy at least two beers at the official bars just to break even.

## The Spending Gap

A major [visitor survey](https://www.hln.be/gent/groot-onderzoek-naar-gentse-feesten-93-komt-voor-de-muziek-23-spendeert-minder-dan-20-euro~af634aa6/) conducted in 2025 by Arteveldehogeschool, polling over 4,000 attendees, revealed a troubling trend for organizers: 23% of visitors spend less than €20 per visit, while 55% spend between €20 and €50. Only 22% spend more than €50.

Many visitors bring their own drinks purchased at supermarkets and night shops rather than buying from official festival bars — a practice that directly undermines the revenue stream that funds the entertainment. Despite organizers' appeals, 68% of visitors do consume at least one drink at an official bar, but the shift in spending habits is putting pressure on the system.

## Creative Solutions and Controversies

The 2026 edition, which opened on July 17 and runs through July 26, introduces several new measures aimed at keeping the festival financially viable:

- **A paid VIP rooftop terrace** on the Vlasmarkt, a first for the traditionally free festival
- **A uniform reusable cup system** across 9 squares, with future plans for chip-enabled cups to prevent theft
- **Cost savings** from shifting the opening ceremony organization to square organizers in 2025, saving the city tens of thousands of euros

These initiatives represent a careful balancing act. As [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/07/15/gentse-feesten-toegankelijkheid-2026/) reported, Alderman for Pleasure Joris Vandenbroucke (Voor Gent) has stated: "The Gentse Feesten are not only the largest folk festival in Europe, we also want to be the most accessible folk festival in Europe."

## What's at Stake

The festival, which dates back to 1843 and was recognized as Flemish intangible cultural heritage in 2021, faces several possible futures if the free model becomes unsustainable:

1. **Introduction of entry fees or wristband charges** — a fundamental shift in character
2. **Reduction in programming quality or quantity** — fewer stages, fewer acts
3. **Increased commercialization** — more paid attractions within the festival zone
4. **Reduced accessibility** — pricing out lower-income visitors who make up a significant portion of attendees

According to [Wikipedia](https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gentse_Feesten), the festival's roots lie in a 19th-century "general fair" designed to keep workers from taking unauthorized days off. Its modern incarnation was revitalized in the 1970s by counterculture figures like Walter De Buck, who transformed it into the inclusive, artistic celebration it is today.

## The Bigger Picture

The Gentse Feesten is not alone in facing these pressures. Across Europe, free public festivals are grappling with rising costs for artists, security, infrastructure, and insurance. But the Gentse Feesten's decentralized model — where dozens of independent square organizers bear the financial risk — makes it uniquely vulnerable.

Vandenbroucke has firmly ruled out introducing entry fees for now. "The Gentse Feesten will remain free, without discussion," he said following the 2025 survey results. But as costs continue to rise and city subsidies shrink, the question is whether goodwill alone will be enough to sustain Europe's biggest free street festival for another 183 editions.

For now, the answer lies in the hands of the 1.6 million visitors who flood Ghent each July — and whether they choose to buy that second beer at the official bar.
