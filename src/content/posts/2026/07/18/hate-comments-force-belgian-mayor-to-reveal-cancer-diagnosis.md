# Hate Comments Force Belgian Mayor to Reveal Cancer Diagnosis

Marc Wynants, the mayor of Linter, a municipality in the Belgian province of Flemish Brabant, has been forced to publicly disclose his prostate cancer diagnosis after a sustained campaign of online harassment. A small group of social media users accused him of lying about being ill to gain sympathy, leaving him with no choice but to share deeply personal medical information to silence the speculation.

## Context

According to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/07/17/marc-wynants-ziek-kanker-linter/), Wynants discovered his condition through a routine annual blood test in late June 2026. "The good news is that I experience almost no side effects from the treatment. The bad news is that the cancer is there," he told the Flemish public broadcaster.

The mayor had not intended to make his diagnosis public. However, a small group of individuals who had been targeting him online for years began posting hateful comments beneath his social media posts, claiming he could not possibly attend public events while being seriously ill. "They wrote that I was lying about being sick to gain sympathy," Wynants said. "Those reactions hit me very deeply."

## A Pattern of Harassment

This is not the first time Wynants has faced severe online abuse. In July 2025, he filed a police complaint after a Facebook user called for someone to "put a bullet through [the mayor's] head," as reported by [HLN](https://www.hln.be/linter/burgemeester-van-linter-dient-klacht-in-nadat-inwoner-via-facebook-oproept-om-hem-een-kogel-door-de-kop-te-jagen~a18c606b/). The death threats were linked to a dispute over a vacant property tax imposed more than a year earlier. Wynants reduced his public appearances for safety reasons following that incident.

The harassment has taken a toll on his family as well. His wife, Dominique Vandersmissen, spoke publicly about the impact of the abuse in an interview with [HLN](https://www.hln.be/linter/dominique-vandersmissen-55-echtgenote-van-de-burgemeester-van-linter-die-doodsbedreigingen-hakken-er-serieus-in~a2bfe12d/), describing Facebook as "a trash can where people vent their negative bile with impunity." She revealed that the death threats "hit hard" and that she worries constantly about her husband's wellbeing.

## Public Support and Legal Action

Despite the harassment, Wynants has received an outpouring of support. Approximately 2,000 people expressed solidarity after his disclosure. "That does something to a person," he said.

The mayor is now considering filing a formal complaint against those behind the hateful messages, on the advice of the public prosecutor. "There's no end to those messages," he explained. "I've now blocked the accounts, but of course they always create new ones."

## Broader Implications

The case highlights a growing crisis across Europe: the escalating online harassment of local politicians. Local officials are particularly vulnerable due to their accessibility, often sharing personal contact information and appearing regularly at community events. This accessibility, while essential for democratic engagement, also makes them easy targets for coordinated harassment campaigns.

In Belgium, the mayor of Oupeye was forced into hiding in 2023 after receiving threats following a fatal police shooting, as [NOS](https://nos.nl/artikel/2487560-belgische-burgemeester-duikt-onder-na-bedreigingen) reported. In the Netherlands, research by Pointer found that 440 mayors and aldermen were threatened, insulted, or intimidated in 2024-2025, as documented by [COPS in Cyberspace](https://copsincyberspace.wordpress.com/2026/01/19/burgemeesters-en-wethouders-ook-in-2025-weer-bedreigd-en-geintimideerd/). A 2024 Dutch study found that 77% of mayors reported experiencing aggression or violence, up from 50% in 2020, indicating a troubling upward trend.

The Wynants case also underscores the inadequacy of current platform moderation. The mayor noted that blocking accounts is ineffective because harassers simply create new ones, pointing to the need for more robust identity verification and content moderation systems on social media platforms. Under Belgian law, online harassment, defamation, and death threats are criminal offenses, but enforcement remains challenging, particularly with anonymous or rapidly recreated accounts.

## What's Next

Wynants continues to exercise regularly — cycling and walking — and doctors say his good fitness is beneficial to his treatment. He remains committed to his role as mayor, but the experience has left him reflective about the state of public discourse. As he noted in 2025: "Someone who wants to enter politics these days must be half crazy. People are hardly ashamed of anything anymore and misuse social media to express their displeasure, pillory someone, and incite hatred."

The case raises urgent questions about the adequacy of platform moderation, the legal protections available to public figures, and the broader health of local democracy in an age of online toxicity.
