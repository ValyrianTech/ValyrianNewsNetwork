# Belgium Cracks Down on Football Hooligans with Tough New Law

The Belgian federal government has approved a major reform of the country's Football Law (Voetbalwet) in first reading, introducing substantially higher fines, longer stadium bans, a centralized national database of offenders, and expanded police powers. The reforms, championed by Minister of Security and Home Affairs Bernard Quintin, represent the most significant overhaul of the legislation since its enactment in 1998.

## Background: A Law in Need of Reform

The original Football Law, dating from December 21, 1998, established the legal framework for safety at Belgian football matches. However, persistent violence and disruptive behavior — particularly during the 2024-2025 season — exposed the limits of the existing framework. According to official statistics from the [Federal Public Service Home Affairs](https://www.besafe.be/nl/besafenieuws/sanctiecijfers-voetbalwet-seizoen-2024-2025), the Football Safety Service processed 1,219 police reports that season, resulting in 14,161 months of stadium bans and €759,250 in fines. Currently, 1,357 individuals have active stadium bans across Belgium.

## The Trigger: Standard Liège vs. Charleroi

The immediate catalyst for accelerating the reform was the May 23, 2026 match between Standard Liège and Charleroi. As [Het Laatste Nieuws](https://www.hln.be/binnenland/nieuwe-voetbalwet-pakt-hooligans-harder-aan-stadionverbod-betekent-straks-ook-echt-geen-toegang-meer~abc55ba2/) reported, the match descended into chaos when masked hooligans stormed the pitch after Charleroi goalkeeper Koné provocatively waved a club flag. Rival supporters attacked each other with torn-down fences and fire extinguishers, fireworks were fired at visiting team buses, and four police officers were injured. Six people were arrested.

## Key Measures in the New Law

### Doubled Fines and Tougher Penalties
The minimum administrative fine for general offenses has been doubled from €250 to €500. Penalties for racism and xenophobia have been significantly increased: the minimum fine rises to €2,000, and the minimum stadium ban increases from 30 months to three years. Physical violence now carries a minimum fine of €2,500, up from €2,000, while illegal use of pyrotechnic materials incurs a minimum fine of €500.

### A National Database of Stadium Bans
Perhaps the most consequential change is the creation of a centralized national database of all stadium bans — administrative, judicial, and civil — managed by the Federal Public Service Home Affairs. As Minister Quintin explained in an [official government statement](https://www.besafe.be/nl/besafenieuws/hervorming-van-de-voetbalwet-voor-veiligere-stadions): "Rioters who ruin the image of our football do not belong in a stadium." Football clubs are now legally required to consult this database before selling tickets, ensuring that banned individuals cannot purchase entry.

### Expanded Police Powers and Perimeter
Police can now impose immediate stadium bans on the spot for aggressive, threatening, or provocative behavior. The law's scope has been extended to a five-kilometer perimeter around stadiums, where offenses can also lead to bans. Courts can require individuals with stadium bans to report to a police station during match times. Camera surveillance has been expanded to parking areas and access roads, with live feeds enabling real-time identification.

### Future Biometric Integration
Looking ahead, the government is exploring the integration of biometric data with ticketing systems from 2028. This would allow automated identity verification at stadium entrances, making it virtually impossible for banned individuals to enter using another person's ticket.

## Industry and Political Support

The reform has broad support from football stakeholders. Lorin Parys, CEO of the Pro League, stated: "Safety in our stadiums is a shared responsibility, but also a common ambition. Together with the minister and the KBVB, we want to make every match a football celebration, where passion never goes hand in hand with violence." Peter Willems, CEO of the Royal Belgian Football Association (KBVB), echoed this sentiment, calling safety "an absolute priority."

## What Comes Next
The law was approved in first reading on July 18, 2026, and must now proceed through further legislative steps, including a second reading and royal assent. The reform is part of the broader government agreement under Prime Minister Bart De Wever's administration, developed through intensive consultation with the Pro League and KBVB.

## Looking Ahead
While the measures have been welcomed by football authorities, questions remain about enforcement capacity, privacy implications of the national database and biometric data collection, and whether higher fines alone will deter determined offenders. The coming months will reveal how effectively Belgium can translate these tough new laws into safer stadiums for all supporters.
