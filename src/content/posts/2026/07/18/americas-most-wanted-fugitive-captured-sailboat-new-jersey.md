# America's Most Wanted Fugitive Captured on Sailboat After 20 Years

Ronald L. Fischer, a 70-year-old former anesthesiologist who had been one of Rhode Island's longest-wanted fugitives, was captured on July 16 aboard a 56-foot sailboat off the coast of New Jersey, ending a manhunt that spanned more than two decades. Fischer had been on the run since fleeing his 2005 trial for first-degree sexual assault in Newport, Rhode Island, where he was subsequently convicted in absentia.

## The Capture

Deputy U.S. Marshals and U.S. Coast Guard personnel intercepted Fischer's vessel, "The Silver Lining," approximately one hour off the New Jersey coast using a 45-foot Coast Guard Response Boat. According to [Fox News](https://www.foxnews.com/us/americas-most-wanted-fugitive-captured-sailboat-nj-coast-20-years-run), Fischer was taken into custody without incident.

The breakthrough came after the FBI received a tip from an "online sleuth," which led investigators to the sailboat registered under Fischer's alias, Richard Graydon. FBI Boston Special Agent in Charge Ted E. Docks credited the tip with enabling a multi-state law enforcement operation that resulted in the capture.

## A Decades-Long Manhunt

Fischer, a former chairman of the anesthesia department at Memorial Hospital in Pawtucket and a former professor at Brown University Medical School, was described by authorities as a "master yachtsman" and "world traveler." His flight from justice began in 2005 when he disappeared during his trial in Newport Superior Court, leaving an email titled "Goodbye" for his attorney stating he planned to leave the United States.

He was featured multiple times on the television show "America's Most Wanted" and remained on Rhode Island's Most Wanted list for over 20 years. The U.S. Marshals Service characterized the investigation as a "worldwide search."

## The Crime

Fischer's conviction stemmed from a 2003 assault on his 60-foot yacht, the "Lion King," docked at Hinckley's Marina in Portsmouth, Rhode Island. The victim, Cheryl Gingerich, a 46-year-old mother of four and an English professor, had met Fischer online for what she believed was a first date.

This was not Fischer's first offense. In 1996, he pleaded guilty to two felony counts of assault and battery for forcing a date to have sex on his boat in Quincy, Massachusetts, resulting in the loss of his medical licenses in both Massachusetts and Rhode Island.

## The Victim's Response

In an exclusive interview with NBC 10/WJAR following the arrest, Gingerich expressed relief and a sense of justice finally served. "I was happy to think when I woke up. I thought I slept last night in my bed and Ronald Fischer slept last night in a jail," she said. "That made me — I'm sorry to say it — but truthfully, it made me happy."

Gingerich, who developed PTSD and agoraphobia as a result of the assault and the decades-long fear that Fischer remained at large, said she "always lived in fear of seeing him again because I never felt that he was far away."

## Law Enforcement Coordination

U.S. Marshal Wing Chau for the District of Rhode Island emphasized the persistence of investigators. "This arrest demonstrates that time does not erase accountability," Chau said. "For more than twenty years, Ronald Fischer believed he had successfully escaped justice. The men and women of the Rhode Island Violent Fugitive Task Force, together with our partners, remained committed to ensuring that day would eventually come."

Rhode Island State Police credited generations of troopers who pursued the case over two decades, stating they "never stopped seeking justice for his victim."

## Sophisticated Evasion

Investigators found that Fischer had used more than a dozen aliases, including "Richard Graydon" and "Robert Graydon." He employed an "onion router" to mask his internet activity, and authorities discovered books on evading law enforcement at his residence. Emails also indicated that family members had been assisting him during his years as a fugitive.

## What's Next

Fischer was arraigned in Manhattan on July 17, where he waived extradition. He is currently being held in federal custody pending transfer to Rhode Island for sentencing on his 2005 first-degree sexual assault conviction. Questions remain about whether he will face additional charges related to his flight and whether family members who allegedly helped him will be prosecuted.

First Assistant U.S. Attorney Charles C. Calenda summed up the message of the case: "You can run, but you cannot hide from justice."
