# Hunter Biden on Public Shame: 'It Was a Gift'

Hunter Biden has spent years at the center of political firestorms — congressional investigations, federal convictions, and relentless public scrutiny. But in a candid new interview with [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/07/17/nx-s1-5866605/hunter-biden-addiction-publicly-shamed), the former president's son describes that ordeal not as a curse, but as an unexpected gift.

"I was given the gift of being so publicly shamed and humiliated, and literally stripped bare, in some cases literally stripped naked in the public square," Biden told NPR's Scott Simon on the Newsmakers video podcast. "I had nowhere to hide, nowhere left to hide. I had no more secrets that I could keep."

## A Long Road to Recovery

Biden's journey to that perspective was anything but easy. He traces the beginning of his serious struggle with addiction to the death of his brother Beau in 2015, though his drinking had become problematic as early as 2003. After seven years of sobriety, he relapsed in 2010 — a moment he recalls with painful clarity.

"The drink cart rolled by. The flight attendant asked if I'd like a bloody mary. I said, hmm. Sure," Biden recounted. "And here's where addiction is insidious. You're ashamed that you somehow failed because you've just relapsed."

His addiction worsened through 2019, when he began smoking crack cocaine. But today, Biden says he has been substance-free for over seven years. "No, it isn't [a struggle]," he said when asked about staying sober. "It's beautiful. Just days are beautiful, beautiful things."

## The Pardon That Changed Everything

In December 2024, President Joe Biden issued a full and unconditional pardon for his son — a dramatic reversal after publicly stating "I will not pardon him." Hunter Biden now reveals the moment that changed his father's mind.

"My dad woke up after Thanksgiving and he read that Donald Trump had just appointed Matt Gaetz to be the Attorney General," Hunter said. "I think that he made a decision then that Donald Trump had made a decision not to actually adhere to the Constitution."

Crucially, Hunter insists his father would not have pardoned him had he won the 2024 election. "If my dad had won, he would have kept his word," he said. The pardon came after Trump's victory, and Hunter rejects comparisons to Trump's pardons of January 6 defendants, calling the argument "insane."

## The Burisma Question

Biden also addressed one of the most persistent controversies of his public life: his position on the board of Ukrainian energy company Burisma. He acknowledged that his role was "100%" due to his father being Vice President at the time.

"I accept the criticism of the idea that I should not have taken that job because of the fact that my dad was at the time in the last year of his vice presidency," Biden said. However, he pushed back on suggestions he was unqualified, noting his chairmanship of the World Food Program USA and vice chairmanship of Amtrak's board.

## An Unconventional Media Strategy

Since reactivating his X account in May 2026, Biden has posted over 1,343 times — averaging 23 posts per day. But more striking than the volume is the company he keeps. He has sat down with conservative commentator Candace Owens, who [apologized](https://www.newsweek.com/hunter-biden-interview-candace-owens-key-moments-11986428) for years of mocking his addiction, and with far-right influencer Nick Fuentes in a Philadelphia motel room — a conversation that nearly erupted into a physical altercation.

"I wanted to go into the lions' den with the people that have pursued me the most vociferously over the past seven years," Biden explained. "We may see the humanity in each other."

His outreach to Owens yielded an emotional exchange. "I feel… terrible," Owens told him, apologizing for her past attacks. Biden responded, "It means the world." The two bonded over faith and even discussed traveling to the Vatican together.

## A Message for Millions

Throughout the interview, Biden positioned himself as an advocate for the estimated 50 million Americans dealing with addiction. "Addiction is a family disease. It is a communal disease, and you are not alone," he said.

Reflecting on his father's age — a central issue in the 2024 campaign — Biden acknowledged that Joe Biden "slowed down" during his presidency but insisted the changes were "purely associated and typical with growing older" and did not indicate cognitive decline.

## What's Next

As Biden continues his unconventional media blitz — launching a "Where's Hunter" Substack, appearing at live events, and even joking about starting an OnlyFans account — the question remains whether his strategy represents genuine bridge-building or calculated image rehabilitation. The upcoming release of a documentary financed by his close friend Kevin Morris, and the yet-to-air Channel 5 interview with Nick Fuentes, may offer further clues.

For now, Biden seems at peace with a life lived in the spotlight — whether by choice or by circumstance. "I am more grateful for my life and all of it right now than I've ever been in my entire life," he said.
