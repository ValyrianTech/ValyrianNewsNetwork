# Trump $1 Coin Faces Legal Challenges, Experts Say

The U.S. Treasury has begun minting a new $1 gold-colored commemorative coin featuring President Donald Trump's likeness, sparking immediate legal controversy as constitutional law experts argue it violates federal statutes that prohibit living individuals from appearing on American currency. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent [unveiled the design](https://www.npr.org/2026/07/17/nx-s1-5897380/trump-coin-face-laws-treasury-250) on July 15, confirming that the U.S. Mint in Philadelphia had already begun pressing the coins as part of America's 250th anniversary celebrations.

## The Legal Dispute

At the heart of the controversy are three federal statutes that legal experts say the coin may violate. The Thayer Amendment of 1866, codified at [31 U.S. Code § 5114](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title31-section5114&num=0&edition=prelim), states plainly that "only the portrait of a deceased individual may appear on United States currency and securities." The Presidential $1 Coin Act of 2005 further requires that presidents be deceased for at least two years before appearing on dollar coins.

Jeremy Paul, a professor and former dean at Northeastern University School of Law, told NPR that "the law prohibits, currently, on currency, the likeness of any person — not just the president — who is alive." Paul described the administration's legal reasoning as "inconsistent with the principles of our country."

The Treasury Department defends the coin under the [Circulating Collectible Coin Redesign Act of 2020](https://www.congress.gov/bill/116th-congress/house-bill/1923), which authorized the Treasury secretary to issue $1 coins "with designs emblematic of the United States Semiquincentennial" during 2026. Bessent has argued that the Thayer Amendment applies only to paper currency, not coins, pointing to the 1926 Calvin Coolidge Sesquicentennial half dollar as precedent.

## The Coolidge Precedent

The 1926 half dollar remains the only previous instance of a sitting president appearing on a U.S. coin during his lifetime. However, legal experts note crucial differences. As [CoinWeek](https://coinweek.com/trump-1-semiquincentennial-coin-facts-law-and-the-coolidge-precedent/) reports, the law has been significantly strengthened since then. The Presidential $1 Coin Act of 2005 explicitly prohibits living presidents from appearing on dollar coins, and the 2020 Redesign Act restricts portraits of living persons on the reverse of 2026 issues.

Moreover, the Coolidge coin was a commercial failure: over 859,000 of the 1 million pieces produced were ultimately returned to the Mint and melted, according to numismatic records.

## Lawsuit Dismissed, But More Expected

A retired Portland attorney named James Rickher filed a pro se federal lawsuit in March 2026 seeking to block the coin, arguing it would directly harm him as a novice coin collector. A federal judge [dismissed the case](https://www.documentcloud.org/documents/28318081-immergut-rickher-ruling/) in June on standing grounds — meaning the court did not rule on the underlying legality of the coin. Legal experts say future lawsuits face the same procedural hurdle: proving concrete injury.

Richard Painter, former chief White House ethics lawyer for President George W. Bush, told NPR that "one of the reasons for Congress wanting to prohibit this is that we don't want presidents acting like kings." Painter suggested a hypothetical scenario where a vendor or customer refuses to accept the coin as legal tender, which could establish standing for a future challenge.

## Legislative Response

Democratic Senators Jeff Merkley and Catherine Cortez Masto introduced the [Change Corruption Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/3408/text) in December 2025, which would explicitly prohibit the likeness of a living or sitting president from appearing on any U.S. currency. The bill remains pending in Congress.

## Broader Pattern of Trump Imagery

The coin is part of a broader pattern of placing Trump's image on government items. According to NPR, Trump's likeness has already appeared on U.S. passports, national park passes, and government building banners during his second term. The Treasury has also prepared designs for a $250 bill featuring Trump's face, though congressional approval for the new denomination does not appear imminent.

Rep. Joyce Beatty (D-Ohio) criticized the administration's priorities, writing on social media: "They can put Trump on a $1 coin, but can't keep a decades-old promise to put Harriet Tubman on the $20 bill??? This Treasury is funding presidential vanity projects."

## What's Next

The coin, which has a gold-like finish but contains no precious metals, is expected to be available for purchase in fall 2026. Its legal status remains uncertain. The Treasury and Mint maintain that the 2020 Redesign Act provides sufficient authority, while legal experts and Democratic lawmakers argue the coin violates both the letter and spirit of federal law.

Future court challenges with proper standing, the fate of the Change Corruption Act, and the commercial reception of the coin itself will determine whether this becomes a lasting addition to American numismatics or a footnote in the nation's 250th anniversary celebrations.
