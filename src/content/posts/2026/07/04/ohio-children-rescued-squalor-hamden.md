# 16 Children Rescued From Squalor in Ohio: How Did It Go Unnoticed?

Authorities in rural southeastern Ohio discovered 16 children living in horrific, squalid conditions inside a small five-room home in Hamden, Vinton County, on June 30, 2026. The children — ranging from 18 months to 18 years old, including three sets of twins — were confined to a single 12-by-12-foot room contaminated with human waste, had no school enrollment, and received little to no medical care. Four adults were arrested and charged with 16 counts each of felony child endangerment, and the case has sent shockwaves through the tight-knit community and beyond.

## The Discovery

Law enforcement officers from the Ohio Bureau of Criminal Investigation and the Vinton County Sheriff’s Office executed a search warrant on Ohmer Street in Hamden as part of an unrelated investigation. What they found inside was, in the words of Ohio Attorney General Andy Wilson, “pure evil.” According to [AP News](https://apnews.com/article/hamden-ohio-children-abuse-removed-fa8efadfabab556f8478c6c0439d9d6e), seven children were taken to hospitals, two were flown by helicopter, and one was in critical condition. Some of the children were unable to speak, and an 18-year-old with developmental disabilities could not write her name.

“Most of our livestock was kept in better conditions than the children,” Vinton County Sheriff Ryan Cain said at a press conference. “Just a disgusting scene.”

Wilson added that if authorities had arrived 24 hours later, “there was a very high probability that we’d be dealing with a death or multiple deaths of these children,” as [WOUB Public Media](https://woub.org/2026/07/01/sixteen-children-rescued-vinton-county-home-shared-small-room/) reported.

## The Four Adults Charged

Gary Siders Jr., 36, and Elizabeth Siders, 33 — the children’s parents — along with paternal grandparents Gary Siders Sr., 73, and Christina Siders, 67, were arrested at the scene. Each pleaded not guilty to 16 counts of second-degree felony child endangerment, and bond was set at $300,000 per person.

Public records obtained by the [Columbus Dispatch](https://www.dispatch.com/story/news/courts/2026/07/02/info-public-records-tied-vinton-county-abuse-case-ohio-16-kids-rescued-house-elizabeth-gary-siders/90781239007/) reveal that Elizabeth married Gary Jr. at age 15 in March 2008 in Mason County, West Virginia. The oldest child was born in May 2008. Elizabeth left school after the 11th grade and had no prior criminal history. Gary Jr. had pending misdemeanor indecent exposure charges from May 2026 and a 2017 citation for lacking a booster seat for a child.

## How Did This Go Undetected?

The case has raised profound questions about how such extreme abuse could remain hidden for years. Investigators say the family moved around southeastern Ohio over the past two decades and appears to have deliberately avoided creating a medical or government paper trail. None of the children were enrolled in the Vinton County Local School District — the primary mechanism by which child welfare issues are typically flagged.

“These folks were pretty good at hiding these kids,” Wilson said.

Neighbors told investigators they had never seen children at the home. Emily Collins, a local business owner, lamented to AP News: “Right under our noses and nobody was able to help them sooner.”

The house sits on a road tucked alongside a steep railroad embankment in a village of fewer than 1,000 residents, with neighbors separated by trees and thick brush — factors that contributed to the family’s isolation.

## A Debate Over ‘Pure Evil’ vs. Isolation

A central tension in the case has emerged between the attorney general’s characterization of the family as “pure evil” and the defense’s framing of the situation as one of extreme isolation. Thomas Stolly, Elizabeth Siders’ attorney, told AP News that his client was “crying and exhausted” when he met her, and her first question was about her children.

“Evil requires malice, and I did not see any malice in Elizabeth,” Stolly said. “I think that this is more so a case of isolation than a case of evil.”

Stolly noted that Elizabeth married at 15 and had been shaped by a worldview limited to the home for 17 or 18 years. Dorian Baum, attorney for Gary Siders Sr., urged the public to “take a deep breath, step back, and let the case play out.”

## The Road Ahead for the Children

The children are now in temporary custody of the Ohio Department of Jobs and Family Services, but the challenges ahead are daunting. As [Spectrum News 1](https://spectrumnews1.com/oh/columbus/news/2026/07/03/challenges-remain-for-children-rescued) reports, Ohio — like many states — faces a shortage of foster families, and not all can accommodate children with such severe needs. Some children may require specialized residential treatment centers.

Scott Britton, assistant director of the Public Children Services Association of Ohio, told Spectrum News that one in four children entering Ohio’s foster care system do so not because of abuse or neglect, but due to behavioral health issues, developmental disabilities, or juvenile justice involvement. Worker turnover rates in child welfare range from 20% to 40% in some areas due to burnout.

The case echoes the 2018 Turpin family abuse case in California, where 13 children were rescued from similar conditions. In that case, six siblings later reported abuse in foster care, and a 2022 report found the county’s social services system was short-staffed and underfunded.

## What’s Next

The investigation remains ongoing, with authorities reviewing whether the family was ever reported to children’s services agencies. More charges may be filed as evidence is gathered. The case has reignited calls for stronger oversight of vulnerable children, particularly those outside the school system, and for greater investment in Ohio’s child welfare infrastructure.

For the 16 children rescued from Ohmer Street, the path to recovery will be long. As Wilson put it: “It’s going to take a lot of work to address the emotional harm and some of the issues that are going to result from this.”
