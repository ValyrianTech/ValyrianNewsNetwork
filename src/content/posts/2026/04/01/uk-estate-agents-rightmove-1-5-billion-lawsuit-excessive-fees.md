# Estate Agents Launch £1.5bn Rightmove Lawsuit Over Fees

Major UK estate agents have launched a £1.5 billion ($2 billion) class action lawsuit against Rightmove, Britain's dominant property portal, alleging it has abused its market position by charging excessive and unfair subscription fees. The legal action, filed in the Competition Appeal Tribunal (CAT) on April 1, 2026, claims Rightmove has exploited its dominant position in the online property listings market, with shares in the FTSE 100 company falling nearly 9% following the announcement, wiping approximately £300 million off its market value.

## Context: Rightmove's Market Dominance

Rightmove has established itself as the UK's primary property portal, with the company's own research suggesting it commands an [80% share of time spent on property portals](https://plc.rightmove.co.uk/content/uploads/2025/05/RIG001_2024AR_WEB_250321_160525.pdf). The platform has consistently reported profit margins around 70%, making it one of the most profitable companies in the FTSE 100 index. According to [BBC News](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/cn0yle5rrp8o), estate agents feel compelled to use Rightmove despite rising costs, with many reporting that the portal delivers the majority of their leads while charging significantly more than competitors like Zoopla.

## The Legal Challenge

The claim, led by former Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) panel member Jeremy Newman, alleges that Rightmove has charged thousands of estate agents and new home developers "excessive and unfair subscription fees" that have more than doubled for some businesses in recent years with little change in services provided. Newman, an accountant by profession, described the situation as "David and Goliath," telling the BBC that "estate agents are having to employ fewer people because they can't afford them alongside their fees to Rightmove."

Over 250 estate agencies have expressed interest in and support for the claim, which follows a pre-action letter sent to Rightmove in November 2025. The formal £1.5 billion claim represents one of the largest collective proceedings in UK competition law history.

## Estate Agent Testimonies: The Financial Strain

Alisa Zotimova, founder of AZ Real Estate in London, told the BBC her fees have "more than doubled" in seven years, describing the rise as "unsustainable." She explained, "You don't have to sign up but it feels like I'm pushed into a bit of a corner with my customers expecting me to use it." Zotimova warned that if smaller agents cannot afford these fees, consumers would face "higher fees, less choice of agents if smaller ones can't compete."

Chris, who owns two estate agents in Northamptonshire, reported being charged over £5,000 per month for a basic membership allowing him to advertise 30-50 properties online. "It's a lot of money to find every single month," he said, noting the cost equates to two full-time staff salaries. "When you're trying to run and operate a small business and a particular company has complete control of that business, is a challenge."

## Rightmove's Defense and Value Proposition

Rightmove has vigorously denied the allegations, stating the claim is "without merit, and we will defend it vigorously." The company maintains it provides significant value, claiming on its [facts page](https://www.rightmove.co.uk/press-centre/rightmove-facts/) to give agents "access and brand exposure to the UK's largest and most engaged home-moving audience."

A Rightmove spokesperson said, "We are confident in the value we provide to our partners and consumers, who are at the core of our business solutions and digital platform. As one of the most efficient parts of the UK housing market, we help people across the UK to move home by bringing buyers, sellers, renters, landlords and agents together."

Some agents acknowledge Rightmove's value despite the costs. Andy Keogh, a Midlands estate agent, believes the platform gives "value for money," with 80% of his leads coming from Rightmove. "If you don't like it, don't use them," he told the BBC, though he acknowledged Rightmove has a "monopoly on the market." Keogh noted that for lettings, Zoopla charges £450-500 monthly compared to Rightmove's £1,710-£1,850 for similar services.

## Market Context and Industry Pressures

The lawsuit emerges during a challenging period for the UK housing market, with falling demand, higher interest rates, and flat property prices squeezing estate agent margins. The research compiled by NewsResearchAgent notes that market conditions have been further impacted by broader economic factors, including international conflicts affecting economic stability.

Rightmove's financial performance has remained robust despite these market conditions, with the company's high profit margins drawing scrutiny from industry participants who argue fees should reflect market realities rather than the portal's dominant position.

## Legal and Regulatory Implications

The case represents a significant test for UK competition law and collective proceedings mechanisms. The Competition Appeal Tribunal, which handles such class actions for competition law breaches, will need to determine whether Rightmove's fees constitute an abuse of dominant position under UK competition law.

Successful claims can result in substantial damages awards, potentially setting precedents for other industries with dominant digital platforms. The case may also influence the Competition and Markets Authority's approach to regulating digital platforms in the property sector and beyond.

## What Happens Next

The Competition Appeal Tribunal will now review the claim, with proceedings expected to unfold over the coming months. Key questions the tribunal will address include:

1. Whether Rightmove holds a dominant position in the UK property portal market
2. What constitutes "excessive" fees in relation to services provided
3. The appropriate methodology for calculating damages if the claim succeeds

Industry observers will be watching closely, as the outcome could reshape the economics of property marketing in the UK. A successful claim might force Rightmove to revise its pricing strategy, potentially lowering costs for estate agents and, by extension, consumers. It could also encourage greater competition in the property portal sector, which has been dominated by Rightmove for years.

For now, estate agents continue to navigate the difficult balance between Rightmove's indispensable market reach and what many describe as unsustainable financial demands. As Jeremy Newman summarized, "Rightmove is exploiting a self-evident dominant market position, and are charging too much for it." The tribunal's decision will determine whether the courts agree.
