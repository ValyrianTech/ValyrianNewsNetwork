# BBC Knew About Scott Mills Probe Since 2017

The BBC confirmed on Wednesday it was aware of a police investigation into sexual offences allegations against Radio 2 DJ Scott Mills in 2017, raising serious questions about internal procedures and the timing of his recent dismissal. According to [BBC News](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cwywvrye76lo), the broadcaster only terminated Mills' contract last week after obtaining "new information" about his conduct, despite knowing about the police investigation for nearly nine years.

Mills, 53, was sacked on Friday, 27 March 2026, following allegations related to his personal conduct. The former Radio 1 presenter, earning £355,000-£359,999 annually as the BBC's 11th highest-paid star, was taken off air on Tuesday, 24 March, with his last Radio 2 breakfast show sign-off stating "Back tomorrow"—a promise never fulfilled.

## Context: A Troubled Timeline

The Metropolitan Police investigation began in December 2016 following a referral from Hampshire Constabulary. It related to allegations of serious sexual offences against a teenage boy reported between 1997 and 2000, when Mills would have been in his mid-twenties. Police confirmed on Tuesday the alleged victim was under 16.

According to the BBC's statement, the corporation learned of the police investigation in 2017. Mills was questioned under caution in July 2018, and the Crown Prosecution Service closed the case in May 2019 due to insufficient evidence. BBC News understands then-Director General Tony Hall was unaware of the allegations.

## Recent Developments and "New Information"

The BBC stated: "In recent weeks, we obtained new information relating to Scott and we spoke directly with him. As a result, the BBC acted decisively in line with our culture and values and terminated his contracts on Friday 27 March." The corporation hasn't specified what this new information entails or what role the earlier police investigation played.

Separately, the BBC apologized on Tuesday for failing to investigate a 2025 query about "inappropriate communications" involving Mills. According to [The Telegraph](https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/2026/03/31/bbc-knew-about-scott-mills-allegations-almost-a-year-ago/), a freelance journalist contacted the BBC in May 2025 with limited information, but the corporation didn't follow up.

"We received a press query in 2025 which included limited information," the BBC stated. "This should have been followed up and we should have asked further questions. We apologise for this and will look into why this did not happen."

## Internal Procedures Under Scrutiny

The disclosure intensifies scrutiny of BBC's misconduct handling procedures. This follows the 2023 Huw Edwards scandal and Tim Westwood allegations, which prompted a 2025 independent culture review establishing new behavioral expectations.

"Separately, we can confirm the BBC was made aware in 2017 of the existence of an ongoing police investigation, which was subsequently closed in 2019 with no arrest or charge being made," a BBC spokesperson said. "We are doing more work to understand the detail of what was known by the BBC at this time."

The statement added the BBC has made "a significant commitment to improve its culture, processes and standards," noting: "Last year, following an independent culture review, we set out the behavioral expectations for everyone who works with or for the BBC and we were clear action would be taken if these were not met."

## Career Impact and Fallout

Mills' dismissal ends his 28-year BBC career, which began at Radio 1 in 1998. He moved to Radio 2 in 2022, replacing Steve Wright, before taking the Breakfast Show from Zoe Ball in January 2025. His show reached 6.5 million listeners, making it the UK's most popular breakfast radio program.

Neuroblastoma UK has cut ties with Mills, who was previously a patron. The BBC plans to continue with a Race Across The World podcast Mills was due to host but will appoint a new host, [according to Deadline](https://deadline.com/2026/04/bbc-scott-mills-race-across-the-world-detour-going-ahead-1236769960/).

Gary Davies and OJ Borg will cover the Breakfast Show as speculation mounts about a permanent replacement.

## Analysis: Broader Implications

This case highlights systemic issues in BBC's misconduct handling. The nine-year gap between awareness and dismissal raises questions about internal processes for high-profile presenters.

The failure to investigate the 2025 query suggests breakdowns in complaint mechanisms despite recent reforms. As [The Mirror first reported](https://www.mirror.co.uk/3am/celebrity-news/scott-mills-sacked-bbc-police-36946152), the timing of Mills' sacking—days after police confirmed the alleged victim was under 16—suggests this may have been part of the "new information."

The case illustrates the complex relationship between legal and employment standards. While police found insufficient evidence for criminal charges in 2019, the BBC determined Mills' conduct warranted termination based on internal behavioral expectations.

## What's Next: Ongoing Questions

Key questions remain: What specific "new information" prompted March 2026 action? Who at the BBC knew about the 2017 investigation and why wasn't the Director General informed? What were the "inappropriate communications" alleged in 2025?

The BBC has committed to understanding "what was known by the BBC" during 2017-2019. This internal review comes during a leadership transition, with Director General Tim Davie in his final week.

For the media industry, this represents another chapter in reckoning with historical allegations and safeguarding. It underscores challenges public service broadcasters face balancing fair process with protecting vulnerable people and maintaining public trust.

The effectiveness of BBC's 2025 cultural reforms will be tested. Transparently addressing systemic issues while maintaining programming standards is crucial for restoring public confidence after multiple high-profile presenter scandals.
