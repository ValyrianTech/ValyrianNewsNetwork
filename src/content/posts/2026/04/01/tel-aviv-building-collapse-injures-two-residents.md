# Tel Aviv Building Collapse Injures Two Residents

A six-story residential building partially collapsed in central Tel Aviv on Wednesday, injuring two people and raising concerns about building safety in older urban areas. The incident occurred at Simtat HaShuk Street near Levinsky Market, with emergency services rescuing a 62-year-old woman and a 35-year-old man who were hospitalized in moderate condition.

According to [Ynet](https://www.ynet.co.il/news/article/rjtvowqiwx#autoplay), the collapse affected the top floor of the building, causing significant damage to four apartments. Both injured individuals were extracted conscious and transported to Ichilov Hospital for treatment. Tel Aviv District Police Commander Haim Sargaroff confirmed at the scene that the incident was not related to the ongoing conflict with Iran or any criminal activity.

## Context and Building History

The collapsed building had a history of safety concerns. In 2018, the structure was declared dangerous under Section 3 of Tel Aviv's municipal bylaw for dangerous buildings. The Tel Aviv Municipality filed a lawsuit to address defects in the ground floor and basement, which were reportedly repaired by 2020. However, officials emphasized that these previously identified defects were not in the upper floors that collapsed.

This incident highlights ongoing challenges with aging infrastructure in Tel Aviv's historic neighborhoods. The Levinsky Market area features densely packed buildings from the early to mid-20th century, creating complex safety oversight issues for municipal authorities.

## Emergency Response and Rescue Operations

Emergency services including fire, police, medical teams, and Home Front Command responded to the scene. The area was secured, and additional buildings in the vicinity were evacuated as a precautionary measure.

Commander Sargaroff described the scene as "very difficult" and warned that the building could collapse further, noting it was leaning at an angle toward where rescue forces were positioned. "I ask residents and the curious not to come to the area," he stated. "The police and rescue units here are risking themselves, working hard, and need to finish quickly and move away from this dangerous place."

## Witness Accounts and Immediate Aftermath

Residents and bystanders described terrifying moments as the building gave way. Eran, who lives on the fifth floor of the affected building, told Ynet: "I was in the apartment when suddenly the whole thing shook, all the walls shook. I was sure it was a missile, and then I just escaped outside and saw that part of the building collapsed, 20 meters from my apartment."

Gabi, a neighbor in an adjacent building, reported: "I heard a boom, and said to myself there's no alert, but maybe it's a missile. When I went outside I saw everything collapsed."

Roi, a passerby, described the scene: "I passed by the street and suddenly heard a noise like when construction waste is thrown. I thought it was just workers throwing waste, even when it intensified, but suddenly there was a big white dust cloud. I moved away from it, and still didn't understand it was a building collapse, and suddenly several people started running and shouting that the building is collapsing."

Michal, who works nearby, added: "Suddenly without any connection to anything there was a noise of many bricks falling, like when they dismantle at a construction site, and there was a crazy dust cloud. We looked when it cleared and saw that half the building collapsed."

## Structural Assessment and Safety Concerns

The exact cause of the collapse remains under investigation, though initial assessments indicate it was not the direct result of a missile impact. The building was reportedly already in poor condition, and repeated shocks from missile shrapnel impacts in the area combined with rainy weather may have contributed to the structural failure.

This incident raises significant questions about building inspection and enforcement protocols in Tel Aviv's older neighborhoods. While the building had been flagged as dangerous years earlier, the repairs addressed only ground-level issues, leaving potential vulnerabilities in the upper floors unaddressed.

## Broader Implications for Urban Infrastructure

The Tel Aviv building collapse occurs against a backdrop of ongoing concerns about aging infrastructure in Israeli cities. Many residential buildings in historic areas like Levinsky Market were constructed before modern building codes were established, creating challenges for maintenance and safety compliance.

Municipal authorities face difficult balancing acts between preserving historic neighborhoods and ensuring resident safety. The incident is likely to trigger renewed scrutiny of dangerous building regulations and enforcement mechanisms across Israeli municipalities.

## What's Next: Investigations and Policy Review

Structural engineers and municipal inspectors are conducting thorough examinations of the collapsed building and surrounding structures. The investigation will seek to determine the precise structural deficiencies that led to the collapse and whether similar risks exist in other buildings in the area.

The Tel Aviv Municipality has emphasized that the cause of the collapse remains unknown and is under investigation. Officials will need to review whether current inspection protocols adequately identify risks in multi-story buildings and whether repair mandates should extend beyond specifically identified defects to comprehensive structural assessments.

As rescue operations transition to recovery and investigation phases, the incident serves as a stark reminder of the ongoing challenges in maintaining urban infrastructure safety. The two injured individuals continue to receive treatment at Ichilov Hospital, while displaced residents await assessments of when—or if—they can return to their homes.

This partial building collapse in one of Tel Aviv's historic neighborhoods underscores the complex interplay between urban preservation, resident safety, and municipal oversight that cities worldwide must navigate as their infrastructure ages.
