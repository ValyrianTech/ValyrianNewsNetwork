# 23 Ambulances Damaged in Iran Conflict, 8 Destroyed

**TEHRAN** — Iranian emergency services have reported significant damage to medical infrastructure during the ongoing "Ramadan War," with 23 ambulances damaged in Tehran alone and eight completely destroyed, according to official statements from the Tehran Province Emergency Center.

The damage highlights the severe impact of the month-long conflict on healthcare infrastructure and emergency medical services in Iran's capital. Mohammad Esmail Tavakoli, Head of Tehran Province Emergency Center, [revealed the extent of the damage](https://www.tasnimnews.ir/fa/news/1405/01/12/3554132/1262-iranians-returned) during a field inspection on April 1, 2026, noting that nearly 10 operational support vehicles were also affected.

"To date, 23 ambulances and nearly 10 operational support vehicles have been damaged," Tavakoli stated. "Eight vehicles have been completely destroyed and two are still under rubble, and due to the volume of impacts we have not been able to extract them."

## Emergency Personnel Casualties

The human toll on emergency responders has been equally severe. Tavakoli reported that 56 emergency personnel were injured during rescue operations, with one colleague "martyred" in western Tehran while providing aid to war casualties.

"Also, 56 of our brave colleagues were injured and one of our colleagues in western Tehran was martyred while providing aid to war casualties due to renewed attacks at the scene," he said.

Despite the damage, emergency services have maintained operations, completing over 3,000 war-related missions and approximately 50,000 routine missions since the conflict began on February 28. About 27 percent of routine missions involved traffic accidents, largely attributed to Nowruz holiday travel.

## National Scale of Healthcare Damage

The Tehran figures represent only part of a broader national crisis. According to [United Nations reports](https://news.un.org/en/story/2026/03/1167208), nearly 100 ambulances have been damaged or destroyed across Iran since the war began, with 17 Red Crescent centers struck and 21 attacks on health workers and facilities recorded by the World Health Organization.

Maria Martinez, International Federation of Red Cross and Red Crescent Societies (IFRC) Head of Delegation in Iran, emphasized the critical nature of the ambulance losses: "These are not just vehicles. They are often the only hope people have when the bombs fall."

## Civilian Impact and Government Response

Tavakoli highlighted the disproportionate impact on civilians, stating that over 85 percent of casualties in Tehran metropolitan area are ordinary citizens, particularly women and children. Emergency services have provided care to more than 7,000 wounded individuals in the capital region.

The Iranian official acknowledged government support during the crisis, mentioning direct involvement from senior officials including Dr. Pourmohammadi, Head of the Planning and Budget Organization, and Dr. Hajimirzaei, Head of the Presidential Office.

"From the first moment of the war, Dr. Pourmohammadi personally contacted us, and Dr. Hajimirzaei follows up on the situation with us every night," Tavakoli said.

## Broader Conflict Context

The "Ramadan War" began on February 28, 2026, with US-Israeli surprise airstrikes on sites across Iran, including the assassination of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. Iranian forces responded with missile and drone strikes against Israel, US bases, and US-allied countries in the Middle East.

According to Iranian Health Ministry figures cited in research materials, the conflict has resulted in 2,076 people killed and 26,500 injured nationwide as of early April.

## Humanitarian and Operational Challenges

The damage to emergency medical infrastructure comes amid broader humanitarian challenges. Martinez described Tehran as "a city of roughly nine million people [that] feels completely empty," with nationwide internet blackouts since February 28 and water and electricity cuts in some southern cities.

Despite these challenges, Tavakoli emphasized the resilience of Iran's emergency services: "Iran's emergency services, especially in Tehran province, are among the most powerful operational and planning collections in the region."

He contrasted Iranian emergency operations with those of "the enemy," noting that while published images show enemy emergency forces operating with military and protective equipment, "our forces are present in the field with courage, bravery, and pure spirit."

## Public Support and Volunteerism

The crisis has sparked significant public solidarity, with Tavakoli expressing gratitude for widespread volunteer support. "When the central emergency headquarters of the province was damaged, we did not expect such a large volume of public accompaniment," he said.

"From all over the country, from different provinces and cities, people contacted us to help—from specialist doctors to volunteer drivers. This solidarity and companionship is very valuable to us."

## International Humanitarian Law Concerns

The widespread damage to healthcare infrastructure raises serious concerns under international humanitarian law. Attacks on medical facilities, personnel, and vehicles are prohibited under the Geneva Conventions, which protect healthcare services during armed conflicts.

The UN has repeatedly emphasized the protection of healthcare in conflict zones, with the WHO maintaining a surveillance system to document attacks on health facilities globally.

## Looking Ahead

As the conflict enters its second month, the strain on Iran's emergency medical services continues to grow. The loss of ambulances and damage to emergency centers reduces response capacity at a time when civilian casualties are mounting.

Robert Blanchard, WHO Emergency Operations Team Lead at the Dubai Logistics Hub, reported some improvement in aid shipments: "The first two weeks of the crisis really set us back. But we're now receiving bookings for commercially scheduled cargo, and we're able to begin moving supplies out as we were in the past."

However, with nearly 100 ambulances damaged or destroyed nationwide and emergency personnel operating under extreme duress, Iran's healthcare system faces unprecedented challenges in providing lifesaving care amid ongoing hostilities.

The situation underscores the broader humanitarian crisis unfolding in the region, where civilian infrastructure—particularly healthcare—has become collateral damage in a conflict with no immediate resolution in sight.
