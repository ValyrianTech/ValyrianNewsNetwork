# Oreshkin: Russia's Financial System Most Efficient, Banking Must Transform

Russian presidential aide Maxim Oreshkin made bold claims about his country's financial superiority while predicting fundamental transformation for traditional banking during a forum in Moscow on Wednesday. Speaking at the TOLK-2026 forum organized by T-Bank, the deputy head of the Presidential Administration asserted that Russia's financial system is "the most efficient and modern in the world" while forecasting radical changes for the global banking sector.

According to [TASS](https://tass.ru/ekonomika/26960365), Oreshkin declared that "Russia's case is that our financial system is one of the most efficient in the world. It doesn't devour as much from the economy and society as American banks do." He elaborated that "when you go to any country, you understand that the Russian financial system is one of the most modern in the world," acknowledging remaining problems but emphasizing that "the efficiency and cost of these services for our society is one of the lowest in the world."

## Context of Financial Transformation

Oreshkin's statements come amid Russia's ongoing financial system restructuring following extensive Western sanctions imposed since 2022. The country has developed domestic alternatives to international financial infrastructure, including the Mir card payment system and SPFS financial messaging system as replacements for Visa/Mastercard and SWIFT respectively. These developments have occurred alongside increased focus on digital financial services and BRICS financial cooperation.

The 43-year-old official brings banking expertise to his government role, having worked at major Russian and foreign banks before serving as Minister of Economic Development (2016-2020), economic adviser to President Vladimir Putin (2020-2024), and now deputy chief of staff of the Presidential Executive Office.

## Banking Sector Transformation Predictions

In a separate but related assessment reported by [RBC](https://www.rbc.ru/finances/01/04/2026/69ccd4de9a79475cf2e96cc3), Oreshkin predicted radical changes for traditional banking institutions. "Banks in their traditional form should, in fact, die," he stated, explaining that "the traditional banking model has exhausted itself."

Oreshkin argued that competition in financial services now centers on "quality of algorithms and services" rather than "closed information arrays." He identified high financial intermediation costs as the core problem, specifically criticizing the American financial system for "devouring 2% of world GDP"—what he called "too large a payment by humanity for a fairly simple service of managing funds."

The former banker also expressed surprise at compensation levels within the industry, stating: "As someone who worked in the banking system, I always wondered why such high salaries are paid in the banking system. Forgive me, Lord, I received a high salary, as if not understanding why I receive much more than an engineer inventing an airplane or car."

## Platform Economy Transition

Oreshkin identified the transition to platform-based economic models as the driving force behind banking sector transformation. He described platforms not as merely new business types but as "a new way of organizing economic activity that comes to replace traditional market rails." According to his analysis, this shift will reduce transaction costs and pressure traditional banking structures, with platform models already affecting taxi services and retail markets.

The English-language publication [Oreanda-News](https://www.oreanda-news.com/en/gosudarstvo/oreshkin-assessed-russia-s-financial-system/article1588713/) confirmed Oreshkin's core message, quoting him as saying: "Our financial system is one of the most efficient in the world, and it doesn't consume as much of the economy and society as American and European banks."

## Regulatory Challenges and Geopolitical Dimensions

Oreshkin acknowledged regulatory challenges accompanying the platform economy transition, warning about the natural tendency of platforms toward monopolization. He stated that regulators worldwide face the task of balancing prevention of "platform feudalism"—where platforms capture all participant profits—with maintaining development incentives.

The geopolitical context of Oreshkin's statements is significant. His criticism of Western financial systems aligns with broader Russian narratives positioning the country as an alternative to Western economic dominance. These comments emerge as Russia continues operating under extensive financial sanctions while seeking to demonstrate economic resilience and develop alternative financial architectures, particularly within BRICS cooperation frameworks.

## Analysis and Implications

Oreshkin's dual claims—about Russian financial system efficiency and traditional banking's transformation—serve multiple purposes. The efficiency assertion appears primarily political, aimed at boosting domestic confidence in Russian institutions amid economic challenges from sanctions and military expenditures. Meanwhile, his banking transformation thesis reflects genuine global fintech trends while signaling government thinking about financial sector reform.

The platform economy warning reveals Kremlin concerns about monopolization risks in emerging economic models. As Russia accelerates digital transformation in its financial sector, Oreshkin's perspective as both former banker and senior official provides insights into government approaches to balancing innovation with regulatory oversight.

## What's Next

Financial analysts will monitor whether Oreshkin's statements translate into specific policy initiatives regarding banking sector reform or platform economy regulation. International observers will assess how Russia's efficiency claims align with objective metrics like financial inclusion, transaction costs, and system stability. The banking industry's response to Oreshkin's predictions—particularly within Russia's sanctioned financial environment—will indicate whether institutions accelerate digital transformation in anticipation of the changes he forecasts.

As global financial systems continue evolving amid technological disruption and geopolitical realignment, Oreshkin's comments highlight how national financial narratives increasingly intersect with technological transformation debates and international positioning strategies.
