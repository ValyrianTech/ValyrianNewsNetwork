# Belgium's Third Wave of Unemployment Cuts Hits 42,000

Belgium has implemented the third and largest wave of unemployment benefit restrictions today, cutting off approximately 42,000 people who have been unemployed for 2-8 years. According to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/01/derde-golf-beperking-werkloosheidsuitkering-grootste-groep/), this marks a historic shift from Belgium's previous system of unlimited unemployment benefits to a maximum 24-month limit, representing the most significant social policy reform in decades.

## Context and Background

Belgium has historically maintained one of Europe's most generous unemployment benefit systems, with unlimited duration for those meeting eligibility criteria. This "Belgian exception" in European social policy has now been replaced by a phased implementation of benefit restrictions. The federal government decided last year that unemployment benefits would no longer be unlimited, capping them at 24 months maximum.

The reform is being implemented in three waves, each targeting different groups based on unemployment duration. The first wave on January 1, 2026, affected approximately 20,000 people unemployed for 20+ years. The second wave on March 1 impacted about 35,000 people unemployed for 8-20 years. Today's third wave is the largest, targeting those unemployed for 2-8 years.

## The Current Wave: Demographics and Impact

The 42,000 individuals affected in this wave represent a distinct demographic profile. According to statistics from the RVA (National Employment Service), just over half are men, and the group is significantly younger than previous waves. The largest age group is 25-35 years old (38%), followed by 35-45 years (34%). Three-quarters are Belgian citizens, and the majority are low-to-medium skilled workers.

Annemie Clerckx, head of social services at OCMW Leuven, told VRT NWS that her office is dealing with 160 affected individuals, with only 70 currently in contact. "This group of people is quite far from the labor market, they've been left to their own devices for quite some time," Clerckx said.

## Strain on Social Welfare Systems

The implementation is placing significant strain on Belgium's social welfare centers (OCMWs/CPASs). Clerckx reported that her team couldn't process 22 files in March due to capacity issues and expects a tripling of cases by July. "We're making an emergency plan for how we're going to survive the summer in Leuven," she stated.

The pressure extends beyond administrative capacity. Clerckx noted that many affected individuals exhibit "high psychological vulnerability" and distrust toward government institutions. "The distrust toward OCMWs or the government is also very large," she observed, adding that some withdraw their applications when faced with required social investigations and home visits.

## Systemic Challenges and Alternative Approaches

Clerckx offered a stark assessment of the systemic challenges: "We have to be honest: many of these people are actually no longer capable of working. These people are simply in the wrong system." She pointed to the decline of the social economy and lack of adapted jobs as significant barriers to labor market reintegration.

Rather than focusing solely on employment activation, Clerckx suggested alternative approaches: "People do volunteer work or caregiving. Let them do that, that's human capital for society." She emphasized building self-worth, social networks, and addressing loneliness as preliminary steps before labor market activation.

## Political and Regional Dimensions

The reform has sparked political tensions, particularly in Brussels where 12,989 residents are affected in this wave alone. In a November 2025 article in The Bulletin, Brussels PS Chairman Ahmed Laaouej warned that ["without compensation, local authorities, the last social safety net, will collapse"](https://www.thebulletin.be/impending-disaster-almost-50000-unemployed-people-belgium-lose-benefits). In the same article, Brussels MP Celia Groothedde of Groen described the reform as part of "N-VA's strategy to undermine Brussels," while Brussels Minister for Employment Bernard Clerfayt expressed concern that promised compensation funds may not be delivered.

The federal government has allocated €26 million to CPASs for implementation costs, with promises of €300 million in compensation.

## Healthcare Implications

One positive note for affected individuals is that their right to healthcare reimbursement is guaranteed for 2026-2027 if they received benefits in 2024-2025. Health insurance funds like CM are preparing for the administrative challenges, though uncertainty remains about coverage after 2027.

## Looking Ahead

The coming months will test whether Belgium's social welfare system can handle the influx of new cases. OCMWs across the country are struggling with staffing shortages, with Clerckx noting they may need to call recently retired staff to return to work.

This reform represents a fundamental shift in Belgium's social contract, moving from a system of unlimited support to one emphasizing activation and time limits. As the largest European country to maintain unlimited unemployment benefits until now, Belgium's experience will be closely watched by policymakers across the continent.

The success or failure of this historic reform will depend not only on whether it increases employment but also on whether adequate support systems exist to prevent vulnerable individuals from falling into poverty. As Annemie Clerckx of OCMW Leuven starkly noted, the coming summer will be a critical test of Belgium's social safety net resilience.
