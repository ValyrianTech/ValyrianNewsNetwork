# Belgium-Algeria Return Deal Signed After 20 Years

Belgium has finalized a landmark return agreement with Algeria after nearly two decades of negotiations, marking a significant breakthrough in migration policy cooperation between the two countries. The agreement, signed on March 31, 2026, establishes streamlined procedures for returning Algerian nationals without legal residence in Belgium while offering visa waivers for Algerian diplomatic and service passport holders.

According to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/01/akkoord-algerije-over-terugkeerbeleid-van-bossuyt/), Belgian Foreign Minister Maxime Prévot (Les Engagés) and Asylum and Migration Minister Anneleen Van Bossuyt (N-VA) signed the agreement with Algerian Foreign Minister Ahmed Attaf in Brussels. The deal represents what Van Bossuyt called a "historic agreement that has been awaited for almost 20 years."

## Context and Background

The agreement comes at a critical time for Belgium's migration and prison systems. Official statistics from [InfoMigrants](https://www.infomigrants.net/en/post/67422/belgium-links-prisonovercrowding-plan-to-wider-migration-strategy) show Belgian prisons operating at 118% capacity, with approximately 13,000 inmates in facilities designed for 11,000. Foreign nationals constitute 43% of the prison population, with around 31% (4,500 inmates) lacking legal residence status. In 2024, authorities conducted [1,261 forced returns from prisons](https://emnbelgium.be/news/1261-prisoners-were-subjected-forced-return-belgiums-prisons-2024), highlighting the connection between migration policy and institutional overcrowding.

Algeria represents a particularly challenging case for Belgian authorities. According to the VRT NWS report, in 2025, 2,251 people claiming Algerian nationality received deportation orders, but only 85 were actually repatriated—a return rate of less than 4%. Currently, 780 individuals claiming Algerian nationality are in Belgian prisons, with 700 lacking legal residence status.

## Key Provisions of the Agreement

The agreement consists of two linked components: a return/readmission agreement for irregular migrants and a visa waiver for diplomatic and service passport holders. This linkage creates mutual accountability, as Van Bossuyt explained: "In exchange for the visa waiver for diplomatic and service passports, there will be more efficient returns. If one of the two partners fails to fulfill its commitment, this will also have consequences for the other agreement."

Key operational changes include:

1. **Faster Identification**: Algeria commits to confirming nationality within approximately 15 days, down from the current several-month timeframe. This addresses what Van Bossuyt identified as a major obstacle: "Some migrants falsely claim to be Algerian precisely because return to that country is so difficult."

2. **Extended Document Validity**: Return travel documents ("laissez-passer") will be valid for 30 days instead of the current 24 hours, reducing logistical failures in the return process.

3. **Group Returns**: Multiple individuals can now be returned on the same flight, including indirect flights, increasing efficiency and reducing costs.

4. **Algerian Escorts**: Belgium can utilize Algerian escorts for forced returns, reducing the burden on Belgian federal police.

## Political and Economic Dimensions

The agreement follows Foreign Minister Prévot's visit to Algiers in July 2025, aimed at rebuilding bilateral relations strained over two decades. Beyond migration, the improved relations open doors for economic cooperation. According to [The Brussels Times](https://www.brusselstimes.com/belgium/2055932/belgium-and-algeria-sign-agreements-on-return-of-undocumented-migrants), annual trade between Belgium and Algeria stands at €1.7 billion, with potential for expansion in construction, port dredging, and heavy vehicle sectors.

Energy cooperation also features in discussions, though it remains separate from the migration agreement. Algeria is the European Union's fourth-largest gas supplier and second-largest by pipeline, making energy security a strategic consideration. Van Bossuyt noted the broader context: "We see the impact of the conflict in the Middle East on our energy prices. Since Algeria is an important gas supplier, it's obviously important to maintain good relations."

## Human Rights Considerations

The agreement raises important human rights questions that require careful implementation. International organizations have expressed [concerns about Algeria's human rights record, particularly regarding freedom of expression, assembly, and media](https://www.hrw.org/world-report/2025/country-chapters/algeria). Additionally, reports document arbitrary arrests and collective expulsions of sub-Saharan African migrants from Algeria.

Human rights advocates emphasize the need for individual risk assessments before deportations to ensure compliance with international obligations. The European Court of Human Rights has consistently ruled that states must conduct proper assessments to prevent returns to situations where individuals might face torture, inhuman or degrading treatment.

## Political Implications and Next Steps

The agreement represents a significant victory for Minister Van Bossuyt's hardline approach to migration. She has consistently emphasized that "illegal residence is still a crime in this country" and framed the agreement as crucial for "our fight against illegality, for less pressure on our prisons and to make our society safer."

Both agreements now require approval from the Belgian federal parliament, with debates expected in April 2026. The parliamentary process will likely examine the human rights safeguards, implementation mechanisms, and the broader implications for Belgium's migration policy.

Looking ahead, Belgian energy envoy is scheduled to visit Algiers in late April 2026 for talks on energy cooperation, indicating that the migration agreement forms part of a broader diplomatic and economic rapprochement.

## Broader EU Migration Policy Context

The Belgium-Algeria agreement fits within a wider European Union trend of pursuing bilateral return agreements with African countries. As EU member states grapple with migration pressures, such agreements offer a framework for managed returns while addressing root causes through development cooperation and legal migration pathways.

For Algeria, the agreement comes as the country itself faces migration pressures from sub-Saharan Africa, creating complex regional dynamics. The North African nation has increasingly positioned itself as a partner in EU migration management while seeking economic and diplomatic benefits.

## What to Watch Next

The implementation of the agreement will be closely monitored on several fronts:

1. **Operational Efficiency**: Whether Algeria consistently meets the 15-day identification deadline and how this affects actual return numbers.

2. **Human Rights Compliance**: How Belgium conducts individual risk assessments and ensures due process protections.

3. **Parliamentary Approval**: The debate and voting in the Belgian parliament, which may reveal political divisions over migration policy.

4. **Broader Impact**: Whether this agreement serves as a model for similar arrangements with other countries, such as the Democratic Republic of Congo and Turkey, with which Belgium also seeks improved return cooperation.

After 20 years of negotiations, the Belgium-Algeria return agreement marks a significant milestone in migration diplomacy. Its success will depend not only on efficient implementation but also on balancing migration management objectives with human rights protections and broader diplomatic relations between Europe and North Africa.
