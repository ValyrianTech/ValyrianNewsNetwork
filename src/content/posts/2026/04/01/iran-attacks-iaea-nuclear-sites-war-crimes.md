# Iran Declares Attacks on Safeguarded Nuclear Facilities as War Crimes

Iran's Atomic Energy Organization has declared that recent US-Israeli strikes on nuclear facilities under International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) supervision constitute war crimes under international law. Behrouz Kamalvandi, spokesperson and vice president for international affairs of Iran's Atomic Energy Organization (AEOI), made the statement on April 1, 2026, following attacks on the Shahid Khondab Heavy Water Complex in Arak and the Ardakan yellowcake production plant in Yazd Province. [According to Tasnim News Agency](https://www.tasnimnews.ir/fa/news/1405/01/12/3554120/kamalvandi-attack-safeguarded-facilities-war-crime), Kamalvandi asserted that "attacking nuclear facilities that are under IAEA safeguards is totally inconsistent with international principles and such an international offense, even against a heavy water complex, is definitely a war crime."

## Context: Escalating Conflict and Nuclear Targeting

The declaration comes amid an escalating conflict that began on February 28, 2026, when US-Israeli military operations against Iran commenced. The strikes on nuclear facilities represent a significant escalation, targeting infrastructure with special protection under international humanitarian law. The Arak heavy water plant had previously been attacked in June 2025 and was not operational at the time of the March 27, 2026 strike, while the Bushehr Nuclear Power Plant area experienced its third attack since the conflict began.

Iran's nuclear program has been under IAEA safeguards since 1974, with comprehensive monitoring agreements in place. The targeted facilities—particularly the heavy water complex at Arak—are subject to regular IAEA inspections, raising legal questions about attacks on verified civilian nuclear infrastructure. This situation echoes historical precedents including Israel's 1981 strike on Iraq's Osirak reactor and the 2007 strike on a Syrian reactor, though those targets were not under international safeguards at the time.

## Legal Framework and Iranian Position

Kamalvandi's war crime assertion finds basis in Article 56 of Additional Protocol I to the Geneva Conventions (1977), which provides special protection for "works or installations containing dangerous forces" including nuclear power stations. The provision prohibits attacks that may cause severe losses among civilian populations, though protection ceases if such facilities provide "regular, significant and direct support" to military operations.

"We have legally documented the incidents and are consulting both domestic and international legal experts," Kamalvandi stated in comments reported by PressTV. "The matter will be pursued through the Foreign Ministry and the office of the vice president for legal affairs." The Iranian official emphasized that facilities under IAEA safeguards enjoy specific legal protections.

## Specific Facilities and Attack Timeline

The research identifies three primary nuclear facility targets in the recent strikes:

1. **Shahid Khondab Heavy Water Complex (Arak)**: Targeted for the second time on March 27, 2026, having been previously attacked in June 2025. Heavy water facilities are monitored under Iran's safeguards agreement with the IAEA.

2. **Ardakan Yellowcake Production Plant (Yazd Province)**: Struck on March 27, 2026. Yellowcake is a type of uranium concentrate powder used in nuclear fuel production.

3. **Bushehr Nuclear Power Plant Area**: Experienced its third strike since February 28 on March 28, 2026. Preliminary reports indicate the main facility was not damaged.

No casualties or contamination risks have been reported from these nuclear facility strikes, though research compiled from multiple international reports indicates the broader conflict has resulted in significant casualties: over 1,900 in Iran, 1,100+ in Lebanon, 18 in Israel, and 13 US troops as of March 27.

## International Reactions and Conflicting Narratives

The attacks have drawn varied international responses reflecting divergent geopolitical alignments. Russian state media TASS reported that "attacks on Iran's nuclear facilities deserve severe condemnation," according to research. China's state media has similarly criticized the actions, while Western positions have emphasized preventive self-defense arguments.

Israeli Defense Minister Israel Katz framed the strikes differently, stating in comments reported by Fortune/Associated Press, "Iran will pay heavy, increasing prices for this war crime. Despite the warnings, the firing continues. And therefore attacks in Iran will escalate and expand to additional targets." This reflects the Israeli-US narrative of preventive action against perceived nuclear weapons development, invoking Article 51 of the UN Charter regarding self-defense.

Seyed Majid Moosavi, Aerospace Force Commander of Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC), issued a stark warning in statements carried by international media: "You tested us once before; the world has once again seen that you yourselves started playing with fire and attacking infrastructure. This time, the equation will no longer be 'an eye for an eye,' just wait."

## Strategic Implications and Escalation Risks

The declaration that attacks on safeguarded nuclear facilities constitute war crimes carries significant strategic implications:

1. **Legal Precedent**: Establishing such attacks as war crimes could create new norms in international humanitarian law regarding civilian nuclear infrastructure.

2. **Escalation Dynamics**: Iran's warning of retaliation "no longer an eye for an eye" suggests potential asymmetric responses beyond proportional retaliation.

3. **Nuclear Proliferation Concerns**: Attacks on nuclear facilities may incentivize countries to pursue nuclear weapons for deterrence rather than rely on international safeguards.

4. **Regional Stability**: Crossing previous red lines in targeting nuclear infrastructure increases risks of miscalculation and broader regional conflict.

5. **Economic Impact**: Research indicates the conflict has already driven Brent crude oil prices to $104.81 (from $70 pre-war) and affected 20% of global oil shipments through the Strait of Hormuz blockade.

## Diplomatic Context and Ceasefire Efforts

The nuclear facility strikes occur within a complex diplomatic landscape. According to research compiled from international reports, the United States delivered a 15-point "action list" for ceasefire via Pakistan, to which Iran responded with a 5-point counter-proposal including reparations and recognition of its sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz. US President Donald Trump issued an ultimatum demanding Iran reopen the Strait of Hormuz by April 6 or face destruction of Iranian energy plants.

G7 foreign ministers have called for an immediate halt to attacks on populations and infrastructure, research indicates, though the statement did not specifically address nuclear facility strikes. The IAEA has not issued an official statement regarding the attacks on safeguarded facilities as of April 1, 2026.

## Analysis: Legal Merit and Broader Implications

Kamalvandi's war crime claim possesses substantial legal merit under international humanitarian law. The special protection afforded to nuclear electrical generating stations under Additional Protocol I reflects recognition of the catastrophic consequences that could result from their damage. However, the legal analysis becomes complex when considering:

1. **Military Utility**: Whether the facilities provided "regular, significant and direct support" to military operations, which would negate their protected status.

2. **Proportionality**: Whether the military advantage gained justified the potential risks to civilian populations.

3. **Preventive vs. Preemptive**: The distinction between preventive self-defense (against potential future threats) and preemptive self-defense (against imminent threats) under international law.

Experts note that while the 1981 Osirak strike was widely condemned, it established a precedent for preventive action against nuclear programs perceived as threatening. The current situation differs significantly as the targeted facilities were under active IAEA safeguards with verified peaceful purposes.

## Conclusion: A Threshold Crossed in Modern Conflict

Iran's declaration that attacks on its safeguarded nuclear facilities constitute war crimes represents more than rhetorical escalation—it highlights a fundamental tension in 21st-century conflict between preventive self-defense arguments and the protection of civilian infrastructure under international law. The strikes on Arak and Ardakan facilities, while causing no reported casualties, cross a significant threshold in targeting verified civilian nuclear infrastructure.

The situation tests the resilience of international legal frameworks designed during the Cold War era, challenges the non-proliferation regime's credibility, and raises profound questions about how nations balance security concerns with legal obligations. As Kamalvandi noted in PressTV comments, "The enemy will definitely fail to obliterate Iran's nuclear knowledge through these attacks," suggesting that beyond immediate military objectives, the conflict engages deeper issues of technological sovereignty, legal norms, and the evolving nature of warfare in an era of complex international interdependencies.

The coming weeks will reveal whether this declaration remains a diplomatic maneuver or evolves into substantive legal action, and whether the international community develops consensus on the protection of safeguarded nuclear facilities in conflict zones—a question with implications far beyond the current Iran-Israel-US confrontation.
