# Israeli Plan to Bypass Strait of Hormuz Faces Iranian Skepticism

Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's proposal to create alternative energy routes bypassing the strategically vital Strait of Hormuz has drawn skeptical analysis from Iranian media, which questions both the technical feasibility and strategic effectiveness of such plans. The proposal comes amid ongoing tensions in the Persian Gulf, where Iran's control over the critical maritime chokepoint gives it significant leverage over global energy markets.

## The Strategic Stakes at Hormuz

The Strait of Hormuz represents one of the world's most critical energy transit points, with approximately 20% of global oil exports passing through its narrow waters. Bordering Iran to the north and Oman and the United Arab Emirates to the south, the 33-kilometer-wide waterway has long been a geopolitical flashpoint. Recent Iranian actions, including a blockade and naval mine-laying operations, have highlighted the vulnerability of this route, disrupting global energy markets and underscoring the urgency of finding alternatives.

Even if current obstacles are cleared, experts estimate it could take months for normal oil tanker traffic to resume through the strait, creating immediate pressure on energy-importing nations to secure alternative supply routes.

## Netanyahu's Pipeline Proposal

In a recent interview, Netanyahu outlined what he described as "long-term solutions" to the region's energy security challenges. "Long-term solutions include rerouting energy pipelines westward, across Saudi Arabia to the Red Sea and Mediterranean, bypassing Iran's geographic choke point," the Israeli leader stated, according to media reports.

The proposal builds on existing infrastructure that could theoretically create a land-based energy corridor. Israel's Eilat-Ashkelon pipeline, originally constructed in the 1960s as a joint venture with pre-revolutionary Iran, connects the Red Sea to the Mediterranean with a capacity of 600,000 barrels per day, according to energy industry data. Meanwhile, Saudi Arabia's East-West Pipeline (Petroline) spans 1,200 kilometers from the kingdom's eastern oil fields to the Red Sea port of Yanbu, capable of transporting 5 million barrels daily, based on petroleum industry reports.

As analyzed by [Tasnim News Agency](https://www.tasnimnews.ir/fa/news/1405/01/12/3553973/netanyahu-strait-hormuz), the concept envisions oil arriving from the Gulf through the Saudi pipeline to the Red Sea, then being transported to Eilat, pumped through the Eilat–Ashkelon pipeline, and finally shipped to Europe via the Mediterranean. This would create a complete land-based bypass of the Strait of Hormuz.

## Iranian Analysis: Questioning Feasibility

Iranian media has responded to Netanyahu's proposal with detailed skepticism, highlighting what it describes as fundamental challenges that have stalled similar plans for decades. According to the Tasnim analysis, "The Zionist regime proposed a similar plan about two decades ago to reduce dependence on common maritime routes; a plan whose axis is the port of 'Eilat' in the south of the occupied territories."

The analysis identifies several key obstacles:

**Geographic Limitations**: Eilat port is located in Israel's remote southern desert region, approximately 130-140 kilometers from the country's main population and industrial centers. Southern Israel remains the country's least populated area due to climatic conditions, and previous efforts to develop settlement in the region have met with limited success.

**Infrastructure Challenges**: Previous proposals for an Eilat-Dead Sea canal, which would have allowed ships to bypass the port of Eilat and reach points closer to consumption centers, failed to materialize despite their apparent simplicity. "With all the relative simplicity of the idea and the short distance, this project has not been implemented for more than two decades," notes the analysis, suggesting that "even more limited plans face serious technical, financial, and executive obstacles."

**Security Concerns**: Perhaps most fundamentally, the Iranian analysis questions whether alternative routes truly address security issues. "Such plans (bypassing the Strait of Hormuz) even if realized do not necessarily solve the security problem," it argues. "Alternative routes and infrastructure are also exposed to the same geopolitical risks and cannot be considered immune from regional tensions."

The analysis concludes that while such proposals "may seem attractive at the theoretical level," they face "serious ambiguities from an executive and security perspective" and cannot be considered "a definitive solution for replacing existing strategic routes."

## Historical Context and Regional Dynamics

The current proposal exists within a complex historical and geopolitical context. The Eilat-Ashkelon pipeline itself symbolizes shifting alliances—built as an Israel-Iran partnership before the 1979 Islamic Revolution, it now represents potential cooperation between Israel and Gulf states against Iranian influence.

Regional analysts have noted that successful implementation would require unprecedented cooperation between Israel and Saudi Arabia, two nations that have moved toward normalization in recent years but still face significant political obstacles. The proposal also intersects with other ambitious infrastructure concepts, including the long-discussed Ben Gurion Canal project—a proposed 293-kilometer canal from the Gulf of Aqaba to the Mediterranean with an estimated cost of $200 billion, which remains largely a "proposal on paper," according to infrastructure development analyses.

## Geopolitical Implications

The debate over Hormuz bypass routes reflects broader shifts in Middle Eastern geopolitics. For Israel, successfully positioning itself as an energy transit hub between Asia and Europe would represent both an economic opportunity and a strategic achievement, reducing regional dependence on Iranian-controlled chokepoints.

For Iran, the proposal represents a direct challenge to one of its key strategic advantages. Control over the Strait of Hormuz has long provided Tehran with leverage in regional disputes and negotiations. Successful implementation of alternative routes would diminish this leverage while potentially strengthening ties between Israel and Gulf states.

For global energy markets, the discussion highlights growing concerns about supply security in an increasingly volatile region. Even partial implementation of bypass routes could begin to reshape global energy transit patterns, though experts caution that the Strait of Hormuz will likely remain critical for the foreseeable future given the sheer volume of oil that transits through it.

## Looking Ahead

In the short term, continued disruption in the Strait of Hormuz is likely to keep alternative route discussions prominent in energy security debates. Diplomatic efforts to resolve the current blockade and clear naval mines will proceed alongside technical and feasibility studies for pipeline connections and other infrastructure projects.

Medium-term prospects depend heavily on regional political developments, particularly the evolution of Israel-Gulf state relations and the resolution of broader Iran-West tensions. Environmental impact assessments for the sensitive Gulf of Eilat region will also play a crucial role in determining whether expanded infrastructure projects can proceed.

Longer-term, the vision of an integrated Red Sea-Mediterranean energy corridor represents both a significant technical challenge and a potential geopolitical realignment. While Iranian skepticism highlights real obstacles, the strategic imperative to secure energy supplies may drive renewed interest in overcoming them.

As global energy markets seek stability amid regional tensions, Israel's potential role as an energy transit corridor between Asia and Europe represents both an economic opportunity and a strategic repositioning in Middle Eastern geopolitics. Whether Netanyahu's proposal moves beyond theoretical discussion to practical implementation will depend on navigating the very challenges that Iranian analysis has highlighted—technical feasibility, financial viability, and the complex security dynamics of a volatile region.
