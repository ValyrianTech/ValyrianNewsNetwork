# Two-Month War Could Trigger Energy Market Catastrophe

An Iranian economic analysis warns that if the ongoing Iran-Israel conflict continues for two months, it could trigger a catastrophic scenario in global energy markets, potentially pushing oil prices to $200 per barrel and creating severe worldwide economic consequences. The analysis, published by [Tasnim News Agency's "Tafsir Jang" (War Interpretation) group](https://www.tasnimnews.ir/fa/news/1405/01/12/3553975/2-month-war-energy-market-catastrophe), projects that a 60-day continuation of current conflict conditions would create a cumulative oil deficit of approximately 150 million barrels.

## Context and Current Market Situation

The warning comes amid volatile energy markets that have already seen Brent crude prices surge approximately 60% since the conflict began on February 28, 2026. According to the International Energy Agency, the current situation represents the "largest supply disruption in history of global oil market." Markets experienced a brief rally on April 1 after U.S. President Donald Trump stated the war would end in "two to three weeks," causing Brent crude to drop 15% to $99.78 per barrel. However, the Iranian analysis suggests this optimism may be premature if the conflict persists.

The analysis examines the war's progression from its inception through key developments including Iran's closure of the Strait of Hormuz on March 4, QatarEnergy's force majeure declaration on LNG exports, and Houthi entry into the conflict in late March. Each escalation has compounded energy market disruptions in a region responsible for approximately 20% of global oil supply.

## Quantifying the Energy Market Disruption

According to the detailed analysis, normal Strait of Hormuz flow accounts for approximately 20 million barrels per day. Current disruptions affect about 16 million barrels daily after accounting for alternative routes, with Iran's exports reduced by ~2 million barrels/day and Saudi Arabia's exports affected by 4-5 million barrels/day via alternative pathways. This creates a current daily deficit of approximately 2.5 million barrels.

Several mitigating factors are already in play, including global refining capacity reductions of ~2.2 million barrels/day due to periodic maintenance, strategic reserve releases of 2-3 million barrels/day by various countries, and floating storage supply of ~700,000 barrels/day. Despite these measures, the analysis identifies day 61 as a critical threshold where cumulative deficits reach ~150 million barrels and G7 strategic reserves become insufficient to compensate.

## Cascade Effects Beyond Oil Markets

The analysis warns of broader economic impacts extending well beyond crude oil prices. Approximately 50% of global urea and sulfur fertilizer exports transit the Strait of Hormuz, meaning prolonged disruptions could threaten global food security through fertilizer price increases and subsequent crop yield reductions. Middle Eastern airlines have already been largely grounded, with global flight paths significantly disrupted.

Regional economic stability faces particular threats, with the Gulf Cooperation Council economic model potentially facing "systemic collapse." By mid-March, approximately 70% of the region's food imports were already disrupted, while attacks on desalination plants threaten drinking water supplies. Major aviation carriers including Emirates and Qatar Airways face what the analysis describes as "near-total cessation" of operations.

## Market Psychology Versus Physical Reality

A key insight from the analysis highlights the divergence between market psychology and physical supply realities. While markets have reacted optimistically to political statements about imminent conflict resolution, physical supply disruptions will continue for months even if active hostilities cease. The Strait of Hormuz closure and associated infrastructure damage require time to repair, and shipping rerouting via the Cape of Good Hope adds significant costs and delays.

Emma Wall, Chief Investment Strategist at Hargreaves Lansdown, noted this disconnect, stating: "Trump's comments suggest that the US will call victory soon and remove their presence from the region, despite no deal being reached with Iran. While this is expected to provide an immediate boost to stocks, energy disruption would continue for some months, and likely impact both inflation and economic growth."

## Historical Context and Geopolitical Implications

The current crisis finds historical parallels in the 1973 oil crisis, 1979 Iranian Revolution, 1990 Gulf War, and 2022 Russia-Ukraine conflict, though analysts suggest the current disruption may be more severe due to the Middle East's central role in global energy markets. The region was already facing economic challenges pre-conflict, with Iran under severe sanctions and global inflation elevated.

Geopolitically, the analysis suggests that "a war of attrition and its continuation, more than benefiting Western parties, could put additional pressure on the global economy and fundamentally change the equations." This perspective positions the warning as both economic analysis and potential political messaging about the costs of prolonged conflict.

## Outlook and Critical Uncertainties

The analysis presents two primary scenarios for the coming months. If the conflict continues for two months, a catastrophic scenario with $200 oil becomes possible. Even if hostilities end sooner, gradual recovery will still face months of supply disruptions. Both scenarios likely necessitate global economic growth downgrades.

Several critical uncertainties remain unresolved, including the accuracy of the 2.5 million barrel/day deficit calculations, actual remaining capacities of G7 strategic reserves, and how major energy importers like China and India will respond to prolonged shortages. Diplomatic efforts to reopen the Strait of Hormuz also remain unclear.

## Conclusion

The Iranian analysis represents a significant warning about the fragile interdependence of global energy markets and the disproportionate impact Middle Eastern conflicts can have on worldwide economic stability. While markets may respond to short-term political developments, the physical realities of energy infrastructure and supply chains suggest that significant challenges will persist regardless of when active hostilities cease.

As the situation develops, key indicators to monitor include the actual duration of conflict beyond the "2-3 week" projection, progress in reopening the Strait of Hormuz, depletion rates of strategic petroleum reserves, secondary effects on fertilizer and food prices, and diplomatic efforts toward sustainable resolution. The analysis underscores how regional conflicts can rapidly escalate into global economic crises in today's interconnected world, with energy markets serving as the primary transmission mechanism for such shocks.
