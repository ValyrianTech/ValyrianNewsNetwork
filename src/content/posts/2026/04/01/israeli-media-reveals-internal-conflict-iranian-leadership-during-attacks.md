# Israeli Media Reveals Iran Leadership Conflict in Attacks

Israeli media has reportedly obtained detailed information about significant internal conflicts within Iran's leadership during recent US-Israeli attacks, revealing deep divisions between civilian officials and military commanders as the country faces economic collapse and mounting military pressure.

According to a report by Israel's Channel 14, detailed by [Euronews Persian](https://parsi.euronews.com/2026/04/01/israeli-leak-iran-leadership-internal-conflict-irgc-bunker-march-2026), heated disagreements erupted between President Masoud Pezeshkian, Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Qalibaf, and Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) Commander-in-Chief Ahmad Vahidi while they sheltered in bunkers during sustained attacks in March 2026. The revelations suggest the IRGC has effectively seized control of the country, sidelining both reformist politicians and senior clerics.

## Context: War and Economic Crisis

The conflicts occurred against the backdrop of an ongoing US-Israel war on Iran that began February 28, 2026. According to [Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/3/31/us-israel-launch-major-attacks-as-iranian-authorities-maintain-defiance), Iranian authorities report more than 2,000 people killed in strikes, with extensive damage to civilian infrastructure including pharmaceutical plants, steel manufacturers, and universities. The economic situation has deteriorated rapidly, with inflation reaching 105-115% for basic necessities and widespread reports of unpaid salaries for security forces.

## Civilian Leadership Pushes for Negotiations

The Channel 14 report, as detailed by Euronews Persian, reveals that both Pezeshkian and Qalibaf have been pushing for negotiations with the United States to prevent total economic collapse. According to the leaked accounts, Qalibaf—a former IRGC commander himself—reportedly told IRGC commanders during a bunker meeting: "You have taken all the money and now your soldiers are suffering because there are no salaries. Return what you have stolen."

Qalibaf allegedly expressed deep skepticism about the IRGC's military capabilities, stating: "I don't trust you to stop the Americans at Khark and Hormuz." The parliament speaker reportedly believes continued war will lead to complete loss of IRGC control over Iran, while military commanders maintain they can withstand the pressure despite heavy costs.

## Pezeshkian's Frustration with IRGC Control

President Pezeshkian, elected in 2025 as a reformist candidate, appears particularly frustrated with his limited authority. According to the leaked accounts, he told Vahidi: "You run the country like suicide bombers. We have no chance to improve the situation. If this situation continues for another three weeks, no matter what we do, the economy will collapse."

The president reportedly added: "The people of Iran voted for me by a large margin because they wanted hope, because they wanted to endure. What you are doing is exactly the opposite. You are nullifying the will of the people." Pezeshkian allegedly described himself as feeling like a hostage, stating: "You don't let me resign and you don't let me do anything. My only role is to read the ridiculous lines you dictate."

## IRGC Consolidates Power

Multiple sources indicate the IRGC has effectively seized power in Iran following the attacks. According to the Channel 14 report, the IRGC now controls decision-making, manages communications with the new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei (whose status remains unclear), and dominates the economy. Senior clerics have reportedly been sidelined, with some allegedly saying: "This is not the Islamic Republic we knew."

The IRGC's consolidation of power appears systematic. Iran International previously reported that the IRGC pressured Pezeshkian to appoint Mohammad Bagher Zolghadr as Secretary of the Supreme National Security Council, effectively imposing their preferred candidate on the civilian government.

## Economic Pressure as Driving Factor

All sources highlight economic collapse as the central concern driving civilian leadership's push for negotiations. Pezeshkian reportedly warned that without a ceasefire, Iran's economy could face total collapse within three to four weeks. Qalibaf's concerns reportedly stem from information about increasing desertions among security forces due to unpaid salaries.

Iran's banking system shows signs of failure, with ATMs frequently empty or non-functional and online banking disrupted. The currency has collapsed in value, exacerbating already severe inflation that was affecting basic necessities even before the war began.

## Analysis: Credibility and Implications

The Channel 14 report gains credibility through corroboration by Al Jazeera, which independently confirmed that the Israeli outlet reported on Pezeshkian seeking negotiating powers. The narrative also aligns with patterns previously reported by Iran International about disagreements between Pezeshkian and Vahidi.

However, the specific dramatic quotes attributed to Iranian leaders cannot be independently verified and may contain elements of psychological warfare, given the ongoing information conflict between Iran and Israel. Channel 14 is a conservative Israeli outlet with potential intelligence connections, raising questions about sourcing and potential bias.

### Short-term Implications

1. **Negotiation Posture**: Internal conflicts may weaken Iran's bargaining position in any potential ceasefire talks.
2. **Command Cohesion**: Divided leadership could impact military effectiveness and decision-making during critical moments.
3. **Domestic Stability**: Economic collapse combined with leadership fractures could lead to civil unrest.

### Long-term Implications

1. **Regime Transformation**: The IRGC's apparent power grab suggests a potential shift from the Islamic Republic's unique clerical-military balance toward military dictatorship.
2. **Succession Crisis**: Uncertainty about Mojtaba Khamenei's status and authority creates a legitimacy vacuum.
3. **Regional Impact**: Internal conflicts could affect Iran's coordination with regional proxy networks in Lebanon, Syria, Yemen, and Iraq.

## What's Next

The revelations come as US President Donald Trump continues to threaten escalation, including potential ground attacks on strategic Iranian islands. The IRGC has responded with defiance, with spokesman Ebrahim Zolfaghari stating that US desires to control the Strait of Hormuz are "a wish they will take forever to the grave."

As the conflict enters its second month, the internal divisions within Iran's leadership may prove as significant as the external military pressure. Whether civilian leaders can reassert authority or whether the IRGC will consolidate its control remains uncertain, but the reported conflicts suggest fundamental challenges to Iran's political structure during its most severe crisis in decades.

The situation continues to develop, with international observers watching for signs of whether internal pressures might force Iran toward negotiations or whether the IRGC's consolidation of power will lead to more aggressive resistance against US-Israeli demands.
