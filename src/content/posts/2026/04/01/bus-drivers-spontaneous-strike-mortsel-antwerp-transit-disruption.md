# Bus Drivers Strike in Mortsel, Disrupting Antwerp Transit

Bus drivers at De Lijn's Mortsel depot launched a spontaneous strike on Wednesday, April 1, 2026, causing major disruptions to public transportation throughout the Antwerp region. According to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/01/spontane-staking-stelplaats-mortsel/), approximately 90% of buses from the critical Mortsel facility returned to the depot, severely impacting both city and regional bus services. The sudden labor action, which began in the late morning, forced transit authorities to cancel numerous routes and implement emergency contingency plans.

## Context and Background

The Mortsel depot is a major transportation hub serving both Antwerp city and the broader regional network. The strike occurred against a backdrop of ongoing austerity measures at De Lijn, where unions have staged multiple protests in recent weeks against government-imposed budget cuts. This spontaneous action highlights growing tensions between public transport workers and management over working conditions and service reductions.

## Key Developments

De Lijn spokesperson Jens Van Herp confirmed the scale of the disruption, stating that "the willingness to strike is very high" and noting that "90 percent of the buses from the Mortsel depot have returned." He emphasized that "the impact on bus availability is significant" and explained that because it was "not a planned action, we couldn't arrange alternative service in advance."

The strike was triggered by the cancellation of staff transport services due to budget cuts. According to Geert Vermunicht, secretary of the socialist union ACOD, the elimination of Line 31—a shuttle bus that transports drivers from Berchem station to the Mortsel depot—was a primary grievance. "That bus is being eliminated," Vermunicht said, adding that drivers would now need to use personal transport to reach their duty start points.

Additional frustration stemmed from the cancellation of staff transport to a relief point in Hoboken. "So drivers have to use their own means and own transport to get to the relief points," Vermunicht explained. "That news hit like a bomb."

## Service Impact and Resolution

The spontaneous nature of the strike meant De Lijn could not arrange alternative transportation services. Cancelled trips were marked in the De Lijn app and website, while trams continued to operate normally and buses operated by subcontractors remained in service.

Following afternoon negotiations between unions and De Lijn management, drivers agreed to return to work on Thursday, April 2, with normal service expected to resume. Management indicated they would review the staff transport issues, with further negotiations scheduled after the Easter holiday.

## Analysis and Implications

This strike represents more than a temporary disruption—it highlights systemic challenges facing public transportation in Flanders. The conflict between necessary budget constraints and essential worker accommodations has created a volatile labor environment at De Lijn.

The incident also raises questions about contingency planning for spontaneous labor actions. Unlike planned strikes, spontaneous walkouts leave little time for implementing alternative service arrangements, disproportionately affecting commuters who rely on public transportation.

## What's Next

While service is expected to resume Thursday, the underlying issues remain unresolved. Further negotiations between unions and De Lijn management are scheduled after the Easter holiday, with the outcome likely to influence labor relations at the transit authority.

Notably, De Lijn's website lists additional planned strikes across Flanders in early April, including a provincial strike in Antwerp on Thursday, April 9, which could affect the Mortsel region again. These scheduled actions suggest that without sustainable solutions to the staff transport and broader labor concerns, further disruptions may be inevitable.

The Mortsel strike serves as a reminder of the delicate balance between fiscal responsibility and maintaining reliable public transportation services—a challenge facing transit authorities across Europe as they navigate post-pandemic financial realities while meeting both employee needs and public expectations.
