# Antwerp Port Shipping Resumes After Oil Spill

Shipping traffic has partially resumed in Europe's second-largest port following an oil pollution incident, though container terminals remain closed as intensive cleanup operations continue in Antwerp's Waaslandhaven area. The spill, which occurred during a bunkering operation Thursday night, threatens protected bird breeding grounds along the Scheldt River estuary.

According to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/11/waaslandhaven-antwerpen-vervuiling-opruiming-scheepvaartverkeer/), the situation is "cautiously improving" after a night of intensive cleaning operations. Smaller vessels can now navigate the port, but the large container terminals remain shuttered, with full operational capacity not expected until Sunday.

## Context and Background

The Port of Antwerp-Bruges is Europe's second-largest port after Rotterdam, covering 12,068 hectares and serving as a critical gateway for international commerce. The incident marks the second serious bunkering accident in two years at the same Deurganck Dock location, raising questions about prevention measures in this environmentally sensitive area.

The Scheldt River estuary contains protected nature areas (Natura 2000 sites) including Galgenschoor, Oude Doelpolder, and Hertogin Hedwigepolder—important bird breeding and feeding grounds. As De Standaard reports, these areas are now at risk from the spreading oil.

## Key Developments and Cleanup Operations

The oil spill occurred during refueling operations of the container ship MSC Denmark VI in the Deurganck Dock on Thursday night, April 9-10. A crack in the hull caused the leak, which was quickly stopped but allowed oil to spread toward the Scheldt River overnight.

Initial response saw the Scheldt fully closed to traffic between buoy 80 and the Deurganck Dock entrance, with Zandvliet and Berendrecht locks also shut due to oil slicks. At peak disruption, approximately 70 ships were waiting to enter or leave the port.

The [Port of Antwerp-Bruges crisis update](https://www.portofantwerpbruges.com/en/news/crisis-update-oil-spill-deurganckdock) details the gradual reopening: by Saturday morning, the Berendrecht lock had reopened overnight, and by 14:00, the Zandvliet lock resumed operations—meaning all locks on the right bank were functional. However, the Deurganck Dock itself remains closed with cleanup operations focusing on the dock, Noordzee and Europaterminal, and Berendrecht-Zandvliet lock complex.

Specialized vessels and Civil Protection teams continue containment and cleanup efforts. "We regret the impact of this incident on our port and the vulnerable nature areas along the Scheldt," stated the port authority. "Protecting our environment is an absolute priority, and we are doing everything possible to minimise both operational and ecological damage."

## Environmental Impact and Concerns

The ecological consequences remain uncertain but potentially severe. A 4,000-hectare breeding ground for tens of thousands of birds is threatened, with oil-contaminated seagulls already found and sent to Vogelopvangcentrum Oostende bird rescue center.

"We have no idea how big the disaster could become," said Lieven Nachtergaele of the Agency for Nature and Forest in the VRT NWS report. "If the pollution mainly goes downstream, it will be manageable. If it enters those nature areas, a breeding ground of tens of thousands of birds is threatened."

Conflicting reports exist about the extent of contamination. While initial reports suggested significant oil reached nature areas, Jeroen Denaeghel, spokesperson for the Agency for Nature and Forest, told De Standaard that "no oil has washed ashore in the Doelpolder, that would technically be impossible."

Additional concern comes from the approaching spring tide in three days, which could spread remaining oil residues across the entire polder zone. "When their feathers become contaminated, they lose their water-repellent properties, become soaked and hypothermic, which in the worst case can lead to death," explained Jef Van De Wiele, chairman of Natuurpunt Waasland.

## Economic and Regulatory Implications

The economic impact on one of Europe's most critical logistics hubs is significant. The [Belga News Agency](https://www.belganewsagency.eu/oil-spill-brings-port-of-antwerp-traffic-to-a-halt) reported that "the impact is significant and it is unclear how long this will last." Container terminals remain closed, affecting cargo operations throughout European supply chains.

Flemish Minister of Environment Jo Brouns emphasized the "polluter pays" principle during a site visit. "The polluter will pay. You are responsible for the damage you cause," he stated in the VRT NWS coverage.

Political scrutiny is mounting, particularly given this is the second incident in two years. "This is the second serious bunkering incident in two years in the same dock," noted Ilse van Dienderen, Groen (Green Party) municipal council member. "We want to know which structural adjustments have been made since then and whether an updated emergency plan was ready."

International maritime publication [The Maritime Executive](https://maritime-executive.com/article/oil-spill-while-bunkering-containership-disrupts-port-of-antwerp) provided technical details, noting that "Antwerp is Europe's second busiest port, and the Duerganck is home to the single largest container terminal in Europe, operated by PSA." The publication also recalled a similar incident in June 2024 that took several days to resolve.

## What's Next

The Port of Antwerp-Bruges expects full operational capacity to return by Sunday, April 12. However, the environmental assessment and potential long-term ecological monitoring will continue beyond the immediate cleanup.

Key unanswered questions remain: the exact volume of oil spilled, specific preventive measures implemented since the previous incident two years ago, and the total economic impact of the port disruption. Authorities continue to investigate the exact cause of the hull crack that led to the spill.

As cleanup operations proceed through the weekend, the focus remains on minimizing environmental damage to the Scheldt's protected ecosystems while restoring normal operations at one of Europe's most vital economic arteries. The incident underscores the ongoing challenge of balancing industrial activity with environmental protection in sensitive coastal areas.
