# March Inflation Hits 3.3%, Highest in Nearly 2 Years

Consumer prices surged 3.3% in March from a year ago, marking the highest inflation level in nearly two years as the U.S. war with Iran triggered a sharp spike in energy costs, according to [Labor Department data released Friday](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/pdf/cpi.pdf). The monthly increase of 0.9% was the largest in nearly four years, with gasoline prices accounting for nearly three-quarters of the overall rise and creating significant challenges for the White House and Federal Reserve.

## Context: From Stabilization to Shock

Inflation had been gradually declining from pandemic-era highs, reaching 2.4% in February after peaking at 9.1% in June 2022 following Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Progress on stabilizing prices had fizzled out in 2025, partly due to President Trump's tariffs, but the Iran war has dramatically reversed the trend. "We were making progress, making progress. Then we kind of stalled out and now it's been inching itself up the other way," Chicago Federal Reserve Bank President Austan Goolsbee [told the Detroit Economic Club this week](https://www.npr.org/2026/04/10/nx-s1-5780604/inflation-consumer-prices-economy).

The geopolitical trigger came in late March when the U.S. and Israel launched an attack on Iran, prompting Tehran to block the Strait of Hormuz—a critical chokepoint through which 20% of the world's oil passes. Despite a tentative ceasefire announced in April, the strategic situation in the strait remains unchanged, keeping energy markets volatile.

## Key Developments: Energy Prices Dominate

The March Consumer Price Index report revealed the war's immediate economic impact. Gasoline prices jumped 21.2% in March alone, with the national average reaching $4.15 per gallon on Friday—up nearly 40% from $2.98 before the war began, according to [motor club AAA data cited by AP News](https://apnews.com/article/inflation-prices-gas-federal-reserve-trump-bf00c3105d5da88a0b01d9107ed4ecee). Energy prices overall rose 10.9% for the month.

The ripple effects extended beyond the pump. Airline fares jumped 2.7% in March and are 14.9% higher than a year ago as carriers passed on higher jet fuel costs. Delivery services including UPS and FedEx have announced fuel surcharges, raising shipping costs for businesses and households. Clothing costs rose 1% monthly and are up 3.4% year-over-year, while used car prices fell 0.4%.

Core inflation, which excludes volatile food and energy prices, rose 2.6% year-over-year, up from 2.5% in February. The modest 0.2% monthly increase in core prices suggests rising gas prices haven't yet spread widely to other categories, but economists warn of potential spillover effects.

## Consumer and Business Impact

Consumer sentiment plunged to a record low in April, with the University of Michigan's Index of Consumer Sentiment falling to 47.6 from 53.3 in March. "Many consumers blame the Iran conflict for unfavorable changes to the economy," said Joanne Hsu, the university's director of consumer surveys, in the AP News report.

Businesses are feeling the pinch. Kyle LaFond, founder of American Provenance, a small manufacturer of personal care products near Madison, Wisconsin, told AP News his shipping costs have risen 30-40%. After absorbing tariff costs for months and raising prices 20-30% last September—the company's first increase since 2021—he may need to raise prices again if the fuel price spike continues. "I'd really hate to do that because that would be two years of consecutive price increases, which for us, we've never done that before," LaFond said. "But for the business to survive, then that might be necessary."

Grocery prices slipped 0.2% in March but are expected to rise as diesel fuel costs surge through the food supply chain. "As energy prices increase, the costs associated with producing and delivering food also rise," said Andy Harig, vice president at FMI-The Food Industry Association.

## Analysis: Different from 2022, But Still Painful

Economists note important differences between the current inflation spike and the 2022 surge. "That's where this really differs, is that we aren't seeing anywhere near the strength of demand," Alan Detmeister, an economist at UBS, told AP News. In 2021 and 2022, income growth "was increasing really strongly. We aren't seeing that now."

The job market shows mixed signals: employers added 178,000 jobs in March after cutting workers in February, but the overall labor market remains weaker than during the pandemic recovery. There are no large government stimulus checks spurring demand this time.

Michael Pearce, chief U.S. economist at Oxford Economics, described the current situation as "a short, sharp shock" compared to 2022. "It's painful in the near term," Pearce said. "It's going to get more painful in April" when further gas price increases will lift inflation higher.

## Federal Reserve and Political Implications

The inflation surge almost certainly postpones Federal Reserve interest rate cuts for months. Fed officials had projected one quarter-point cut for the year at their March 18 meeting, but will now likely delay any easing. The central bank faces a difficult balancing act between inflationary pressures from the war and a weakening job market.

Politically, the report represents the worst inflation reading of President Trump's second term, coming as the administration has staked much of its economic credibility on bringing costs down. The White House has framed the inflation surge as a "short-term disruption," with spokesman Kush Desai telling The New York Times that "the American economy remains on a solid trajectory thanks to the Administration's robust supply-side agenda of tax cuts, deregulation, and energy abundance."

However, the spike could have electoral consequences. An AP-NORC Center for Public Affairs Research poll last month found about six in 10 Republicans are at least "somewhat" concerned about affording gas in the coming months. The inflation surge could make it more difficult for the president's party to hold House and Senate seats in November's midterm elections.

## What's Next

The critical uncertainty is how long the energy price shock will last and whether it will lead to broader, persistent inflation. While economists believe a widespread increase similar to 2022 is unlikely, the duration depends on geopolitical resolution in the Strait of Hormuz.

Goolsbee expressed concern about inflation expectations becoming entrenched: "I think it's from uncertainty. I think that's what happens when businesses are uncertain and they say we're just going to sit on our hands until we figure out, is the war going to be a temporary shock?"

For now, Americans face continued pressure at the pump and potential spillover to other prices, while the Federal Reserve watches closely to determine whether this represents a temporary disruption or a more fundamental shift in the inflation trajectory.
