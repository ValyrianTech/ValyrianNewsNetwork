# Women Got 94% of New Jobs, Health Care Drives Growth

Women have received a staggering 94.3% of all new jobs created in the United States over the past year, according to Labor Department data analyzed by [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/04/10/nx-s1-5773327/women-men-jobs-health-care-manufacturing). Of the 369,000 jobs created since the start of President Trump's second term, 348,000 went to women while only 21,000 went to men—a nearly 17:1 ratio that highlights dramatic shifts in gender employment dynamics. The overwhelming driver has been growth in the health care sector, where women hold nearly 80% of positions and which added 390,000 jobs in the past 12 months alone.

## Historical Context of Gender Employment Trends

This unprecedented gender imbalance represents more than a statistical anomaly—it marks the culmination of decades-long structural changes in the U.S. economy. For the first time in American history outside of recession periods, women now hold more jobs than men in the nonfarm economy. According to an [Indeed Hiring Lab analysis](https://www.hiringlab.org/2026/03/26/how-women-have-closed-the-workforce-gender-gap/), the male-female employment gap that stood at nearly 7 million jobs in the early 1990s has now completely closed, with women holding slightly more jobs as of early 2026.

The shift reflects fundamental economic transformation: the U.S. has moved from a manufacturing-based economy that employed nearly 30% of workers in the 1950s to a service-based economy where manufacturing now accounts for less than 8% of employment. This structural change disproportionately affects male workers, as manufacturing was historically 75-80% male, while service sector growth favors female-dominated fields like health care and education.

## Sectoral Analysis and Political Context

The health care sector's explosive growth—adding 680,500 jobs from March 2025 to March 2026 according to [Bureau of Labor Statistics data](https://www.bls.gov/opub/ted/2026/health-care-and-social-assistance-employment-increased-by-2-9-percent-or-680500-from-march-2025-to-march-2026.htm)—has far exceeded overall economic expansion, making up for job losses in male-dominated sectors. Manufacturing employment decreased by 0.6% (75,000 jobs) over the same period, while transportation and warehousing fell by 1.8% (121,200 jobs) and information sector employment dropped by 2.7% (76,000 jobs).

This reality contrasts sharply with the Trump administration's economic focus. "We have seen a year of a president absolutely fixated on growing the manufacturing sector," says Betsey Stevenson, professor of public policy and economics at the University of Michigan. "There's not enough of those jobs for men as a whole to thrive." The White House pointed to March's addition of 15,000 manufacturing jobs as evidence that "the best days for American workers, manufacturers, and families are still ahead," despite the sector remaining down 82,000 jobs from when Trump took office.

Labor Department spokesperson Courtney Parella cautioned that raw job counts present a "misleading snapshot," stating that "under President Trump's leadership, we're creating opportunities across a multitude of industries, including mortgage-paying manufacturing jobs. Both men and women are benefitting from a strong economy."

## Expert Analysis and Workforce Implications

"This is happening at a time where it's become verboten to talk about diversity, equity and inclusion," Stevenson told NPR. "And yet the people we need to be talking about right now are men." She notes that men are more likely than women to have occupational identity tied to particular fields, making transitions to female-dominated sectors psychologically challenging.

Richard Reeves, president of the American Institute for Boys and Men, sees the trend as unsurprising given long-term patterns. "There is no cause for panic here," he says. "But I do think we should be alert to signs that the labor market might be moving even more quickly in directions that are leaving too many men behind." Reeves advocates for policies and programs to draw male workers into growing fields like nursing, teaching, and social work, similar to efforts that successfully increased female representation in STEM fields.

The gender gap in labor force participation rates tells a parallel story of structural change. In 1948, men's labor force participation rate exceeded women's by 54.7 percentage points. By 1990, that gap had narrowed to about 19 percentage points, and today stands at just 10 percentage points—the lowest recorded level in U.S. history, reached in February 2026.

## Challenges and Policy Considerations

Stevenson suggests reframing growing sectors to appeal to male workers. "I think there are ways for us to talk about those jobs as being particularly masculine," she says, noting that health care jobs could be framed as requiring strength to lift patients, while preschools could highlight the need for positive male role models. "I know some people will be offended by such gender stereotyping," she acknowledges. "But I do want to encourage us to realize that we have to help men understand that they can do caregiving roles and stay masculine."

The employment shift has significant implications for workforce development, economic policy, and gender dynamics. For employers, talent pipelines are shifting toward an increasingly female workforce, requiring adaptation in recruitment strategies and workplace cultures. For workers, the transition means different wage patterns—female-dominated sectors often pay less than traditional male jobs—and different career mobility paths.

## Looking Ahead

The data suggests these trends are structural rather than cyclical, driven by demographic aging (increasing demand for health care), educational attainment gaps (women now earn 60% of bachelor's degrees), and long-term sectoral shifts in the economy. As the Indeed Hiring Lab analysis concludes: "The gender gap in employment is not disappearing because women have simply entered the workforce in greater numbers. It is disappearing because men are participating less, and that distinction matters enormously for how employers, policymakers, and workforce planners should be thinking about the decade ahead."

Successful economic policy will need to address these structural realities rather than nostalgic visions of economic past. As Stevenson noted in a 2016 op-ed that remains relevant today, the solution may involve "making girly jobs appeal to manly men"—not through regression to gender stereotypes, but through thoughtful reimagining of how care work is valued and presented in American society.
