# Ukrainian Drones Hit Russian Oil Depots in Tver, Krasnodar

Ukrainian drones targeted Russian oil infrastructure in coordinated overnight attacks, hitting depots in Tver and a key pipeline station in Krasnodar Krai for the second time in a week, according to Ukrainian officials. The strikes represent a continued escalation of Kyiv's campaign to disrupt Moscow's war financing capabilities by attacking energy export facilities deep inside Russian territory.

## Context: Ukraine's Economic Warfare Strategy

Since 2024, Ukraine has increasingly targeted Russian energy infrastructure, particularly oil refineries and export terminals, as part of a strategic effort to reduce Moscow's revenue from oil exports which fund its war effort. As [Al Jazeera reported](https://www.aljazeera.com/features/2026/4/6/smell-of-war-comes-to-st-petersburg-as-ukraine-hammers-russian-refineries), these attacks aim to "drain Russia's war chest" by striking facilities that handle significant portions of Moscow's seaborne oil exports. The campaign has expanded in range and frequency, with drones now capable of reaching targets over 1,000 kilometers from Ukrainian territory.

## Key Developments in Overnight Attacks

According to [Andriy Kovalenko](https://www.ukrinform.ua/rubric-ato/4111409-u-rosii-atakovani-naftobazi-v-tveri-ta-naftoprovidna-stancia-u-krasnodarskomu-krai-cpd.html), head of the Center for Countering Disinformation at Ukraine's National Security and Defense Council, "Russia was under attack at night. Hits in the city of Tver. There, in particular, oil depots are located." Kovalenko added via Telegram that in Krasnodar Krai, Ukrainian forces attacked the "Krymskaya" oil pipeline station - "it's being finished off for the second time in a week."

The attacks occurred between 20:00 April 10 and 07:00 April 11 Moscow Time, targeting multiple locations. In Krasnodar Krai, [Ukrainska Pravda reported](https://www.pravda.com.ua/news/2026/04/11/8029734/) that drone debris caused a fire at an oil depot in Krymsk, which local authorities extinguished using 29 units of equipment by morning. The report noted that drone debris also damaged apartment buildings and fell on a warehouse of an unknown enterprise.

The Tver attack, approximately 400 kilometers from the Ukrainian border, demonstrates Ukraine's extended drone capabilities. While specific damage details from Tver remain unclear, the strike on oil depots in the city northwest of Moscow represents one of the deeper penetrations of Ukrainian drones into Russian territory to date.

## Russian Response and Conflicting Narratives

The Russian Ministry of Defense claimed its air defense systems intercepted and destroyed 99 Ukrainian fixed-wing unmanned aerial vehicles overnight. According to [Caliber.Az](https://caliber.az/en/post/russia-air-defences-down-99-drones-overnight-across-multiple-regions), the ministry reported drones were shot down over Krasnodar Krai, Belgorod, Bryansk, Rostov, Kaluga, and Kursk regions, occupied Crimea, and over the waters of the Azov and Black Seas. [RIA Novosti similarly reported](https://www.nampa.org/text/22907469) the Russian defense ministry's claim of 99 drones destroyed.

This represents a familiar pattern in the conflict: Ukrainian sources emphasize successful strikes on strategic infrastructure, while Russian sources highlight interception rates while acknowledging some damage occurred. The discrepancy between claimed interceptions and actual damage highlights the challenges of verifying battlefield claims in the conflict.

## Strategic Implications and Analysis

The attacks carry significant strategic implications beyond immediate physical damage. First, they demonstrate Ukraine's growing capability to strike deep inside Russia with precision. The Tver attack, in particular, shows drones can now reach targets approximately 400 kilometers from Ukrainian territory, expanding the range of vulnerable Russian infrastructure.

Second, the repeated targeting of the "Krymskaya" pipeline station—twice in one week—suggests Ukrainian forces are systematically degrading key nodes in Russia's oil transportation network. This facility is part of Russia's pipeline system operated by state-owned Transneft, critical for moving oil to export terminals like Novorossiysk on the Black Sea.

Third, these strikes are part of Ukraine's broader economic warfare strategy. As [UAWire noted](https://uawire.org/ukrainian-drones-strike-oil-facilities-in-russia-s-tver-region-causing-massive-explosions), facilities like Tvernefteprodukt (part of Surgutneftegaz) store and deliver fuel to Russia's military, making them legitimate military targets. By disrupting oil infrastructure, Ukraine aims to increase repair costs, insurance premiums, and shipping risks for Russian oil exports—all of which drain resources that could otherwise fund military operations.

The timing is also significant, coming just days after Ukrainian forces struck oil infrastructure at Novorossiysk terminal on April 6 and conducted a massive drone attack on Krasnodar Krai on April 9. This pattern suggests an intensified campaign against Russian energy targets during this period.

## What's Next: Escalating Energy Infrastructure Campaign

Ukraine's drone campaign against Russian oil infrastructure shows no signs of abating. The demonstrated range extension to Tver suggests even more distant targets may become vulnerable as Ukraine continues to develop its drone capabilities. The repeated attacks on the same Krasnodar facilities indicate a strategy of "finishing off" damaged infrastructure to prevent rapid repair and return to operation.

For Russia, the attacks expose vulnerabilities in air defense coverage and the difficulty of protecting extensive energy infrastructure across vast territory. While Moscow claims high interception rates, the continued damage to facilities suggests some drones are penetrating defenses.

The economic impact will depend on the extent of damage and repair timelines. Temporary disruptions to oil flows could affect Russia's export revenues at a time when Moscow faces increasing financial pressures from sanctions and war expenditures. However, the long-term strategic impact will be determined by whether Ukraine can sustain this campaign and significantly degrade Russia's oil export capacity over time.

As the conflict enters its fifth year, Ukraine's asymmetric warfare tactics—particularly the drone campaign against energy targets—represent a cost-effective means of imposing economic pain on Russia while conserving Ukraine's own military resources. The overnight attacks on Tver and Krasnodar demonstrate this strategy remains a central component of Kyiv's approach to the war.
