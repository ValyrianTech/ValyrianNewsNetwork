# Trump Admin Admits Medicaid Error Amid DHS Shutdown Recall

The Trump administration acknowledged a significant tenfold overestimate in Medicaid cost calculations while simultaneously recalling furloughed Department of Homeland Security employees during an ongoing partial government shutdown, according to federal notices and admissions to news organizations. These developments occur against a backdrop of historical analysis revealing how today's political gridlock contrasts sharply with the bipartisan legislative achievements of the 1990 Congress, which passed landmark bills on environment, disability rights, and immigration with overwhelming support from both parties.

## Context: Expanding Fraud Crackdown and Shutdown Dynamics

The Medicaid error admission comes amid an expanding federal anti-fraud campaign targeting at least five states, including New York, California, Florida, Maine, and Minnesota. In March 2026, President Donald Trump signed an executive order creating an anti-fraud task force led by Vice President JD Vance, which has temporarily halted $243 million in Medicaid funding to Minnesota over fraud concerns—a move now subject to litigation. Meanwhile, the partial government shutdown has persisted for nearly two months, with Congress failing to agree on fiscal 2026 funding for DHS amid Democratic criticism of Trump's immigration crackdown and his use of Immigration and Customs Enforcement agents.

## Medicaid Calculation Error: A Tenfold Overestimate

According to an [AP News exclusive](https://apnews.com/article/new-york-medicaid-fraud-dr-oz-trump-342285a3c5d5b71f36ce3f3c77ec72c5), the Trump administration admitted this week to overestimating New York's Medicaid personal care services utilization by approximately tenfold. Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services Administrator Dr. Mehmet Oz had claimed in a social media video and a letter to New York Governor Kathy Hochul that the state's Medicaid program provided personal care services to 5 million people in 2025—approximately 73% of the state's 6.8 million Medicaid enrollees.

CMS spokesman Chris Krepich acknowledged the error to The Associated Press on April 10, 2026, stating the agency "misidentified New York's approach to applying billing codes and had since refined its methodology." The actual number of New Yorkers who used personal care services last year was about 450,000, or between 6% and 7% of total enrollees.

Despite the error admission, CMS maintains the fraud probe continues due to other concerns about New York's Medicaid oversight, including high per-beneficiary spending and the state's employment of many personal care aides. Michael Kinnucan, senior health policy adviser at the Fiscal Policy Institute, whose analysis called attention to the inaccurate claim, told AP News: "These numbers could have been cleared up in a phone call, so it's really slapdash."

New York officials responded sharply to the admission. Cadence Acquaviva, senior public information officer for the New York Department of Health, called Oz's mischaracterizations "a targeted attempt to obscure the facts." Governor Hochul's spokesperson Nicolette Simmonds added: "The initial claim by CMS was patently false, and we are glad they now admit it."

## DHS Recalls Furloughed Staff Despite Shutdown

Simultaneously, the U.S. Department of Homeland Security has ordered all furloughed employees to return to work despite the ongoing partial government shutdown, [according to Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/legal/litigation/us-dhs-calls-furloughed-staff-back-work-despite-shutdown-2026-04-11/). A notice sent to employees on Friday, April 10, 2026, stated that all currently furloughed employees should return to duty on their next regularly scheduled workday, which for most would be Monday, April 13.

The recall cites President Trump's April 3 emergency order to pay every DHS employee the equivalent compensation and benefits lost during the partial shutdown of the agency. A Trump administration official confirmed that DHS has told all employees to return to work and that Trump's directive from last week orders that they be paid.

DHS has 270,000 employees, most deemed "essential" and required to work during shutdowns. A DHS spokesperson told the Federal News Network that DHS chief Markwayne Mullin "will be utilizing available funding to recall the entire DHS workforce" to get employees back to work, while blaming Democrats for the partial shutdown. The spokesperson said the workforce's "paychecks are now being processed."

## Historical Contrast: The Bipartisan Congress of 1990

These administrative challenges unfold against a stark historical contrast revealed in [Washington Post analysis](https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/2026/04/11/bipartisan-congress-bush-trump-immigration-environment/), which examines how the current broken Congress differs fundamentally from the bipartisan achievements of 1990. During the 1989-90 congressional session under Republican President George H.W. Bush and a Democratic-controlled Congress, four landmark bills passed with overwhelming bipartisan support:

1. **Clean Air Act Amendments** (Senate: 89-10, House: 401-25)
2. **Americans with Disabilities Act** (Senate: 91-6, House: 377-28)
3. **Immigration Act of 1990** (Senate: 89-8, House: 264-118)
4. **Budget Enforcement Act** (more contentious but passed)

Today, 2025 was ranked as the most partisan Congress since 1953, with 85.3% of roll calls being party unity votes—meaning a majority of each party was on opposite sides—compared to 54% in the Senate and 49% in the House in 1990.

The Washington Post analysis notes that the Trump administration is actively undoing key parts of the 1990 legislative legacy through executive actions. Trump has exempted dozens of power plants from having to comply with parts of the Clean Air Act and announced what his administration called the "Single Largest Deregulatory Action in U.S. History," dismantling an Obama administration rule that had regulated global warming emissions as a public health threat.

## Analysis: Connecting Threads of Governance Challenges

These seemingly disparate events—the Medicaid calculation error, the DHS recall during shutdown, and the historical comparison—collectively illustrate significant challenges in contemporary governance:

**1. Administrative Competence and Credibility:** The Medicaid error undermines the administration's anti-fraud campaign credibility and raises questions about data integrity in other state investigations. As health analyst Michael Kinnucan noted, the approach appears "slapdash" and risks politicizing what should be collaborative efforts to address program integrity.

**2. Executive Power Expansion:** The DHS recall using emergency funding during a shutdown without congressional appropriation represents an expansion of executive authority. This move sets a precedent for bypassing traditional appropriations processes during political impasses, potentially altering the balance of power between branches of government.

**3. Legislative Dysfunction:** The contrast with 1990 highlights how today's political environment has shifted from bipartisan legislating to executive action and partisan gridlock. Former Senator Bob Dole, who championed the Americans with Disabilities Act, captured the 1990 ethos when he said: "If somebody is at a two and you are at four, there ought to be some way to get to three. And you settle on three."

**4. Historical Legacy Reversal:** The undoing of 1990 achievements represents a significant policy reversal on environment, disability rights, and immigration. The Trump administration has canceled Temporary Protected Status for people from 13 countries, affecting 1.5 million people, and is dismantling Clean Air Act provisions that had successfully reduced acid rain-causing emissions by 94% from 1990 to 2023.

## Implications and Forward Look

The Medicaid error admission may chill state innovation in Medicaid programs due to fear of federal scrutiny based on potentially faulty data. It also raises questions about how many other state investigations might be based on similar calculation errors.

The DHS recall during shutdown tests the limits of executive authority to fund agencies without congressional approval, potentially establishing new norms for governance during political stalemates.

Most fundamentally, the historical comparison reveals a transformation in American politics from an era where moderate Republicans and conservative Democrats created a large center for compromise to today's environment characterized by safe districts, unlimited political spending following the Citizens United decision, and intense partisan polarization.

As the midterm elections approach, the question remains whether mechanisms exist to revive the bipartisan legislating model of 1990 or whether governance will continue to rely increasingly on executive actions and administrative maneuvers. The events of this week—an admitted administrative error, a controversial shutdown workaround, and the ghost of bipartisan achievements past—collectively underscore the challenges facing American governance in an era of deep political division.
