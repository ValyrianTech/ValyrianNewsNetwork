# Illegal Dumping Closes Street Near A12 in Londerzeel/Meise

Police have closed a street on the border between Londerzeel and Meise in Belgium after discovering a significant illegal dump in the middle of the road. The incident, discovered on Saturday morning, April 11, 2026, marks the latest in a series of waste disposal problems plaguing the area near the A12 highway connecting Brussels and Antwerp.

According to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/11/meise-londerzeel-opnieuw-sluikstort-a12-daders/), the illegal dump was found in Patatestraat, between Kerkhofstraat and the A12 highway. Unlike previous incidents that involved construction waste, this dump consisted of household items including dishes and work materials. The location is on the opposite side of the A12 from where similar incidents have occurred recently.

## Persistent Problem Area

This location has been a persistent trouble spot for illegal dumping. In recent months, construction waste has been dumped four times at nearby locations along the A12. The problem became particularly severe in December 2025 when at least 14 truckloads of construction waste created what local media described as a "garbage mountain" at the Neromhof entrance along the A12 in Wolvertem, part of the municipality of Meise.

As [HLN reported in December 2025](https://www.hln.be/meise/gigantisch-sluikstort-langs-a12-in-meise-minstens-14-vrachtwagens-dumpen-bouwafval~a3a340ee/), the systematic dumping began on December 17, 2025, and continued despite municipal efforts to block access with barriers. "Enige tijd geleden was het verder aan de A12. Toen hebben we blokken gezet, maar dat hielp eerst zelfs niet," said Mayor Gerda Van den Brande (N-VA) at the time. "Uiteindelijk heeft het probleem zich elders verplaatst, zoals we nu zien."

## Current Incident and Response

The latest dumping has forced police to close Patatestraat entirely. The street, which runs alongside crane company Sarens, will remain closed until the waste is removed. Police zone KLM (covering Kapelle-op-den-Bos, Londerzeel, and Meise) is investigating the incident for clues that might identify the perpetrators.

[HLN's coverage of the current incident](https://www.hln.be/meise/politie-moet-patatestraat-afsluiten-door-enorme-sluikstort~a700465a/) notes that the illegal dumping problem in the region continues to persist. "Enkele weken geleden werd er maar liefst vijf keer sluikstort aangetroffen langs de A12 in Wolvertem (Meise)," the report states. While two suspects were identified in those cases, the problem clearly continues.

Once the illegal dump is cleaned up, the road will be reopened. The perpetrators, if identified, will be billed for cleanup costs and face substantial fines. This follows the standard procedure for such incidents, though catching those responsible has proven challenging.

## Proposed Solutions and Challenges

Mayor Gerda Van den Brande has previously called for additional measures against illegal dumping along the highway. The mayors of police zone KLM now want to install additional surveillance cameras to identify and deter future offenders.

"De burgemeesters van politiezone KLM (Kapelle-op-den-Bos, Londerzeel en Meise) willen extra camera's plaatsen om toekomstige daders te identificeren en te ontmoedigen," according to VRT NWS. This represents a shift from reactive cleanup to proactive prevention.

The strategic location near the A12 highway presents particular challenges. The area offers easy access from the major transportation route while providing relative seclusion for illegal activities. As Mayor Van den Brande noted in December, "Als de A12 wordt afgesloten, zal het vanzelf veel moeilijker worden om te sluikstorten. Maar dat zal nog even duren. Ik hoop alvast dat we hen zullen pakken!"

## Broader Implications

The recurring illegal dumping incidents highlight several ongoing challenges in waste management and environmental enforcement:

1. **Economic Factors:** Legal disposal of construction and household waste involves fees that some individuals and businesses seek to avoid, creating financial incentives for illegal dumping.

2. **Enforcement Difficulties:** Remote areas near highways are difficult to monitor continuously, making it challenging to catch perpetrators in the act.

3. **Environmental Impact:** Illegal dumping creates pollution risks and cleanup burdens that ultimately fall on municipalities and taxpayers.

4. **Community Disruption:** Street closures like the current one in Patatestraat disrupt local traffic and access for residents and businesses.

## What Happens Next

The immediate priority is cleaning up the Patatestraat dump and reopening the road to traffic. Police will continue their investigation in hopes of identifying those responsible. Meanwhile, municipal authorities are moving forward with plans to install additional surveillance cameras in problem areas.

The shift from construction waste to household waste in the latest incident suggests the problem may be expanding beyond professional operators to include individual residents. This could indicate either increased enforcement pressure on commercial dumpers or a broader cultural issue with waste disposal.

As Belgium and other European nations work toward circular economy goals and improved waste management, incidents like these highlight the ongoing challenges of enforcement and the need for comprehensive solutions that address both the economic incentives for illegal dumping and the practical difficulties of prevention.

The situation in Londerzeel and Meise serves as a microcosm of waste management challenges facing many communities—balancing accessibility to legal disposal options with effective enforcement against environmental crimes that undermine public spaces and community wellbeing.
