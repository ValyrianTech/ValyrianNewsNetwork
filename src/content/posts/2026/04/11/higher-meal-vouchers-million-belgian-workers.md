# Higher Meal Vouchers for Over 1 Million Belgian Workers

More than one million Belgian employees are receiving higher meal vouchers this year, marking the first increase in the benefit's value since 2016. According to [calculations published by Het Nieuwsblad and reported by RTBF](https://www.rtbf.be/article/plus-d-un-million-de-belges-recoivent-des-cheques-repas-plus-eleves-cette-annee-11708303), approximately 70% of private sector workers in Belgium now benefit from meal vouchers with a new maximum daily value of €10, up from the previous €8 cap. The government-mandated increase, which took effect on 1 January 2026, represents a significant boost to worker compensation within Belgium's constrained wage system.

## Context: Belgium's Meal Voucher System

Meal vouchers ("chèques repas" in French, "maaltijdcheques" in Dutch) are a widespread employee benefit in Belgium, providing tax-advantaged purchasing power for food expenses. The system has become particularly important in recent years as Belgium's strict wage laws have limited direct salary increases. With an estimated market size of €4 billion, the meal voucher program represents a substantial component of worker compensation packages, especially in the private sector where coverage reaches approximately seven out of ten employees.

## Key Developments and Implementation

The increase to €10 per day was formalized through a Royal Decree dated 10 November 2025, published in the Belgian Official Gazette on 17 November 2025. This policy change was part of the federal government's coalition agreement, reflecting an intention to "better reward work" amid economic pressures and inflation.

Implementation occurs through sectoral collective agreements or individual company decisions. The Federal Public Service for Employment (FOD Werkgelegenheid/SPF Emploi) has registered collective agreements for 42 sectors, covering 712,000 employees. However, the actual reach is broader. According to meal voucher issuer Edenred, [agreements have been reached in 130 sectors, benefiting around 950,000 employees](https://www.brusselstimes.com/2072965/over-a-million-workers-get-higher-meal-vouchers), as revealed by MP Anja Vanrobaeys of the Vooruit party.

In 67 sectors—just over half of those with agreements—the increase amounts to the full €2. Additionally, 16 sectors that previously did not provide meal vouchers have now introduced them, expanding the benefit's coverage. Some companies have opted to raise voucher values independently, outside of sector-specific agreements.

## Economic Implications and Analysis

The voucher increase carries multiple economic implications. For employees, it represents enhanced purchasing power for food expenses—a meaningful benefit during periods of inflation. For employers, while costs increase, they benefit from a government decision to double the tax deduction for meal vouchers from €2 to €4. This fiscal measure reduces the net cost to businesses while stimulating consumer spending in participating restaurants and retail establishments.

Labour economist Stijn Baert notes that Belgium's strict wage laws, which have limited salary increases in recent years, have influenced the decision to enhance voucher benefits as an alternative compensation mechanism. The policy effectively allows for increased worker remuneration within the constraints of Belgium's wage moderation system.

The Brussels Times reports that the rise in meal voucher value is partly attributed to the government's decision to increase the tax deduction. This creates a win-win scenario: workers gain tangible benefits, employers receive tax relief, and the government supports consumer spending while working within wage law parameters.

## What's Next: Further Increases and Monitoring

A second increase of €2 is expected later, which would bring the maximum daily value to €12. The timeline for this additional hike remains unspecified, but it signals the government's continued commitment to enhancing worker benefits through alternative compensation channels.

Monitoring implementation across sectors will be important, particularly for ensuring smaller businesses and less organized sectors can participate equitably. The discrepancy between registered agreements (42 sectors) and those reported by Edenred (130 sectors) suggests some administrative lag in formalizing arrangements, though benefits are already flowing to workers.

## Conclusion

The meal voucher increase represents a significant policy achievement affecting over one million Belgian workers. By leveraging an existing benefit system, the government has found a pathway to improve worker compensation despite wage law constraints. The measure demonstrates how targeted fiscal policies can deliver tangible benefits to employees while supporting businesses through tax incentives. As the second planned increase approaches, the meal voucher system will continue to play a crucial role in Belgium's compensation landscape, balancing worker needs with economic realities.
