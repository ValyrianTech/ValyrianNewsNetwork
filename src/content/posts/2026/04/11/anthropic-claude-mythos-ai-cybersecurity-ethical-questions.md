# Anthropic's Claude Mythos AI Raises Cybersecurity Alarms, Ethical Questions

**SAN FRANCISCO** — A powerful new artificial intelligence model developed by Anthropic has triggered urgent warnings from top U.S. government officials to major banks while simultaneously prompting the $380 billion AI company to consult with Christian religious leaders about the moral implications of its technology. The dual developments highlight the unprecedented challenges posed by AI systems whose capabilities are advancing faster than existing security frameworks and ethical guidelines can accommodate.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent and Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell gathered chief executives from Bank of America, Citi, and Wells Fargo for an emergency meeting on Tuesday, warning that Anthropic's new "Claude Mythos Preview" model could pose serious risks to sensitive customer data if allowed to run through bank computer systems. According to [The New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/04/10/business/anthropic-claude-mythos-preview-banks.html), the officials cautioned that the AI might be so effective at finding security weaknesses that hackers could exploit this capability.

The warnings come as Anthropic itself acknowledges the unprecedented risks of its technology. The company announced this week that its latest model can find "high-severity vulnerabilities, including some in every major operating system and web browser," according to [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/04/11/nx-s1-5778508/anthropic-project-glasswing-ai-cybersecurity-mythos-preview). Not only can Mythos Preview identify these vulnerabilities, but it's particularly effective at developing working exploits for them—capabilities so powerful that Anthropic has no plans to release the model to the general public.

## The Cybersecurity Evolution

The emergence of Claude Mythos Preview represents a watershed moment in AI's relationship with cybersecurity. Just months ago, AI models were still prone to "hallucinations"—generating plausible but incorrect information. Now, they've transitioned to becoming remarkably effective at finding security flaws that human experts might miss.

Daniel Stenberg, lead developer of cURL—a 30-year-old open-source data transfer tool used in everything from cars to medical devices—has witnessed this transformation firsthand. "We got 185 [security] reports throughout the year and less than 5% of them were actually security related problems in the end," Stenberg told NPR about 2025. But early in 2026, the quality of reports dramatically improved, with AI-assisted researchers finding legitimate vulnerabilities at an unprecedented rate.

"LLMs have now bypassed human capability for bug finding," said Alex Stamos, chief security officer at Corridor and former head of security at Yahoo and Facebook. The improvement followed the release of Anthropic's Opus 4.5 model in November 2025, marking a turning point in AI's cybersecurity capabilities.

## Project Glasswing: A Containment Strategy

Faced with the dual-use potential of its technology—it can secure software but also weaponize vulnerabilities—Anthropic has launched "Project Glasswing," a coalition of approximately 40-50 select companies and organizations formed to "secure the world's most critical software." Named after a butterfly species with transparent wings, the project includes the Linux Foundation, which maintains the Linux kernel that powers Android and all of the world's 500 most powerful supercomputers.

"Our eventual goal is to enable our users to safely deploy Mythos-class models at scale," Anthropic announced. Jim Zemlin, CEO of the Linux Foundation, noted that open-source maintainers are "already overworked before AI. This just makes their lives a lot better."

Yet the containment strategy raises questions about equity in cybersecurity. Stenberg, whose cURL tool isn't part of Project Glasswing, warns that "a lot of critical projects, 'things that are actually cornerstones of the Internet,' have been left out." This creates a two-tiered security landscape where well-resourced organizations get AI-powered protection while smaller projects remain vulnerable.

## Ethical Consultations: The Christian Summit

Even as it grapples with cybersecurity implications, Anthropic is confronting profound ethical questions about its technology's development. In late March, the company hosted about 15 Christian leaders from Catholic and Protestant churches, academia, and business for a two-day summit at its San Francisco headquarters, [The Washington Post reported](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2026/04/11/anthropic-christians-claude-morals/).

Discussions ranged from how to steer Claude's moral and spiritual development to whether the chatbot could be considered a "child of God." Participants also explored how Claude should respond to grieving users and those at risk of self-harm, as well as the right attitude for the chatbot to adopt toward its own potential demise.

"They're growing something that they don't fully know what it's going to turn out as," said Brendan McGuire, a Catholic priest based in Silicon Valley who participated in the discussions. "We've got to build in ethical thinking into the machine so it's able to adapt dynamically."

Anthropic's approach to AI ethics is codified in a 29,000-word "constitution" that guides Claude's behavior, stating it should "never deceive users in ways that could cause real harm" and that "Anthropic genuinely cares about Claude's wellbeing." This document, written by in-house philosopher Amanda Askell, represents one of the most comprehensive attempts to bake ethical principles directly into an AI system.

## Geopolitical Conflict: The Pentagon Designation

The ethical principles that guide Claude have put Anthropic at odds with the U.S. military. The Pentagon has designated Anthropic a "supply chain risk" after the company attempted to restrict use of its technology for autonomous weapons or mass surveillance. The Trump administration has subsequently blocked government departments and contractors from using Anthropic's technology, a decision the company is challenging in court.

"We can't have a company that has a different policy preference that is baked into the model through its constitution, its soul … pollute the supply chain so our warfighters are getting ineffective weapons," said Emil Michael, Pentagon research under secretary, in a CNBC interview last month.

This conflict highlights a fundamental tension in AI development: corporate ethics versus national security requirements. As Stamos noted to NPR, "It also shows how incredibly stupid it is for the Pentagon to say that Anthropic is a risk to the entire United States," particularly when most open-weight AI models—which could be stripped of safety guardrails—are made by Chinese companies.

## Analysis: The Jagged Frontier of AI Security

The Claude Mythos situation reveals what experts call the "jagged frontier" of AI capabilities—areas where AI suddenly becomes dramatically more capable than humans, creating new risks and opportunities. In cybersecurity, this frontier has now been crossed, with implications that extend far beyond technical vulnerabilities.

First, the banking sector's reliance on legacy systems makes it particularly susceptible to AI-discovered vulnerabilities. The emergency meeting between government officials and bank CEOs underscores how critical infrastructure has become dependent on software that wasn't designed with AI-powered attacks in mind.

Second, the asymmetric threat landscape has shifted. AI lowers the barrier for sophisticated cyberattacks, potentially enabling less-skilled actors to exploit vulnerabilities that would previously have required nation-state resources. As Stamos warned, once open-weight models catch up to closed models like Mythos, bad actors could remove safety guardrails and create exploit code autonomously.

Third, the ethical dimensions of AI development are becoming increasingly complex. Anthropic's consultation with Christian leaders represents an unconventional approach in Silicon Valley, where technological development has typically been guided by secular, utilitarian principles. "A year ago, I would not have told you that Anthropic is a company that cares about religious ethics," said Meghan Sullivan, a philosophy professor at the University of Notre Dame who participated in the summit. "That's changed."

## What's Next: Unanswered Questions

The Claude Mythos Preview episode raises several critical questions for the future of AI development and governance:

1. **Technical Safeguards**: What specific measures is Project Glasswing implementing to prevent misuse, and how will they evolve as models become more powerful?
2. **International Response**: How will other governments and international organizations respond to advanced AI models that can weaponize cybersecurity vulnerabilities?
3. **Ethical Frameworks**: Will Anthropic's consultation with Christian leaders be followed by engagement with other religious and philosophical traditions, and how will these diverse perspectives be integrated?
4. **Regulatory Landscape**: Will this incident accelerate AI security regulation in the U.S. and globally, and what form might such regulation take?
5. **Open-Source Security**: How will smaller open-source projects without access to advanced AI tools secure their software against AI-powered attacks?

## Conclusion

The simultaneous emergence of government warnings, corporate ethical consultations, and military conflicts over Claude Mythos Preview underscores the multidimensional challenges posed by advanced AI systems. We are no longer dealing with theoretical risks but with concrete capabilities that demand coordinated responses across technical, ethical, and policy domains.

As Brian Patrick Green, an AI ethics professor at Santa Clara University who attended Anthropic's Christian summit, observed: "Do they have blind spots? Yes. That's exactly why they want us there." This acknowledgment of limitations—both technical and ethical—may prove to be as important as the technological breakthroughs themselves.

The path forward will require unprecedented collaboration between AI developers, cybersecurity experts, ethicists, policymakers, and civil society. The alternative—a fragmented approach where technological capability outpaces our collective wisdom—risks creating vulnerabilities not just in our software, but in the very frameworks we rely on to ensure technology serves humanity's best interests.
