# ULB Medical Students Lose Guard Allowances in ONSS Dispute

Approximately 150 medical students at Université Libre de Bruxelles (ULB) have lost their guard allowances following what the university claims was an inspection by Belgium's National Social Security Office (ONSS). However, [ONSS denies responsibility](https://www.rtbf.be/article/des-etudiants-en-medecine-perdent-leurs-indemnites-de-garde-l-ulb-accuse-un-controle-de-l-onss-qui-nie-etre-a-l-origine-de-la-decision-11707849) for the decision to eliminate payments of €35 for weekday guards and €55 for weekend guards, creating a contentious situation that threatens both student finances and hospital staffing.

The decision, announced to students on April 8 and applied retroactively from March 1, affects Master year 4, 5, and 6 students performing mandatory guard duties at three hospitals in the Hôpital Universitaire de Bruxelles (HUB) network: Institut Jules Bordet, Hôpital Erasme, and Hôpital universitaire des enfants Reine Fabiola.

## Context: Guard Duties in Belgian Medical Education

Medical studies in Belgium typically span six years, with the final three master years involving increasing clinical rotations and hospital placements. Guard duties—night and weekend shifts—are mandatory components of clinical training that provide essential hands-on experience. Unlike employed doctors, these students are not under employment contracts; the payments were considered "defraiements" (allowances) meant to cover expenses rather than serve as remuneration.

The payment system had been in place for many years and was considered a privilege specific to ULB medical students. By comparison, students at UCLouvain who perform guards on a voluntary basis at Saint-Luc clinics do not receive such allowances.

## Conflicting Claims: ULB vs. ONSS

In communications to affected students, ULB and HUB administrators stated the suspension resulted from an ONSS inspection requiring "clarification of what these payments cover" and demanding individual tripartite contracts (university, supervising doctor, student) for each student. Pierre Wauthy, Dean of ULB's Medical Faculty, explained the administrative challenge: "With the number of students we have and the number of placements they complete at ULB, this would represent several thousand conventions to generate each year, and administratively, unfortunately, that's not possible."

However, ONSS issued a contradictory statement obtained by RTBF: "Within the framework of this investigation, our inspectors did not request the suppression of guard allowances granted to students on placement." The social security office confirmed an investigation is ongoing but explicitly distanced itself from the decision to eliminate payments.

## Student Impact and Financial Pressure

For affected students, the loss represents significant financial hardship. Elias Fariss, President of the Comité Inter-Universitaire des Étudiants en Médecine (CIUM), noted the allowances covered essential costs: "These are allowances ranging from about thirty to fifty euros depending on whether it's a weekend guard or a weekday guard." He emphasized the retroactive application created particular difficulties: "This timeline really left little adaptation time to find alternatives, at least for students who were put in sometimes very delicate situations."

Students already bear costs for mobility, housing, and daily expenses related to hospital placements. Many cannot take part-time jobs due to demanding schedules. Emma Hebrard, a Master 2 student, told [RTL](https://www.rtl.be/actu/belgique/societe/ne-peut-pas-sen-sortir-prives-de-leur-defraiement-de-garde-les-futurs-medecins/2026-04-11/article/785486), "It's not just observation... They very quickly need us and so we can find ourselves doing guards in emergency departments, going to see patients ourselves."

## Administrative Burden vs. Student Welfare

ULB maintains the administrative requirements imposed—or perceived to be imposed—by ONSS make continuation of the payment system impractical. Dean Wauthy stated, "It in no way represents a lack of consideration, it's simply an administrative imperative that forces us to suspend these allowances for now."

The university claims creating individual contracts for each student for each rotation would require "several thousand conventions per year" and is "administratively impossible." This interpretation suggests ULB may have chosen suspension rather than attempting to reform the payment system to meet perceived regulatory requirements.

## Broader Implications for Medical Education

The controversy raises fundamental questions about how clinical training should be financially supported and highlights the precarious position of medical students in Belgium's healthcare system. Students occupy a gray area between learners and essential healthcare contributors, performing duties critical to hospital operations without employment status or corresponding protections.

The Fédération des étudiant·e·s francophones (FEF) expressed concern about "a measure they deem prejudicial to medical students' living conditions" and called for a legal solution guaranteeing financial security. The situation occurs against a backdrop of rising educational costs, including increased "minerval" (tuition fees), adding to student financial pressures.

## Mobilization and Forward Outlook

According to [Le Soir](https://www.lesoir.be/740096/article/2026-04-10/la-fin-du-defraiement-des-gardes-lhub-crispe-les-etudiants-en-medecine), the decision has generated significant discontent among medical students, with many threatening to refuse guard duties if allowances are not reinstated. This potential mobilization could strain hospital staffing, particularly in emergency and critical care departments where student participation is most valuable.

The core dispute—whether ONSS requested elimination of allowances or merely clarification of their purpose—remains unresolved. A transparent investigation and clear communication between all parties appear necessary to resolve the situation. Possible solutions include simplified contract frameworks, modified payment structures, or clarified regulatory guidance that acknowledges the unique position of medical students in clinical training.

For now, approximately 150 future doctors face unexpected financial shortfalls, hospital administrators grapple with administrative interpretations, and Belgium's social security office denies instigating a decision that threatens to disrupt both medical education and healthcare delivery. The outcome will likely influence how Belgian universities structure clinical training allowances and define the financial relationship between teaching hospitals and their student practitioners.
