# Kamala Harris Considers 2028 Presidential Run, Says 'I'm Thinking About It'

Former Vice President Kamala Harris publicly acknowledged for the first time on Friday that she is actively considering a run for president in 2028, delivering her clearest signal yet about her political future following her 2024 loss to President Donald Trump. The comments came during an on-stage interview with Reverend Al Sharpton at the National Action Network Convention in New York City, where Harris received enthusiastic chants of "run again" from a packed ballroom of predominantly Black activists and Democratic power brokers.

## Context: A Crucial Democratic Cattle-Call

The National Action Network Convention, founded by civil rights activist Al Sharpton, has evolved into a critical early stop for Democratic presidential hopefuls seeking to court Black voters, who represent a substantial portion of the party's primary electorate. This year's gathering served as the first major cattle-call for the potential 2028 Democratic field, with more than a half-dozen contenders appearing over four days to test their messages and gauge support.

Harris, who made history as the first Black woman to win a major party's presidential nomination in 2024, arrived at the convention with unique standing. Despite her loss to Trump, she received more votes than any presidential candidate in American history except Trump and former President Joe Biden, as [Sharpton noted](https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/2026/04/10/kamala-harris-2028-presidential-run/) in his introduction. Her appearance drew the largest crowd of any potential contender, with attendees filling every seat and standing several deep along the walls of the Sheraton Hotel ballroom in Times Square.

## The Announcement and Audience Reaction

When Sharpton directly asked Harris whether she planned to run for president again in 2028, the crowd erupted before she could answer. After quieting the audience, Harris responded: "Listen, I might, I might. I'm thinking about it. I'm thinking about it," according to [CNN's report](https://edition.cnn.com/2026/04/10/politics/kamala-harris-2028-presidential-election) from the event. The declaration marked her most explicit public remarks to date about another White House bid, surpassing previous teasing comments in interviews.

Harris emphasized her executive experience, telling the audience: "I served for four years being a heartbeat away from the presidency of the United States. I spent countless hours in my West Wing office, footsteps away from the Oval Office. I spent countless hours in the Oval Office, in the Situation Room. I know what the job is. And I know what it requires." She added that during her tenure, she met with over 150 world leaders and traveled the globe extensively, building foreign policy credentials she contrasted sharply with Trump's approach.

The former vice president also addressed the political landscape more broadly, stating: "I've been traveling the country the last year... And the one thing I'm really clear about also, is the status quo is not working, and hasn't been working for a lot of people for a long time." She urged Democrats to embrace "transactional voting," encouraging voters to demand specific returns for their support—a nod to concerns about declining Democratic performance among some Black and Latino male voters in 2024.

## A Crowded Field of Democratic Contenders

Harris was among several potential 2028 Democratic presidential contenders who appeared at the convention, reflecting the party's ongoing search for direction after the 2024 loss. The lineup included Pennsylvania Governor Josh Shapiro, Illinois Governor JB Pritzker, Maryland Governor Wes Moore, former Transportation Secretary Pete Buttigieg, Arizona Senator Ruben Gallego, California Representative Ro Khanna, and Kentucky Governor Andy Beshear, who was scheduled to speak Saturday.

Each contender offered carefully calibrated messages, with Shapiro promoting his "get s*** done" governing philosophy, Moore discussing redistricting battles in Maryland, and Buttigieg criticizing Transportation Security Administration staffing shortages. Foreign policy emerged as a common theme, with multiple Democrats condemning Trump's "war of choice" in Iran. As [Politico reported](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/04/10/kamala-harris-signals-2028-bid-00867069), the event revealed both the breadth of the emerging field and the early jockeying for position within it.

Notably, Harris received the most enthusiastic reception of any speaker, with [NBC News noting](https://www.nbcnews.com/politics/2028-election/kamala-harris-confirms-might-run-president-2028-rcna273682) that her appearance prompted the only standing ovation and that many attendees streamed out of the auditorium after her speech—leaving a half-empty room for Buttigieg's subsequent appearance as they sought selfies with the former vice president.

## Harris's Political Standing and Challenges

Harris enters the 2028 conversation with significant advantages but also substantial challenges. She leads very early polls of Democratic preferences for 2028, though this is partly attributable to her high name recognition from two previous presidential campaigns and four years as vice president. A September 2025 Washington Post-Ipsos poll found 72% of Black Democrats and Democratic leaners have favorable views of Harris, compared to 50% for California Governor Gavin Newsom, another potential contender.

However, the same poll revealed that among all U.S. adults, Harris faces a 52% unfavorable rating versus 36% favorable—a reminder of the broader electoral challenges she would confront in a general election. Some Democratic voices have expressed skepticism about another Harris candidacy, with Michigan Senator Elissa Slotkin telling [CBS News](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/national-action-network-convention-offers-first-glimpse-2028-democratic-field/) that while Harris's 2024 baggage might not be "insurmountable," the "strongest feeling I get... is that people want something new."

A former senior Harris campaign official echoed this sentiment anonymously, stating: "People don't like looking in the rearview mirror... She has a steep uphill climb." These comments reflect a tension within the Democratic Party between those who value Harris's historic achievements and proven vote-getting ability and those seeking fresh leadership after consecutive presidential losses.

## Analysis: Implications for the 2028 Race

Harris's comments effectively launch her exploratory phase for 2028, forcing other contenders to clarify their positions and potentially freezing some donor commitments. Her emphasis on foreign policy experience suggests a campaign that would highlight national security credentials—an area where she can draw sharp contrasts with Trump's handling of the Iran conflict and relationships with traditional allies.

The former vice president's upcoming travel schedule underscores her strategic focus: she plans events in South Carolina, North Carolina, Georgia, and Arkansas in coming weeks, as [The New York Times reported](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/04/10/us/politics/eying-2028-ambitious-democrats-court-black-voters.html). These Southern states represent both opportunities to consolidate support among Black voters—who play decisive roles in early primaries—and challenges in regions where Democrats have struggled in recent federal elections.

Harris's continued criticism of Trump also establishes clear general election contrast points. During her convention appearance, she accused the president of lying about economic promises and condemned his foreign policy decisions, stating: "The American citizens will always pay for war... He told the American people on Day 1 he was going to break down prices and costs and he lied." Such rhetoric previews potential attack lines in a 2028 campaign.

## What's Next for Harris and the Democratic Field

Harris told Sharpton she would "keep you posted" on her decision, indicating no immediate timeline for a formal announcement. However, her activities suggest serious consideration: she released a campaign memoir in late 2025, embarked on a book tour, and recently launched a political action committee. In an October 2025 BBC interview, she stated "I am not done" regarding her political future, and in February 2026, she said "I might" when asked about another presidential run.

The Democratic Party now faces a period of reflection and potential recalibration. The 2028 primary structure remains undetermined, with questions about the calendar sequence, debate qualifications, and the role of superdelegates. Harris's potential candidacy could influence these decisions, particularly if she maintains strong support among the party's base.

As [AP News summarized](https://apnews.com/article/kamala-harris-president-democrat-2028-sharpton-9d609ed146b82a27f6eeec0f03bcf5c4), the convention revealed both Harris's enduring appeal within core Democratic constituencies and the crowded, competitive nature of the emerging 2028 field. Whether she can translate that convention enthusiasm into a successful national campaign remains one of the most compelling questions in American politics—one that will unfold over the coming months as Harris continues to "think about" her political future.
