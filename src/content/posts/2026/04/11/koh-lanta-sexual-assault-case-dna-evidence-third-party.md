# Koh Lanta Assault Case: DNA Evidence Reveals Third Contestant's DNA

A sexual assault case involving the French reality television show Koh Lanta has taken a dramatic turn with forensic analysis revealing DNA from a third contestant on the alleged victim's clothing, creating competing narratives in a high-profile legal battle that has captivated France. According to [Le Monde](https://www.lemonde.fr/societe/article/2026/04/11/agression-sexuelle-a-koh-lanta-bataille-de-recits-autour-d-une-expertise-adn_6679242_3224.html), genetic expertise conducted in November 2025 identified male DNA inside the leggings worn by accuser Candide Renard during the alleged 2018 assault—but the genetic material belongs to another contestant, Fabien J., not the accused, Eddy Guyot.

## Context: The 2018 Incident and Investigation

The case stems from events during the filming of Koh Lanta Season 19 in Fiji in May 2018. Candide Renard, then 21 and daughter of former French women's football coach Hervé Renard, accused fellow contestant Eddy Guyot, a 33-year-old fairground merchant, of sexually assaulting her while she slept. Renard reported the incident to production via emergency walkie-talkie, alleging non-consensual kissing and touching. Production company Adventure Line Productions (ALP) immediately suspended filming and separated the participants, ultimately cancelling the season entirely.

Upon returning to France, Renard filed a formal complaint on May 13, 2018. Guyot was arrested at Roissy airport the following day but released after questioning. The Nancy prosecutor's office opened a judicial investigation on May 16, 2018, leading to Guyot's formal indictment (mis en examen) for sexual assault in November 2022 after multiple changes in investigating judges.

## The DNA Evidence: A Spectacular Twist

The judicial investigation concluded on January 14, 2026, but not before what Le Monde describes as "a spectacular twist at the end of the procedure." Genetic expertise conducted on November 24, 2025, revealed male DNA—specifically epithelial cells (skin tissue), not semen—inside Renard's leggings at crotch level. Forensic analysis identified the DNA as belonging to Fabien J., another contestant who slept nearby during the alleged assault, not to Eddy Guyot.

This finding has created what legal teams describe as a "battle of narratives" around the forensic evidence. Renard maintains her accusations against Guyot despite the DNA results, telling the investigating judge on January 12, 2026: "I know it was Eddy Guyot who assaulted me. Is there a way to find someone's DNA on clothing other than through assault? We turned leggings inside out to dry them on the clothesline."

## Competing Legal Narratives

The DNA evidence has fueled competing interpretations from legal teams. Guyot's attorney, Jérémie Assous, argues the findings support his client's innocence. "The only objective material element is DNA, and it is not Eddy Guyot's," Assous told Le Monde. The defense has suggested Fabien J. could be the actual perpetrator and previously accused Renard and Fabien J. of conspiring to eliminate Guyot from the competition.

Renard's legal team, led by attorney Léon del Forno, maintains her consistent identification of Guyot as her assailant. "The time elapsed... has not altered Candide Renard's determination," del Forno stated. Renard points to Guyot's "very tactile" and "sometimes aggressive" behavior noted by other participants as supporting her account.

Fabien J., whose DNA was identified, denies any "intimate or physical rapprochement" with Renard during filming, suggesting "genetic pollution" due to close sleeping arrangements. Notably, Renard and Fabien J. had a brief romantic relationship after returning to France, a fact highlighted by the defense.

## Judicial Assessment and Next Steps

The investigating judge has navigated these competing claims with careful nuance. In March 2026, the judge declined a defense request for a three-way confrontation between the parties, noting that the DNA evidence "does not in any way implicate Fabien J. as perpetrator of sexual assault" while simultaneously stating it "does not remove charges against Eddy Guyot." The judge deemed Renard's explanation for Fabien J.'s DNA—potential transfer during laundry drying—as "perfectly coherent."

With the judicial investigation now concluded, the judge must decide whether to send the case to trial. This decision will determine whether the eight-year legal saga proceeds to courtroom proceedings or is dismissed based on the forensic complications.

## Broader Implications

The case raises significant questions beyond the immediate parties. Legally, it presents challenges about how courts should weigh DNA evidence against consistent victim testimony, particularly when secondary DNA transfer is possible in close living conditions. For the television industry, it highlights duty-of-care responsibilities for production companies overseeing contestants in high-stress reality environments where cameras are typically turned off at night.

Societally, the case emerges amid the global #MeToo reckoning with sexual misconduct, testing public perceptions of sexual assault allegations when forensic evidence complicates straightforward narratives. The intense media scrutiny—from Le Monde's detailed investigative reporting to more sensational coverage in outlets like Closer—reflects the case's resonance at the intersection of celebrity culture, forensic science, and legal procedure.

## What's Next

As the judge deliberates on whether to proceed to trial, all parties await a resolution to a case that has already spanned eight years. The outcome will not only determine justice for Renard and Guyot but could establish precedents for how similar allegations are investigated in both legal and entertainment contexts. For Koh Lanta producers ALP and broadcaster TF1, the case serves as a stark reminder of the complex responsibilities inherent in reality television production, where dramatic competition must be balanced with participant safety and ethical oversight.

The French public continues to follow developments closely, watching as forensic science and human testimony intersect in a legal drama as compelling as the television show that spawned it.
