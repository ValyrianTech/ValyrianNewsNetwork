# Paris-Roubaix Cobblestone Theft Raises Safety Concerns Ahead of Race

Organizers of the historic Paris-Roubaix cycling race have issued urgent safety warnings as thieves systematically steal cobblestones from the iconic race route, creating dangerous holes that could cause serious crashes during Sunday's event. Race director Thierry Gouvenou told [Le Figaro](https://www.lefigaro.fr/sports/cyclisme/cyclisme-ce-que-font-ces-gens-peut-etre-extremement-dangereux-les-vols-de-paves-inquietent-les-organisateurs-de-paris-roubaix-20260411) that the thefts are "extremely dangerous" and could influence the outcome of both the women's and men's races.

## The Cobblestone Crisis

Cobblestones, known as pavés in French, are being stolen from two of Paris-Roubaix's most famous sectors: the Trouée d'Arenberg (Forest of Wallers) and Carrefour de l'Arbre. According to Gouvenou, collectors and fans are taking the stones as souvenirs, often displaying them on fireplaces as mementos of the legendary race. The thefts occur under cover of darkness, with organizers discovering new holes regularly in the lead-up to the April 12 event.

"This race is already brutal enough," said Gouvenou, a former professional cyclist who now oversees course preparation. "These are mythical sectors and people steal cobblestones to take them home as souvenirs. What these people do can be extremely dangerous. Imagine what would happen if riders hit one of those holes… They go through this sector at 50 km/h."

## Safety at Stake

The safety implications are severe. Riders navigate the cobbled sectors at approximately 50 km/h (31 mph), and deep holes created by missing stones could cause catastrophic crashes. Gouvenou warned that such incidents "could certainly cause falls" and in worst cases could be deadly. The race director expressed particular concern about the potential impact on star riders like defending champion Mathieu van der Poel and contender Tadej Pogacar, who are expected to battle for victory.

"It would be deeply sad if one of these giants were to crash because someone thought a Paris-Roubaix cobblestone would look nice on their fireplace," Gouvenou lamented in an interview with [De Telegraaf](https://www.telegraaf.nl/sport/wielrennen/kasseiendieven-slaan-toe-op-route-parijs-roubaix-parcoursbaas-woedend-levensgevaarlijk-voor-mathieu-van-der-poel/145352389.html).

## Race Organizers' Response

Faced with the growing problem, organizers are conducting repeated inspections and hastily filling holes to prevent interference with the race. Teams are checking the route regularly, with a comprehensive final inspection planned for the end of the week. Gouvenou has made public appeals pleading with people to stop taking cobblestones, emphasizing that the thefts compromise both rider safety and race integrity.

According to [CyclingUpToDate.com](https://cyclinguptodate.com/cycling/cobblestone-thieves-strike-parisroubaix-route-as-thierry-gouvenou-pleads-leave-those-cobblestones-alone-this-race-is-already-brutal-enough), the issue has escalated in recent years, with organizers "dealing with this more and more." The website reported that Gouvenou's team works tirelessly to inspect, protect, and repair the course, recognizing that the holes "can also have a major impact on how the race unfolds."

## Historical Irony

The theft situation represents a historical irony for Paris-Roubaix, a race first held in 1896 and known as "L'Enfer du Nord" (The Hell of the North). For decades, preservation of the cobbled sectors has been a constant battle, but previously the threat came from modernization rather than theft.

In the 1960s and 1970s, mayors along the route would surface cobblestones to avoid appearing "backward" on television. This prompted the formation of Les Amis de Paris–Roubaix in 1983, a volunteer group dedicated to preserving the iconic cobbled sectors. According to [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris%E2%80%93Roubaix), the group spends €10,000-15,000 annually on restoration and works with horticulture students to maintain the historic road surfaces.

Alain Bernard, president of Les Amis de Paris–Roubaix, once noted that Belgian fans historically took cobblestones as souvenirs, but the problem has now expanded beyond border regions. The current situation represents a reversal: where municipalities once paved over cobbles to avoid embarrassment, they now preserve them for tourism and sporting heritage—only to see them stolen by over-enthusiastic fans.

## The Trouée d'Arenberg Legacy

The Trouée d'Arenberg holds particular significance in Paris-Roubaix lore. The 2,400-meter cobbled sector was laid during Napoleon I's time and introduced to the race in 1968. It's considered the race's most iconic challenge, though it has also been the site of serious incidents. In 1998, Johan Museeuw nearly lost his leg after a crash here, leading organizers to reverse the course direction in 1999 to reduce entry speed.

Winners of Paris-Roubaix have received a mounted cobblestone as a trophy since 1977, adding to the stones' symbolic value. This tradition may inadvertently contribute to their desirability as collector's items, though race organizers emphasize that only officially awarded stones should serve as souvenirs.

## Balancing Enthusiasm and Responsibility

The cobblestone theft highlights the tension between fan enthusiasm and sporting responsibility. While taking a stone represents deep engagement with race history, it creates tangible safety hazards that could unfairly influence competition outcomes. The incident raises questions about event security along remote race sectors and the need for fan education about preserving historic sporting venues.

Gouvenou's plea—"leave those cobblestones alone. This race is already brutal enough"—encapsulates the frustration of organizers who must balance maintaining the race's legendary difficulty with ensuring competitor safety. The thefts represent an artificial alteration to course conditions that could provide unfair advantages or disadvantages to riders, depending on where holes appear.

## Looking Ahead to Race Day

As riders prepare to start from Compiègne at 10:50 on Sunday, with finishes expected around 16:30 at the Roubaix Velodrome, organizers will continue their vigilance. The women's and men's races will proceed as scheduled, but with additional precautions and last-minute repairs.

The incident serves as a reminder of the challenges in maintaining historic sporting events in the modern era. As Paris-Roubaix celebrates 130 years of history in 2026, organizers face the paradoxical task of protecting the very obstacles that define the race's character while ensuring they don't become unreasonably dangerous.

For cycling purists, the cobblestones represent more than just road surface—they embody the essence of a race that tests endurance, skill, and courage. Their preservation ensures future generations can experience the authentic challenge that has made Paris-Roubaix a monument of the sport. As Gouvenou's team makes final preparations, the hope is that respect for racing heritage will prevail over the temptation of taking a piece of history home.
