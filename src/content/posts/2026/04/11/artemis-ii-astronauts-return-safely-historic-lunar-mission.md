# Artemis II Astronauts Return Safely After Historic Lunar Mission

The four astronauts of NASA's Artemis II mission have successfully returned to Earth, splashing down in the Pacific Ocean off the coast of California on April 10, 2026, after completing a historic journey around the Moon. The mission marks the first crewed lunar orbit since Apollo 17 in 1972 and represents a significant milestone in renewed international efforts to explore Earth's celestial neighbor.

According to [France Info](https://www.francetvinfo.fr/sciences/espace/mission-artemis-vers-la-lune/en-images-quel-periple-les-astronautes-de-la-mission-artemis-ii-de-retour-sur-terre-apres-leur-voyage-historique-autour-de-la-lune_7930766.html), the Orion capsule carrying American astronauts Reid Wiseman, Christina Koch, Victor Glover, and Canadian astronaut Jeremy Hansen landed within 2 kilometers of its targeted splashdown location near San Diego. The crew's safe return concludes a 10-day mission that began with launch from Kennedy Space Center in Florida on April 1.

## Historic Context and Mission Significance

Artemis II represents the first crewed mission to the Moon in over five decades, bridging the gap between the Apollo era and a new chapter of lunar exploration. The mission tested critical systems for future crewed Moon landings, including the Orion spacecraft's life support, communication, and navigation capabilities. As [Le Monde](https://www.lemonde.fr/sciences/article/2026/04/11/artemis-2-retour-en-photos-sur-une-mission-historique-du-decollage-a-l-amerrissage_6679357_1650684.html) reported, the crew composition itself made history, featuring the first woman, first Black astronaut, and first non-American to travel to lunar orbit.

The mission achieved several notable milestones during its journey. On April 6, the spacecraft reached a distance of 248,655 miles (400,171 km) from Earth, setting a new record for the farthest distance traveled by humans, surpassing the previous record set by Apollo 13 in 1970. NASA's [official gallery](https://www.nasa.gov/gallery/return-to-earth/) documents this achievement and other key moments from the mission.

## Mission Timeline and Technical Details

The Artemis II mission followed a carefully orchestrated timeline that began with the Space Launch System rocket and Orion spacecraft rollout to Launch Complex 39B in January 2026. After final preparations and dress rehearsals, the crew launched on April 1 at 18:35 local time from Kennedy Space Center.

Following two Earth orbits for systems testing, the spacecraft performed a translunar injection burn to begin its journey toward the Moon. The four-day transit culminated in lunar orbit operations from April 7-9, during which the crew tested spacecraft systems and conducted observations.

The return journey featured a dramatic re-entry into Earth's atmosphere, with the Orion capsule reaching speeds exceeding 39,000 km/h (approximately 24,000 mph). Mission Commander Reid Wiseman's first words after splashdown—"Quel périple!" ("What a journey!")—captured the historic moment, as reported by French media covering the event.

## Diverse Crew Makes History

The Artemis II crew composition represents a significant departure from the exclusively white, male, American crews of the Apollo era. Mission Specialist Christina Koch became the first woman to orbit the Moon, while Pilot Victor Glover made history as the first Black astronaut to reach lunar distance. Canadian Space Agency astronaut Jeremy Hansen's participation marked the first time a non-American has traveled to the Moon's vicinity.

This diversity reflects the international and inclusive nature of modern space exploration. As Hansen stated ten minutes before launch on April 1: "Nous partons pour l'humanité tout entière" ("We depart for all of humanity").

## International Cooperation and French Involvement

French media provided extensive coverage of the mission, reflecting France's significant role in European space contributions to the Artemis program. France joined the Artemis Accords on June 7, 2022—the 60th anniversary of its space program—and the French space agency CNES contributes to European Space Agency programs supporting Artemis, including the International Space Station, Lunar Gateway, and Mars Sample Return missions.

The European Service Module, which powers the Orion spacecraft, represents a key European contribution to the mission. French coverage emphasized this international cooperation aspect, with both France Info and Le Monde publishing comprehensive photo retrospectives of the mission from launch to splashdown.

## Observations from Space and Earth

Unique perspectives on the mission came from both space and Earth. Astronauts aboard the International Space Station observed the Artemis II re-entry, with ISS astronaut Chris Williams describing the view: "We first saw a bright light and a trail as the service module burned up. We didn't see the Orion capsule... but we saw the vapor trail."

On Earth, crowds gathered at the San Diego Air & Space Museum to celebrate the successful return, with applause marking the moment of splashdown. The recovery operation involved U.S. military personnel who retrieved the astronauts from the Pacific Ocean and transported them to the USS John P. Murtha.

## Implications for Future Exploration

The successful Artemis II mission validates critical systems for future lunar exploration. The mission serves as a crucial test for technologies that will be used in Artemis III, which aims to land astronauts on the lunar surface—potentially as early as the late 2020s.

Beyond immediate lunar goals, the Artemis program represents a stepping stone for future human missions to Mars. The international cooperation demonstrated by Artemis II, involving NASA, the European Space Agency, the Canadian Space Agency, and other Artemis Accords signatories, establishes a model for collaborative deep space exploration.

## Looking Ahead to Artemis III and Beyond

With Artemis II successfully completed, attention now turns to Artemis III, which will attempt the first crewed lunar landing since 1972. The mission will build on lessons learned from Artemis II, particularly regarding spacecraft performance, crew operations, and international coordination.

The Artemis program aims to establish a sustainable human presence on the Moon, serving as a proving ground for technologies needed for Mars missions while enabling scientific research and potential resource utilization. The successful return of the Artemis II crew represents not just the conclusion of one mission, but the beginning of a new era in human space exploration—one characterized by diversity, international partnership, and ambitious goals beyond Earth orbit.

As NASA's welcome message displayed during the splashdown broadcast simply stated: "Bienvenue à la maison" ("Welcome home"). For the Artemis II crew, their historic journey may be complete, but humanity's return to the Moon has only just begun.
