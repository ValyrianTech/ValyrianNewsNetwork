# US-Iran Begin Historic Peace Talks in Islamabad

**ISLAMABAD** – The United States and Iran began direct peace talks in Pakistan's capital on Saturday, marking the highest-level face-to-face negotiations between the two nations since Iran's 1979 Islamic Revolution. The talks, mediated by Pakistan, aim to end a devastating six-week war that has killed thousands, disrupted global energy markets, and threatened to engulf the wider Middle East in conflict.

According to [Iranian state media reports](https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/2026/04/11/us-iran-islamabad-hormuz-ceasefire/), negotiations commenced Saturday afternoon in Islamabad after both delegations held separate meetings with Pakistani Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif. The U.S. delegation is led by Vice President JD Vance, accompanied by President Donald Trump's special envoy Steve Witkoff and son-in-law Jared Kushner. Iran's team is headed by Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Qalibaf, who arrives with full authority from Supreme Leader Ayatollah Mojtaba Khamenei to "make a deal or walk away."

## A Mediator Emerges from Unlikely Quarters

Pakistan's emergence as the central mediator in this conflict represents one of the most surprising diplomatic developments of the war. The South Asian nation, which maintains relations with both Washington and Tehran, leveraged its unique position to broker the fragile two-week ceasefire that preceded Saturday's talks.

"Pakistan offered to play a mediating role because further escalation could spark a wider conflict," retired Pakistani army general and defense analyst Tariq Rashid Khan told The Associated Press. Prime Minister Sharif had earlier called the negotiations a "make or break" moment for regional peace, according to reporting from Islamabad.

The mediation success stems partly from the close relationship between Pakistani Army Chief Field Marshal Asim Munir and President Trump, who has referred to Munir as his "favorite field marshal." This personal connection, combined with Pakistan's historical experience in regional diplomacy and China's quiet support, created an opening that more traditional mediators could not.

## Delegations of Substance and Symbolism

The composition of both delegations signals the seriousness with which each side approaches these negotiations. Iran's team includes 71 people—negotiators, technical experts, financial specialists, and security personnel—reflecting what analysts describe as a comprehensive approach to what could be a transformative agreement.

"What we can read from Iran's delegation is that they have not come to stonewall," said Vali Nasr, a professor of Middle Eastern studies at Johns Hopkins University and former State Department official. Such a large, expert-heavy delegation would typically only be deployed for final-stage negotiations, not initial discussions, according to diplomatic analysts monitoring the talks.

Notable Iranian officials include Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi, National Security Council member Ali Bagheri Kani, former Revolutionary Guards chief of staff Admiral Ali Akbar Ahmadian, and Central Bank Governor Abdolnasser Hemati. Their presence addresses the multiple dimensions of any potential agreement: diplomatic, security, and economic.

The U.S. delegation represents both continuity and change in American diplomacy toward Iran. Vance, who repeatedly expressed opposition to a regime-change war before the conflict began, now leads negotiations to end it. He is joined by Witkoff and Kushner, both of whom have previous experience negotiating with Iran on nuclear issues.

## The Weight of History and Distrust

As talks began, the shadow of nearly five decades of hostility loomed large. Qalibaf set the tone upon arriving in Islamabad Friday evening, stating, "We have good will, but we do not have trust." He pointed to two previous negotiation attempts—in June and February 2026—that ended with U.S. military strikes instead of agreements.

Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi echoed this sentiment, saying Iran enters the talks with "deep distrust" because of attacks during previous negotiations, according to diplomatic sources briefed on the discussions.

The war began on February 28, 2026, when U.S. and Israeli attacks killed many of Iran's top leaders, including Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei. Iran retaliated with missile and drone strikes against U.S. bases and allies across the Middle East and took the strategically decisive step of closing the Strait of Hormuz, through which 20% of global oil shipments pass.

## Sticking Points: Hormuz, Lebanon, and Frozen Assets

Three primary issues dominate the negotiation agenda, each representing both leverage points and potential deal-breakers.

**Strait of Hormuz:** Iran's control of this vital waterway gives it significant economic leverage but also makes the country vulnerable to international pressure. President Trump claimed on social media Saturday that the U.S. is "starting the process of clearing out the Strait of Hormuz," though the specifics remain unclear. Shipping data indicates only a handful of ships have passed through the strait since the ceasefire began, highlighting the practical challenges of reopening it.

The economic stakes are immense. European airports face a "systemic jet fuel shortage" within three weeks if the strait doesn't reopen, according to a warning from Airports Council International-Europe obtained by The Associated Press.

**Lebanon Conflict:** Israel's continued attacks on Hezbollah in Lebanon represent the most immediate threat to the ceasefire. Iran insists any truce must cover Lebanon, while Israel views Hezbollah as a separate threat. On Saturday alone, Israel struck more than 200 Hezbollah targets in Lebanon, according to regional security officials.

The human cost has been devastating. Lebanon's health ministry reported at least 1,953 people killed in the latest fighting, including 357 in a single wave of Israeli strikes on Wednesday.

**Frozen Assets:** Iran demands the release of its frozen overseas assets as a precondition for substantive talks. Iranian state media reported Saturday that the U.S. had agreed to this demand, but a U.S. official denied that anything had been formally agreed, speaking on condition of anonymity to discuss internal talks.

## Domestic Pressures on Both Sides

Both governments face significant domestic constraints that shape their negotiating positions.

In the United States, the war has proven unpopular, with rising gas prices and political dissent creating election-year pressures for President Trump. The delegation itself reflects internal divisions: Vance opposed the war's initiation but now leads efforts to end it.

In Iran, the government balances hardliner opposition to negotiations with public war-weariness after weeks of devastating airstrikes. At a recent march in Tehran commemorating the death of Ayatollah Khamenei, some demonstrators held signs reading: "Cease-fire is prohibited. It's time for revenge" and "No compromise, no surrender. Fight until victory," according to eyewitness accounts.

Pakistan, too, faces domestic considerations. While basking in diplomatic relevance, the country struggles with political and economic volatility at home. "There is a disconnect between Pakistan's diplomatic achievement and the situation back home," Ishtiaq Ahmad, professor emeritus at Quaid-i-Azam University in Islamabad, [told NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/04/11/nx-s1-5781760/pakistan-hosts-peace-talks-us-iran).

## Regional Alignments and External Actors

The talks occur against a backdrop of shifting regional alliances and external interference. Saudi Arabia announced Saturday that Pakistani fighter jets had arrived at King Abdulaziz Air Base as part of a mutual defense pact—the first invocation of the Strategic Mutual Defence Agreement signed last year, according to the Saudi defense ministry.

More concerning for U.S. officials is intelligence indicating China may be preparing to ship air defense systems to Iran within weeks, according to security analysts monitoring Chinese military movements. This would represent a significant escalation in Chinese involvement and could complicate Trump's planned visit to China next month.

Russia has already provided satellite intelligence to help Iran target American assets in the region, though Moscow appears to have ruled out providing offensive military equipment.

## The Path Forward: Scenarios and Stakes

As Saturday's talks continue behind closed doors at Islamabad's Serena Hotel, several scenarios appear possible.

A successful agreement would need to address verification mechanisms for reopening the Strait of Hormuz, a sustainable ceasefire covering Lebanon, sanctions relief and asset unfreezing for Iran, and assurances regarding Iran's nuclear program. Such a deal would not only end the immediate conflict but potentially reshape U.S.-Iran relations after nearly five decades of hostility.

Failure could lead to resumption of full-scale conflict with severe global economic consequences. European jet fuel shortages, already looming, would become reality. Global oil prices, which have stabilized somewhat during the ceasefire, would likely spike again.

For Pakistan, success would significantly enhance its global diplomatic standing, validating its unconventional approach to mediation. "That global relevance, especially in the eyes of the US, is something that Pakistan has pushed for," Professor Ahmad told NPR.

## A City Transformed, A World Watching

Islamabad itself has been transformed by the talks. The normally bustling capital appeared deserted Saturday as security forces sealed roads. Billboards showing American, Pakistani, and Iranian flags side-by-side have popped up around the city, declaring the "Islamabad Talks April 2026."

At the Jinnah Convention Center, dozens of journalists gathered in a state-of-the-art media facility but remained largely in the dark about the proceedings. "No one knows when, where, or how these talks are taking place," Pakistani journalist Nadir Guramani told local media.

For 19-year-old Islamabad resident Khizra Zaheer, Pakistan's sudden diplomatic prominence came as a surprise. "When did Pakistan get so influential?" she asked NPR, capturing the disbelief many Pakistanis feel at their country's central role in resolving one of the world's most dangerous conflicts.

As negotiations continue into the evening, the world watches to see whether decades of hostility can give way to dialogue, and whether an unlikely mediator can succeed where others have failed. The stakes—for regional peace, global energy security, and the future of great power competition—could hardly be higher.
