# Supreme Court Retirement Speculation Grows Before 2026 Elections

WASHINGTON — Intense speculation surrounds potential retirements from the aging U.S. Supreme Court as the 2026 midterm elections approach. Justices Samuel Alito (76) and Clarence Thomas (77), the court's oldest members, face strategic decisions that could give President Donald Trump a fourth appointment. According to [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/legal/government/aging-us-supreme-court-justices-politics-retirement-looms-large-2026-04-11/), the political timing creates a narrowing window for Trump to act with a Republican-controlled Senate.

## Context and Background

The Supreme Court's demographic landscape places Alito and Thomas as its oldest members, both approaching the average retirement age of 80 for justices since 2000. Trump has already appointed three justices during his first term — Neil Gorsuch, Brett Kavanaugh, and Amy Coney Barrett — creating a 6-3 conservative majority. The last president to appoint four justices was Richard Nixon, who served from 1969 to 1974.

Republicans currently hold a 53-47 Senate majority, but Democratic gains in the November midterms could shift that balance. This political calculus has created what former Justice Department lawyer John Yoo describes as a closing window: "The window for Trump to nominate a Supreme Court justice — or any federal judge — with a friendly Republican Senate could be closing by the end of this year."

## Recent Developments Fueling Speculation

Speculation has mounted in recent months, particularly around Justice Alito. As [The New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/04/11/us/politics/alito-trump-retirement-supreme-court.html) reports, a gathering last month at the National Constitution Center in Philadelphia featured Justice Alito's former law clerks, academics, and conservative federal judges considered possible contenders to succeed him. The event marked Alito's 20 years on the court.

Alito's forthcoming book, "So Ordered: An Originalist's View of the Constitution, the Court, and Our Country," scheduled for release on October 6, has added to retirement rumors. Georgetown University law professor Steve Vladeck noted, "The October publication date is a pretty big tell since one can't exactly go on a book tour during the first argument session of the term."

Health incidents have also fueled speculation. Alito was briefly hospitalized for dehydration on March 20 after feeling ill during an event in Philadelphia. Thomas missed arguments in 2022 when hospitalized with flu-like symptoms.

## Strategic Considerations and Historical Precedents

Conservative justices face a strategic dilemma: retire now to ensure like-minded successors or risk liberal replacements if political control shifts. Cornell Law School professor Michael Dorf pointed to Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg's fateful choice to remain on the bench rather than step down during President Obama's presidency. "I think it has to loom over everybody's decision," Dorf said.

Ginsburg's death in 2020 enabled Trump to replace her with a conservative nominee, creating the current conservative supermajority. Similarly, when Justice Antonin Scalia died in 2016, Senate Republicans refused to consider President Obama's nominee, allowing Trump to appoint Gorsuch the following year.

For Alito, legacy considerations may influence his decision. "Generations from now, if somebody looks up Samuel Alito in Wikipedia or whatever replaces it, that will be the first line," Dorf said. "He's the justice who wrote the opinion overturning the right to abortion." Alito authored the landmark 2022 decision overturning Roe v. Wade.

## Political Implications and Potential Outcomes

If either justice retires before November, Trump would have the opportunity to nominate a fourth justice. While replacing Alito or Thomas with another conservative wouldn't change the court's 6-3 ideological balance, it would allow Trump to install a younger justice who could potentially serve for decades.

Judge James Ho of the 5th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals is viewed by conservatives as a possible top Supreme Court contender if a vacancy opens under Trump. Josh Hammer, a former clerk to Judge Ho, recently discussed retirement possibilities on his Newsweek podcast, stating, "I am simply saying that if you are being as risk-averse and cautious as possible, that this is as good a time as any for one of, potentially both of them, to step down."

Yoo expects conservative calls for retirements to remain muted unless Trump signals support. "I think they will not come out in public unless President Trump gives some signal — that has been the story with the MAGA world generally on most issues," he said.

## What to Watch For

Several milestones could influence retirement decisions in coming months. Thomas is on course next month to become the second-longest serving justice in Supreme Court history, after Justice William O. Douglas. He could set the record as longest-serving justice if he stays through spring 2028.

The court has two other septuagenarian members: Chief Justice John Roberts and Justice Sonia Sotomayor, both 71. None of the court's liberal justices — Sotomayor, Elena Kagan (65), and Ketanji Brown Jackson (55) — is expected to retire soon, nor are Trump's three appointees: Kavanaugh (61), Gorsuch (58), and Barrett (54).

As the November elections approach, Washington will watch closely for any signals from the court's oldest members. Their decisions could determine the ideological balance of the Supreme Court for a generation. The shadow of Justice Ginsburg's miscalculation serves as a cautionary tale about the risks of mistiming retirement in an increasingly politicized judicial landscape.
