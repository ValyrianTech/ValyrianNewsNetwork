# 1 in 5 Flemish Schools Fails Quality Standards as Education Declines

Nearly one in five schools in Flanders fails to meet basic educational quality standards, according to the 2026 Education Mirror report released by the Flemish education inspectorate. The alarming findings show that 18% of all inspected schools received an "unfavorable assessment," with the situation particularly dire in secondary education where nearly 25% of schools failed to meet standards. [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/01/onderwijsspiegel-2026-bijna-1-op-5-scholen-haalt-lat-niet/) reports that the results represent a worsening trend compared to previous years, raising serious concerns about the future of education in Belgium's northern region.

## Context: A 20-Year Decline

The 2026 report confirms a troubling pattern that education experts have warned about for years. Flemish Education Minister Zuhal Demir (N-VA) acknowledged that "education has been declining for 20 years" and that "this is a trend that cannot be easily reversed." The latest data comes from 621 full inspections and 598 interim visits conducted during the 2024-2025 school year, providing the most comprehensive assessment of educational quality in recent memory.

Historically, Flanders was known for its high-performing education system, but international assessments like PISA have shown declining student performance over the past two decades. The transition from knowledge-based to skills-based education, increasing administrative burdens on teachers, and societal changes affecting student behavior have all contributed to the systemic challenges facing Flemish schools.

## Key Findings and Structural Problems

According to the [Flemish Government's education inspection department](https://www.vlaanderen.be/onderwijsinspectie/nieuws/inspectie-stelt-onderwijsspiegel-2026-voor), the inspectorate identified several structural problems plaguing the education system. Schools struggle to translate their pedagogical vision into classroom practice, resulting in significant differences between teachers' approaches and effectiveness. The report also noted insufficient use of data in decision-making and evaluation, as well as student guidance that is not always targeted effectively.

"Schools know where they want to go, but they don't always succeed in bringing that vision into the classroom," the inspectorate stated in its official release. "A less well-developed policy is felt on the classroom floor—in the way of teaching, in evaluating students, and in guidance. This ultimately also impacts learning outcomes."

The full [Education Mirror 2026 report](https://data-onderwijs.vlaanderen.be/documenten/bestanden/onderwijsspiegel-2026.pdf) details how schools with unfavorable assessments receive mandatory guidance and follow-up inspections. Inspector-General Katrien Bonneux emphasized that "our education has many strengths" and that by "focusing on high expectations and clear goals, schools can make a difference."

## Political Reactions and Minister's Response

Education Minister Zuhal Demir responded candidly to the findings, telling VRT NWS that "the figures are worse than last year. We must not close our eyes to this." She attributed the poor results to policy changes that are only now being implemented, including new minimum goals for education, increased focus on order and calm in classrooms, and emphasis on effective didactics starting in September 2026.

"We have made radical choices with the Flemish government in the past half-year," Demir said, acknowledging criticism of her approach. "You cannot expect different results as long as that policy is not yet rolled out. And even after that, it still takes time, so I expect that we will get bad reports in the coming months and years."

The minister also addressed parents directly: "Take your responsibility too. Give basic upbringing at home, then we can build on that at school. And have confidence in teachers and schools."

## Opposition and Stakeholder Concerns

Political opposition parties expressed grave concerns about the findings. CD&V (Christian Democrats) member Loes Vandromme emphasized the importance of pedagogical guidance services, stating that "the difference they make is extremely important for our schools. They sit down together and think along with the school team. That yields the most sustainable results."

Groen (Greens) member Kim Buyst was more critical, calling it "time for a different approach" and stating that the N-VA "fails on education" after seven years of leading the education ministry.

The business community sounded perhaps the most alarming warning. Frank Beckx, managing director of Voka (Flemish employers' organization), called the decline in education quality "a ticking time bomb under Flemish prosperity." In comments reported by [HLN](https://www.hln.be/binnenland/op-bijna-een-op-de-vijf-vlaamse-scholen-schiet-onderwijskwaliteit-tekort-slechtere-cijfers-dan-vorig-jaar~aaa87625/), Beckx stated, "The focus of education must return to quality and knowledge acquisition. The business world is a partner to make specialized training more attractive and qualitative."

## Analysis: Implications for Flanders' Future

The deteriorating education quality has far-reaching implications for Flanders' economic competitiveness and social mobility. As the region's prosperity has historically been built on a well-educated workforce, continued decline threatens its position in the global knowledge economy. The business community's warning about a "ticking time bomb" reflects genuine concern about future workforce quality.

The political dimension is equally significant. With the N-VA having controlled the education portfolio for seven years, the opposition is likely to make education a central issue in upcoming elections. Minister Demir's admission that improvements may take years leaves her government vulnerable to criticism that it has failed to address a fundamental crisis.

Teacher morale represents another critical factor. Continued negative reports and public scrutiny may further demoralize teaching staff already struggling with administrative burdens ("planlast") and challenging classroom environments. The success of any reform will depend heavily on teacher buy-in and support.

## What's Next: Reform and Recovery

Looking forward, several key developments will determine whether Flanders can reverse its educational decline. The implementation of new minimum goals and teaching approaches starting in September 2026 represents the government's primary response. However, as Minister Demir acknowledged, results from these changes will take time to materialize.

Parents concerned about their children's schools can check performance data through the Flemish government's school performance portal. This transparency initiative allows families to make informed choices while holding schools accountable.

The inspectorate's recommendation for "near leadership"—directors who are visible on the classroom floor—suggests a shift toward more hands-on school management. By regularly observing classrooms, school leaders can more quickly identify what works and where adjustments are needed.

Ultimately, the 2026 Education Mirror serves as both a warning and a roadmap. While the data confirms a serious crisis, it also identifies specific areas for improvement: clearer implementation of pedagogical vision, more consistent teaching approaches, better use of data, and more targeted student guidance. Whether Flanders can translate these insights into meaningful change will determine the future of its education system—and by extension, its economic and social future.
