# US Labor Stable as Trade Deficit Widens Amid Iran War

New applications for U.S. unemployment benefits unexpectedly fell last week to near a two-year low, signaling continued labor market stability even as the nation's trade deficit widened in February amid ongoing geopolitical tensions. According to [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/us/us-weekly-jobless-claims-fall-layoffs-remain-low-2026-04-02/), weekly jobless claims dropped by 9,000 to a seasonally adjusted 202,000 for the week ended March 28, while the trade deficit expanded 4.9% to $57.3 billion. These mixed economic indicators emerge against the backdrop of a month-long U.S.-Israeli war with Iran that has sent oil prices soaring over 60% and erased $3.2 trillion from stock markets in March.

## Labor Market Resilience Amid Uncertainty

The latest labor market data reveals a "low-hire, low-fire" environment that has characterized the U.S. economy throughout 2026. Initial claims for state unemployment benefits fell to 202,000, well below economist forecasts of 212,000, and have moved within a narrow 201,000-230,000 range this year. Continuing claims, which serve as a proxy for hiring activity, increased 25,000 to 1.841 million for the week ended March 21, though they remain below last year's elevated levels.

This labor market stability persists despite significant headwinds. Private nonfarm payrolls have averaged just 18,000 jobs per month in the three months through February, reflecting what economists describe as lingering uncertainty caused by President Donald Trump's aggressive trade policies and reduced labor supply due to immigration restrictions. The Bureau of Labor Statistics [reported](https://www.reuters.com/business/us-job-openings-fall-february-hiring-lowest-since-pandemic-2026-03-31/) that job openings fell more than expected in February and hiring dropped to the lowest level in nearly six years.

## Trade Deficit Expansion and Policy Context

The U.S. trade picture presents a more complex story. February's trade deficit widened to $57.3 billion, less than economist forecasts of $61.0 billion but still representing a significant increase from January's revised $54.7 billion. Imports surged 4.3% to $372.1 billion, driven by a 5.0% increase in goods imports to $291.5 billion, while exports jumped 4.2% to a record high $314.8 billion.

This trade data comes amid ongoing volatility in U.S. trade policy. In February, the Supreme Court struck down Trump's broad tariffs imposed under national emergency powers, prompting the administration to impose a [temporary 10% global tariff](https://www.reuters.com/world/china/trump-orders-temporary-10-global-tariff-replace-duties-struck-down-by-us-supreme-2026-02-20/) for up to 150 days. Trump has defended these measures as necessary to address trade imbalances and revive the nation's industrial base, though 100,000 factory jobs have been lost since January 2025.

## Geopolitical Shock: Iran War Economic Impact

The month-long U.S.-Israeli conflict with Iran has introduced substantial economic volatility that threatens to undermine recent stability. According to [Reuters energy reporting](https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/oil-prices-fall-trump-pauses-attacks-iranian-energy-plants-2026-03-27/), Brent crude prices have surged over 60% since February 27, briefly approaching $120 per barrel, while the national average retail gasoline price topped $4 per gallon for the first time in more than three years.

The Strait of Hormuz closure on March 4 has stranded oil and liquefied natural gas exports, creating what the International Energy Agency has described as a crisis "worse than the two 1970s oil shocks combined." About 11 million barrels per day have been removed from global oil supply, with shipping restrictions affecting goods ranging from energy products to fertilizers.

"We expect weaker job growth and a higher unemployment rate for 2026 than we had been forecasting prior to the war," said Nancy Vanden Houten, lead U.S. economist at Oxford Economics. "But the war's impact on the labor market will take a bit more time to materialize."

## Economic Forecasts and Forward Outlook

The Atlanta Federal Reserve has already cut its first-quarter GDP growth estimate by 0.3 percentage points to a 1.6% annualized rate, reflecting concerns about the conflict's economic impact. Trade subtracted from GDP growth in the fourth quarter of 2025, when the economy expanded at a modest 0.7% pace, and may continue to be a drag in the current quarter.

Michael Gapen, chief economist at Morgan Stanley, noted that "the effect of the ongoing oil disruption will likely be evident in the March trade data," suggesting that February's figures may represent only the beginning of broader trade disruptions.

Economists warn that higher energy costs and stock market losses—approximately $3.2 trillion erased in March alone—will likely slow consumer spending, raise business costs, and further restrain hiring. The conflict has added "another layer of uncertainty for businesses, which were trying to navigate a forever-shifting trade policy," according to Reuters analysis.

## Looking Ahead: March Employment Report

Attention now turns to the March employment report scheduled for release on April 3. Economists surveyed by Reuters forecast a rebound in nonfarm payrolls of 60,000 following February's unexpected drop of 92,000 jobs, which was partly attributed to a healthcare worker strike and harsh weather. The unemployment rate is expected to hold steady at 4.4%.

While the labor market has demonstrated remarkable resilience in the face of multiple challenges, the combined effects of trade policy uncertainty, energy price shocks, and geopolitical conflict present significant tests for the U.S. economy. The coming months will reveal whether domestic economic fundamentals can withstand these external pressures or whether the convergence of these factors will push the economy toward more pronounced weakness.

As President Trump vowed "more aggressive strikes on Iran" on April 2, financial markets responded with losses, with the Dow Jones Industrial Average down approximately 600 points. The delicate balance between labor market stability and external economic shocks will likely define the U.S. economic trajectory through the remainder of 2026.
