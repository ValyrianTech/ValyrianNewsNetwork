# Medical Schools Replace D.E.I. with Nutrition Under Kennedy

Medical schools across the United States are undergoing a significant transformation in accreditation standards, shifting emphasis from diversity, equity, and inclusion (D.E.I.) initiatives toward nutrition education under pressure from political figures including Health Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. The changes represent a fundamental reorientation of medical education priorities and highlight growing political influence over academic standards through accreditation mechanisms.

## Accreditation Standards Overhaul

The Commission on Osteopathic College Accreditation (COCA), which accredits 46 osteopathic medical schools serving more than a quarter of U.S. medical students, has permanently removed D.E.I. requirements from its curriculum standards. According to [The New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/04/02/us/medical-schools-accreditation-rfk-jr.html), the accrediting agency is stripping diversity standards from its curriculum requirements and adding a focus on nutrition that tracks with Secretary Kennedy's agenda.

The D.E.I. provision requiring schools to "incorporate diversity, equity, and inclusion into its curriculum to the extent permitted by law" has been replaced with a requirement to "ensure that comprehensive evidence-based nutrition education" is part of curriculums. COCA initially suspended its D.E.I. rules in April 2025 until May 2026, making the recent change permanent.

## Political Context and Leverage

President Trump has called accreditation a "secret weapon" to change schools regarded as hostile to conservative values. Schools risk losing federal financial aid eligibility if they lose accreditation, giving accrediting agencies significant leverage over curriculum decisions. This represents a shift from traditional academic independence norms in American higher education.

"Academia has typically guarded its independence ferociously," the Times reported. "But the decision by the accrediting agency shows how the Trump administration is shaping academic life across the country."

Antoinette Flores, a senior Department of Education official during the Biden administration who is now director of higher education accountability at New America think tank, noted: "A lot of the things that we've seen the administration target individual colleges for, it appears that they're hoping to accomplish those things writ large through accrediting agencies."

## Nutrition Education Initiative

The accreditation changes align with a broader nutrition education initiative promoted by Secretary Kennedy. According to the Times, Kennedy announced in March 2026 that more than 50 medical schools would embrace a federal framework for nutrition education. The initiative is part of the Trump administration's "Make America Healthy Again" (MAHA) agenda.

Kennedy has emphasized the public health rationale for the shift, stating that chronic disease is bankrupting the health system and poor nutrition sits at the center of that crisis. The Department of Health and Human Services is dedicating $5 million through a National Institutes of Health nutrition education challenge to support curriculum development.

## Historical Context and Training Gaps

The move addresses a well-documented gap in medical education. A 2022 survey found medical students receive an average of just 1.2 hours of formal nutrition education per year, with only 14% of residency programs requiring nutrition curriculum. Three-fourths of U.S. medical schools do not require clinical nutrition courses.

Proponents argue that improved nutrition training could better equip doctors to address diet-related chronic diseases like diabetes, heart disease, and obesity. Many medical organizations have supported increasing nutrition education in medical training for years.

## Broader Implications

The shift away from D.E.I. requirements aligns with broader conservative efforts to eliminate diversity initiatives in education, which accelerated after the 2023 Supreme Court decision ending affirmative action in college admissions. Several states have passed laws restricting D.E.I. programs in public institutions.

Critics warn that reduced D.E.I. training might diminish physicians' ability to address health disparities in diverse populations. The removal of diversity requirements raises questions about how medical schools will continue to train doctors to understand and address health inequities related to race, ethnicity, socioeconomic status, and other social determinants of health.

## Institutional Responses

The nutrition initiative has attracted schools from both Republican and Democratic-leaning states, suggesting cross-partisan appeal of nutrition education. Participating institutions include University of Florida, Tulane, Dartmouth, University of Alabama, University of Kentucky, Texas Tech, UC Irvine, George Washington, NYU, and Tufts.

However, some elite institutions including Brown, Columbia, and Cornell universities have declined to participate despite previous funding agreements with the administration. This mixed response highlights the complex political landscape surrounding medical education reform.

## Looking Forward

The accreditation changes and nutrition initiative represent a fundamental reorientation of medical education priorities that could shape healthcare delivery for decades. The long-term impacts on physician training, healthcare disparities, and the doctor-patient relationship will unfold over coming years as these curriculum changes take effect.

Key questions remain about how the removed D.E.I. requirements will affect training on health disparities and cultural competency, what specific nutrition curriculum will be implemented, and whether allopathic (MD-granting) medical schools will follow the osteopathic schools' lead in removing D.E.I. requirements.

As medical schools implement these changes in the fall of 2026, educators, policymakers, and healthcare professionals will be watching closely to see how this significant shift in medical training priorities affects both physician education and patient care outcomes.
