# Engie May Operate Belgian Nuclear Reactors Beyond 2035

Belgian energy company Engie has signaled a significant policy shift by not ruling out the possibility of operating Belgium's two newest nuclear reactors, Doel 4 and Tihange 3, beyond their currently scheduled 2035 closure date. In an interview with [RTBF](https://www.rtbf.be/article/faire-tourner-les-reacteurs-nucleaires-de-doel-4-et-tihange-3-apres-2035-engie-ne-l-exclut-plus-11703923) on April 2, 2026, Engie Belgium CEO Vincent Verbeke stated that "a second extension is technically possible" and decisions must be made by 2029 at the latest. This marks a notable change from Engie's previous opposition to further nuclear extensions and reflects growing energy security concerns and political pressure from Belgium's new government.

## Context: Belgium's Nuclear Policy Evolution

Belgium's nuclear energy policy has undergone dramatic reversals in recent years. The country originally planned to phase out all nuclear power by 2025, but following energy security concerns after the Ukraine conflict, authorities extended the operation of Doel 4 and Tihange 3 until 2035. These two reactors—each with approximately 1,000 megawatts of capacity—represent Belgium's youngest and most powerful nuclear units, owned primarily by Engie's Electrabel (89.8%) with minority participation by EDF's Luminus (10.2%).

The political landscape shifted further with the 2024 elections, which brought the "Arizona coalition" government to power under Prime Minister Bart De Wever. This coalition—comprising N-VA, MR, Les Engagés, Vooruit, and CD&V—has made nuclear expansion a key policy priority. Their government agreement explicitly calls for "a minimum additional 10-year extension" beyond 2035 for the two reactors and aims to build new nuclear capacity, including small modular reactors (SMRs).

## Engie's Changing Position

Engie's evolution from outright opposition to cautious openness represents a significant corporate policy shift. In January 2025, Verbeke had called keeping nuclear power plants open even longer "unthinkable," drawing sharp criticism from then-Energy Minister Mathieu Bihet who described the comments as "unacceptable." 

However, by February 2026, Engie Group CEO Catherine MacGregor struck a more conciliatory tone, telling Reuters that the company was "cautiously optimistic" about discussions with the Belgian government and would consider studying the "economic, operational safety feasibility" of an extension, as reported by RTBF. MacGregor emphasized that Engie would only proceed with a "stable, clear and predictable" dismantling provision framework.

In his latest interview, Verbeke framed the current extension agreement as essential infrastructure: "What's important today is to ensure there's a bridge that allows us to envision a future for nuclear power in Belgium. And that bridge is the extension of Doel 4 and Tihange 3." He described the ongoing upgrade works as "gigantic" but necessary for Belgium's energy transition.

## Technical and Economic Considerations

The potential extension faces several technical and financial hurdles. Verbeke noted that decisions about a second extension must be made "at the latest around 2029"—five years before the scheduled 2035 closure—to allow sufficient time for necessary investments and regulatory approvals.

Financially, Engie has expressed concerns about decommissioning costs. The company disagrees with Belgian regulator's "conservative" estimates for dismantling the reactors and seeks clearer frameworks before committing to further extensions. Engie CFO Pierre-Francois Riolacci discussed these concerns in February 2026, emphasizing the need for financial predictability. A final report on decommissioning costs is expected by the end of April 2026.

Despite these challenges, Verbeke acknowledged the government's position: "It's above all a government wish. (...) In any case, as the main player in Belgium, we are always there, to be alongside the government."

## Broader Implications for European Energy

Belgium's nuclear reconsideration reflects broader European trends following the energy crisis triggered by Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Several European countries have reversed or delayed nuclear phase-outs, recognizing nuclear power's value as low-carbon baseload electricity that complements intermittent renewable sources.

The Belgian Nuclear Forum, an industry group, has supported the government's ambitions, noting in February 2025 that "if Engie maintains its stance against a further extension... the government will have to switch to an alternative plan... find another interested operator(s)." This suggests the government might consider alternative operators if Engie ultimately refuses further extension.

## What's Next for Belgian Nuclear Policy

The coming years will be critical for Belgium's energy future. Between now and 2029, Engie and the Belgian government must resolve several key issues:

1. **Technical feasibility studies** for extending reactor operations beyond 2035
2. **Financial agreements** on decommissioning costs and investment requirements
3. **Regulatory frameworks** for extended operation and potential new nuclear builds
4. **Integration planning** with renewable energy expansion and grid modernization

Verbeke emphasized that Doel 4 and Tihange 3 should "serve as a bridge" toward "a more reliable, more sovereign, more affordable and more sustainable energy system." This transition, he noted, requires greater electrification powered by renewables "or even new nuclear capacities."

## Conclusion

The potential extension of Doel 4 and Tihange 3 beyond 2035 represents more than just a technical decision about two reactors—it symbolizes a fundamental shift in Belgium's energy strategy. Driven by energy security concerns, political will, and evolving economic calculations, this development reflects Europe's broader reconsideration of nuclear power amid climate and security challenges.

While technical feasibility exists, financial arrangements and regulatory frameworks remain significant obstacles. The alignment between Engie's evolving corporate strategy and the Belgian government's ambitious nuclear plans suggests that further extensions are increasingly likely, though the exact terms and timing remain to be negotiated. As Europe continues to navigate its energy transition, Belgium's experience may offer lessons for other countries reconsidering their nuclear futures.
