# Bishop: Opera with Naked Nuns is 'Psychological Mockery'

Antwerp Bishop Johan Bonny has strongly criticized Opera Ballet Vlaanderen's production "Sancta," a feminist opera featuring naked nuns on roller skates and explicit sexual acts, calling it "psychological mockery as a form of violence." The controversial production, which premiered in Antwerp during Holy Week, has sparked a heated debate about artistic freedom versus religious sensibilities in Belgium.

According to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/01/operavoorstelling-sancta-kritiek-johan-bonny/), Bishop Bonny expressed particular concern about the timing of the production during Christianity's most sacred period. "I have nothing against naked ladies flying through the air," Bonny stated. "What concerns me is the identification with religious life. The actresses all have a nun's veil, which makes it clear that it's a religious community providing the spectacle."

## The Controversial Production

"Sancta," created by Austrian theatermaker Florentina Holzinger, is described as a "feminist high mass on roller skates" that combines opera, liturgical music, pop, and metal into what producers call a "radically new mass." The production is based on the 1922 scandal play "Sancta Suzanna," which tells the story of a nun unable to control her sexuality during prayer.

The opera features explicit content including naked nuns on roller skates, sexual acts, fake blood, piercings, and wound infliction. Opera Ballet Vlaanderen's website includes content warnings for nudity, explicit sexual acts, descriptions of sexual violence, and fake blood. The production previously caused controversy in Stuttgart, Germany in 2024, where eight visitors fainted and ten others reported feeling nauseous or shocked during performances.

## Religious Criticism and Double Standards

Bishop Bonny, in an op-ed published in De Standaard, argued that the production represents a double standard in how religious communities are treated in Belgium. "To the Jewish community here in Antwerp, police and army are sent to combat the slightest hint of possible anti-Semitism," Bonny wrote. "For the Muslim community, extreme vigilance is exercised to let Ramadan pass quietly. And rightly so, we support that too. But psychological mockery is also a form of violence."

The bishop particularly objected to what he called making money "on the backs" of elderly religious people. "All those elderly religious people will no longer react, they can no longer take to the streets, but it's too easy to make money and sell shows on their backs," he told VRT NWS.

## Political Dimension and Public Funding

The controversy has also entered the political arena, with Vlaams Belang politician Filip Dewinter criticizing the production as an "anti-Christian freak show" and "a prolonged provocation at the expense of the Flemish taxpayer." Dewinter questioned whether similar productions would be made about other religions, asking if this would "over pakweg de islam" (roughly translated as "about Islam, for instance").

As a publicly funded institution, Opera Ballet Vlaanderen's programming decisions have come under scrutiny, raising questions about whether taxpayer money should support art that offends religious communities.

## Artistic Defense and Invitation to Dialogue

Jan Vandenhouwe, artistic director of Opera Ballet Vlaanderen, defended the production and warned against what he called a "witch hunt" on critical or feminist art. "We must not fall back into a kind of witch hunt on art that is critical or feminist, or touches on difficult themes," Vandenhouwe stated.

He explained that the production engages with Christian imagery that has shaped Western art history. "The visual language of the Catholic faith has determined our Western art history," Vandenhouwe said. "If you go to museums and churches, you see those images of crucifixions, violence and blood too. It is those images with which Holzinger enters into dialogue."

The artistic director extended an invitation to Bishop Bonny to see the production for himself. "We keep a seat free for him, I would like to talk to him afterwards," Vandenhouwe offered. However, Bonny declined, stating, "I am not going to watch to respect my dignity. My thoughts are with the suffering and death of Jesus Christ this week, and all the people who are experiencing that in this world now."

## Historical Context and Feminist Perspective

Florentina Holzinger, described as a "phenomenon within the performing arts," is known for creating boundary-pushing performances with high production values that examine feminist perspectives on important works and social norms. "Sancta" aims to question the sexual oppression of women in religious contexts, particularly within the Catholic Church.

The production's inspiration from the 1922 play connects to a long history of artistic engagement with religious themes and sexual repression. Holzinger's work continues this tradition while employing contemporary performance techniques and explicit content to make its feminist critique.

## Broader Cultural Implications

The controversy highlights ongoing tensions in Belgian and European societies between artistic freedom and religious sensitivity. As societies become increasingly pluralistic, questions about the limits of artistic expression regarding religion continue to provoke debate.

The timing during Holy Week adds another layer to the controversy, with religious leaders arguing that respect for sacred periods should influence cultural programming decisions. Opera Ballet Vlaanderen has stated that the scheduling was coincidental due to international production logistics.

## What's Next

Despite the controversy, all performances of "Sancta" have sold out weeks in advance, indicating significant public interest in the production. The debate is likely to continue in Belgian cultural and political circles, potentially influencing future discussions about public funding for provocative art and religious sensitivity in cultural programming.

As the production continues its run in Antwerp, the broader questions it raises about artistic freedom, religious respect, and the role of publicly funded institutions in navigating these complex issues remain unresolved. The controversy serves as a case study in the ongoing negotiation between creative expression and community values in contemporary European society.
