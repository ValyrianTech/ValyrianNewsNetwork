# Inez Weski Denies Being Pass-Through for Ridouan Taghi

Prominent Dutch criminal defense lawyer Inez Weski has continued to deny allegations that she acted as a pass-through for her former client, convicted drug kingpin Ridouan Taghi, during an emotional court hearing in Rotterdam. Prosecutors have requested a 4.5-year prison sentence for the 71-year-old attorney, who faces charges of membership in a criminal organization for allegedly passing messages between Taghi and his associates while he was held in maximum security detention.

According to [NOS](https://nos.nl/artikel/2608784-weski-ontkent-opnieuw-tijdens-behandeling-van-haar-strafzaak), Weski told the court she cannot discuss her contact with Taghi due to her professional confidentiality obligations. "I'm not hiding behind my robe, as the OM maliciously claims," she stated. "I carry too many secrets and responsibility for lives. I cannot break that." The prosecution alleges Weski exchanged approximately 8,000 encrypted messages with Taghi's family and criminal associates, facilitating his drug trafficking and money laundering operations from prison.

## Context and Background

The case represents one of the most significant tests of attorney-client privilege in Dutch legal history. Weski, known as the "pitbull in toga" for her aggressive defense style during her 45-year career, represented Ridouan Taghi following his December 2019 arrest. Taghi, the alleged leader of the "Mocro Mafia" criminal network, was sentenced to life imprisonment in February 2024 for six murders and four attempted murders in the landmark Marengo trial.

Dutch law traditionally protects confidentiality between lawyers and clients, but prosecutors argue this privilege does not apply when the lawyer becomes a suspect themselves. The case unfolds against a backdrop of extreme violence in Dutch organized crime circles, including the 2019 murder of Taghi's previous lawyer, Derk Wiersum, which highlighted the dangers faced by attorneys representing high-profile criminals.

## Key Developments and Allegations

Prosecutors allege that Weski used a Sky ECC encrypted phone to communicate with Taghi's son Faissal and sister Nora, with the device consistently connecting to a cell tower near her home. NOS reports that messages allegedly concerned drug trafficking operations, money laundering, and maintaining Taghi's criminal organization while he was incarcerated in the Extra Beveiligde Inrichting (EBI) maximum security prison in Vught.

During Thursday's hearing, the court presented examples of intercepted messages that prosecutors believe demonstrate Weski's involvement. These included communications such as "I think it's best that the lawyer asks... [Taghi]" and "Can she tell my father that F. has been arrested." Another message stated, "That lawyer is acting strange, hasn't emailed once since she visited."

Weski became emotional during the proceedings, telling the court, "The past period was unbearable for me," according to [NU.nl](https://www.nu.nl/misdaad/6391342/inez-weski-in-tranen-ben-afgebrand-kapot-en-al-gekwalificeerd-als-doorgeefluik.html). "It's a kind of ring of fire that was only stoked further and you stand there in the middle and you actually think you can extinguish everything." She added, "I have been burned, broken and already qualified as a pass-through."

## Defense Arguments and Health Considerations

Weski's defense team, led by lawyers Geert-Jan and Carry Knoops, argues that the prosecution's case is incomplete and excludes exculpatory evidence. They point to intercepted messages showing Taghi's family complaining that Weski never communicated with them, with one relative stating, "She hasn't even let us know how he's doing."

The defense also highlights Weski's deteriorating health, with the 71-year-old attorney stating she has suffered "potentially fatal damage" from the ordeal. Prosecutors acknowledged her age and frail health in their sentencing recommendation but noted they did not consider alleged coercion in their request. They argued that Weski, as an experienced lawyer, should have sought help from the bar association or authorities if she felt threatened.

## Analysis: Testing Legal Boundaries

This case raises fundamental questions about the limits of attorney-client privilege in high-stakes criminal defense. Legal experts are watching closely as the court balances the need for lawyer-client confidentiality against allegations of criminal facilitation. The outcome could establish important precedents for how lawyers interact with clients accused of serious organized crime.

The prosecution's theory suggests Weski crossed from legitimate legal representation into active participation in criminal activities. However, the defense maintains that without access to all communications and proper context, the allegations represent a dangerous erosion of attorney-client protections that could undermine the justice system.

Ethical considerations are particularly complex given the violent context of Taghi's criminal network. Previous murders of legal professionals connected to the case create legitimate security concerns for defense attorneys, potentially complicating assessments of whether Weski acted under duress or voluntarily.

## What's Next

The trial will continue with Weski's defense presenting its case on subsequent hearing days. The court must rule on the admissibility of the approximately 8,000 encrypted messages as evidence, a decision that will significantly impact the proceedings. Both sides are expected to appeal regardless of the verdict, suggesting this legal battle may continue for years.

Beyond the immediate case, the Weski trial may prompt broader discussions about monitoring lawyer-client communications in high-security contexts and establishing clearer guidelines for attorneys representing clients accused of serious organized crime. The Dutch legal community awaits the outcome with particular interest, as it could redefine professional boundaries in one of Europe's most challenging criminal justice environments.

A final verdict is expected in the coming months, but the case's implications for attorney-client privilege and legal ethics in the Netherlands will likely resonate far longer.
