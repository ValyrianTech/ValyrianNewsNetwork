# One Year After Trump's 'Liberation Day' Tariffs: Flemish Businesses Struggle with €5.7 Billion Loss

One year after U.S. President Donald Trump announced sweeping "Liberation Day" tariffs on April 2, 2025, Flemish companies continue to grapple with the economic fallout, suffering €5.7 billion in losses despite a February 2026 Supreme Court ruling that declared the tariffs illegal. According to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/en/2026/04/02/liberation-day-one-year-on-flemish-companies-struggle-to-replac/), Flemish exports to the U.S. dropped by 21.6%, hitting small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) particularly hard and demonstrating how trade policy disruptions can cause lasting economic damage even after legal remedies are applied.

## The 'Liberation Day' Announcement and Its Aftermath

On April 2, 2025, President Trump stood in the White House Rose Garden and declared "Liberation Day," announcing sweeping import tariffs on goods from nearly every country the U.S. traded with. The initial 10% baseline tariff, followed by higher "reciprocal" rates of 11-50% based on trade deficit calculations, sent shockwaves through global markets. The policy was justified under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA), which the administration claimed gave the president authority to "regulate" trade during emergencies.

The immediate impact was dramatic. As [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/business/2026/apr/02/trump-liberation-day-us-tariffs-trade) reported, "Investors quickly understood that chaos was an essential tool in Trump's armoury." The dollar began a steady decline against other currencies as investors sold dollar-denominated assets and sought opportunities in Europe, Asia, and South America.

For Flemish businesses, the consequences were immediate and severe. "From one day to the next, even from one hour to the next, that market came to a complete standstill," Ingeborg D'hoker, owner of AncientVintageBeads in Antwerp, told VRT NWS. She lost 70% of her customers, most of whom were from the United States.

## Economic Impact on Flanders: The Numbers

Data from Flanders Investment & Trade reveals the scale of the damage:
- **21.6% drop** in Flemish exports to the United States
- **€5.7 billion total loss** for Flemish companies
- **5,617 Belgian companies** export to the U.S., with **92% being SMEs**

A study by the small business organization Unizo shows how SMEs attempted to adapt:
- **15%** cut costs since tariffs were imposed
- **11%** tried to renegotiate prices
- **14%** actively sought new markets
- **40%** indicate they will not be able to invest in coming months

"The most concerning figure relates to investments," said Bart Buysse of Unizo. "Forty percent of all SMEs indicate they will not be able to invest in the coming months."

## Case Studies: The Human Cost of Trade Policy

### AncientVintageBeads: 70% Customer Loss

Ingeborg D'hoker's Antwerp-based business, which sells antique and vintage beads mainly to jewelry designers and collectors, saw her U.S. market evaporate overnight. Before "Liberation Day," 70% of her customers were American.

"I mainly sell beads from Africa, and there's a strong connection between the U.S. and Africa," she explained. "That's why the market in the U.S. quickly grew larger than that in Europe."

The additional costs were prohibitive. "Due to import duties and fixed administrative costs, the price of an order could easily double," D'hoker said. She temporarily offered a 20% discount to cushion the blow but had to cover shipping expenses herself.

"Weekly sales used to be large, but now they've dropped to a minimum," she reported. "That amounts to thousands of euros in losses per month. Those customers aren't coming back."

### Lennertson Shoes: Navigating Uncertainty

Robin Carlo Kuijpers and Robin Todde's Genk-based shoe brand faced different challenges. As a direct-to-consumer company selling through their online store, they initially weren't subject to the new import tariffs. Yet they still saw immediate sales drops.

"The political turmoil caused by the Trump administration created a climate of fear and uncertainty," Kuijpers told VRT NWS. "American consumers have an ear for our brand, because online shopping is part of the culture."

After the de minimis exemption was abolished, American customers faced approximately $100 extra per pair of shoes. "We can't absorb these costs ourselves; otherwise, it becomes unprofitable," Kuijpers explained.

The company also encountered logistical challenges. "Customs and courier companies aren't quite sure where they stand," he noted. "They act like cowboys and do whatever they please. They don't apply the laws consistently."

## Supreme Court Intervention and Continued Uncertainty

On February 20, 2026, the U.S. Supreme Court delivered a 6-3 ruling that struck down the tariffs as illegal. Chief Justice John Roberts, joined by Trump appointees Neil Gorsuch and Amy Coney Barrett along with the court's three liberal justices, ruled that the IEEPA "does not authorize the president to impose tariffs."

"When Congress has delegated its tariff powers, it has done so in explicit terms and subject to strict limits," Roberts wrote in the opinion. "Had Congress intended to convey the distinct and extraordinary power to impose tariffs, it would have done so expressly."

As [BBC News](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cn8146l0n55o) reported, Trump responded by calling the Republican-appointed justices who voted against his policy "fools" and "lap dogs," saying they were "very unpatriotic and disloyal to our Constitution."

The president immediately imposed a new 10% global tariff under Section 122, a never-used law that allows tariffs up to 15% for 150 days before Congress must intervene. This new tariff took effect on February 24, 2026, maintaining trade barriers despite the court's ruling.

## Broader Economic Context: A Failed Policy

Economic analysts have been blunt in their assessment of the tariffs' effectiveness. "One year after liberation day, the evidence is in," said Bryan Riley, director of the National Taxpayers Union Foundation's free trade initiative. "Tariffs failed even by the Trump administration's own terms. They did not shrink the trade deficit, did not revitalise manufacturing and did not help farmers."

The Guardian's analysis revealed broader economic damage:
- U.S. manufacturing lost **100,000 jobs** between January 2025 and March 2026
- The ratio of manufacturing workers to total nonfarm employment fell to its **lowest point since 1939**
- The U.S. goods deficit expanded to an **all-time high in 2025**
- Consumer confidence reached **near record lows** by the end of 2025

Dario Perkins, head of global research at TS Lombard, offered a stark assessment: "If it was possible for Trump to have spent the last 14 months on the golf course, we would be in a better place."

## The Refund Battle and Legal Complications

The Supreme Court ruling opened the door to potentially billions of dollars in tariff refunds, but the process remains uncertain. Trump indicated that refunds would be "tied up in court for years," and the ruling didn't directly address the refund mechanism, leaving that question to the Court of International Trade.

Hundreds of firms, including Costco, Alcoa, and Bumble Bee, have filed lawsuits contesting the tariffs. California Governor Gavin Newsom demanded refunds "with interest," calling the tariffs an "illegal cash grab." Illinois Governor JB Pritzker sent Trump a $9 billion invoice, equivalent to $1,700 per family in the state.

Diane Swonk, chief economist at KPMG US, warned that litigation costs could make recouping funds difficult for smaller firms. "Unfortunately, I'd say curb your enthusiasm, although I understand the desire for relief," she said.

## European Response and Future Outlook

The European reaction has been measured. "We take note of the ruling by the U.S. Supreme Court and are analysing it carefully," said European Commission spokesman Olof Gill. French President Emmanuel Macron noted that the ruling showed the benefit of having "checks and balances in democracies."

For Flemish businesses, the future remains uncertain. While some companies like Lennertson see American customers returning as costs become clearer, others like AncientVintageBeads face permanent market loss.

"I've learned that the situation can change from one hour to the next," D'hoker reflected. "A few years ago, I would have panicked, but now I think: we'll see. It's become so unpredictable. We're waiting for the end of Trump's term, and then things might start moving more smoothly again."

Kuijpers of Lennertson acknowledged the cultural challenge of replacing the American market: "American consumers have a deeply ingrained culture of ordering online. We see results in other markets, but nowhere near enough to replace the American market."

## Conclusion: Lasting Damage and Policy Lessons

One year after "Liberation Day," the story of Trump's tariffs reveals several critical lessons about trade policy and economic resilience:

1. **Legal victories don't undo economic damage:** While the Supreme Court provided a check on presidential authority, it couldn't reverse the €5.7 billion loss suffered by Flemish businesses.

2. **SMEs are disproportionately vulnerable:** Small and medium-sized enterprises lack the resources to navigate sudden trade policy changes, making them particularly susceptible to market disruptions.

3. **Market losses can be permanent:** Customers who switch suppliers during trade disruptions often don't return, creating structural changes in global supply chains.

4. **Policy uncertainty has lasting effects:** Even after the Supreme Court ruling, the immediate imposition of new tariffs under different authority maintains uncertainty that continues to hinder business planning and investment.

The "Liberation Day" tariffs story underscores the complex interplay between political trade policy and economic reality. While courts can provide legal checks on executive authority, they cannot easily repair the economic damage inflicted on businesses and trading relationships. For Flemish companies still counting their losses one year later, the legacy of April 2, 2025, serves as a stark reminder of how quickly global trade relationships can change—and how slowly they recover.
