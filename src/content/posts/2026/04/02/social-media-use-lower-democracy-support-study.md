# Heavy Social Media Use Linked to Lower Democracy Support

A groundbreaking national study reveals a paradoxical relationship between social media usage and democratic values in the United States. According to research from the Charles F. Kettering Foundation and [Gallup](https://news.gallup.com/poll/704198/social-media-linked-mixed-views-democracy.aspx), Americans who spend five or more hours daily on social media platforms feel more politically empowered but simultaneously express significantly lower support for democracy as a system and greater acceptance of anti-democratic norms.

The nationally representative survey of 20,338 U.S. adults, conducted between July and August 2025, found that while 72% of non-social media users agree democracy is the best form of government, that figure drops to just 57% among heavy users. This 15-point gap persists even when controlling for age, income, and other demographic factors, suggesting social media itself may be shaping political attitudes in concerning ways.

## The Democratic Paradox

The study uncovers what researchers describe as a "democratic paradox." Heavy social media users report feeling more valued and politically effective than their offline counterparts—42% believe their experiences and beliefs are respected by others, compared to 31% of non-users. They also express greater confidence in citizen power, with 44% believing ordinary people can create meaningful change versus 30% of non-users.

Yet this heightened sense of political efficacy coexists with troubling attitudes toward democratic institutions. Heavy users are more likely to endorse political violence, reject compromise, support government influence over news media, and question whether every citizen should have voting rights. As [The Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/nation/2026/04/02/social-media-use-democracy-belief/) reported, "Americans who spend at least five hours a day on social media are more likely to feel heard, but also more open to political violence and less supportive of democracy."

## Erosion of Democratic Norms

The research documents a clear correlation between social media usage and the erosion of foundational democratic principles. Agreement that "every citizen should have the right to vote" declines from 78% among non-users to 69% among heavy users. Support for political compromise similarly diminishes with increased platform engagement.

Perhaps most alarmingly, heavy social media users show greater acceptance of anti-democratic measures. They are more likely to favor increased government control over news reporting, stronger executive power at the expense of legislative checks, and the use of violence for political goals. These findings arrive amid growing global concerns about democratic backsliding and the role digital platforms play in political polarization.

## Gender Differences in Platform Effects

The study reveals notable gender differences in how social media affects political attitudes. For men, heavier usage shows stronger connections to both feelings of political empowerment and departures from democratic norms. Male heavy users particularly demonstrate increased acceptance of political violence and rejection of compromise.

For women, the strongest relationship appears in evaluations of democracy itself. With rising social media usage, women become significantly less likely to agree that democracy represents the best form of government. This gender gap widens progressively with more hours of daily platform engagement, suggesting social media may affect political attitudes through different psychological or social pathways for different demographic groups.

## The Democracy for All Project Context

This research represents the second report in the multi-year "Democracy for All Project," a collaboration between Gallup and the Kettering Foundation aimed at understanding how Americans experience democracy across different groups and generations. The project's first report focused on how citizens view democracy as an ideal versus its current performance.

John Dedrick, Kettering Foundation Executive Vice President and COO, emphasized the study's significance: "Democracy begins with the people: the norms they share and the ways they participate every day. This study shows the multiple ways many citizens are participating—but too many still face obstacles preventing them from doing so."

## Algorithmic Amplification and Echo Chambers

While the study demonstrates correlation rather than causation, researchers point to several potential mechanisms driving these concerning trends. Social media algorithms that prioritize engaging—often extreme—content may create information ecosystems that reinforce anti-democratic attitudes. Platform design that fosters echo chambers and limits exposure to diverse perspectives could further polarize political views.

Additionally, the gamification of political discourse, reducing complex issues to simple "like/share" interactions, may contribute to a more transactional view of democracy. As [Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/03/31/democracy-social-media-heavy-users) noted, "Heavy social media users are less likely to think democracy is the best form of government and more likely to stray from democratic norms."

## Civic Education as Potential Counterweight

The research also highlights the protective role of civic education. Americans who received both formal civic instruction in school and informal education from parents or other adults show significantly higher engagement with democratic processes. They are more than twice as likely to volunteer and express stronger belief in citizen power compared to those with little civic education.

Justin Lall, principal at Gallup, noted the importance of addressing participation barriers: "This report makes clear that when Americans find ways to participate, whether through volunteering, attending community events, or learning about civics, they develop stronger confidence in democracy itself. The challenge is removing the barriers that keep too many people on the sidelines."

## Implications for Democratic Societies

The study's findings raise urgent questions for policymakers, educators, and platform designers. With approximately one in four Americans reporting little to no civic education, there appears to be significant opportunity to strengthen democratic resilience through educational initiatives. Simultaneously, platform design considerations—including algorithmic transparency and features promoting diverse perspective exposure—may help mitigate negative effects on democratic attitudes.

As [The Daily Beast](https://www.thedailybeast.com/heavy-social-media-users-warm-to-ditching-democracy/) summarized, "Heavy internet users are less committed to the idea of democracy, a new poll has found." The research underscores the complex relationship between digital platform engagement and political values in an era of unprecedented connectivity.

## Looking Forward

The Gallup/Kettering Foundation study provides compelling evidence that social media usage is associated with a paradoxical set of democratic attitudes—simultaneously increasing feelings of political efficacy while decreasing commitment to democratic norms. This presents a significant challenge for democratic societies navigating the digital transformation of public discourse.

Future research will need to establish causal direction—whether social media causes these attitudes or whether people predisposed to them are drawn to heavy platform usage. Platform-specific effects, content type influences, and longitudinal impacts also represent important areas for further investigation.

For now, the study serves as a critical reminder that digital platform design and civic education may play increasingly important roles in preserving democratic health in the 21st century. As social media continues to reshape how citizens engage with politics and each other, understanding its complex effects on democratic values becomes ever more urgent.
