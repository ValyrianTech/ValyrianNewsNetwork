# AI Helps Two Build $1.8 Billion Healthcare Company

Artificial intelligence has enabled two brothers to build Medvi, a telehealth company projected to reach $1.8 billion in sales this year, according to a [feature story in The New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/04/02/technology/ai-billion-dollar-company-medvi.html). The company's remarkable growth—from $401 million in revenue during its first full year to its current trajectory—demonstrates how AI tools are fundamentally reshaping entrepreneurship, allowing unprecedented efficiency and scale with minimal human resources.

## The AI-Powered Startup Journey

Matthew Gallagher, 41, launched Medvi from his Los Angeles home in early 2025 with just $20,000 and more than a dozen AI tools. According to the Times report, he used artificial intelligence to write the software code powering his company, produce website copy, generate images and videos for advertisements, and handle customer service. He also created AI systems to analyze business performance and outsourced tasks he couldn't manage himself.

The telehealth platform, which focuses on GLP-1 weight-loss drug prescriptions, gained 300 customers in its first month and added 1,000 more in its second month. After achieving $401 million in sales during 2025—its first full year of operation—Gallagher hired his only employee: his younger brother, Elliot. The two-person team now projects $1.8 billion in sales for 2026.

## Context: The Rise of AI-Native Entrepreneurship

Medvi's story exemplifies a broader trend toward "AI-native" startups where founders leverage artificial intelligence to replace traditional business functions. This development validates predictions made by industry leaders like Sam Altman, CEO of OpenAI, who said in 2024 that a one-person business worth $1 billion "would have been unimaginable without A.I., and now it will happen."

The healthcare technology sector has seen particular disruption from AI-driven models. The GLP-1 medication market—including drugs like semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy) and liraglutide (Saxenda)—has created substantial opportunities for telehealth providers. With brand-name medications costing $1,300 to $1,500 monthly without insurance, platforms offering compounded alternatives at lower prices have attracted significant demand.

## Business Model and Operations

Medvi operates through a three-entity model: MEDVi, LLC provides the technology platform for patient intake and administrative coordination; OpenLoop Health supplies licensed medical providers who evaluate patients and prescribe medications; and partner compounding pharmacies prepare and dispense the drugs. The company charges $179 for the first month of compounded GLP-1 injections and $299 for monthly refills, with all payments processed through Stripe and no insurance accepted.

What makes Medvi particularly notable is its extreme operational efficiency. While traditional healthcare companies might employ hundreds or thousands for similar revenue levels, Medvi's entire operation runs with just the Gallagher brothers. AI handles everything from software development and marketing to customer service and business analytics.

## Implications for the Future of Work

The Medvi case study raises important questions about the future of work in an AI-augmented economy. As The New York Times notes, big technology companies including Pinterest and Block have [cited AI efficiencies when cutting thousands of workers](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/02/26/technology/block-square-job-cuts-ai.html) in recent months. Medvi represents the logical extreme of this trend—a company achieving billion-dollar scale with virtually no traditional workforce.

This development presents both opportunities and challenges. For entrepreneurs, AI dramatically lowers barriers to entry, enabling rapid scaling with minimal capital and personnel. For workers, however, it suggests potential displacement in functions that AI can automate, from customer service and marketing to software development and data analysis.

## Regulatory and Healthcare Considerations

Medvi operates in the complex, highly regulated healthcare sector, which presents unique challenges. The company navigates different regulatory pathways for compounded medications (under FDA Sections 503A and 503B) compared to FDA-approved drugs. The agency has issued warnings about dosing errors with compounded semaglutide, highlighting quality control concerns that AI-driven platforms must address.

Telehealth regulation continues to evolve, with increasing scrutiny on patient safety, data privacy, and clinical outcomes. As AI assumes more responsibilities in healthcare delivery, regulators face the challenge of ensuring appropriate oversight without stifling innovation that could improve access and affordability.

## Analysis: Beyond the Hype

While Medvi's financial achievements are impressive, several questions remain unanswered. The company's revenue figures, though reportedly verified by The New York Times through access to company data, would benefit from independent audit. Profitability—distinct from revenue—hasn't been disclosed, leaving uncertainty about the sustainability of the business model.

Long-term scalability represents another consideration. Whether a two-person team can sustainably manage $1.8 billion in operations remains untested, particularly as regulatory complexity increases and competitive responses intensify. Established healthcare companies may develop their own AI capabilities or acquire successful startups, potentially changing the competitive landscape.

Patient outcomes represent the most critical unknown. While Medvi provides access to weight-loss medications, comprehensive data on clinical effectiveness, safety, and patient satisfaction haven't been publicly released. As with any healthcare innovation, ultimate success depends on improving patient health rather than merely achieving financial scale.

## What's Next for AI-Driven Entrepreneurship

Medvi's story likely represents just the beginning of AI's transformation of business creation and scaling. As AI tools become more sophisticated and accessible, we can expect more entrepreneurs to leverage them across diverse sectors. The healthcare industry, with its complex regulations and data-intensive nature, may see particularly significant disruption.

The coming years will test whether Medvi's model proves sustainable and replicable. Key developments to watch include regulatory responses to AI-driven healthcare, competitive moves by established companies, and whether similar efficiency gains emerge in other industries. Additionally, societal discussions about the future of work, income distribution, and economic structure will intensify as AI enables more companies to achieve massive scale with minimal human labor.

Medvi demonstrates that AI's impact extends far beyond product development to fundamentally reshape how businesses are built, operated, and scaled. As this case study shows, the technology isn't merely automating tasks—it's enabling entirely new business paradigms that challenge traditional assumptions about entrepreneurship, workforce requirements, and industry structure.
