# EncroChat Leader Gets 15 Years in Prison

Abdelwahab G., a 58-year-old central figure in an international criminal organization uncovered through the decryption of EncroChat and Sky ECC encrypted messaging services, has been sentenced to 15 years in prison by the Brussels Court of Appeal. The sentence, delivered on Thursday, represents a reduction from the 17-year term imposed in the first instance trial, according to [RTBF Info](https://www.rtbf.be/article/proces-encro-abdelwahab-g-condamne-a-15-ans-de-prison-par-la-cour-d-appel-de-bruxelles-11704188).

The case is part of Belgium's largest-ever drug trafficking prosecution, involving a sophisticated criminal network that imported narcotics from Latin America. The court also ordered the confiscation of assets equivalent to 2 million euros—significantly less than the 15 million euros imposed initially—and imposed a fine of 640,000 euros with a three-month subsidiary prison sentence if unpaid.

## Context: The EncroChat and Sky ECC Takedowns

The sentencing marks another milestone in the ongoing "Procès Encro" (Encro Trial) series. The EncroChat network provided modified Android phones guaranteeing anonymity, used almost exclusively by criminal organizations until French authorities intercepted messages starting in early 2020.

When EncroChat was compromised in June 2020, criminal networks migrated to Sky ECC. Belgian police cracked that encryption in March 2021, monitoring approximately 3 million messages daily, focusing on Antwerp—Europe's major drug entry point.

The coordinated international operation has led to over 6,500 arrests, the seizure of more than 100 tonnes of cocaine, and collective prison sentences exceeding 7,000 years across Europe.

## The Case Against Abdelwahab G.

Abdelwahab G. was convicted of importing narcotics from Latin America and managing the logistics of the criminal network. Federal Prosecutor Julien Moinil had requested a 20-year sentence due to multiple prior convictions, but the appeal court settled on 15 years.

According to court documents, the defendant acknowledged providing logistical support to Eridan M.G., considered the main leader of the organization. His defense team contested the "number two" characterization, arguing for proportionate sentencing.

The reduction in both prison time and financial penalties reflects the appeal court's reassessment of his precise role. The 2 million euro confiscation represents a dramatic reduction from the initial 15 million euro penalty.

## Related Case: Kamel A.'s Reduced Sentence

In a related ruling, Kamel A. had his sentence reduced from 14 to 9 years. The Brussels Court of Appeal determined he was a "decision-maker" rather than a "leader" of the organization.

Kamel A. was also ordered to forfeit assets equivalent to 750,000 euros and pay a fine of 520,000 euros. This distinction highlights the judicial system's nuanced approach to assessing responsibility within complex criminal networks.

## Analysis: Legal and Investigative Implications

The EncroChat and Sky ECC cases represent a watershed moment in combating organized crime digitally. The technical sophistication required to penetrate "impenetrable" encrypted networks demonstrates significant investigative advancement.

However, the cases have raised legal questions about evidence admissibility. Defense lawyers have challenged intercepted data integrity, with some cases adjourned due to evidentiary disputes. The legality of using hacked encrypted data may reach the European Court of Justice.

From a criminal justice perspective, the sentencing reductions illustrate how courts distinguish between responsibility levels within criminal organizations while maintaining substantial penalties for leaders.

## The Broader Impact on Organized Crime

Despite massive arrests, the fight against organized crime remains ongoing. The professionalization of drug trafficking networks and their ability to adapt to new technologies present continuous challenges.

The operations exposed not only drug trafficking but also planned torture, kidnapping, and contract killings. They revealed corruption within law enforcement, with officers implicated in leaking information.

By October 2024, the Sky ECC case alone had resulted in 119 convictions with collective prison sentences totaling over 700 years—Belgium's largest drug trial.

## What's Next

The sentencing represents progress in holding EncroChat/Sky ECC participants accountable, but numerous related cases remain pending. Law enforcement continues to analyze approximately 1 billion messages from 70,000 phones.

Internationally, these operations have prompted discussions about regulatory frameworks for encrypted services and improved cross-border cooperation. The technological arms race between criminals and law enforcement continues.

For Belgium, the cases highlight both organized crime's scale and the justice system's response capabilities. The balance between effective law enforcement and digital rights protection remains subject to ongoing debate.
