# US Tariffs Continue to Impact Flemish Businesses One Year On

Flemish businesses continue to face severe economic consequences from US import tariffs one year after their implementation, with exports to the United States falling by 21.6% and representing a €5.7 billion loss. Despite a February 2026 US Supreme Court ruling that declared the original tariffs illegal, immediate presidential action created a new tariff regime, perpetuating uncertainty for European exporters. Small and medium enterprises (SMEs), which constitute 92% of affected Belgian companies exporting to the US, are bearing the brunt of the impact according to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/01/jaar-na-liberation-day-impact-amerikaanse-invoertarieven-vlaamse/).

## Context and Timeline

The trade disruption began on April 2, 2025, when then-US President Donald Trump announced new global import tariffs on what he termed "Liberation Day." The measures took effect three days later, triggering reciprocal tariffs and creating what businesses describe as a "period of chaos and uncertainty" for the global economy. In February 2026, the US Supreme Court ruled that tariffs imposed under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) were illegal, offering legal recourse for tariff refunds.

However, on the same day as the Supreme Court decision, Trump issued a new tariff proclamation under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, establishing a 10% additional tariff on most-favored-nation rates. The president subsequently announced on social media that the rate would be 15%, though the Turnberry Agreement from July 2025 caps total EU-US tariffs at 15%.

## Economic Impact Statistics

The financial toll on Flemish businesses has been substantial. Data from Flanders Investment & Trade reveals the 21.6% decline in exports translates to €5.7 billion in lost revenue. Of the 5,617 Belgian companies exporting to the US, 92% are SMEs, making them particularly vulnerable to trade disruptions.

Sectoral analysis shows pharmaceuticals as the most affected industry, with €15.24 billion in 2024 exports facing potential €2.29 billion in tariffs—a dramatic increase from previous €300,000 in tariff costs. The chemicals sector has seen a 30% decline in exports to the US, while automotive and machinery businesses report significant supply chain disruptions.

## Business Case Studies

The human impact of these trade measures is evident in businesses like AncientVintageBeads, an Antwerp-based retailer of antique and vintage beads. Owner Ingeborg D'hoker told VRT NWS that her company lost 70% of its US customers overnight when the tariffs were implemented. "From day to day, even from hour to hour, that market completely stalled," she said. "I lost about 70% of my customers."

D'hoker initially absorbed the costs by giving customers a 20% discount, but additional expenses forced her to subsidize shipments personally. "In the beginning, you had to send packages via DDP, where I paid all import duties for the customer," she explained. "Those costs were so variable that I could hardly absorb them and had to add money myself."

The company now focuses more on the European market but faces limitations. "That's not such a big market," D'hoker noted. "The American market is much larger and they bought larger quantities." She estimates monthly losses in the thousands of euros.

Another affected business is Lennertson, a Genk-based direct-to-consumer shoe brand. While initially exempt from tariffs, the company saw immediate sales declines due to what co-founder Robin Kuijpers described as "a climate of fear and uncertainty" among American customers. After the abolition of the de minimis exemption, US customers now pay approximately $100 extra per pair of shoes.

"We notice that customs and courier companies don't really know where they stand," Kuijpers told VRT NWS. "They seem like cowboys and they just do whatever. They don't apply consistent laws."

## Business Adaptation and Challenges

According to a survey by Unizo, the Flemish entrepreneurs' organization, 15% of businesses have implemented cost-saving measures since the tariffs began, while 11% have renegotiated prices with partners. Most concerning, 40% of SMEs indicate they cannot invest in the coming months.

Market diversification presents its own challenges. While 14% of Flemish SMEs are actively seeking new markets, European alternatives cannot fully replace the US market. "The reality is that Europe is not an integrated consumer market," Kuijpers observed. "There is a big cultural difference. A German doesn't just order something from a Belgian website."

He contrasted this with American consumers who have "a deeply ingrained culture of ordering from a distance." While Lennertson has achieved results in other markets, Kuijpers acknowledged they are "nowhere near enough to replace the American market."

## Legal and Political Context

The Supreme Court's February 2026 ruling declared that IEEPA does not authorize presidential tariff imposition, affecting "Fentanyl" tariffs on China, Canada, and Mexico; reciprocal tariffs; "free speech" tariffs on Brazil; and secondary tariffs on India. However, Section 232 tariffs on steel, aluminum, and copper (50% duties) were excluded from the decision.

The new tariff regime under the Trade Act of 1974 lasts 150 days initially and requires Congressional approval for extension. Exceptions include energy products, certain metals, and critical minerals. For EU companies, the Turnberry Agreement means the full 15% cannot be reclaimed if the most-favored-nation tariff is greater than zero, and no additional duties apply if MFN tariffs are 15% or higher.

## Outlook and Implications

The ongoing uncertainty continues to affect business planning and investment. D'hoker of AncientVintageBeads expressed a pragmatic outlook: "I've learned that the situation can change from hour to hour. A few years ago I would have panicked, but now I think: we'll see. It has become so unpredictable. We're waiting for the end of Trump's term and then maybe things will go more smoothly."

For the broader Flemish economy, projections from October 2025 suggested potential 2% GDP reduction, with Brussels-Capital Region and Flanders most affected. Long-term risks include structural export reductions of up to 26% for certain sectors and potential permanent loss of US market share.

As businesses navigate refund procedures for illegal tariffs while adapting to the new regime, the fundamental challenge remains: balancing immediate survival with strategic positioning for whatever trade environment emerges after the 2028 US presidential election.
