# Trump Vows More Iran Attacks, Oil Spikes, Markets Tumble

President Donald Trump's prime-time address on the Iran war triggered immediate economic turmoil on Thursday, with oil prices surging and global stock markets tumbling as he vowed to intensify attacks while declaring U.S. military objectives "nearing completion." The speech, delivered from the White House on Wednesday evening, offered no clear timeline for ending the conflict and placed responsibility for reopening the critical Strait of Hormuz on U.S. allies, drawing sharp international criticism and deepening economic uncertainty.

According to [The New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/live/2026/04/02/world/iran-war-trump-news), Trump declared the U.S. would hit Iran "extremely hard over the next two to three weeks" and threatened to bring the country "back to the Stone Ages where they belong." Despite claiming military success, the president provided no specific end date for the war that began on February 28 with U.S.-Israeli air strikes, leaving markets jittery about prolonged disruption to global energy supplies.

## Economic Fallout and Market Reactions

The economic impact was immediate and severe. Brent crude oil prices surged 6-8% to around $107-$109 per barrel following the address, while U.S. stock markets opened sharply lower with the S&P 500 falling as much as 1.5% before a partial recovery. Asian markets bore the brunt of the selloff, with South Korea's Kospi index sliding 4% and Japan's Nikkei dropping 2.4%, according to [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/china/global-markets-wrapup-1-2026-04-02/).

The Washington Post reported that European gas prices rose and stock markets traded around 1% lower, while U.S. mortgage rates hit 6.46%, their highest level since September. The average gasoline price reached $4.08 per gallon, a 37% increase since the war began, adding to inflationary pressures that the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development predicts will push U.S. inflation to 4.2% this year.

## International Criticism and Diplomatic Fallout

The address drew sharp criticism from U.S. allies, particularly French President Emmanuel Macron, who accused Trump of "shifting goals every day" and weakening the NATO alliance. "When we're serious, we don't say the opposite of what we said the day before every day, and maybe one shouldn't speak every day," Macron told reporters during a trip to South Korea, as reported by The New York Times.

Britain responded by hosting a virtual meeting of more than 30 countries—excluding the United States—to discuss contingency plans for maintaining oil flows through the Strait of Hormuz. British Foreign Secretary Yvette Cooper accused Iran of hijacking "an international shipping route to hold the global economy hostage," noting that maritime traffic through the strait had shrunk from about 150 vessels daily to just 25, with approximately 2,000 ships stranded nearby.

## The Strait of Hormuz Crisis

At the heart of the economic crisis is Iran's effective blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, a critical chokepoint that normally handles about 20% of global oil consumption. Trump's address placed responsibility for reopening the waterway on other nations, telling countries that rely on Gulf oil to "build up some delayed courage" and secure the passage themselves.

"The countries of the world that do receive oil through the Hormuz Strait must take care of that passage," Trump said, according to The Washington Post. "They must cherish it. They must grab it and cherish it. Go to the strait and just take it, protect it, use it for yourselves."

European allies have rejected this approach, with Macron calling a military operation to force open the strait "unrealistic" and saying it would expose ships to Iranian attacks. Instead, European nations are pursuing diplomatic solutions and have stated they will only help secure the strait after a ceasefire is established.

## Domestic Political Context

Trump's address came amid growing domestic skepticism about the war. An AP-NORC poll conducted in mid-March found that 59% of Americans believe U.S. military action against Iran has been excessive, while 45% are "extremely" or "very" concerned about affording gasoline in the coming months, as reported by [Associated Press](https://apnews.com/article/trump-iran-war-address-to-nation-patience-940c2cd13a8c45f9d6d35a4750b7b499).

Senator Chris Murphy (D-Conn.) criticized the speech as "grounded in a reality that only exists in Donald Trump's mind," while the president's overall approval rating has fallen to 36%, its lowest since his return to the White House, according to a Reuters/Ipsos poll.

## Iranian Defiance and Regional Escalation

Iran responded to Trump's threats with defiance. Esmail Baghaei, a spokesman for Iran's foreign ministry, stated on state television that "we will not tolerate this vicious cycle of war, negotiations, cease-fire and then repeating the same pattern." Iranian military officials warned of "more crushing, broader and more destructive" attacks in store for U.S. and Israeli targets.

The conflict has expanded regionally, with Hezbollah firing approximately 100 rockets at Israel in the past 24 hours and Houthi militants in Yemen launching missiles toward Israel. The U.S. embassy in Baghdad warned American citizens to leave Iraq, anticipating attacks by Iran-allied militia in the coming days.

## Analysis: Economic and Geopolitical Implications

The immediate market reaction underscores the fragile state of the global economy and its vulnerability to Middle East instability. The surge in oil prices threatens to reignite inflationary pressures just as central banks were gaining control, potentially forcing them to maintain higher interest rates for longer.

Geopolitically, the address has widened cracks in the NATO alliance, with Trump repeatedly criticizing European nations for not offering unflinching support. His administration has suggested the U.S. could pull support for NATO and weapons for Ukraine if European leaders do not acquiesce to his demands, creating the most significant strain in transatlantic relations in decades.

The Strait of Hormuz situation presents a particularly complex challenge. Even if a ceasefire is achieved, reopening and securing the waterway will require substantial naval assets for demining and deterrence, a task that European nations seem reluctant to undertake without U.S. leadership.

## What's Next

Several developments will shape the coming weeks:

1. **UN Security Council Action:** A vote is scheduled on a Bahrain-drafted resolution authorizing military force to open the Strait of Hormuz, supported by Arab Gulf states but facing uncertain prospects.

2. **Allied Coordination:** Britain will host military planners from about 30 nations next week to discuss "viable options to make the Strait of Hormuz accessible and safe" for shipping.

3. **Economic Containment:** Asian and European governments are implementing measures to offset the oil price shock, with India's central bank already intervening to stabilize its currency.

4. **Domestic Pressure:** Trump faces the 60-day mark under the War Powers Act when he must seek congressional approval to continue military operations, potentially testing his executive authority.

As the conflict enters its second month with no clear exit strategy, the economic and geopolitical consequences continue to mount. The disconnect between Trump's declaration of imminent victory and the reality of ongoing military escalation and economic disruption suggests further volatility ahead for global markets and international relations.
