# House Delays DHS Funding Vote, Extending Record Government Shutdown

The U.S. House of Representatives declined to take up Department of Homeland Security spending legislation on Thursday, extending the longest partial government shutdown in American history. The move came despite a Senate-passed compromise and a deal between Republican leaders, leaving tens of thousands of DHS employees unpaid since mid-February and highlighting deep divisions within the Republican Party over immigration enforcement funding.

## Context and Background

The shutdown, now entering its seventh week, stems from a fundamental conflict over immigration policy during President Trump's second term. According to [The New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/live/2026/04/02/us/trump-news), Democrats have refused to support funding for immigration enforcement agencies without reforms following the fatal shooting of two American citizens by federal immigration agents in Minneapolis earlier this year. The proposed restrictions included banning officers from wearing masks, requiring body cameras, and mandating judicial warrants for home entries.

Republicans, meanwhile, have sought unrestricted funding for aggressive immigration enforcement through the end of Trump's presidency. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) and Border Patrol operations have continued throughout the shutdown using a "slush fund" from the 2025 tax cut bill, allowing Republicans to claim operations aren't affected while Democrats argue the funding is improper.

## Key Developments

The Senate moved early Thursday to try again to reopen the Department of Homeland Security, sending spending legislation to the House about 90 minutes before the chamber's brief ceremonial session. However, House Republicans opted not to take up the bill, leaving a quick resolution to the stalemate out of reach for now.

Hard-right House Republicans remain infuriated about the deal, which omits money for immigration enforcement. As The New York Times reported, Speaker Mike Johnson called the Senate plan "a joke" last week before abruptly endorsing it on Wednesday after President Trump appeared to warm to the idea.

In a social media post on Thursday, President Trump announced he would "soon sign an order to pay ALL of the incredible employees at the Department of Homeland Security," claiming he was forced to do so "Because the Democrats are fully and 100% committed to the Radical Left Policy of Open Borders and Zero Immigration Enforcement."

The White House Office of Management and Budget declared in a memo distributed to lawmakers that existing funds are sufficient to "fully fund all ICE and Border Patrol operations, activities and employees," and that the administration had authority to move money around to do so.

## Impact on Government Operations

The shutdown has caused significant disruptions across homeland security agencies:

- **TSA Operations**: 50,000 Transportation Security Administration officers began receiving pay on Monday after Trump signed an executive order last week. Prior to this, daily absences exceeded 10%, causing chaos and long security lines at airports.
- **Unpaid Employees**: Tens of thousands of DHS civilian employees at Coast Guard, CISA, FEMA, and other agencies have not been paid since February.
- **Law Enforcement**: Approximately 80,000 sworn law enforcement agents (Secret Service, immigration officers) have continued receiving pay throughout the shutdown.

## Political Dynamics and Analysis

The failure to act exposes deep rifts within the Republican Party. Senate Republicans pragmatically accepted a compromise, while House Republican leadership initially rejected then accepted the deal. Hard-right members continue to oppose it, reflecting ongoing tensions between governing and ideological purity.

Senator John Thune (R-SD), the majority leader, told reporters, "I don't know the particulars around what the House will do with it, but my assumption is at some point hopefully they will move it." He rejected Democratic claims of victory, insisting Republicans had won the standoff by holding firm against any restrictions on federal immigration agents. "They got none of that," Thune said of Democrats. "They got zero of the reforms that they were advocating for."

Democrats, meanwhile, placed blame squarely on House Republicans. Senator Chuck Schumer (D-NY), the minority leader, said in a statement, "House Republicans own the longest government shutdown in history. The deep division and dysfunction among House Republicans is needlessly extending the D.H.S. shutdown and hurting federal workers who are missing another paycheck."

## What's Next

The House's next opportunity to act on the measure would be Monday, when the chamber is scheduled to hold another ceremonial session. However, with the House in a two-week recess until the week of April 13, any one lawmaker can block efforts to pass the bill unanimously during brief procedural sessions.

According to a Republican lawmaker who participated in a raucous conference call with Speaker Johnson on Thursday afternoon, far-right lawmakers vented about his plan to take up the Senate bill. The House is not planning to return next week to consider the legislation, meaning the shutdown is expected to continue until at least the week of April 13.

Republicans plan to use the budget reconciliation process later this spring to skirt the filibuster and push through legislation providing money for ICE and Border Patrol over Democratic opposition. However, this process is highly complex and faces challenges given the small Republican margin in the House and the likelihood that lawmakers will be eager to add other priorities to the filibuster-proof legislation.

## Conclusion

The House's failure to act on DHS funding represents more than just a procedural delay—it highlights the enduring political fault lines over immigration policy that continue to paralyze Washington. As the shutdown becomes the longest in U.S. history, the fundamental question remains whether Republicans can bridge their internal divisions or whether executive action will become the new normal for funding critical government functions. With midterm elections approaching, both parties are calculating the political costs of a prolonged shutdown that leaves homeland security agencies in limbo and thousands of federal employees without pay.
