# Judges Increasingly Use AI to Draft Rulings and Prepare for Hearings

A growing number of federal judges across the United States are turning to artificial intelligence to assist with judicial duties, from drafting rulings to preparing for hearings, according to a [Northwestern University study](https://news.northwestern.edu/stories/2026/03/northwestern-study-finds-a-significant-number-of-federal-judges-are-already-using-ai-tools/) published in March 2026. The research found that 60% of surveyed federal judges have used AI tools in their judicial work, marking a significant shift in how the legal system operates amid ongoing debates about transparency, accountability, and potential biases in AI systems.

## Study Reveals Widespread Judicial AI Adoption

The Northwestern study, conducted in December 2025 and published by the Sedona Conference, surveyed 112 federal judges from a random sample of 502. While 60% reported using AI at least occasionally, only 22% use AI tools daily or weekly in their judicial duties. The most common applications include legal research (30%), reviewing documents (15.5%), drafting rulings and orders, preparing for hearings, and analyzing court filings.

Judicial policies on AI use vary significantly across courts. According to the study, 25.9% of judges permit AI use in their chambers, while 7.4% actively encourage it. Conversely, 20% formally prohibit AI use, 17.6% discourage but don't formally prohibit it, and 24.1% have no official policy. A notable training gap exists, with 45.5% of judges reporting that AI training was not provided by court administration.

## Judges on the Frontlines of AI Integration

Several judges have emerged as early adopters of AI technology in their judicial work. Judge Xavier Rodriguez of the U.S. District Court for the Western District of Texas, who co-authored the Northwestern study, uses AI to create case timelines, suggest questions for hearings, identify weaknesses in arguments, and draft rulings.

"My law clerks would be wasting 30, 45 minutes, an hour, developing a chronology of events. This thing does it instantaneously," Rodriguez [told The Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/nation/2026/04/02/judges-ai-hearings-rulings/). He emphasized that AI serves as an augmentation tool rather than a replacement: "I'm doing my own preparation. I'm not strictly relying on an AI tool. It's just an extra set of eyes."

Other judges are exploring AI's potential in different contexts. Judge Samantha Jessner of the Los Angeles County Superior Court uses AI as a research aide to identify controlling authorities, while Judge Christopher Patterson, chief judge of Florida's 14th Circuit Court, employs AI to analyze whether legal claims are supported by good law.

"We're very cautious about this, obviously," Patterson said. "We're just trying to learn the AI tool - is this right for us, and is it accurate, and is it saving [time]?" He stressed the importance of human oversight: "It's the human oversight that we just can't do away with. That's the message that I've got to my judges, to my staff attorneys. Don't let [AI] substitute your judgment for the tool's judgment."

## Court Systems Formalize AI Partnerships

The judicial adoption of AI is becoming increasingly institutionalized. In March 2026, the Los Angeles County Superior Court announced a pilot program with Learned Hand, a legal AI startup whose technology is already used in trial courts in 10 states and the Michigan Supreme Court. Meanwhile, legal information giants Thomson Reuters and LexisNexis have contracts to provide AI tools to the federal judiciary.

Daniel Linna, a Northwestern law professor and co-author of the study, sees both potential and risk in judicial AI use. "Judges see the potential here. Of course there's risks, but there are benefits if we get this right," he [told Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/legal/government/majority-us-federal-judges-are-using-ai-study-finds-2026-03-30/).

## Concerns About AI Hallucinations and Accountability

The legal profession has faced numerous instances of AI "hallucinations"—where AI tools generate false citations, cases, or legal reasoning. A 2024 Stanford University study found that legal AI tools from LexisNexis and Thomson Reuters made mistakes in 17-33% of queries. In 2025, two federal judges—Henry T. Wingate of the Southern District of Mississippi and Julien Xavier Neals of the District of New Jersey—admitted that AI-generated errors appeared in their court filings, including citations to nonexistent cases and false quotes.

These incidents have fueled concerns among legal experts about the reliability of AI in judicial contexts. Eric Posner, a law professor at the University of Chicago, expressed skepticism about judicial AI use: "Judges, they're responsible for making decisions that are very important to people and resolving disputes that are very significant. They just can't gamble with a technology that is not fully understood and that is known to hallucinate."

## Efficiency Versus Accuracy Trade-Off

Proponents argue that AI can significantly improve court efficiency, particularly in addressing backlogs and enhancing access to justice, especially in under-resourced courts. Judges without law clerks are particularly interested in AI assistance for routine tasks like document review and legal research.

However, the trade-off between efficiency and accuracy remains a central concern. Unlike human law clerks, AI reasoning processes can be opaque, making it difficult to audit or challenge AI-assisted decisions. There are also concerns about AI potentially influencing judicial reasoning or creating uniformity in legal analysis that could undermine judicial independence.

## The Path Forward for AI in Courts

As AI adoption grows in the judiciary, several key questions remain unresolved: How will appellate courts handle appeals involving AI-assisted rulings? What standards will emerge for disclosing AI use in judicial decision-making? How will the judiciary address potential biases in AI training data? And what long-term effects might AI have on the development of law clerks' skills?

Michael Navin, a consultant at the National Center for State Courts studying legal AI use, notes that the technology is evolving rapidly. "We're in the early stages of understanding how AI will transform judicial work," he said. "The key will be developing appropriate safeguards while harnessing the efficiency benefits."

## Balancing Innovation with Judicial Integrity

The expanding role of AI in the judiciary represents a fundamental shift in how justice is administered. While AI tools offer promising efficiency gains for overburdened courts, they also raise profound questions about transparency, accountability, and the preservation of judicial independence.

As Judge Patterson summarized the judicial approach: "We're proceeding with caution, recognizing both the potential benefits and the serious risks. The human element must remain central to judicial decision-making, even as we explore how technology can assist us in serving justice more effectively."

The coming years will likely see continued debate and development of standards for AI use in courts, as the judiciary navigates the complex intersection of technological innovation and timeless principles of justice.
