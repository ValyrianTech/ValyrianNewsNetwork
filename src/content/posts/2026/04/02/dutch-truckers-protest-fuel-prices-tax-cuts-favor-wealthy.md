# Dutch Truckers Protest Fuel Prices as Tax Cuts Favor Wealthier Drivers

Dutch truck drivers staged coordinated slow-driving protests on major highways Wednesday to demand political action against soaring fuel prices, even as new research reveals that proposed tax reductions would disproportionately benefit higher-income groups while providing minimal relief to the low-income households most affected by the crisis. Approximately 15 truckers created traffic disruptions on the Afsluitdijk (A7) and A4 highway from Rotterdam to The Hague beginning around 06:00, according to [NOS](https://nos.nl/artikel/2608768-actie-van-truckers-op-afsluitdijk-en-a4-tegen-hoge-brandstofprijzen).

## Context: Global Pressures Driving Prices Higher

Fuel prices in the Netherlands have reached record levels, with gasoline and diesel approaching €2.50 per liter, driven primarily by the ongoing Middle East conflict and the closure of the Strait of Hormuz. This marks the second major energy price shock in three years, following the 2022 crisis triggered by Russia's invasion of Ukraine. The transportation sector, small businesses, and households dependent on cars for work are bearing the brunt of the increases.

## Highway Protests Disrupt Morning Commute

Organized by truck driver Hans Visser, the protest saw drivers moving at slow speeds to create congestion without completely blocking traffic. "With the prices as they are now, it's no longer doable," one participant told NOS. Police monitored the action, escorting the convoy with motorcycles and issuing one ticket to a motorist who attempted to bypass the protest.

The disruption caused a 9-kilometer traffic jam on the A4 and intermittent standstills on the Afsluitdijk. "Everyone is shouting and screaming, but nobody is doing anything," said Visser, who hopes the protest will spur political action. "There has to be something done about these fuel prices. This can't continue like this."

## Small Businesses Face Difficult Choices

Meanwhile, small businesses across the Netherlands are grappling with whether to pass on increased fuel costs to customers. Food truck operator Ron Kievits in Wageningen has raised his menu prices by 50 cents after adding 10 cents per liter for diesel. "I find it exciting," he told [NOS](https://nos.nl/artikel/2608565-doorberekenen-of-niet-dure-brandstof-dwingt-ondernemers-tot-keuzes). "Customers might find it too expensive."

Market vendor Meeuwis Drost in Utrecht reports his products are now 15% more expensive due to diesel costs, while fishmonger Lubert de Graaf faces €400-500 in additional weekly fuel expenses. Cleaning business owners Ingrid and Manja Schuurmans from Winterswijk absorb costs for local jobs but pass them on for distant projects. "We notice the fuel is more expensive," said Schuurmans, "but we work mostly locally."

## Research Reveals Inequality in Proposed Solutions

New research from TNO (Netherlands Organization for Applied Scientific Research) reveals that a proposed 10-cent fuel tax reduction would save households an average of €95 annually, but the benefits would be unevenly distributed. According to [NU.nl](https://www.nu.nl/economie/6391297/brandstofaccijns-omlaag-scheelt-tientjes-maar-vergroot-ongelijkheid.html), low-income households would save approximately €80 per year, while higher-income households would save around €120.

"A tax reduction helps immediately but is inefficient," said TNO researcher Peter Mulder. Only about 30% of the total benefit from such a reduction would reach low-income households, with the remainder going to middle- and higher-income groups. This disparity occurs because higher-income households drive more kilometers and therefore consume more fuel.

## Low-Income Households Bear Disproportionate Burden

The TNO study, also reported by [NOS](https://nos.nl/artikel/2608806-lage-inkomens-het-minst-geholpen-met-accijnsverlaging-aan-de-pomp), finds that low-income households spending above-average time on the road can devote up to 17.6% of their income to fuel at current prices. Approximately 225,000 households fall into this vulnerable category, primarily located in Northern Netherlands, Zeeland, Limburg, and suburbs of major cities where car dependency is high and public transportation alternatives are limited.

"Low incomes are helped the least by fuel tax reduction at the pump," Mulder noted, pointing out that higher-income households are better positioned to absorb price shocks or transition to electric vehicles. "Isn't the core of the problem that we're dependent on fossil fuels?"

## Policy Dilemma: Quick Relief vs Structural Solutions

The Dutch government faces a difficult choice between providing immediate relief through tax reductions and investing in longer-term structural solutions. A 10-cent tax cut would cost the treasury approximately €1 billion annually while offering limited benefits to those most in need.

Mulder acknowledges the complexity: "The technological solution takes longer, but in the long term it has much more effect." He suggests that targeted support for low-income households to transition to electric vehicles or use shared transportation would be more effective than blanket tax reductions, though more complicated to implement.

## What's Next: Behavioral Changes and Political Pressure

The TNO research anticipates behavioral changes similar to those seen during the 2022 energy crisis, including reduced driving, increased carpooling, and greater use of public transportation. Meanwhile, political pressure is mounting in parliament for government intervention, with growing calls for measures to address fuel price impacts.

The truckers' protest highlights broader economic strains affecting households and businesses across the Netherlands. As global oil markets remain volatile due to geopolitical tensions, the country faces not just a fuel price crisis but a fundamental challenge in balancing immediate economic relief with long-term energy transition goals. The resolution will test the Netherlands' capacity for adaptive governance in an era of intersecting economic, social, and environmental challenges.
