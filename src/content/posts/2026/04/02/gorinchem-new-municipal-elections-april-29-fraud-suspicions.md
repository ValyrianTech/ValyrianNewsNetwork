# Gorinchem to Hold New Municipal Elections on April 29 After Fraud Suspicions

The Dutch municipality of Gorinchem will hold new municipal elections on Wednesday, April 29, 2026, following suspicions of fraud in the original March 18 vote. The municipal council made the unprecedented decision after evidence emerged of potential vote harvesting through proxy voting, casting doubt on the legitimacy of the initial results.

According to [NOS](https://nos.nl/artikel/2608809-nieuwe-gemeenteraadsverkiezingen-in-gorinchem-zijn-op-29-april), the council voted 13-12 in favor of new elections during a late-night session on March 30-31. Mayor Reinie Melissant requested an extension from the Ministry of Internal Affairs due to Easter and May holidays falling within the legally required 30-day window, but the ministry [denied the request](https://gorinchem.bestuurlijkeinformatie.nl/Reports/Item/42a9f9d4-c449-48da-a25c-11038448f337) as legally impossible.

## Context: Proxy Voting System Under Scrutiny

The suspected fraud centers on the Netherlands' unique proxy voting system, which allows voters to authorize others to cast ballots on their behalf. This system has long been criticized by international observers as vulnerable to manipulation. In Gorinchem's case, a candidate allegedly visited polling stations five times with individuals carrying multiple proxy authorizations, raising red flags about organized irregularities.

As [NU.nl reported](https://www.nu.nl/gemeenteraadsverkiezingen/6391350/gorinchem-houdt-op-29-april-nieuwe-verkiezingen-na-vermoeden-van-fraude.html), the municipality received signals that voters were pressured to grant proxy votes, prompting both police and the Public Prosecution Service to launch investigations. The situation has historical precedent in Dutch electoral history, with a similar case occurring in Huissen in 1962 involving forged signatures and elderly voters being deceived.

## Key Developments and Timeline

The Gorinchem election saga has unfolded over several weeks:

1. **March 18:** Original municipal elections held
2. **Late March:** Doubts emerge about election integrity, particularly regarding proxy votes
3. **March 30-31:** Municipal council debates and narrowly votes (13-12) for new elections
4. **April 1:** Mayor Melissant sends [letter to the ministry](https://gorinchem.bestuurlijkeinformatie.nl/Reports/Item/c44b6efc-d57f-40d1-86f9-5eb76506eccc) questioning whether the proxy voting system "still meets modern standards of integrity, transparency, and protection against misuse"
5. **April 2:** Municipality announces April 29 as election date after ministry denies extension request
6. **Week of April 20:** New voting passes to be distributed to 31,007 eligible voters
7. **April 29:** New elections scheduled at same polling stations as March 18

In an [official statement](https://www.gorinchem.nl/nieuws/op-woensdag-29-april/), Mayor Melissant emphasized: "The debate underscores the great importance attached locally to the integrity, reliability, and credibility of the election process." The municipality expressed regret that elections couldn't be held after the May vacation when more residents would be in town.

## Analysis: Democratic Integrity vs. Logistical Challenges

This case represents a rare instance of a Dutch municipality re-running elections due to fraud suspicions, demonstrating commitment to electoral integrity despite significant logistical and financial costs. The decision creates a "devil's dilemma," as described by council members torn between ensuring democratic legitimacy and facing practical challenges.

The timing presents considerable difficulties: the May holiday (April 25 to May 3) means some polling stations, including schools, will be closed, and both residents and election staff may be on vacation. Normally, voting cards must be sent two weeks in advance, but the ministry allowed delivery "a few days before" in this exceptional case.

More fundamentally, the Gorinchem situation highlights systemic vulnerabilities in Dutch election procedures. Mayor Melissant's letter to the ministry signals potential national review of proxy voting, asking whether this component of the voting system "still sufficiently aligns with the requirements we set today for integrity, transparency, and resilience against abuse."

## Implications and What's Next

The Gorinchem case has several important implications:

1. **Democratic Precedent:** Reinforces that election integrity takes precedence over convenience, even when investigations are ongoing
2. **Systemic Review:** Likely to trigger national discussion about proxy voting vulnerabilities and potential reforms
3. **Legal Framework:** Tests the strict 30-day rule for election re-runs, showing limits of administrative flexibility
4. **Public Trust:** Impacts confidence in election outcomes, though the decision to re-run may ultimately strengthen trust in the system

Looking ahead, all eyes will be on the April 29 elections and the ongoing police investigation. The outcome may influence whether other municipalities with similar concerns follow Gorinchem's example. Additionally, the Ministry of Internal Affairs may face pressure to review the proxy voting system nationally, potentially leading to electoral reforms.

As [municipal officials noted](https://www.gorinchem.nl/nieuws/gemeenteraad-gorinchem-besluit-tot-herstemming/), the signals of possible organized irregularities in proxy voting "have, regardless of their eventual assessment, major impact on the trust of residents, on the administrative and political debate in the municipal council, and on the perception of the reliability of the election results."

The Gorinchem case serves as a reminder that electoral integrity requires constant vigilance and willingness to address vulnerabilities, even when doing so creates significant practical challenges. The April 29 elections will not only determine Gorinchem's municipal leadership but may also shape the future of Dutch electoral procedures.
