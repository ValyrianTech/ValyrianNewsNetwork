# White House Requested Changes to Ballroom Commission Documents

The White House asked an independent planning commission to soften language about its own authority over President Donald Trump's controversial White House ballroom project, according to emails obtained by [The Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/2026/04/02/white-house-ballroom-documents-changed/). The National Capital Planning Commission (NCPC) complied with the request and changed a public FAQ document before approving the $400 million project on April 2, 2026, despite a federal judge's ruling that halted construction unless Congress approves the massive addition.

## Context: The Ballroom Project and Legal Battle

The 90,000-square-foot ballroom project, which would be nearly twice the size of the White House mansion itself, has been a source of controversy since Trump first announced it in summer 2025. In October 2025, the president demolished the East Wing with little warning to make way for the addition. The project has faced opposition from preservation groups and the public, with the National Trust for Historic Preservation filing a lawsuit that led to a significant legal ruling.

On March 31, 2026, U.S. District Judge Richard Leon ordered a halt to construction unless Congress approves the project, stating in his ruling: "The President of the United States is the steward of the White House for future generations of First Families. He is not, however, the owner! No statute comes close to giving the President the authority he claims to have." The Justice Department appealed the ruling the following day.

## Document Changes Requested by White House

According to emails obtained through a public records request, a White House staffer asked the NCPC to "soften language about the commission's authority over the project" in what would become a public FAQ document. The commission complied and released [the modified document](https://www.ncpc.gov/files/projects/2025/8733_East_Wing_Modernization_Project_Project_FAQs_Jan2026.pdf) ahead of its January 8, 2026 meeting.

The NCPC is the federal agency tasked with approving construction on federal property in the Washington region. Its approval was the final procedural obstacle for the ballroom project. The 12-person commission includes three members appointed by Trump, including chair Will Scharf, a top White House aide who has spoken in support of the ballroom addition.

## NCPC Approval Despite Legal Ruling

On April 2, 2026, the NCPC voted to approve the ballroom project despite Judge Leon's ruling. Commission spokesperson Stephen Staudigl told [PBS NewsHour](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/politics/trumps-white-house-ballroom-gets-final-approval-despite-judges-ruling-halting-work) that the commission went ahead with the vote because the judge's ruling "affects construction activities, not the planning process."

The vote had initially been scheduled for March but was pushed to April due to high public interest. According to the PBS report, public comments were "overwhelmingly opposed to the ballroom," with protesters gathering outside the NCPC building displaying boxes containing 35,000 public comments, 97% of which opposed the project.

## Analysis: Implications for Independent Commissions

The White House's request for document changes raises significant questions about political interference in independent government processes. Independent commissions like the NCPC are designed to provide objective oversight of federal projects, free from executive branch influence.

"Records show a staffer asked the commission to soften language about its own authority over the president's project — and it complied," The Washington Post reported. This creates a perception that the commission may be deferential to the White House rather than maintaining its statutory independence.

The commission's composition adds to these concerns. With three Trump appointees, including his former personal lawyer as chair, critics argue the NCPC's independence has been compromised. Phil Mendelson, an NCPC member and Chairman of the D.C. Council, expressed concerns about the project's scale, telling reporters: "I think that we can have a ballroom. To me that's not the issue. It's just too large."

## Broader Implications for Presidential Authority

Judge Leon's ruling establishes an important precedent regarding presidential authority over federal property. By stating that presidents don't "own" the White House and need congressional approval for major structural changes, the ruling reinforces the system of checks and balances.

Trump disputed this interpretation, telling reporters after the ruling: "We built many things at the White House over the years. They don't get congressional approval." However, legal experts note that no previous president has undertaken a project of this scale without congressional consultation.

The case highlights the tension between presidential legacy projects and historic preservation. The White House is both a functioning executive residence and a historic landmark protected by laws like the Antiquities Act of 1906 and the National Historic Preservation Act.

## What's Next for the Ballroom Project

Despite the NCPC's approval, the ballroom project faces significant legal hurdles. Judge Leon's ruling remains in effect pending appeal, meaning construction cannot proceed without congressional approval. Representatives for the House and Senate committees with jurisdiction over the project did not return requests for comment, and Congress is currently on spring break.

The National Trust for Historic Preservation, which brought the original lawsuit, is likely to continue its legal challenge. The organization argues that the project sets a dangerous precedent for presidential modifications to historic federal properties.

As the legal battle continues, the White House's request for document changes will likely fuel further scrutiny of how independent commissions interact with the executive branch. The case may lead to calls for stronger protections for commission independence and more transparent processes for presidential projects affecting historic federal properties.

The ballroom project represents not just a physical addition to the White House but a test of institutional norms, separation of powers, and the balance between presidential ambition and historical preservation. Its outcome will establish important precedents for future administrations and their relationship with independent oversight bodies.
