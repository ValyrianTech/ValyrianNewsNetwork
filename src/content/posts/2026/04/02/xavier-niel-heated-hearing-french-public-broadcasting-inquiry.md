# Xavier Niel's Confrontational Hearing Before French Public Broadcasting Inquiry

Billionaire entrepreneur Xavier Niel and financier Matthieu Pigasse faced a tense, confrontational hearing before France's parliamentary commission investigating public broadcasting on Thursday, with the session quickly devolving into accusations of political theater and "circus" tactics. The three-hour hearing revealed deep tensions over media ownership and political influence in France's public broadcasting system, according to reports from [Le Monde](https://www.lemonde.fr/economie/article/2026/04/02/cirque-demarche-politique-l-audition-electrique-de-xavier-niel-et-matthieu-pigasse-devant-la-commission-d-enquete-sur-l-audiovisuel-public_6676190_3234.html) and [Le Figaro](https://www.lefigaro.fr/medias/commission-d-enquete-sur-l-audiovisuel-public-l-audition-de-xavier-niel-vire-a-la-foire-d-empoigne-20260402).

## Context and Background

The hearing marked the return of Niel, Pigasse, and Jérôme Nommé (president of KKR France) after their initial scheduled appearance on February 26 was canceled at the last minute due to what Niel called an "urgent impediment." The three were summoned as co-founders and shareholders of Mediawan, Europe's leading audiovisual production group and a major supplier of programs to France Télévisions, the public broadcaster.

The commission of inquiry was created at the request of the UDR (Union des droites pour la République), a party allied with Marine Le Pen's Rassemblement National (RN), which has advocated for the privatization of public broadcasting. The commission's focus on "neutrality" and "financing" of public media has been viewed by critics as part of a broader political agenda.

## The Confrontational Hearing

The atmosphere turned electric immediately when commission president Jérémie Patrier-Leitus (Horizons party) asked Niel to justify his February absence. "You are not a prosecutor," Niel fired back before explaining that his entrepreneurial obligations had prevented his attendance.

Niel, the founder of Iliad (parent company of Free) and an individual shareholder of Le Monde group, then used his opening statement to launch a blistering attack on commission rapporteur Charles Alloncle (UDR), accusing him of spreading "many approximations, fake news, lies here and on your social networks, without any form of contradiction."

"Demanding transparency on the use of French people's money is necessary," Niel stated. "But I think it was possible to do it, Mr. Rapporteur Charles-Henri Alloncle, without transforming your commission into a circus." He added pointedly: "Thank you for the invitation, but I am not a clown."

## Mediawan and Political Tensions

Mediawan, founded by Pierre-Antoine Capton, Xavier Niel, and Matthieu Pigasse, has become a major force in European audiovisual production, supplying popular programs like "C à vous" and "C dans l'air" to France Télévisions. The company generates approximately €110 million annually from France Télévisions, representing less than 5% of its global turnover.

The hearing highlighted the political dimensions of media ownership in France. Pigasse, owner of media group Combat (which includes Les Inrocks and Radio Nova), is known for his left-leaning political positions and opposition to the RN. This has created perceptions of potential bias in public broadcasting content, a central concern for the right-wing commission.

Pigasse joined Niel in criticizing the commission's approach, stating: "You are incredible, you pose as a victim while you multiply false information, you pour torrents of mud on people and you are surprised that we respond." He questioned whether the commission was being used as "a tool of ideological combat."

## Broader Implications

The confrontation reflects deeper tensions in French media and politics. The RN and its allies have long criticized public broadcasting as biased toward left-wing perspectives, while media entrepreneurs like Niel and Pigasse view the commission's inquiry as politically motivated harassment of successful French businesses.

Niel criticized the commission's cost, estimating it at "tens of millions of euros" for what he characterized as "a TV show." He also made a provocative statement about the future of public broadcasting: "I would love to buy these channels... France Télévisions channels are incredible, Radio France radios are incredible. We have incredible public media. I hope they remain public but if they become private, I would be delighted to participate in the tenders that might occur to buy them."

## Analysis and Political Context

The hearing represents more than just a parliamentary procedure—it's a microcosm of France's ongoing culture wars around media, influence, and political power. The commission, dominated by right-wing parties skeptical of public broadcasting, faces off against media entrepreneurs who have built significant influence through private investment.

Charles Alloncle, visibly taken aback by the intensity of the attacks, responded: "I have difficulty understanding the very offensive nature of your attacks from the beginning, and the tone also extremely offensive. This is the first time in all the hearings we have done."

The session had to be temporarily suspended to restore calm, underscoring the depth of animosity between the parties. Pigasse raised the possibility of legal consequences, questioning "the judicial consequences of false accusations" made during parliamentary proceedings.

## What's Next

The commission is scheduled to conclude its hearings next week, with a final report due by the end of April. France Télévisions CEO Delphine Ernotte is expected to be re-audited as part of the process.

The hearing's fallout may extend beyond the commission room. Pigasse's suggestion of potential legal action for defamation raises questions about the boundaries of parliamentary immunity when making accusations during inquiries. Additionally, the public nature of the confrontation could influence perceptions of parliamentary procedures and their effectiveness.

As France continues to debate the role and funding of public broadcasting, Thursday's hearing demonstrated that the issue remains deeply polarized, with significant implications for media independence, political influence, and the relationship between business and government in one of Europe's most important media markets.
