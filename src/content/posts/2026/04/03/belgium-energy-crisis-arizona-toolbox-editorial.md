# Belgium Lacks Arizona's 'SOS Energy' Toolbox, Editorial Says

A leading Belgian newspaper editorial has sharply criticized the government's response to the country's ongoing energy crisis, questioning why Belgium lacks a comprehensive "SOS energy" toolbox similar to what Arizona has implemented for emergency utility assistance. The opinion piece by Béatrice Delvaux, chief editorialist at [Le Soir](https://www.lesoir.be/738661/article/2026-04-03/et-la-boite-outils-sos-energie-de-larizona-elle-est-ou), contrasts systematic approaches to energy emergencies with what she describes as political procrastination and division within Belgium's federal government.

## Context: Four Years Later, Back to Energy Crisis

"Four years later, here are Belgians back to square one: 'energy crisis'," Delvaux writes in her April 3 editorial. The critique comes as energy prices regain momentum at the pump and households face renewed financial pressure. The editorial references Prime Minister Bart De Wever's recent statement that "Arizona doesn't have money to 'water people by helicopter'"—a metaphor for direct financial aid that has become a point of political contention.

Delvaux argues that while Arizona has developed a multi-layered emergency response system for energy crises, Belgium's government "procrastinates, divides, argues." She describes a political landscape where "one faction delays decisions, another agitates and makes threats," with communication serving political constituencies rather than addressing structural problems.

## Arizona's Comprehensive 'SOS Energy' Approach

The editorial highlights Arizona's systematic approach to energy emergencies, which includes:

- **LIHEAP (Low Income Home Energy Assistance Program)**: Federal assistance for residential energy bills, utility deposits, and crisis bill support
- **DES Utility Assistance**: Arizona Department of Economic Security aid to prevent service disconnections
- **Project SHARE**: Donation-funded program for immediate energy needs
- **Heat Relief Assistance**: Specialized support for Maricopa County residents during high-heat periods
- **Utility Provider Programs**: Financial assistance, payment plans, and discounts from major providers
- **Energy Efficiency Resources**: Weatherization assistance and energy-saving programs

This "toolbox" approach represents decades of development in response to Arizona's extreme heat challenges, creating a safety net that operates at federal, state, county, and utility levels simultaneously.

## Belgian Government's Fiscal and Political Constraints

The editorial emerges against a backdrop of significant political and fiscal constraints. Prime Minister Bart De Wever has rejected calls for additional government support, citing budget limitations and increasing geopolitical uncertainty tied to Middle East tensions. Belgium's fragile fiscal position—with high national debt and rising interest rates—leaves little room for additional spending.

Finance Minister Vincent Van Peteghem reinforced this position on March 30, stating there is "no more question of 'watering Belgians with aid by helicopter.'" This reference to past direct aid policies reflects government concern about creating long-term fiscal liabilities.

## Structural Energy Vulnerabilities

Delvaux's critique extends to Belgium's energy policy decisions, particularly the transition from nuclear power to gas-fired plants. "We have further increased our energy dependency in recent years by replacing our nuclear capacity with gas power plants," she notes, adding that Belgium is now "among the most vulnerable European countries to external energy disruptions."

This structural vulnerability is exacerbated by current Middle East tensions, including U.S.-Israeli actions against Iran, which have contributed to global energy market volatility. The editorial suggests that past policy decisions have left Belgium poorly positioned to weather such shocks.

## Political Divisions and Communication Strategies

The editorial describes a government divided along political lines, with different factions communicating to their respective constituencies. Georges-Louis Bouchez, president of the Reformist Movement (MR), represents what Delvaux calls "the low-income worker" constituency, while De Wever's New Flemish Alliance (N-VA) appeals to "supporters of seriousness."

This political segmentation, according to the editorial, results in messaging that serves electoral interests rather than developing coherent policy responses. "Their communication serves their electorate... and has the merit, solely political, of occupying space," Delvaux writes.

## Analysis: Reactive vs. Proactive Crisis Management

The core of Delvaux's argument centers on the difference between reactive political responses and proactive structural solutions. Arizona's approach represents systematic planning for predictable emergencies (extreme heat), while Belgium's response appears ad-hoc and politically driven.

This contrast raises fundamental questions about how governments prepare for recurring crises. Energy emergencies—whether driven by geopolitical events, market volatility, or extreme weather—are increasingly predictable in their occurrence if not their precise timing. Systematic "toolbox" approaches allow for faster, more equitable responses when crises hit.

## Implications for Vulnerable Populations

Without comprehensive assistance programs, vulnerable populations face disproportionate hardship during energy crises. Arizona's multi-layered system specifically targets low-income households, elderly residents, and those with medical needs requiring continuous power. Belgium's current approach, focused on broad fiscal constraints rather than targeted assistance, may leave similar populations without adequate protection.

The editorial implicitly questions whether fiscal responsibility and social protection must be mutually exclusive, suggesting that well-designed programs can address both concerns through means-tested assistance and efficiency investments.

## Forward Look: Lessons for European Energy Policy

Delvaux's critique extends beyond Belgium to broader European energy policy challenges. As EU energy chief Dan Jørgensen has warned, energy prices could remain high even with diplomatic resolutions to current conflicts. The International Energy Agency has described the current crisis as worse than past oil shocks.

European countries face similar tensions between fiscal constraints, energy security, and social protection. Arizona's model—developed in a very different context—nevertheless offers insights into systematic emergency response that could inform European approaches.

## Conclusion: Beyond Political Maneuvering

The Le Soir editorial concludes with an implicit call for moving beyond political positioning to substantive policy development. As energy crises become more frequent and severe due to climate change and geopolitical instability, the need for systematic responses grows more urgent.

"The federal government, meanwhile, procrastinates, divides, argues," Delvaux writes, contrasting this with the comprehensive approach she attributes to Arizona. The question posed by her editorial title—"And the 'SOS energy' toolbox from Arizona, where is it?"—remains unanswered as Belgium grapples with both immediate price pressures and long-term structural vulnerabilities in its energy system.

The editorial reflects broader public frustration with government responses to soaring energy costs and suggests that without more proactive, systematic approaches, Belgium may find itself returning to "square one" in future crises with similarly inadequate tools at its disposal.
