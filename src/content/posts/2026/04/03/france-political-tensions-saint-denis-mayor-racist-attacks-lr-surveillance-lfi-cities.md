# Saint-Denis Mayor Faces Racism as LR Targets LFI Cities

Political tensions in France have escalated as Bally Bagayoko, the newly elected La France Insoumise (LFI) mayor of Saint-Denis, confronts racist attacks while Bruno Retailleau, president of Les Républicains (LR), announces surveillance of LFI-governed municipalities. These developments highlight deepening polarization ahead of the 2027 presidential election.

## Historic Election Meets Racist Backlash

Bally Bagayoko's election on March 15, 2026, made him the first LFI mayor of a major French city. The 52-year-old, born in France to Malian parents, leads Saint-Denis (population ~150,000). His election was part of a broader trend that saw around 10 people of African descent elected mayors in 2026, cracking a "glass ceiling" in French politics.

According to [Le Monde](https://www.lemonde.fr/politique/article/2026/04/03/bally-bagayoko-le-pari-de-saint-denis-le-racisme-n-a-pas-les-memes-yeux-pas-le-meme-cerveau_6676325_823448.html), Bagayoko has faced racist phone calls and online abuse since taking office. The situation escalated on March 27 when psychologist Jean Doridot on CNews compared him to "social animals... part of the ape family" needing a "chief." Essayist Michel Onfray later described him as a "dominant male" with "tribal" behavior.

"We've reached a new level in openly racist remarks," said Kelly Kidou, head of town hall reception. Bagayoko filed a lawsuit against CNews on March 31 for "public insults of a racist nature." Multiple anti-racism organizations filed complaints with media regulator Arcom.

## Government Condemns, LR Escalates

Prime Minister Sébastien Lecornu condemned the "normalization of evil and racism," while Interior Minister Laurent Nuñez called the remarks "despicable." CNews, owned by conservative billionaire Vincent Bolloré, has faced repeated fines for racist content.

Simultaneously, Bruno Retailleau announced on April 3 an "observatory of LFI cities" to monitor municipalities governed by La France Insoumise. According to [Le Figaro](https://www.lefigaro.fr/politique/bruno-retailleau-place-les-villes-dirigees-par-lfi-sous-surveillance-20260403), Retailleau called LFI a "seditious party" and declared "the 'new France' of Jean-Luc Mélenchon is not our France."

The observatory will be chaired by Kristell Niasme, LR mayor of Villeneuve-Saint-Georges. Retailleau stated it would "nourish evidence through statements, methods and practices of LFI mayors" and might "seize justice if needed." He denounced "communitarianism," "racialism," and "intellectual terrorism" from LFI, calling Mélenchon a "leader maximo."

## Political Polarization Intensifies

These developments reveal intersecting crises of representation, racism, and polarization. Bagayoko's experience exposes persistent racial prejudices targeting politicians of color. Kaltoum Gachi of MRAP said referring to someone "as a monkey because of their skin colour" crosses "a red line."

Retailleau's initiative institutionalizes opposition to LFI governance, reflecting strategic escalation before the 2027 election. Since September 2024, LR has designated LFI as the "main enemy of the Republic." With Retailleau a declared presidential candidate, this framing distinguishes LR from both the far-right National Rally and Macron's Renaissance party.

LFI made significant municipal gains, winning cities like Saint-Denis, Roubaix, Vénissieux, and Vaulx-en-Velin. During the campaign, Retailleau denounced "shameful agreements" between the left and LFI in over 100 cities.

## What Comes Next

Bagayoko has announced an anti-racism rally for April 4 in Saint-Denis. Legal proceedings against CNews may result in sanctions and set media accountability precedents.

Longer term, these events shape the 2027 presidential battlefield. Bagayoko's success could influence minority political participation, while Retailleau's surveillance may increase municipal conflicts between LR and LFI.

Other parties must position themselves in this polarized landscape. The traditional republican front against the far-right now faces complications from racist attacks on minority politicians and institutionalized opposition to the far-left.

As France approaches its next presidential contest, these municipal conflicts reveal a nation at a political crossroads.
