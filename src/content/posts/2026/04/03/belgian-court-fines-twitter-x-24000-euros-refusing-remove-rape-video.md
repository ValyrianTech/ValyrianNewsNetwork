# Belgian Court Fines Twitter/X €24,000 for Refusing to Remove Rape Video

A Brussels court has fined Twitter International Unlimited Company, the European subsidiary of X (formerly Twitter), €24,000 for refusing to remove a video depicting the rape of a Flemish woman from its platform. According to [RTBF](https://www.rtbf.be/article/twitter-condamne-par-un-tribunal-belge-a-24-000-euros-d-amende-pour-la-diffusion-d-une-video-de-viol-11704800), the Brussels Correctional Court found the social media company guilty of non-consensual distribution of intimate images after it failed to comply with multiple warnings and a court order to take down the harmful content. The case, brought by Belgium's Institute for the Equality of Women and Men (IEFH/IGVM), highlights growing tensions between European regulators and social media platforms over content moderation responsibilities.

## Context and Background

The case centers on a 6-second video clip from 2019-2020 that shows the rape of a clearly identifiable Flemish woman. As reported by [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/03/twitter-veroordeeld-naaktbeelden-proces-uitspraak/), the video began circulating online in autumn 2019, with the victim contacting the Institute for the Equality of Women and Men shortly thereafter. The clip was posted on Twitter in July 2020 and spread rapidly across the internet, prompting the Institute to file a formal complaint in December 2020 after obtaining legal authority to represent victims of non-consensual intimate image distribution.

Belgium's laws against "revenge porn"—the non-consensual distribution of intimate images—form the legal basis for the prosecution. The case occurs against the backdrop of the European Union's Digital Services Act, which imposes stricter content moderation requirements on large online platforms, though the specific legislation isn't cited in the judgment.

## Key Developments and Court Proceedings

During the trial, Twitter International Unlimited Company argued that reports from the Institute weren't sufficiently clear, claiming the images weren't explicitly sexually explicit and that consent issues were ambiguous. The company also noted that a CD-ROM containing the original 6-second evidence had been lost. However, the court rejected these arguments, stating in its judgment: "We don't see what additional information or context Twitter could have needed to remove the images from its platform."

The video was only removed after an investigating judge issued a court order, and the account of the man who posted the footage was suspended. According to court documents, Twitter had repeatedly asked the Institute's lawyers to prove they had a mandate to act on behalf of the victim and even requested the victim's identity before closing the case without action.

In a surprising twist, the Brussels Public Prosecutor's Office had requested acquittal during a February 20, 2026 hearing, arguing the video didn't clearly show rape or revenge porn. "Is there a rape visible in that video? That may be, but that does not appear from that video. You could just as well deduce that it was consensual sex or that it was staged," the prosecutor stated, according to VRT NWS.

## Legal Analysis and Implications

The court's ruling establishes important legal principles for platform accountability in Belgium and potentially across the European Union. In its judgment, the court emphasized: "Even if the images were made with consent, it's a crime if those same images are distributed without consent." This clarification strengthens legal protections for victims of non-consensual intimate image distribution regardless of how the original content was created.

Frédéric Thiebaut, lawyer for the Institute, highlighted the broader significance of the case: "The importance of this case is great and even transcends the victim in question. This case will be looked at to point out the responsibility to companies with such a revenue model—we're talking about a billion-dollar business here."

The relatively modest €24,000 fine reflects the court's consideration of the lengthy judicial proceedings as a mitigating factor. However, the symbolic value of the judgment may outweigh its financial impact, setting a precedent that platforms cannot indefinitely delay action on clearly illegal content by requesting excessive documentation or claiming ambiguity.

## Platform Responsibility in the Digital Age

This case arrives at a critical juncture for X (formerly Twitter), which has faced criticism for reducing content moderation teams and changing policies since Elon Musk's acquisition. The judgment reinforces that platforms with massive user bases and revenue streams bear corresponding responsibilities for addressing harmful content.

The court noted the severe impact on victims: "The court takes the facts seriously, which can cause considerable damage to the victim. She loses all control over the further distribution of the images. Online revenge porn can have a very large impact on the life of a victim."

## What's Next and Broader Implications

Twitter International Unlimited Company can appeal the ruling, potentially extending the legal battle that began in 2020. Regardless of the appeal outcome, the judgment sends a clear signal to social media platforms operating in Europe that they must take prompt action on reports of non-consensual intimate imagery.

The case also strengthens the position of national equality institutions like Belgium's Institute for the Equality of Women and Men in supporting victims of online sexual violence. As European regulators continue to implement the Digital Services Act, this Belgian judgment may influence how other EU member states interpret platform obligations regarding harmful content removal.

For victims of non-consensual intimate image distribution, the ruling provides legal reinforcement that platforms cannot hide behind procedural delays or claims of ambiguity when presented with clear evidence of violations. The judgment affirms that once platforms have sufficient information to determine content involves non-consensual intimate imagery, they have a legal obligation to act promptly—a principle with potentially far-reaching implications for content moderation across the digital landscape.
