# EasyJet Cabin Crew Strike on Easter Monday Over Conditions

EasyJet cabin crew in France will stage a full-day strike on Easter Monday, April 6, 2026, denouncing what their union calls management "contempt" and addressing widespread exhaustion among flight attendants and stewards. The UNAC union, which represents cabin crew at the British low-cost carrier, has called for the industrial action across all six French bases, potentially disrupting holiday travel during one of the busiest weekends for air traffic. According to [Le Monde](https://www.lemonde.fr/economie/article/2026/04/03/greve-chez-easyjet-le-lundi-de-paques-le-syndicat-qui-a-lance-le-mouvement-denonce-le-mepris-de-la-direction-face-a-l-epuisement-des-hotesses-et-stewards_6676391_3234.html), the strike threatens to affect approximately 1,200 cabin crew members and could lead to flight cancellations or delays.

## Context and Union Demands

The strike announcement comes amid deteriorating labor relations at EasyJet's French operations. The UNAC union, the second-largest cabin crew union at EasyJet France, published its strike notice on April 2, citing "contempt and absence of dialogue" from management. 

Union representatives highlight critical issues including unstable schedules with "countless" last-minute changes, insufficient staffing, and disregard for work-life balance. The union declared that "the countless number of schedule changes, flight rotations, and lack of consideration for our quality of life at work cannot and must not become the new normal." This reflects broader tensions in aviation as airlines maximize efficiency during post-pandemic recovery.

## Strategic Timing and Potential Impact

The choice of Easter Monday targets both a public holiday and spring school holidays for many French families. Union statements indicate this timing deliberately "marks the coup" during "one of the most important weekends for air traffic." The full-day strike will affect all six EasyJet bases in France: Paris-Charles de Gaulle (Roissy), Paris-Orly, Lyon, Nice, Nantes, and Bordeaux.

French labor law requires individual strike notifications by Saturday, April 4 at 00:01, giving EasyJet 48 hours to assess participation and make adjustments. The strike only affects flights with French cabin crews; foreign crews operating in France will not participate.

## Company Response and Passenger Communication

EasyJet expressed disappointment while pledging to limit impact. The company said it was "very disappointed" and promised to "limit the repercussions of this strike as much as possible." The airline stated that "if changes need to be made to our flight schedule, we will inform affected passengers directly and offer them solutions to help reorganize their travel."

The response leverages the 48-hour notification period, allowing assessment before implementing cancellations. This balances operational realities with passenger service during a peak travel period.

## Broader Labor Relations Context

The strike occurs amid ongoing labor tensions in French aviation, which has strong union traditions around holidays. It follows rejection of EasyJet's 2026 annual negotiations agreement by more than half of voting employees, indicating widespread dissatisfaction.

EasyJet is France's second-largest airline by passenger numbers after Air France, focusing on short and medium-haul European routes. Its continental expansion has exposed it to France's robust worker protections and union rights.

## Analysis: Sector-Wide Challenges

The dispute reflects broader aviation industry challenges post-COVID. Airlines balance increased demand against operational constraints and staff welfare. Pandemic-era workforce reductions followed by rapid rehiring created pressures manifesting in schedule instability.

Labor actions during peak periods risk low-cost carriers' business models dependent on high aircraft utilization. Financial implications of cancellations and reputational damage can be substantial during profitable holidays.

## What's Next

The immediate focus is Saturday's deadline for strike notifications, determining the action's scale and enabling EasyJet's contingency plans. Passengers booked on Easter Monday flights should monitor airline communications.

Longer-term, the dispute highlights need for improved management-union dialogue at EasyJet France. The union's characterization of management attitude as "a punitive will" suggests deeper systemic issues requiring substantive negotiations.

As aviation continues recovery, balancing operational demands with staff welfare remains critical. This strike's outcome may influence labor relations strategies across the industry.
