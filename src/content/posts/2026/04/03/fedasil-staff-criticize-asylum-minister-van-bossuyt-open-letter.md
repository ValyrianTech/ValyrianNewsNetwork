# Fedasil Staff Accuse Minister of Rule of Law Violations

Hundreds of Fedasil employees have published an extraordinary open letter accusing Asylum and Migration Minister Anneleen Van Bossuyt (N-VA) of "daily violations of the rule of law and human dignity" in Belgium's asylum policy. According to [BRF](https://brf.be/national/2064589/), approximately 500 staff members signed the letter, which appeared simultaneously in De Standaard and Le Soir on April 3, 2026, marking an unprecedented rebellion within the government agency responsible for asylum reception.

## Legal Context and Court Defeats

The staff criticism comes after two significant judicial defeats for Minister Van Bossuyt's restrictive asylum policies. On February 26, 2026, the Constitutional Court suspended provisions of a July 2025 law that allowed limiting reception for asylum seekers already protected in another EU country. Despite this ruling, Van Bossuyt issued a new instruction to Fedasil on March 2 to continue limiting material assistance to this category of asylum seekers.

This move prompted legal challenges, and on March 27, the Council of State suspended Van Bossuyt's instruction, calling it an "emergency suspension." The court ruled the policy risked leaving affected individuals in "complete destitution" and potential homelessness, noting it should have been submitted for prior review by the Council's Legislation Section.

## Staff Moral Distress and Open Letter

In their open letter, the 477-481 signatories (out of Fedasil's 3,000 total employees) expressed profound moral distress. "We don't want the consequences of your policy on our conscience," they wrote, according to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/03/personeel-fedasil-uit-scherpe-kritiek-op-beleid-van-bossuyt/). The staff added: "We distance ourselves from the daily violations of the rule of law and of human dignity, both towards asylum seekers and the agency personnel under your supervision."

The letter represents extraordinary criticism from civil servants, who typically maintain political neutrality. "The violation of our laws by a minister and the refusal to execute judicial decisions worries us about the state of our democracy," the employees wrote, emphasizing they were writing "out of loyalty to the agency and its mission."

## Parliamentary Reaction and Minister's Defense

The controversy reached Belgium's Parliament, where Green MP Matti Vandemaele read the open letter during an interpellation, calling it "unprecedented." Vandemaele accused the minister of "abandoning her staff," stating: "They make your policy equal to the ground, on all facets, and the only thing you can say is that they didn't understand it."

Minister Van Bossuyt defended her approach, telling Parliament she respects staff work but is trying to keep the asylum system sustainable long-term. "Without strict measures, the pressure on our society becomes unbearable," she argued, according to VRT NWS. The minister noted the letter references practices from previous governments three times and stated: "I will do everything to find solutions with respect for the rule of law."

## New Policy Approach After Court Ruling

Following the Council of State's suspension, Van Bossuyt announced a modified approach. In a written response cited by VRT NWS, she stated: "In line with recent jurisprudence, new working agreements were made with the services. Reception can be refused to persons who already have protection in another EU member state, provided this is done individually and sufficiently motivated. Each file will be assessed separately."

This represents a significant shift from the blanket policy that courts found unlawful, though it maintains the government's goal of reducing "asylum shopping"—where seekers apply in multiple EU countries.

## Broader Implications and Analysis

The staff rebellion highlights several critical issues in Belgium's asylum system:

**Rule of Law Concerns:** The accusation of "daily violations of the rule of law" from within a government agency is politically damaging and suggests serious governance issues. Two consecutive court defeats indicate the minister's policies may have overstepped legal boundaries.

**Humanitarian Impact:** The Council of State's finding that policies risk "complete destitution" and homelessness raises questions about Belgium's compliance with international human rights obligations. Staff moral distress suggests deteriorating conditions in asylum centers.

**Political Ramifications:** The N-VA's hardline asylum policy faces resistance from both judiciary and bureaucracy, potentially affecting coalition dynamics in Belgium's complex federal government. The internal rebellion could undermine public confidence in the asylum system's administration.

**European Context:** Belgium's struggle reflects broader EU tensions between national restrictive policies and European human rights standards. The "asylum shopping" debate continues across Europe as countries grapple with responsibility-sharing for asylum seekers.

## What Comes Next

The immediate focus will be on how Fedasil implements the new requirement for individual case assessments. Key questions remain:

1. Will there be disciplinary consequences for staff who signed the letter?
2. How will this affect Belgium's asylum application numbers and reception capacity?
3. What impact will the judicial and bureaucratic resistance have on the N-VA's political standing?
4. Will the EU Court of Justice provide definitive guidance when the Constitutional Court refers questions about Belgium's approach?

The open letter from Fedasil staff represents more than workplace dissatisfaction—it's a warning about the erosion of institutional integrity and rule of law in Belgium's asylum policy. As the country navigates complex migration challenges, balancing system sustainability with legal and humanitarian obligations remains a formidable task for policymakers and frontline workers alike.
