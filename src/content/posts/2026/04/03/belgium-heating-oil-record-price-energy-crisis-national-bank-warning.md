# Belgium heating oil hits record €1.60 amid energy crisis

Belgium is facing an unprecedented energy crisis with heating oil prices reaching historic highs above €1.60 per liter for the first time, while the National Bank of Belgium warns the government has "no more money to fully absorb the energy crisis shock." The dramatic price surge comes as the federal government meets during the Easter holiday period to address growing pressure from businesses, unions, and political parties for targeted intervention.

## Context: Geopolitical tensions drive energy prices

The current crisis is directly linked to the US/Israel offensive against Iran that began on February 28, 2026, which has disrupted global energy markets. According to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/liveblog/nooit-eerder-was-stookolie-zo-duur-maximumprijs-boven-160-euro~1774343778229/), Iran has effectively blocked the Strait of Hormuz—a crucial oil transport route—pushing Brent crude oil prices to $115 per barrel from $70 before the conflict. European gas prices have risen above €50/MWh following threats of "extremely hard" attacks on Iran by US President Donald Trump.

This marks Belgium's second major energy crisis in four years, following the 2022 price surge triggered by Russia's invasion of Ukraine. However, the current situation differs fundamentally: unlike previous crises, Belgium enters this period with what National Bank Governor Pierre Wunsch describes as a deficit that's "much too large" compared to previous economic shocks.

## Record prices and limited government capacity

Heating oil (gasoil de chauffage H0/H7) reached a maximum price of €1.6063 per liter on April 3 for orders of at least 2,000 liters, according to [Le Soir](https://www.lesoir.be/738730/article/2026-04-03/le-prix-du-gasoil-de-chauffage-atteint-un-nouveau-record). This represents nearly a doubling since February 2026 when prices were around €0.83 per liter. From Saturday, April 4, prices will officially exceed €1.60 per liter, as [BRF reports](https://brf.be/national/2064635/).

Simultaneously, National Bank Governor Pierre Wunsch delivered a sobering assessment of the government's financial capacity. In an interview with [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/02/gouverneur-nationale-bank-geen-geld-meer-om-schok-energiecrisis/), Wunsch stated: "Our capacity to take very general measures to absorb the shock for the entire population is too expensive and we no longer have the means for that." He added that Belgium is "starting this crisis with a deficit that's much too large" compared to previous crises.

Wunsch did leave room for targeted aid, noting that "something very targeted could still be done for the most vulnerable people." His warning was echoed by [BRF](https://brf.be/national/2064548/), which reported that Belgium lacks the financial room for maneuver to cushion the energy crisis consequences for the entire population.

## Political tensions and demands for action

The price surge has triggered intense political debate within Belgium's federal government. Prime Minister Bart De Wever (N-VA) stated in parliament that the government has no budgetary space for "helicopter money," arguing that "the state does not profit from an energy crisis, it tears its pants on it. And not just a little bit."

However, coalition partner MR (Francophone liberals) is demanding immediate implementation of a "reverse cliquet" system that would automatically reduce excise taxes when fuel prices exceed certain thresholds. MR President Georges-Louis Bouchez has threatened to block other government dossiers if no measures are taken, arguing that the state has realized €60 million in excess revenue from higher fuel prices that should be returned to citizens.

Business organizations and unions are adding to the pressure. The Union of Middle Classes (UCM) and the National Employees' Center (CNE) have demanded targeted government aid for struggling households and businesses. According to [RTBF Info](https://www.rtbf.be/article/crise-energetique-l-ucm-et-le-syndicat-central-des-employes-demandent-des-aides-ciblees-au-gouvernement-11704741), UCM Secretary General Anne-Sophie Snyers proposed removing fossil fuels from wage indexation calculations to reduce labor costs, while CNE advisor Clarisse Van Tichelen called for controlling oil company profit margins and providing targeted aid for vulnerable workers.

## Economic impact and broader implications

The energy price surge is having widespread economic effects. Diesel prices have increased by 33% since the start of the Iran war, while gasoline prices have risen over 18%. Eurozone inflation climbed to 2.5% in March 2026—the highest since January 2025—with energy contracts increasing significantly on April 1 (gas contracts up 30-40%).

Transport & Logistics Flanders (TLV) has requested urgent targeted measures for the transport sector, warning that diesel remains the "backbone" of the industry. The organization has asked for the transport sector to be recognized as a priority sector in case of shortages to ensure continued access to diesel.

Internationally, the European Commission has recommended that member states implement measures to reduce oil consumption, while countries like Germany have limited fuel price increases to once daily at noon, Australia has halved fuel excise tax for three months, and Egypt has implemented energy-saving measures including early shop closures.

## What's next for Belgium's energy crisis

As the federal government's kern meets during the Easter period, several scenarios are possible. If the Iran conflict ends quickly, National Bank Governor Wunsch suggests the economic impact could be limited. However, if the war continues, Belgium faces a full energy crisis with potentially severe consequences.

The most likely outcome appears to be targeted measures for vulnerable populations rather than broad-based support. Minister of Consumer Protection Rob Beenders (Vooruit) has proposed five measures to structurally lower energy bills that he claims could reduce costs by €300-700 for over 2 million people without costing the government.

Longer-term, the crisis highlights Belgium's continued energy vulnerability and the need for structural solutions. Debate continues over nuclear plant extensions, development of public transport alternatives, and reconsideration of energy policy to reduce dependence on volatile international markets.

With heating oil prices at unprecedented levels and government capacity constrained, Belgian households and businesses face a challenging period ahead as political negotiations continue amid global energy market turmoil.
