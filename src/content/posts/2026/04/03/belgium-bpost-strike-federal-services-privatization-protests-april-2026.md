# Belgium: Postal Strike, Federal Protests Over Privatization

Belgium is experiencing significant labor unrest with a fifth day of strikes at state-owned postal service Bpost and unions announcing a nationwide strike on April 13 against the privatization of federal services. The dual actions reflect deepening tensions between workers and the government over working conditions and the future of public services in Europe's administrative capital.

According to [RTBF](https://www.rtbf.be/article/greve-chez-bpost-l-entreprise-doit-s-adapter-serieusement-au-metier-qu-elle-fait-maintenant-selon-axel-gauthier-11704735), the Bpost strike began in Wallonia and Brussels before spreading to Flanders, with postal workers protesting new work schedules and increased flexibility requirements in the company's transformation plan. Meanwhile, [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/03/stakingsaanzegging-13-april-gevangenissen/) reports that ACV Openbare Diensten, ACOD, and VSOA unions have called for a 24-hour strike across all federal services starting April 12 at 22:00, targeting what they describe as the government's privatization agenda.

## Context: A Postal Service in Transition

The Bpost strike highlights the fundamental challenges facing traditional postal services in the digital age. Axel Gauthier, an economics professor at HEC Liège, explains the sector's transformation: "For about twenty years, mail has been declining. Over the past ten years, we've lost more than half of mail volume. The main activity of Bpost is becoming parcel distribution and no longer mail distribution."

Bpost, which employs approximately 26,000 people, operates under a universal service obligation requiring mail delivery five days per week nationwide and maintenance of post offices. However, the company faces record parcel volumes—breaking records as recently as December 2025—while traditional mail volumes continue their steady decline.

## The Bpost Standoff: Flexibility vs. Working Conditions

Negotiations between Bpost management and unions stalled on April 1, with the next meeting scheduled for April 4. Thierry Tasset, secretary general of the CGSP Poste trade union, described talks as "deadlocked," while Bpost management called discussions "constructive" but acknowledged work was unlikely to resume before April 7 due to an upcoming public holiday.

Mathieu Goedefroy, a Bpost spokesperson, defended the need for operational changes: "Letter deliveries alone are no longer sufficient. Customers expect parcels to be delivered the day after ordering or even on the same day. This isn't feasible if postmen start work at 7 a.m."

Professor Gauthier supports the need for adaptation: "The company must seriously adapt to the business it does now. We need to have flexibility to adapt to market needs. When we were in the world of mail, we distributed mail in the morning to everyone on every street in Belgium. Here, we're in a much more flexible environment."

## April 13: Federal Services Strike Against Privatization

The planned April 13 strike represents a broader confrontation over the future of Belgium's public sector. Unions are specifically protesting Justice Minister Annelies Verlinden's plan to use private security agents in prisons—discussions about which broke down on April 2—and the government's move away from permanent statutory appointments for new hires.

Johan Lippens, ACV union representative for public services, criticized the government's approach: "We must still have the debate about the core tasks of the government, but we already see that the government is making some advance decisions."

The 24-hour action will affect prisons, closed centers for migrants, and all federal government departments, with unions warning of potential disruptions to essential services.

## Broader Labor Unrest and Political Context

These strikes occur within a wider pattern of Belgian labor unrest that has persisted since November 2024. Recent actions include a National Day of Action on March 12 that closed Brussels and Charleroi airports, rail strikes in January and March, and widespread public transport disruptions across Flanders and Wallonia.

The "Arizona" coalition government's austerity measures and public sector reforms have faced sustained union opposition, creating what some analysts describe as the most significant period of industrial action in Belgium in over a decade.

## International Comparisons and Future Implications

Professor Gauthier points to international examples that may inform Belgium's path forward: "In Denmark, the historical postal operator decided at the beginning of this year to stop mail distribution. So postal workers of the Danish post office no longer deliver mail every day. It's replaced by private operators. The reason they stopped mail distribution is that, given the drop in volumes, it was no longer profitable at all."

He suggests Belgium might reconsider its universal service obligations: "We could imagine that in the future we review some of these obligations. We could, for example, think that five-day-a-week mail delivery could be questioned given the very significant drop in mail volume."

## What's Next: Critical Negotiations and Potential Escalation

With Bpost negotiations resuming on April 4 and the April 13 federal strike looming, Belgium faces critical weeks for labor relations. The outcomes will signal whether the government and public sector employers can find compromises that address both economic realities and worker concerns.

Vanessa Matz, the minister responsible for Bpost, has expressed her intention to help restart social dialogue, but unions remain skeptical given the breakdown of multiple negotiations. The continuous strike pattern since late 2024 suggests workers are prepared for prolonged action if their demands aren't met.

As Belgium navigates these dual labor crises, the fundamental question remains: How can traditional public services adapt to 21st-century economic realities while maintaining the quality employment and service standards that citizens expect? The answers emerging from Brussels may offer lessons for other European nations facing similar transitions in their public sectors.
