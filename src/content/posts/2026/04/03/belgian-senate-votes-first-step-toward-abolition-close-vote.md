# Belgian Senate Votes on First Step Toward Its Own Abolition

The Belgian Senate is preparing for a historic vote that could mark the beginning of the end for Belgium's upper parliamentary chamber. On April 3, 2026, senators are voting on reforming Article 195 of the Constitution—the first of eight required steps to eventually abolish the Senate entirely. The vote requires a two-thirds majority (36 out of 54 seats), with the outcome expected to be extremely close as political divisions surface over the future of Belgium's federal institutions.

## Context and Background

Belgium has maintained a bicameral parliament since its founding in 1831, consisting of the Chamber of Representatives and the Senate. However, over successive state reforms—particularly in 1993 and 2014—the Senate has lost most of its legislative powers. Today, it primarily serves as a meeting point for the country's communities and regions, with proponents of abolition arguing it costs approximately €50 million annually for limited remaining functions.

The move to abolish the Senate is part of the coalition agreement of the De Wever government, which reached consensus on the measure in June 2025. According to [Belga News Agency](https://www.belganewsagency.eu/belgium-takes-first-step-towards-abolition-of-senate), the Senate's abolition is planned to take effect before the 2029 federal elections, not immediately.

## The Constitutional Hurdle

The current vote focuses on reforming Article 195, which governs the procedure for amending the Constitution. This is seen as a necessary preliminary step before the Senate itself can be abolished. The Senate's Institutional Affairs Committee already approved the proposal on March 30, 2026, with support from the coalition parties, Anders, Vlaams Belang, and Groen-Ecolo. The PS voted against and PVDA abstained.

Prime Minister Bart De Wever has emphasized the importance of addressing concerns about minority representation. Speaking in German during committee discussions, he said "we have time to find solutions to every problem" and stressed his intention to resolve the issue "before the end of the process," as reported by Belga News Agency.

## Political Dynamics and Opposition

The main opposition comes from Liesa Scholzen, a German-speaking senator from MR (Mouvement Réformateur). Scholzen opposes the abolition because it would eliminate the only guaranteed federal representation for the German-speaking community, approximately 77,000 people in eastern Belgium. According to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/liveblog/keurt-senaat-straks-eerste-stap-richting-eigen-afschaffing-goed~1771325690894/), Scholzen is voting against "because there are no guarantees for an alternative: a guaranteed German-speaking seat in the Chamber."

The government coalition (known as the "Arizona" coalition) consists of N-VA, MR, Les Engagés, Vooruit, and CD&V. Together they hold 34 votes in the Senate, but with one senator sick and Scholzen planning to vote against, they fall short of the required 36 votes. The coalition is therefore counting on support from three senators from Anders and one from Groen to reach the two-thirds threshold.

Political tensions are evident, with MR and Les Engagés reportedly not wanting the two-thirds majority achieved with support from extreme left (PVDA/PTB) or extreme right (Vlaams Belang) parties. PVDA/PTB has announced it will abstain from the vote.

## Historical Significance and Implications

If successful, this vote would represent one of the most significant institutional reforms in Belgium's recent history, moving the country from a bicameral to unicameral federal parliament. Belgium would join other EU countries like Sweden, Denmark, and Portugal with unicameral systems at the national level.

The abolition process requires a "double two-thirds majority"—two-thirds in both the Chamber and Senate, followed by elections, then another two-thirds vote in both chambers. This complex constitutional procedure reflects the sensitivity of altering Belgium's federal structure, which includes three language communities (Dutch, French, German) and three regions (Flanders, Wallonia, Brussels-Capital).

## Financial and Practical Considerations

Proponents of abolition highlight the potential savings of approximately €50 million annually from eliminating Senate operations. However, critics argue this amount is relatively small compared to Belgium's overall federal budget and that the Senate serves important symbolic and practical functions in representing regional interests.

The Senate currently consists of 60 senators total: 50 regional senators (29 Dutch-speaking, 20 French-speaking, 1 German-speaking) and 10 co-opted senators. Its remaining powers include appointing judges and handling conflicts of interest—functions that would need to be transferred to the Chamber of Representatives if abolition proceeds.

## What's Next

Regardless of today's outcome, the Senate abolition process faces several more hurdles. After this initial constitutional amendment, seven additional steps would be required before the upper house could be formally dissolved. The government aims to complete the process before the 2029 federal elections.

The key unresolved issue remains representation for the German-speaking community. Prime Minister De Wever has acknowledged this concern but offered no specific guarantees yet. How this minority community will maintain a voice at the federal level in a unicameral system will likely dominate subsequent discussions.

Today's vote serves as a critical test for the De Wever government's reform agenda and Belgium's evolving federal structure. The narrow margin reflects ongoing divisions about the role and necessity of the Senate in Belgium's complex political landscape, where regional and linguistic representation remains a fundamental concern.
