# Belgian Fertility Centers Violated Sperm Donor Limits, Probe Finds

At least seven Belgian fertility centers systematically violated legal limits on sperm donor usage, exceeding the maximum of six families per donor within their own facilities, according to an investigation by [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/02/fertiliteitscentra-spermadonoren-zelf-limiet-overschreden-6-gezi/). The Federal Agency for Medicines and Health Products (FAGG) has identified 35 donors who were used more frequently than permitted, with both domestic and international donors affected—some with genetic abnormalities.

## Regulatory Response and Potential Prosecutions

The FAGG has submitted 10 official reports (procès-verbaux) to prosecutors, potentially leading to criminal investigations against six of the centers. One center received only a warning. According to the agency, penalties for such violations can include fines ranging from €8,000 to €80,000, possible prison sentences of 1-5 years for doctors, and potential five-year bans from medical practice.

"The FAGG is not systematically informed about the follow-up of submitted cases," the agency stated, indicating that decisions about whether to open investigations rest with prosecutors. The violations involve donors being used for more than six families within individual centers, despite centers having responsibility for tracking usage within their own facilities.

## Historical Context and Systemic Failures

Belgium established the six-family limit per donor in 2007, but a national donor registry was only implemented in 2024. For 17 years, centers couldn't track donor usage across different facilities, creating what industry experts describe as a "blind spot" in regulation. However, centers were always responsible for monitoring usage within their own institutions.

"We cannot hold centers responsible for an infringement they didn't know they were committing," the FAGG stated during an earlier hearing, referring to cross-center violations. However, the current scandal involves within-center violations where tracking should have been possible.

## Industry Response: Claims of Unintentional Violations

The Belgian Society for Reproductive Medicine (BSRM) claims the violations were not intentional. "We wish to emphasize that the law was not consciously or intentionally violated by the centers," said Shari Mackens, vice president of BSRM. The organization cites regulatory ambiguity, particularly regarding whether foreign intended parents should be counted toward the six-family limit.

"We suspect this may explain a significant number of the identified violations," Mackens told VRT NWS, adding that the FAGG failed to provide clarity on this issue. The society also acknowledges that tracking systems in early years were "not foolproof."

## Timeline of a Growing Scandal

The current revelations represent the latest development in a scandal that began in June 2025. Key milestones include:

- **September 2025**: Investigation launched into UZ Brussel fertility center
- **November 2025**: Inspections revealed serious errors at Belgian fertility centers
- **March 2026**: Scandal expanded to at least 93 donors used more frequently than allowed across centers
- **April 2026**: Current revelation of seven centers violating within-center limits

At the scandal's outset in June 2025, the FAGG claimed there was no signal of "systematic or major problems at the level of the fertility centers themselves." The agency changed its position after compiling historical data and strengthening controls in the scandal's aftermath.

## Expected Escalation and Future Implications

Industry experts expect the number of violations to increase as more data is analyzed. "Given thousands of donors used in Belgium since 2007, it seems unlikely there would only be 35 donors with violations within a single center," Mackens stated. She added that within-center violations likely represent "a minority of the bulk of infringements across centers."

The national donor registry, implemented in 2024, is now being populated with historical data. This process is expected to provide a complete picture of donor usage violations in Belgium. Meanwhile, centers have begun self-reporting violations as awareness increases.

## Comparative International Context

Belgium's six-family limit is among the strictest internationally. The Netherlands allows 25 families per donor, while the United Kingdom permits 10. Some countries, including parts of the United States, have no legal limits. The strict Belgian limit was designed to prevent genetic concentration and psychological issues for donor-conceived children.

## Psychological and Ethical Implications

The scandal raises significant concerns for affected families and donor-conceived children. Psychological impacts may include identity issues, concerns about genetic relationships, and questions about medical history—particularly for children conceived from donors with genetic abnormalities. The violations also undermine trust in Belgium's fertility treatment sector, which has been regarded as advanced and well-regulated.

## Looking Forward: Regulatory Reforms and Family Support

As prosecutors consider the 10 submitted cases, calls are growing for stricter enforcement of existing regulations and potential reforms. The scandal has highlighted the need for:

1. **Retrospective data entry** into the national donor registry
2. **Clearer regulatory guidance** on issues like foreign intended parents
3. **Enhanced oversight mechanisms** for fertility centers
4. **Support systems** for affected families and donor-conceived children

Parents, children, or donors with questions are advised to contact their fertility centers directly. The FAGG continues to strengthen controls and compile historical data as the full scope of violations becomes clearer.
