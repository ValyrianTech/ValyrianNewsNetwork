# China Shifts Housing Policy to People-Centered Development

China is fundamentally reorienting its housing policy from a speculative investment-driven model to a social welfare-focused approach under the 15th Five-Year Plan (2026-2030), with President Xi Jinping emphasizing "people-centered development" and creating "livable and comfortable housing" for citizens. According to a [feature article in People's Daily](http://politics.people.com.cn/n1/2026/0403/c461001-40695013.html), this paradigm shift represents the first time in decades that real estate has been repositioned from an economic driver to a "public well-being" sector, signaling a profound change in China's development priorities.

## Context: From Speculation to Social Welfare

China's housing system has undergone significant transformation since market-oriented reforms began. President Xi's 2016 declaration that "housing is for living in, not for speculation" marked the beginning of a policy correction phase that has now culminated in the social welfare orientation of the 15th Five-Year Plan. Housing Minister Ni Hong stated in January 2026 that the 15th Five-Year Plan period will be "a critical stage for China's housing and urban-rural development sector," acknowledging challenges while emphasizing their potential as "valuable opportunities," according to official government statements.

## Key Developments: Three Models of Implementation

### Xiong'an New Area: Settling Down with Comprehensive Services

The Xiong'an New Area, established in 2017 to relieve Beijing of "non-capital functions," exemplifies the new approach to urban development. The People's Daily report details that by the end of 2025, Xiong'an had 80 elderly care facilities, 105 schools and kindergartens, and 43 community centers operational, creating a "15-minute life circle" for its residents. 

Innovative governance models include "people's sentiment ledgers" tracking 3,764 households' needs and Party-building leadership mechanisms. "Someone cares, someone helps. I adapted within half a year of relocation, and my life is very secure now," said 80-year-old resident Zhang Erlou, who moved from a rural village near Baiyangdian Lake, as reported in the People's Daily article.

### Shanghai Migrant Housing: Integration Through Multi-Level Rental

Shanghai has developed a "a bed, a room, a home" multi-level rental system targeting migrant workers and young professionals. According to the People's Daily article, the "New Era Urban Builders and Managers Homes" program provides over 70,000 beds at 500 yuan ($70) per month, with plans for 25,000-27,000 new affordable rental units and 120,000+ additional beds during the 15th Five-Year Plan period.

"The General Secretary said 'You are also Shanghai's builders and contributors, so Shanghai should also care about you.' This made me more determined to take root, settle, and develop in Shanghai," said 28-year-old migrant worker Wu Tao, who has lived in one of these communities for nearly three years, according to the People's Daily report.

### Beijing Historic Preservation: Balancing Heritage and Modernity

Beijing's approach focuses on preserving historical character while improving living conditions. Through "application-based relocation," "back alley renovation," and "courtyard-wide relocation" programs, the city has transformed neighborhoods like Caochang Sitiao Hutong while maintaining their cultural essence, as detailed in the People's Daily article.

"Now we have shared kitchens, discussion spaces, and small libraries. We live more comfortably and vibrantly, finding new flavor in old hutongs," said 72-year-old resident Li Changlin, referring to the "co-living courtyards" that have become community hubs, according to the People's Daily report.

## Analysis: Implications of the Policy Shift

Wu Jing, Director of the Center for Real Estate at Tsinghua University, notes that "China's housing system must be crystal clear about its foundational purpose—serving basic needs. Only then can we discuss its broader economic contribution," according to policy analysis reports. This perspective underscores the fundamental rethinking of housing's role in China's development model.

The policy shift has several significant implications:

1. **Economic Transition:** The housing sector is evolving from a primary growth driver to a social stabilizer, with increased focus on managing existing stock rather than new construction.
2. **Social Stability:** Improved housing security could enhance social cohesion during China's economic transition, particularly as the property market undergoes correction.
3. **Urban Development Models:** Xiong'an is being presented as a sustainable urban development blueprint, offering an alternative to earlier rapid-urbanization models.

## Challenges and Forward Look

Despite the ambitious vision, significant challenges remain. The 15th Five-Year Plan prioritizes several key areas:
1. A dual-track system emphasizing public rental housing and government-subsidized purchase housing alongside commercial housing
2. City-specific policies tailored to local demographic trends and economic potential
3. New quality standards emphasizing safety, comfort, eco-friendliness, and smart features
4. Urban renewal focusing on old community renovations, elevator installations, and green space creation
5. Risk prevention placing real estate risks at the top of the national risk-prevention agenda

As China implements this people-centered housing vision, the world will be watching whether this social welfare orientation can be sustained amid economic growth pressures and whether the balanced development model exemplified by Xiong'an can successfully address regional disparities in housing markets. The success of this policy shift will significantly influence not only China's domestic stability but also its international presentation as a model for sustainable urban development.
