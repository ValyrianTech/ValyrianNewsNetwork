# Fans Camp Early for Tour of Flanders, Securing Prime Spots

Cycling enthusiasts have already begun camping along the route of the Tour of Flanders days before the iconic classic race takes place this Sunday, continuing a tradition that demonstrates the deep cultural significance of cycling in Flanders. According to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/kijk/2026/04/02/video-supporters-ronde-van-vlaanderen/), fans are securing prime viewing positions at key locations like the Paterberg, one of the race's famous cobbled climbs, with some campers having participated in this ritual for over two decades.

## A Tradition Spanning Generations

The early arrivals represent a cross-section of Flemish cycling fandom, from veteran supporters to groups of friends united by their passion for the sport. Johan Ceule, 66, from Bavikhove, is camping at the Paterberg for the twelfth consecutive year, this time joined by his recently retired wife Magda, 67. "The Tour of Flanders? That is the highlight of highlights," Ceule told reporters, encapsulating the event's significance in the regional sporting calendar.

Rita and François from Lommel represent the longevity of this tradition, having camped along the route for more than 20 years. Their commitment reflects how the Tour of Flanders—known locally as "Vlaanderens Mooiste" (Flanders' Most Beautiful)—transcends mere sporting competition to become a cultural touchstone and community event.

## Community and Camaraderie

For many campers, the experience is as much about social connection as watching the race. Christine from Brugge, part of a group of eight friends supporting cyclist Wout van Aert, explained their early arrival: "We are with 4 campers. If we would come later, we couldn't stay together." The group, hailing from Brugge, Dendermonde, and Erpe-Mere, spends their time together eating, walking, and enjoying aperitifs while awaiting the race.

This social dimension transforms the roadside into a temporary community of like-minded enthusiasts. The camping tradition creates spaces where friendships are strengthened, stories are shared, and the collective anticipation builds toward Sunday's main event. Christine expressed the group's hopes for their favorite rider: "He rides very well. We hope he can be the winner on Sunday."

## Logistical Adaptations and Challenges

This year presents unique logistical challenges due to road works on Parklaan, which has displaced campers from their usual spots. These spaces are now reserved for local residents during construction, forcing enthusiasts to adapt their traditional camping arrangements. Such adjustments highlight the ongoing negotiation between fan enthusiasm and practical urban planning that characterizes major cycling events in densely populated Flanders.

The phenomenon also raises questions about waste management, sanitation, and environmental impact as increasing numbers of fans arrive days in advance. Local authorities must balance accommodating this passionate following with maintaining public order and minimizing disruption to residents along the route.

## Cultural Significance of the Ronde

The Tour of Flanders, first held in 1913 and run annually since (except during the World Wars), is one of cycling's five "Monuments"—the most prestigious one-day races in the professional calendar. Its route through the Vlaamse Ardennen (Flemish Ardennes) features steep, cobbled climbs that have become legendary in cycling lore, including the Oude Kwaremont, Paterberg, and Koppenberg.

The race's cultural importance in Flanders cannot be overstated. It represents regional identity, historical continuity, and sporting passion that crosses generational lines. The early camping tradition exemplifies how the event has evolved from a simple bicycle race to a multi-day cultural festival that engages entire communities.

## Economic and Tourism Impact

The extended stays of cycling fans provide significant economic benefits to the Vlaamse Ardennen region. Local businesses—from cafes and restaurants to grocery stores and camping supply outlets—experience increased revenue in the days leading up to the race. This economic boost extends beyond race day itself, as fans stock up on supplies and occasionally explore nearby towns and attractions.

Tourism authorities have gradually recognized the value of these early arrivals, with some years seeing organized efforts to provide additional camping facilities. VRT coverage has documented the institutional acknowledgment of the tradition's scale and potential for managed growth, reflecting how authorities have adapted to accommodate the passionate fan base.

## The Future of Roadside Fandom

As the Tour of Flanders continues to grow in international prestige, the early camping tradition faces both opportunities and challenges. Increasing media attention may attract more participants, potentially straining existing infrastructure. However, it also offers chances for enhanced community engagement, improved facilities, and greater recognition of this unique aspect of cycling culture.

The dedication displayed by fans like Johan Ceule, Rita and François, and Christine's group ensures the tradition's continuity. Their willingness to take time off work, invest in camping equipment, and brave early spring weather demonstrates a commitment that goes beyond casual fandom to become an integral part of their annual rhythms and social lives.

## What Sunday Brings

When the professional peloton finally speeds past these campers on Sunday, it will represent the culmination of days of anticipation and community building. For the fans who have waited patiently in their roadside encampments, the fleeting moment of the race's passage validates their dedication and reinforces their connection to this iconic event.

The Tour of Flanders remains more than a bicycle race—it's a cultural phenomenon that brings together sport, community, and regional identity in a uniquely Flemish celebration. The early campers along its route are not just spectators but active participants in this living tradition, ensuring that "Vlaanderens Mooiste" continues to inspire passion and community for generations to come.
