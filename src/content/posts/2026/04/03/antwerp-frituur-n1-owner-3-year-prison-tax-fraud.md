# Antwerp Chip Shop Owner Gets 3 Years for Tax Fraud

The owner of Antwerp's iconic Frituur N°1 has been sentenced to three years in prison for systematic tax fraud, marking a significant legal blow to one of the city's most famous culinary institutions. Jacobus 'Koos' Perdaems, 69, was convicted by the Antwerp Correctional Court for hiding over €1 million in income from tax authorities between 2016 and 2022, according to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/03/frituur-n-1-antwerpen-3-jaar-cel-fiscale-fraude-opschorting-mari/). His long-time employee, known locally as 'Maria', was also found guilty in the case that exposes deep-rooted tax evasion practices at the historic chip shop.

## A Landmark Establishment's Downfall

Frituur N°1 has been an Antwerp institution since Perdaems opened it in the early 1980s in Hoogstraat, just steps from the City Hall and Cathedral. The location became legendary among locals and tourists alike for its traditional Belgian fries, but court documents reveal it operated for decades on a cash-only basis specifically to facilitate tax evasion. The business refused to implement Belgium's mandatory electronic cash register system, known as 'white cash registers', which are designed to prevent exactly this type of fraud.

According to court findings reported by [MSN/Belga](https://www.msn.com/nl-be/nieuws/nationaal/rechtbank-antwerpen-eigenaar-van-frituur-n-1-veroordeeld-tot-drie-jaar-cel-voor-fiscale-fraude/ar-AA204hcx), Perdaems built substantial wealth through the popular establishment but systematically underreported his income. Daily practices included removing cash from the register and hiding it in the basement before transporting envelopes of money to his residence in Spain via a courier.

## Systematic Fraud and Repeat Offenses

The court found Perdaems guilty of concealing more than €1 million in revenue from tax authorities over a six-year period. This wasn't his first encounter with tax authorities—in March 2023, he had already been convicted to one year of suspended prison, a €6,000 fine, and an eight-year management ban, with €3,149,000 confiscated in that earlier case.

"The owner could no longer count on any leniency from the judge," VRT NWS reported, noting that Perdaems showed contempt for tax authorities and continued fraudulent practices despite previous convictions. The court imposed a harsh sentence specifically because he was a repeat offender who demonstrated no remorse.

## Employee Complicity and Testimony

Maria Vanlommel, the employee known simply as 'Maria' to generations of customers, played a crucial role in both the fraud and its exposure. She provided extensive confessions detailing how the systematic tax evasion worked, telling authorities, "We generated black income from the start, and that never changed." Her testimony proved instrumental in securing the conviction.

While found guilty, Maria received a suspended sentence with no immediate punishment. The court acknowledged her cooperation but maintained that her years of participation in the fraud scheme warranted a formal conviction.

## Additional Convictions and Financial Penalties

The sentencing included severe financial consequences beyond the prison term. Perdaems received an €80,000 fine and a 20-year professional ban preventing him from operating any business. He, his company, and Maria must also pay €184,000 in unpaid VAT from 2016-2022 plus a €92,000 penalty, totaling €276,000 owed to the Belgian state.

A courier who transported cash to Spain was fined €16,000 (half suspended), while the company behind the frituur received an €8,000 suspended fine. A fifth defendant in the case was acquitted.

## Broader Implications for Hospitality Sector

This case represents more than just the downfall of a single business—it signals Belgian authorities' intensified crackdown on tax evasion in the cash-heavy hospitality sector. Belgium has been implementing stricter measures to combat fiscal fraud, including mandatory electronic cash registers that create transparent transaction records.

The conviction of such a high-profile establishment sends a clear message to other businesses operating primarily in cash. With Frituur N°1 being an Antwerp landmark, the case has garnered significant public attention and may prompt increased scrutiny of similar establishments across the country.

## Historical Context and Business Model

Frituur N°1's business model was emblematic of traditional cash-based operations that have become increasingly problematic in modern tax systems. For decades, the shop accepted only cash payments, creating no electronic trail of transactions. When Belgium introduced requirements for electronic cash registers to improve tax compliance, Perdaems refused to implement the system.

This resistance to modernization, combined with the deliberate daily practice of removing and hiding cash, created what prosecutors called "systematic and deliberate fiscal fraud." The case highlights the tension between traditional business practices and contemporary tax compliance requirements.

## What's Next for Frituur N°1?

The future of the iconic chip shop remains uncertain. With its owner facing three years in prison and a 20-year professional ban, and with substantial financial penalties imposed, the business may struggle to continue operations. The case has already damaged the establishment's reputation, though its prime location and historical significance could make it attractive to new owners willing to operate transparently.

For Belgian tax authorities, the conviction represents a significant victory in their ongoing battle against fiscal fraud. The case demonstrates that even long-established, beloved businesses are not immune to prosecution when engaging in systematic tax evasion.

## Conclusion: A Warning to Cash-Based Businesses

The sentencing of Frituur N°1's owner serves as a stark warning to businesses operating in cash-heavy sectors. As tax authorities worldwide increase scrutiny of electronic payment avoidance and implement systems to track transactions more effectively, traditional methods of tax evasion become increasingly risky.

For Antwerp, the case marks the end of an era for one of its culinary landmarks, but potentially the beginning of more transparent business practices in the hospitality sector. The message from authorities is clear: tax compliance is non-negotiable, regardless of a business's popularity or historical significance.
