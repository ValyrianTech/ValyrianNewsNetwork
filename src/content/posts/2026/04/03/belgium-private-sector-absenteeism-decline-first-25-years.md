# Belgium's Private Sector Absenteeism Drops for First Time in 25 Years

For the first time in a quarter-century, Belgium has recorded a significant decrease in private sector absenteeism, marking a historic reversal of a long-standing trend of steadily increasing sick leave rates. According to a comprehensive study by HR services provider Securex, overall absenteeism dropped from a record 8.49% in 2024 to 8.09% in 2025, representing the first meaningful decline since the turn of the millennium.

The study, based on data from 22,583 employers and 188,857 employees across Belgium's private sector, reveals that medium-term absenteeism (between one month and one year) showed the most pronounced decrease, falling from 2.58% to 2.30%. Short-term absenteeism (up to one month) also declined from 2.58% to 2.43%, while long-term absenteeism (over one year) remained at a historically high level of 3.35%.

## Context and Historical Background

Belgium has experienced a steady increase in private sector absenteeism since 2000, with previous records consistently being broken year after year. The 2024 figure of 8.49% represented the culmination of this 25-year trend, making the 2025 reversal particularly noteworthy for labor market analysts and policymakers.

The persistent rise in sick leave rates had become a significant concern for Belgian businesses and the broader economy, affecting productivity, operational costs, and workforce stability. Previous Securex reports had documented record levels across various categories, including an October 2025 report showing long-term absenteeism reaching new highs in the first half of that year.

## Key Developments and Policy Impact

According to [Securex](https://www.rtbf.be/article/l-absenteisme-en-baisse-de-facon-significative-en-2025-une-premiere-en-25-ans-11704690), the decline in medium-term absenteeism coincides with the implementation of a new "convocation policy" introduced in 2024 and fully operational in 2025. This policy strengthens support from medical advisors and return-to-work coordinators, who now contact employees on sick leave earlier and more frequently to explore possibilities for adapted reintegration.

"We notice in practice that the follow-up of absent employees is faster and better targeted," said Elisabeth Etter, HR consultant at Securex. "Workers feel better informed and supported, while employers and managers think more actively about reintegration possibilities. This common willingness to approach the subject openly strengthens the quality of the reintegration process in practice."

The [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/03/absenteisme-daalt-voor-het-eerst-in-25-jaar/) report corroborates these findings, with Stephanie Heurterre, Senior HR-consultant at Securex, noting: "We notice that absent employees are followed up more quickly and specifically. Employees feel better informed and supported, while employers and line managers think more actively about reintegration possibilities. This shared willingness to have an open conversation strengthens the quality of the reintegration process."

## Demographic Differences and Health Factors

The decline in absenteeism has been more pronounced among blue-collar workers than white-collar employees, a difference experts attribute to the nature of health issues affecting these groups. According to Gianinna Ng, Medical Director at Mutualités Libres/Partenamut, "Among manual workers, the causes are more often physical, while among office staff, psychosocial disorders are more often the cause."

Ng explained that "physical conditions often offer a more predictable recovery path and are better known than psychosocial conditions. The threshold to return to work part-time with the same employer is therefore potentially lower than in the case of strongly work-related psychosocial complaints such as burnout."

This distinction highlights the complexity of addressing workforce health issues, suggesting that different approaches may be needed for different worker categories and health conditions.

## Economic Significance and Business Impact

The reduction in absenteeism represents positive news for the Belgian economy, potentially translating to increased productivity and reduced operational costs for businesses. The [BRF](https://brf.be/national/2064566/) report notes that on a regular workday in 2025, an average of 8.09% of private sector employees were on sick leave, compared to 8.49% in 2024 when the rate reached a new record.

For industries with higher concentrations of blue-collar workers, the benefits may be more immediately apparent, while knowledge-intensive sectors may require continued focus on addressing psychosocial health issues that tend to affect white-collar employees more frequently.

## Government Initiatives and Future Outlook

The Belgian government has been implementing various initiatives to address workforce health challenges, including a federal pilot project scheduled for implementation by the end of 2026. This project will allow employees at risk of long-term illness to temporarily reduce their working hours while receiving supplementary benefits, aiming to prevent complete workforce dropout through early intervention.

Additionally, Flemish and federal governments are working more closely to help long-term sick employees return to work, reflecting a growing recognition of the need for coordinated, preventive approaches to workforce health.

## Analysis and Implications

The historic decline in absenteeism suggests that targeted policy interventions can effectively reverse negative labor market trends. The success of Belgium's convocation policy provides valuable evidence for other countries grappling with similar workforce health challenges, particularly in Europe where absenteeism rates have been a persistent concern.

However, significant challenges remain. Long-term absenteeism continues at historically high levels, and psychosocial issues—particularly among white-collar workers—present complex challenges that may require different intervention strategies than physical health conditions.

The sustainability of the trend reversal beyond 2025 will be crucial to monitor, as will regional variations between Flanders, Wallonia, and Brussels, and sectoral differences in improvement rates.

## What's Next

As Belgium continues to implement and refine its workforce health policies, several developments warrant attention in the coming months:

1. **Continued monitoring** of absenteeism trends throughout 2026 to determine whether the decline represents a lasting reversal or a temporary fluctuation.
2. **Evaluation of pilot projects** and expansion of successful intervention strategies to broader segments of the workforce.
3. **Increased focus on preventive measures** in workplace health, particularly for psychosocial issues affecting white-collar employees.
4. **Potential international interest** in Belgium's approach as other nations seek solutions to similar workforce health challenges.

The 2025 data represents a significant milestone in Belgium's labor market evolution, offering hope that with appropriate policies and collaborative approaches, longstanding negative trends in workforce health can be effectively addressed. As businesses and policymakers continue to refine their strategies, the coming years will reveal whether this historic decline marks the beginning of a sustained improvement in Belgium's workforce health landscape.
