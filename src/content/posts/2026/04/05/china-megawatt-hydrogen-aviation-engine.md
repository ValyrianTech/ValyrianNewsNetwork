# China Flies World's First Megawatt-Class Hydrogen Engine

In a landmark achievement for sustainable aerospace, China has successfully completed the maiden flight of the world's first megawatt-class liquid hydrogen (LH2) turboprop engine. On April 4, 2026, the AEP100 engine, developed by the Hunan Power Machinery Research Institute under the Aero Engine Corporation of China (AECC), powered a 7.5-ton unmanned transport aircraft through a successful test flight at Zhuzhou Lusong Airport in Hunan province.

According to [Xinhua News Agency](https://www.news.cn/tech/20260404/dc3516b93df249f49e3f354248311fe8/c.html), the flight lasted 16 minutes, covering a distance of 36 kilometers at a speed of 220 km/h and reaching an altitude of 300 meters. The mission verified the engine's stability and performance under actual flight conditions, marking a transition from laboratory exploration to practical engineering application.

## The Technical Leap: Why Megawatts Matter

While hydrogen aviation has seen various prototypes in recent years, the "megawatt-class" designation is a critical differentiator. Most previous hydrogen-powered aircraft have relied on fuel-cell electric propulsion, which is efficient for small, light aircraft but struggles to provide the raw power necessary for heavier payloads.

The AEP100 utilizes direct combustion of liquid hydrogen. By using liquid hydrogen (LH2) rather than compressed gas, the engine achieves the high energy density required to generate megawatt-level power. This allows for the propulsion of significantly larger aircraft—in this case, a 7.5-ton platform—which is a substantial leap over the smaller-scale prototypes seen globally.

Engineering this system required solving complex challenges regarding cryogenic fuel delivery. Liquid hydrogen must be stored and transported at extremely low temperatures; maintaining a steady flow and ensuring combustion stability during rapid throttle changes is a primary technical hurdle that the AECC team has now successfully addressed.

## Strategic Alignment: The 'Low-Altitude Economy'

This breakthrough is not an isolated scientific success but a cornerstone of China's strategic "Low-Altitude Economy" policy. The Chinese government has been aggressively promoting the development of unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) and urban air mobility to revolutionize logistics and transport.

Experts from the AECC indicate that the immediate applications for the AEP100 will not be passenger travel, but rather high-capacity, carbon-neutral logistics. Specifically, the technology is targeted toward:

*   **Unmanned Air Cargo:** Enabling heavy-lift logistics without the carbon footprint of traditional kerosene-based engines.
*   **Island Logistics:** Providing sustainable transport links for remote coastal and island regions where traditional infrastructure is lacking.

As the cost of "green hydrogen" (hydrogen produced via renewable energy) continues to decline, the economic and energy security advantages of this propulsion system are expected to become more pronounced.

## Global Competitive Landscape

Globally, the race for zero-emission aviation is intensifying. Western aerospace giants, most notably Airbus with its ZEROe project, are exploring a mix of hydrogen combustion and fuel cells. However, China's successful flight of a megawatt-class turboprop on a multi-ton platform demonstrates a competitive edge in the high-power combustion segment.

While fuel cells are ideal for short-range, light-duty flights, the AEP100's direct-combustion approach is more scalable for the regional and trunk aviation markets. By proving the engineering reliability of the full technical chain—from cryogenic storage to combustion—China has positioned itself to dominate the emerging market for green heavy-lift aviation.

## Looking Ahead

The success of the AEP100 bridges the gap between theoretical research and industrialization. While the current focus remains on unmanned transport, this technology serves as a vital stepping stone toward manned regional aircraft. 

Industry observers suggest that this development will likely trigger a synergistic upgrade across the entire hydrogen value chain, including green hydrogen production, specialized cryogenic storage infrastructure, and the development of new materials capable of withstanding the unique stresses of LH2 combustion. The flight at Zhuzhou Lusong Airport marks the beginning of a new era where heavy aviation is no longer tethered to fossil fuels.
