# Spanish PM Visits China to Strengthen Ties Amid US-China Tensions

Spanish Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez has arrived in China for a high-stakes official visit lasting from April 11 to 15, 202 and, marking a critical attempt to bolster bilateral relations amidst intensifying geopolitical competition between the United States and China. The visit, Sánchez's fourth in just three years, comes at a time when global diplomacy is being reshaped by the economic and security pressures of the Trump administration in Washington.

According to [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com/news/spain-woos-china-risking-clashes-with-trump-and-europeans/), the Spanish mission is characterized by a complex balancing act, as Madrid seeks to secure significant economic benefits while navigating the sensitive security and economic 'de-risking' strategies currently being championed by the European Union.

## The Economic Imperative

A primary driver for the Prime Minister's mission is the urgent need to address a massive bilateral trade imbalance. Spain faces a significant trade deficit with China, which reached approximately €42.2 billion as of 2025. To correct this, Madrid is aggressively pursuing the opening of Chinese markets for Spanish agricultural and industrial exports.

Beyond trade, Spain is actively courting 'productive investment' from Beijing. A cornerstone of this strategy is the continued development of high-tech manufacturing, exemplified by the €4.1 billion battery manufacturing plant in Zaragoza, a joint venture involving CATL and Stellantis. The visit also targets deeper cooperation in the energy, technology, and telecommunications sectors.

## A Diplomatic Tightrope

However, the pursuit of economic prosperity is fraught with geopolitical risk. The visit occurs against a backdrop of heightened tension between the US and China, placing Spain in a difficult position within the Western alliance. While Spain's 'positive' diplomatic tone aims for strategic partnership, it risks creating friction with the US-led security architecture and other EU members.

Specifically, the EU's broader 'de-risking' strategy seeks to reduce economic and security dependence on Beijing. This includes contentious debates over the use of Chinese technology, such as Huawei, in critical European infrastructure. Spain's proactive engagement could be seen as a divergence from the collective security goals of the European Union.

## Key Developments and Itinerary

The official itinerary includes high-level engagements with China's top leadership. Following his arrival on April 11, Sánchez is scheduled to attend a state banquet hosted by President Xi Jinping on Tuesday, April 12. This will be followed by a joint session with Premier Li Qiang, where the two nations are expected to sign various bilateral trade and investment agreements.

Sánchez is also scheduled to meet with top legislator Zhao Leji and engage with the EU Chamber of Prime Minister in China, as well as leaders from the energy and automotive sectors, to discuss the future of industrial cooperation.

## Analysis and Implications

While the economic incentives are clear, experts suggest the long-term implications for Spain's security remain a point of concern. Professor Mario Esteban of Madrid’s Autonomous University notes that while Spain's current approach is unprecedented and potentially friction-causing, the country's fundamental security and defense reliance remains much more closely tied to the United States than to China.

Ultimately, Pedro Sánchez's visit serves as a litmus test for European strategic autonomy. If Madrid can achieve tangible economic gains without triggering diplomatic sanctions or security backlashes from Washington or Brussels, it may embolden other European nations to pursue similar paths. Conversely, if the visit exacerbates the rift within the EU's common foreign policy, it could signal a fragmentation of Western diplomatic cohesion in an increasingly multipolar world.
