# Antwerp's €500,000 Tour of Flanders Start Debate

As approximately 20,000 spectators gather in Antwerp this weekend for the start of the Tour of Flanders, city officials face a pressing question: is the annual €500,000 investment in hosting cycling's premier Flemish event worth continuing beyond 2027? According to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/01/ronde-van-vlaanderen-start-antwerpen/), the current alternating agreement with Bruges expires next year, and negotiations for future start locations have yet to begin—leaving Antwerp's role as host city uncertain.

## The Contract Countdown

The Tour of Flanders, one of cycling's five "Monuments," has started in Antwerp since 2017 under an alternating system with Bruges that began in 2022, according to VRT NWS. Antwerp hosts in even years, Bruges in odd years, with the current contract concluding after Bruges' 2027 hosting. "There are many cities interested, but unfortunately only a few will be chosen," said **Thomas Van Den Spiegel**, CEO of race organizer Flanders Classics in the VRT report. "We are satisfied with the start in Antwerp, but conversations about the future still need to be started."

With "dozens" of other Flemish cities reportedly expressing interest in hosting the prestigious start, Flanders Classics holds significant negotiating power. The organization has indicated that future contracts may require cities to pay "more than 500,000 euros"—potentially increasing the financial burden on host cities.

## The Case for Continuation

Proponents argue the investment delivers substantial city marketing value that transcends direct economic returns. "From a city marketing perspective, it would be stupid not to continue organizing the start in Antwerp," said Annick Schramme, Professor of Cultural Management at the University of Antwerp in the VRT NWS report. "It works connecting for the residents of your city, international press comes, and cycling tourists even come from other countries to Antwerp to catch the start."

Antwerp's Sports Alderman Stijn De Rooster (N-VA) emphasized the event's alignment with the city's broader sports strategy. "If we want to continue profiling ourselves as 'sports capital of Flanders,' then it's not illogical to have such a cycling monument start here," he told VRT NWS. "The start of the Tour of Flanders is very valuable. It's good for our hospitality sector, certainly for the cycling cafes, and it brings people closer together."

Schramme noted that while the event isn't a multi-day affair that generates significant overnight stays, the publicity value is substantial. "There are no figures to be found about it, but it can be very interesting to perform impact measurements on that," she said, advocating for Antwerp to "go all out" to remain the start location in coming years.

## The Critical Perspective

Not all experts view the €500,000 annual expenditure as optimal use of public funds. Inge Derom, Professor of Sports Management at VUB, questions whether the investment serves Antwerp's diverse population. "You can ask yourself as a resident whether all that money couldn't be better spent on something else," Derom said. "On a societal level, there may be more interesting events."

Derom highlighted cycling's demographic challenges, noting that the sport "struggles with the problem that it appeals less to youth and no diverse audience." She suggested alternative investments might better serve Antwerp's community: "Certain urban sports (skating, BMX,...) have much more potential to appeal to that diverse audience."

The professor also pointed to disruption caused by the event, with road closures and traffic impacts affecting residents who choose to avoid the area during race preparations. While approximately 20,000 spectators attend the start, Derom questioned whether this justifies the substantial public investment and logistical challenges.

## Strategic Considerations

The debate reflects broader tensions in modern sports event hosting. Schramme emphasized that successful event integration requires more than just financial investment. "You have to really try to anchor that start in the city, and that happens too little now," she observed, contrasting Antwerp's approach with the deeper community integration seen in the Flemish Ardennes where the race finishes.

Longer-term contracts, like Middelkerke's 10-year agreement to host the Gent-Wevelgem start, could provide more stability for better event integration. However, with multiple cities vying for the Tour of Flanders start, Flanders Classics may prefer maintaining competitive pressure rather than committing to long-term partnerships.

Antwerp's position is further complicated by its broader sports portfolio. "Next week there is the Urban Sports Week in Park Spoor Noord," noted De Rooster. "That attracts a totally different audience than the Tour of Flanders and that's fine." This suggests the city views different events as serving different demographic and strategic purposes rather than competing for the same resources.

## What's Next

With the 2026 start underway and 2027 already allocated to Bruges, decision timelines are becoming pressing. Negotiations for post-2027 arrangements are expected to begin within the coming year, giving Antwerp officials limited time to build their case for continuation.

The city faces competing pressures: maintaining tradition and international visibility versus addressing criticisms about return on investment and demographic reach. As De Rooster cautiously noted, "I don't want to get ahead of the negotiations, but I'm certainly asking to keep the start in Antwerp. The cooperation with Bruges was positive in recent years, so that's also on the table."

Whether stars like Mathieu van der Poel, Tadej Pogacar, and Wout van Aert will continue starting their Tour of Flanders journeys in Antwerp beyond this weekend remains uncertain. The decision will reveal much about how Flemish cities value traditional sporting events in an era of increasing scrutiny over public spending and demographic inclusivity in sports.

As the peloton rolls out from Antwerp's Grote Markt this Sunday, city officials will be watching not just the race, but the broader debate about what constitutes valuable public investment in sporting heritage versus modern community needs.
