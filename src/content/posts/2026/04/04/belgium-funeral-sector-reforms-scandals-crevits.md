# Belgium Announces Funeral Sector Reforms After Scandals

Flemish Minister Hilde Crevits has announced sweeping reforms to Belgium's funeral sector following a series of scandals involving unethical practices. The new regulations, approved by the Flemish Government on April 4, 2026, will ban "whisper deals" between funeral homes and care institutions, require mandatory hearses for body transport, and increase price transparency to protect vulnerable consumers during bereavement.

According to the [official announcement on Minister Crevits' website](https://www.hildecrevits.be/gedaan_met_commerci_le_deals_in_de_uitvaartsector_minister_crevits_legt_hervormingen_op_tafel), the reforms represent a complete overhaul of outdated 2004 legislation governing cemeteries and corpse care in Flanders. "We were truly shocked by the incidents that have occurred in recent weeks and months," Crevits stated, referring to recent revelations about disrespectful treatment of deceased individuals.

## Context and Background

The funeral sector reforms come in response to multiple scandals that have shaken public confidence in Belgium's death care industry. In September 2025, investigations by [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2025/09/16/uitvaartondernemers-kopen-lichamen-van-woonzorgcentra-en-zieke/) first revealed "whisper deals" where nursing homes and hospitals accepted payments of up to €250 per body from funeral homes for preferential access to deceased individuals. This March, authorities discovered an illegal, unlicensed mortuary operating in Zaventem, further highlighting regulatory failures.

Minister Crevits emphasized the ethical imperative behind the reforms: "The body of a deceased person must absolutely not be an object of commerce. In some situations, there is disrespectful treatment of deceased people's bodies. And that is actually very serious." With average funeral costs in Belgium reaching approximately €6,500, as [reported by VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/03/strengere-regels-uitvaartsector/), vulnerable bereaved families have faced both emotional distress and potential exploitation.

## Key Reforms and Measures

The comprehensive reform package includes several critical measures designed to increase professionalism and transparency in the funeral sector:

**1. Ban on Commercial Activities:** The new decree will prohibit all commercial activities involving deceased bodies, specifically targeting "whisper deals" between funeral homes and care institutions that were first exposed in September 2025. It will also forbid crematoria from offering bulk discounts for multiple bodies, a practice Crevits called "totally disrespectful" in her official statement.

**2. Mandatory Hearses:** Deceased individuals must now be transported exclusively in clearly recognizable hearses, banning the practice of stacking multiple bodies in anonymous vans. "Today, multiple bodies can be stacked in an anonymous van and transported, which is difficult to reconcile with respectful and dignified treatment," the minister explained in her announcement.

**3. Enhanced Price Transparency:** Funeral service providers will be required to provide clear breakdowns of all costs and fees. Current regulations mandate price lists but lack transparency about how costs are calculated, leaving bereaved families uncertain about what they're paying for.

**4. Home Preservation Rules:** For families choosing home preservation, the use of cooling plates will become mandatory, with limits on the number of days a body can be kept at home to ensure dignity and respect. These rules address concerns raised by the discovery of an illegal, unlicensed mortuary in Zaventem in March 2026, which highlighted gaps in current regulations.

**5. Consumer Choice Protection:** The reforms guarantee families "reasonable time" to choose funeral services, protecting them from high-pressure tactics that sometimes give them only four hours to make decisions. This addresses concerns about commercial influence from care providers steering families toward specific funeral homes.

## Sector Response and Implementation

The Funeral Union Flanders, representing industry providers, will be consulted during the development of the detailed decree. Minister Crevits acknowledged the need for sector input while emphasizing the urgency of reform. The Flemish Government has exclusive competence over corpse care in Belgium's complex federal structure, giving it authority to implement these changes throughout the Flemish region.

Implementation details, including specific sanctions for violations and the exact timeline for the new rules to take effect, remain to be determined through the decree development process. However, the political commitment from the Flemish Government suggests rapid progress on the legislative framework.

## Analysis and Implications

These reforms represent a significant shift in Belgium's approach to death care regulation, moving from a primarily administrative framework to one emphasizing dignity, transparency, and consumer protection. The measures address both immediate ethical concerns and longer-term structural issues in the sector.

Economically, the reforms may lead to increased operational costs for some funeral service providers, particularly those requiring investment in proper hearses and cooling equipment. However, proponents argue that these costs are necessary to ensure dignified treatment and that transparent pricing will ultimately benefit both consumers and ethical providers.

The Belgian reforms could set precedents for other European countries facing similar challenges in their funeral sectors. Many nations have grappled with balancing commercial interests with the need for dignified treatment of the deceased, particularly as aging populations increase demand for funeral services.

## What's Next

With the Flemish Government's approval secured, Minister Crevits will now oversee the development of the comprehensive new decree. Stakeholder consultations with funeral service providers, consumer advocates, and care institutions will shape the detailed regulations.

Key questions remain about how the reforms will align with regulations in Wallonia and Brussels, Belgium's other regions. While Flanders has exclusive competence over corpse care, coordinated approaches across regions could emerge as best practices.

The success of these reforms will depend on effective enforcement mechanisms and ongoing monitoring to ensure compliance. As Minister Crevits noted, "Respect and reverence will form the common thread" through all aspects of the new regulations, reflecting broader societal expectations about how we treat the deceased and support the bereaved.

For Belgian families facing the difficult process of arranging funeral services, these reforms promise greater transparency, choice, and assurance that their loved ones will be treated with the dignity they deserve.
