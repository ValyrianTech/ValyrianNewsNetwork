# Artemis II: Humans Venture Further Into Deep Space Since 1972

The Artemis II mission has officially reached the midpoint of its journey to the moon, marking a historic milestone as the first crewed flight to venture this far into deep space since the Apollo era ended in 1972. On April 4, 2026, NASA confirmed that the crew has passed the 200,000-kilometer mark, signaling the beginning of a new epoch in human lunar exploration.

## A New Perspective from the Void

As the crew pushed deeper into the void, Mission Commander Reid Wiseman transmitted the first high-resolution images of Earth from their current position. According to [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/liveblog/artemis-ii-is-halverwege-de-maan-astronauten-sturen-eerste-foto~1775231449108/), these images capture the entirety of the planet, including visible auroras in both the northern and southern hemispheres, providing a poignant reminder of global unity.

This mission is not merely a technical achievement but a social one. The crew includes Victor Glover, the first person of color to fly to the moon, and Christina Koch, the first woman to do so. Joining them is Jeremy Hansen, the first non-American astronaut on a lunar mission, representing Canada. Together, they embody a more inclusive era of exploration than the missions of the 1960s.

## Navigating the Realities of Deep Space

The journey has not been without its human and technical frictions. Shortly after reaching the midpoint, the crew reported a "burning smell" emanating from the onboard toilet system, described as being similar to an old electric heater. While such anomalies can be alarming in the vacuum of space, NASA flight teams successfully troubleshot the issue, classifying it as a non-critical anomaly and restoring the system to normal operation.

Beyond the immediate quirks of ship life, the mission carries a heavier technical shadow: the Orion heat shield. Following the uncrewed Artemis I mission in 2023, significant unexpected damage was found on the shield. Rather than a full replacement, NASA opted to modify the re-entry angle to reduce thermal stress—a decision that remains a point of scrutiny for aerospace experts as the crew prepares for their return.

## The Geopolitical Stakes

While the public focuses on the breathtaking imagery, the Artemis II mission is a critical piece of a larger geopolitical chessboard. The United States is racing to establish a permanent lunar presence, a strategic move designed to counter China's CNSA. Beijing has signaled its intent to land a "taikonaut" on the lunar south pole by 2030.

The rivalry, often termed the "New Space Race," is driven by more than just prestige. The lunar south pole is believed to contain water ice and valuable minerals. Establishing a presence there is seen as essential for both scientific advancement and the strategic control of future lunar resources.

## The Road Home

Artemis II is performing a "free-return" trajectory, meaning the spacecraft will not land on the moon but will instead loop around it, using lunar gravity to sling the crew back toward Earth. The mission is expected to reach its maximum distance from Earth—approximately 406,000 kilometers—on April 6.

The mission is scheduled to conclude with a splashdown in the Pacific Ocean on April 10 (local time) or April 11 (Belgian time). Success here will pave the way for Artemis III, which aims to return humans to the lunar surface by 2028, transforming the moon from a place humans once visited into a place where they can stay.
