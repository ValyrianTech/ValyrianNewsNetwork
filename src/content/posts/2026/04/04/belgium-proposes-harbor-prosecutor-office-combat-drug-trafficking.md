# Belgium Plans Harbor Prosecutor's Office to Fight Drug Crime

Belgian Justice Minister Annelies Verlinden has proposed establishing a specialized harbor prosecutor's office to intensify the fight against drug trafficking through the Port of Antwerp, [according to VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/03/minister-verlinden-wil-havenparket-oprichten-in-strijd-tegen-dru/). The proposal received approval in the Council of Ministers on April 3, 2026, with funding allocated starting from 2027.

## Context: Antwerp as Europe's Drug Gateway

The Port of Antwerp has become Europe's primary entry point for cocaine, overtaking Rotterdam in recent years. In 2025 alone, Belgian customs seized approximately 55 tonnes of cocaine at the port, representing a 24% increase from the previous year's 44 tonnes. While significantly below the record 120+ tonnes seized in 2023, the volume underscores the port's attractiveness to criminal networks.

Minister Verlinden's cabinet stated that "the harbor of Antwerp has been an attractive target for criminal networks for years, who misuse the port to organize their illegal trade." The minister emphasized that "the consequences of this trade reach far beyond the port itself. The growth of the cocaine supply on the European market undermines public health, increases security risks, disrupts the economy, and damages local communities."

## The Proposed Harbor Prosecutor's Office

The exact structure and powers of the proposed harbor prosecutor's office remain undefined, but the initiative represents a significant step toward specialized prosecution of port-related drug crimes. According to [Nieuwsblad](https://www.nieuwsblad.be/binnenland/minister-verlinden-wil-havenparket-oprichten-in-strijd-tegen-drugscriminaliteit/145199994.html), the new authority would focus specifically on combating organized drug criminality in the Antwerp port area.

Funding for the new prosecutor's office will be available starting from 2027, indicating a phased implementation approach. Minister Verlinden has also promised to strengthen courts and tribunals to ensure every link in the judicial chain can optimally combat drug trafficking.

## Statistical Evidence of the Problem

The proposal comes amid alarming statistics about drug-related crime in Antwerp port. In 2025, Belgian courts handled 80 cases linked to drug imports via the Antwerp port, resulting in 242 convictions totaling 918 years of prison sentences. The financial penalties were equally substantial: over €4.6 million in fines, nearly €403 million in confiscations, and 1,705 years of port bans.

Customs seizures in 2025 included approximately 55,000 kg of cocaine and 20,000 kg of cannabis in the port. These figures highlight both the scale of the trafficking problem and the substantial enforcement efforts already underway.

## Political and Security Context

The proposal reflects growing concerns about Belgium's role as a European drug trafficking hub. In October 2025, an Antwerp judge anonymously warned that Belgium was evolving into a "narco-state" with the rule of law under threat. The new De Wever government has made combating organized crime a priority, with this initiative representing one of several measures to address the problem.

Previous government efforts have included increased container scanning, enhanced cooperation with Latin American countries, strengthened financial investigations to follow money trails, and the PortWatch system—an anonymous reporting platform for suspicious activities in and around ports.

## Analysis: Implications and Challenges

The creation of a specialized harbor prosecutor's office represents a targeted approach to a complex international problem. By concentrating expertise on port-related drug crimes, authorities hope to improve investigation quality, prosecution success rates, and ultimately deterrence.

However, several challenges remain. The exact organizational structure and powers of the new prosecutor's office need definition, and full operational capacity may take years to develop. Success will depend on adequate staffing with specialized expertise, effective coordination with existing law enforcement agencies, and sustained political support.

Internationally, the initiative could serve as a model for other European ports facing similar challenges with drug trafficking. It may also enhance collaboration with source countries in Latin America, where much of the cocaine originates.

## What's Next

The proposal marks the beginning of a process rather than an immediate solution. Between now and 2027, authorities will need to develop the detailed structure, legal framework, and operational procedures for the harbor prosecutor's office. This will involve consultations with judicial authorities, port operators, customs officials, and international partners.

Minister Verlinden's commitment to also strengthen courts and tribunals suggests a comprehensive approach to the judicial response to drug trafficking. The effectiveness of these measures will ultimately be measured by their impact on reducing drug flows through Antwerp port and dismantling the criminal networks that control them.

As Europe's busiest port continues to grapple with its role as a drug trafficking hub, Belgium's specialized prosecutorial approach represents an innovative response to a persistent transnational crime challenge. The success or failure of this initiative will have implications not only for Belgium but for European efforts to combat organized drug crime more broadly.
