# Belgian Senate Votes to Enable Its Own Abolition After Tumultuous Debate

The Belgian Senate has taken a historic step toward its own dissolution, approving a constitutional amendment that would allow its abolition within the current legislative term. In a dramatic Friday session marked by political maneuvering and coalition tensions, senators voted 36-8-14 to revise Article 195 of the Constitution, [according to BRF](https://brf.be/national/2064701/). The move represents a significant milestone in Belgium's ongoing political reforms, though the actual abolition still requires several more parliamentary votes.

## Context: A Senate Under Scrutiny

Established in 1831 as part of Belgium's foundational constitutional framework, the Senate has faced decades of debate about its role and efficiency in the country's complex federal system. Prime Minister Bart De Wever of the N-VA has been a vocal critic, stating "Belgium's Senate belongs to the past." The current push for abolition gained momentum in June 2025 when the federal government reached agreement on eliminating the chamber as part of broader political reforms.

Belgium's bicameral system has long been criticized for creating unnecessary bureaucracy in one of Europe's most intricate federal structures. Supporters of abolition argue that streamlining governance will improve efficiency, while critics warn that eliminating the Senate removes an important check on power and reduces representation for linguistic communities.

## The Vote Breakdown and Constitutional Procedure

The Friday vote specifically amended Article 195 of the Constitution to allow the addition of a transitional provision. Normally, constitutional articles must be declared revisable in one legislature with actual revision occurring in the next. The amendment enables revision of all Senate-related constitutional articles within the same legislative term, [as detailed by VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/04/03/senaat-maakt-zijn-eigen-afschaffing-mogelijk-na-lang-en-tumultue/).

The vote tally revealed deep political divisions:

- **In favor (36)**: N-VA, Vooruit, CD&V, Vlaams Belang, Eva Platteau (Groen), and 4 members of Les Engagés
- **Against (8)**: PS, Liesa Scholzen (MR - German-speaking Community Senator), Celia Groothedde (Groen), Hajib El Hajjaji (Ecolo)
- **Abstained (14)**: MR (entire faction), PVDA-PTB, Anne-Catherine Goffinet (Les Engagés)

The two-thirds majority required for constitutional changes was achieved, but the path to that majority exposed significant fractures within the governing "Arizona" coalition.

## Political Drama and Coalition Tensions

The plenary session was marked by what [Le Soir's editorial](https://www.lesoir.be/738924/article/2026-04-04/le-senat-ne-meurt-pas-dans-la-dignite) described as "hypocrisy and three-cushion billiards," with parties engaging in tactical maneuvering that left none looking particularly good. The MR, a governing party, requested an adjournment just before the vote and when the Senate president didn't immediately grant it, MR senators walked out of the chamber.

MR and Les Engagés ultimately abstained to avoid achieving the two-thirds majority with support from either the extreme left (PVDA-PTB) or extreme right (Vlaams Belang). The coalition agreement explicitly prohibits forming majorities with these parties. However, the abstentions meant Vlaams Belang's support became necessary to reach the required threshold.

German-speaking Community Senator Liesa Scholzen of the MR voted against the amendment after her own proposed changes were rejected. Her opposition highlighted concerns about representation for linguistic minorities in a post-Senate Belgium.

The political drama was further complicated by simultaneous federal cabinet discussions on energy prices. MR chairman Georges-Louis Bouchez had threatened to link Senate abolition to fuel price measures, calling for a price cap via fiscal mechanisms. The government ultimately rejected MR's fuel price cap proposal, opting instead for targeted support for vulnerable households and commuting employees to be presented on April 17.

## Analysis: Efficiency Versus Oversight

The Senate's move toward self-abolition represents more than just procedural change—it reflects an ongoing debate about the balance between efficient governance and institutional checks in Belgium's complex political landscape. Proponents argue that eliminating the Senate will streamline decision-making in a country often criticized for bureaucratic inertia.

However, critics point to the Senate's role as a chamber of reflection and its function in representing Belgium's linguistic communities. The Senate also serves as a check on the Chamber of Representatives, reviewing legislation and providing a forum for broader debate on national issues.

The vote also reveals the fragility of Belgium's current governing coalition. The tensions between MR and its coalition partners, particularly over tactical voting and energy policy linkages, suggest underlying disagreements that could affect other government initiatives.

## What's Next in the Abolition Process

Friday's vote represents only the first step in a multi-stage process. Several more parliamentary votes are required before actual abolition can occur. The amended Article 195 now allows for a transitional provision that will enable revision of all Senate-related constitutional articles within the current legislature.

The government must now develop detailed plans for redistributing the Senate's functions. Key questions remain about how linguistic community representation will be preserved and what mechanisms will replace the Senate's legislative review role.

As Belgium moves closer to eliminating an institution that has existed for nearly two centuries, the debate will likely intensify. The Senate's potential abolition marks a fundamental transformation of Belgian democracy, reflecting both pragmatic concerns about governance efficiency and deeper questions about the structure of federal power in one of Europe's most complex political systems.

The coming months will reveal whether this historic step leads to the streamlined governance its proponents promise or whether the loss of an institutional check creates unforeseen challenges for Belgium's delicate federal balance.
