# Fact Check: Police Officers Don't Die 10 Years Earlier, Data Shows

A controversial claim that Belgian police officers die 10 years earlier than other civil servants has been circulating in media and political debates, but a [VRT NWS fact check investigation](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2026/03/31/factcheck-check-leven-agenten-10-jaar-minder-lang-cijfers-bewijz/) reveals the statistic is based on flawed methodology and incomplete data. While police work undoubtedly involves significant stressors, experts say the specific 10-year gap claim is not supported by comprehensive evidence.

## The Origin of the Claim

The debate began on March 25, 2026, when police union VSOA made the alarming claim in [Het Nieuwsblad](https://www.nieuwsblad.be/binnenland/politiebonden-trekken-aan-alarmbel-gepensioneerde-agenten-sterven-bijna-tien-jaar-eerder-dan-gemiddelde-belg/144076888.html), citing Federal Pension Service data showing retired police officers die at an average age of 72.5 years compared to 82.4 years for other civil servants. The Christian trade union ACV quickly [repeated the claim](https://www.hetacv.be/acv-openbare-diensten/nieuws/2026/03/25/agenten-leven-tien-jaar-minder-lang) on their website, framing it as evidence of the demanding nature of police work.

Unions pointed to the physically demanding and dangerous aspects of police work, irregular hours, night shifts, and high psychosocial pressure as potential explanations for the alleged gap. The claim quickly gained traction in discussions about retirement age and pension equity for hazardous professions.

## Methodological Flaws Exposed

Within a day, the Federal Pension Service issued a right of reply that fundamentally challenged the statistics. The service revealed that VSOA's figures only included integrated police officers (federal and local police established since 2001) who retired between 2021-2025 and died in the same period.

"This figure only looks at a part (integrated police) of the whole (integrated police + ex-gendarmerie + ex-municipal police)," the service stated. "If you include those groups, the average age at death is 78.5 years, not 72.5."

This correction reduced the gap from 10 years to approximately 3.5 years compared to other civil servants (who average 82.0 years). But even this revised difference requires cautious interpretation, according to experts consulted by VRT.

## Expert Analysis: Why the Numbers Mislead

Demographer Patrick Deboosere of Vrije Universiteit Brussel (VUB) explained the fundamental problem: "On the basis of these figures we can prove almost nothing because everything depends on the composition of the groups."

He noted that comparing police and civil servant groups requires controlling for age, gender composition, and socioeconomic backgrounds—data that wasn't available in the initial analysis. Without this information, meaningful comparisons are impossible.

Pension expert Jeroen Kerkhof (VUB, Ghent University, EDHEC Business School) identified another critical flaw: survivorship bias. "Many people in that system are still not deceased. Everyone who lives long and dies in 2040 is missing from the calculation. That gives you an average that automatically lies lower."

Biostatistician Geert Molenberghs (UHasselt and KU Leuven) added context about life expectancy determinants: "Life expectancy is determined by many different factors, including socioeconomic background. Night work can be a factor, but is often not the only one."

## What the Data Actually Shows

When all former police groups are included—integrated police, former gendarmerie (rijkswachters), and former municipal police—the average age at death rises to 78.5 years. This represents a 3.5-year difference from other civil servants, not the dramatic 10-year gap initially claimed.

Comparative data from the articles shows:
- **Police officers:** 78.5 years (corrected figure)
- **Military personnel:** 80.6 years
- **Teachers:** 84.2 years
- **Other civil servants:** 82.4 years

The Federal Pension Service was unequivocal in its assessment: "On the basis of these figures you cannot state that police officers live 10 years less than other civil servants."

## International Research Context

The fact-check investigation also examined international literature on police life expectancy. Some [American studies](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4734369/) suggest police may have lower life expectancy or die earlier after retirement, while [others](https://www.politifact.com/article/2021/jun/08/do-police-officers-have-shorter-life-expectancy-ge/) show no or smaller differences. There's no unanimous conclusion in the international literature.

For Europe and Belgium, some studies point to negative life factors police encounter—such as threats, violence, and irregular hours—but lack concrete comparative data. According to the VRT investigation, there is currently "no recent reliable comparison of average age at death after retirement between police and other professions in Belgium."

## Broader Implications and Policy Debates

Despite the statistical flaws in the specific claim, the debate has raised legitimate questions about police working conditions and policy. Vincent Houssin, vice-president of police union VSOA, acknowledged the need for better research while maintaining that real concerns exist: "There is definitely something going on and that needs further investigation. We don't want to retire earlier, we mainly want to live as long as people in other professions."

The discussion touches on broader policy questions about retirement age for hazardous professions, pension equity, and workplace health measures. As [P-Magazine noted in its analysis](https://p-magazine.com/nl/articles/agenten-sterven-vroeger-structureel-probleem-of-statistische-vertekening), "Statistieken hebben de reputatie van objectieve waarheid, maar in de praktijk zijn ze zelden meer dan een zorgvuldig geconstrueerde versie van de werkelijkheid."

## Research Gaps and the Path Forward

Experts identified several critical research needs:
1. **Comprehensive longitudinal studies** tracking integrated police cohorts over coming decades as they age
2. **Multivariate analyses** controlling for socioeconomic factors, lifestyle, and gender composition
3. **Belgium-specific comparative research** on police versus other professions' life expectancy
4. **Intervention research** identifying workplace measures that could mitigate health impacts of police work

Ria Janvier, a pension expert at the University of Antwerp, framed the issue in policy terms: "The discussion about the normal retirement age—the age that takes into account the probable survival time in light of a number of socioeconomic characteristics—is not being conducted."

## Conclusion: Statistical Literacy Matters

The fact-check investigation demonstrates how selective data presentation can create misleading narratives. While police work involves significant stressors that may impact health and longevity, the specific 10-year gap statistic results from methodological limitations including selection bias and incomplete data.

The debate reveals deeper questions about how society values hazardous professions and how pension systems should account for different life expectancies. While unions rightly highlight the demanding nature of police work, policymakers require more robust, longitudinal research before making significant changes to retirement or pension systems.

**Bottom line:** The claim that Belgian police officers die 10 years earlier than other civil servants is statistically unsound, but the underlying concern about police health and working conditions merits serious attention and further research.
